GANs. Two networks, one adversarial game.

Every model so far has been discriminative — given input, predict a label or next token. GANs (Goodfellow et al., 2014) introduced a fundamentally different paradigm: two networks competing against each other. A generator creates fake data; a discriminator tries to tell fakes from reals. As they train, the generator gets better at fooling the discriminator, and the discriminator gets better at catching fakes — until the generator produces data indistinguishable from real. For CineMatch, this means generating synthetic movie posters, augmenting sparse interaction data, and understanding a training paradigm that shaped the field from 2014 to 2020.

01 — The game
The adversarial game.

A GAN has two networks trained simultaneously:

Generator G. Takes random noise z ~ N(0, I) as input and produces a fake sample (an image, a sequence, a data point). G wants its output to look as real as possible.

Discriminator D. Takes a sample (either real from the dataset or fake from G) and outputs a probability that the sample is real. D wants to correctly classify reals as real and fakes as fake.

Training alternates: update D to get better at catching fakes, then update G to get better at fooling D. The two losses push against each other — hence "adversarial."

minG maxD   Ex~data[log D(x)] + Ez~noise[log(1 − D(G(z)))] (1.1)

D maximizes this expression (correctly classifying reals and fakes increases both terms). G minimizes it (making D(G(z)) closer to 1, which drives log(1 − D(G(z))) toward −∞).

The counterfeiter analogy

G is a counterfeiter trying to print fake currency. D is a detective trying to catch fakes. As the counterfeiter gets better, the detective must improve. As the detective improves, the counterfeiter must adapt. At Nash equilibrium, the counterfeiter produces perfect fakes and the detective is reduced to coin-flipping: D(x) = 0.5 for all x.

Math Core — deriving the optimal discriminator.

For a fixed generator G, we can find the optimal discriminator D* analytically. The value function V(G, D) for a single point x is:

V(x) = pdata(x) · log D(x) + pG(x) · log(1 − D(x)) (1.2)

Taking ∂V/∂D(x) = 0 and solving:

pdata(x) / D*(x) − pG(x) / (1 − D*(x)) = 0    →    D*(x) = pdata(x) / (pdata(x) + pG(x)) (1.3)

When pG = pdata (optimal generator), D*(x) = pdata/(pdata + pdata) = 0.5 everywhere — confirming the equilibrium. When pG(x) = 0 at some x (the generator hasn't learned to produce samples there), D*(x) = 1 — the discriminator is perfectly confident that real samples from that region are real.

Worked example — one D and G update step.

Hand-computing GAN gradients on a 1D toy problem
Setup: real data x ~ N(3, 1). Generator: G(z) = w·z + b, z ~ N(0,1). Current: w=0.5, b=1.0 → G generates ~ N(1.0, 0.25).Generator is centered at 1.0, needs to reach 3.0
Discriminator: D(x) = σ(v·x + c). Current: v=1.0, c=−2.0 → D(3.0) = σ(1.0) = 0.73, D(1.0) = σ(−1.0) = 0.27.D correctly assigns higher probability to real (3.0) than fake (1.0)

02 — Training loop
The training loop — alternating optimization.
import torch, torch.nn as nn

# Simplified GAN training loop
for epoch in range(n_epochs):
    for real_batch in dataloader:
        B = real_batch.size(0)

        # ===== Train Discriminator =====
        z = torch.randn(B, latent_dim, device=device)
        fake = G(z).detach()  # detach: don't backprop into G here

        d_real = D(real_batch)
        d_fake = D(fake)
        loss_D = -torch.mean(torch.log(d_real + 1e-8)
                           + torch.log(1 - d_fake + 1e-8))

        opt_D.zero_grad()
        loss_D.backward()
        opt_D.step()

        # ===== Train Generator =====
        z = torch.randn(B, latent_dim, device=device)
        fake = G(z)
        d_fake = D(fake)
        # Non-saturating loss: maximize log D(G(z)) instead of minimize log(1-D(G(z)))
        loss_G = -torch.mean(torch.log(d_fake + 1e-8))

        opt_G.zero_grad()
        loss_G.backward()
        opt_G.step()

The non-saturating trick.

The original minimax objective tells G to minimize log(1 − D(G(z))). But early in training, D easily rejects G's outputs, so D(G(z)) ≈ 0 and log(1 − 0) ≈ 0 — the gradient to G is nearly zero. Training stalls. The non-saturating variant flips the sign: G maximizes log D(G(z)) instead. When D(G(z)) ≈ 0, log(0) → −∞, giving G large gradients to improve. Same equilibrium, much better gradient flow early on.

Math Core — gradient comparison, saturating vs non-saturating.

Let p = D(G(z)) be the discriminator's belief that the fake is real. The generator's gradient through D depends on which loss we use:

Saturating:   ∂/∂p [log(1 − p)] = −1/(1 − p) (2.1)
Non-saturating:   ∂/∂p [−log(p)] = −1/p (2.2)
Gradient magnitude at different D(G(z)) values
Early training: D(G(z)) = p = 0.01 (D easily spots fakes)
Saturating gradient: |−1/(1−0.01)| = 1.01 — moderate, but this multiplies ∂p/∂G which is also tiny (D is flat near 0)Effective gradient to G ≈ 0.01 × 1.01 ≈ 0.01 — nearly zero
Non-saturating gradient: |−1/0.01| = 100 — huge signalEffective gradient to G ≈ 0.01 × 100 = 1.0 — strong learning signal
Late training: D(G(z)) = p = 0.45 (G is nearly fooling D)
Saturating: |−1/(1−0.45)| = 1.82. Non-saturating: |−1/0.45| = 2.22.When p ≈ 0.5, both losses give similar gradients — the difference only matters early

The non-saturating loss provides ~100× stronger gradients when G is weak (p ≈ 0) — exactly when G needs the most learning signal. This single change is the difference between a GAN that trains and one that doesn't.

03 — DCGAN
DCGAN — the convolutional recipe that made GANs work.

Early GANs with fully-connected layers produced blurry, unstable results. DCGAN (Radford et al., 2015) established architectural guidelines that made GAN training reliable for images:

Generator: transposed convolutions (ConvTranspose2d) to upsample from latent vector to image. Batch normalization after every layer except the output. ReLU activations, Tanh on the output.

Discriminator: strided convolutions to downsample from image to scalar. Batch normalization (except input and output). LeakyReLU activations. No pooling layers — strided convolutions handle downsampling.

class DCGANGenerator(nn.Module):
    """Generate 64x64 images from 100-dim latent vector."""
    def __init__(self, latent=100, channels=3, feat=64):
        super().__init__()
        self.net = nn.Sequential(
            # z: (B, 100, 1, 1) → (B, 512, 4, 4)
            nn.ConvTranspose2d(latent, feat*8, 4, 1, 0, bias=False),
            nn.BatchNorm2d(feat*8), nn.ReLU(True),
            # → (B, 256, 8, 8)
            nn.ConvTranspose2d(feat*8, feat*4, 4, 2, 1, bias=False),
            nn.BatchNorm2d(feat*4), nn.ReLU(True),
            # → (B, 128, 16, 16)
            nn.ConvTranspose2d(feat*4, feat*2, 4, 2, 1, bias=False),
            nn.BatchNorm2d(feat*2), nn.ReLU(True),
            # → (B, 64, 32, 32)
            nn.ConvTranspose2d(feat*2, feat, 4, 2, 1, bias=False),
            nn.BatchNorm2d(feat), nn.ReLU(True),
            # → (B, 3, 64, 64)
            nn.ConvTranspose2d(feat, channels, 4, 2, 1, bias=False),
            nn.Tanh(),  # output in [-1, 1]
        )

    def forward(self, z):
        return self.net(z.view(-1, z.size(1), 1, 1))

Tensor shape walkthrough — every layer of the generator.

ConvTranspose2d output formula: H_out = (H_in − 1) × stride − 2 × padding + kernel_size
Input: z reshaped to (B, 100, 1, 1)100-dim latent vector as a 1×1 "image" with 100 channels
ConvTranspose2d(100, 512, 4, 1, 0): H = (1−1)×1 − 0 + 4 = 4 → (B, 512, 4, 4)Params: 100×512×4×4 = 819,200
ConvTranspose2d(512, 256, 4, 2, 1): H = (4−1)×2 − 2 + 4 = 8 → (B, 256, 8, 8)Params: 512×256×4×4 = 2,097,152
ConvTranspose2d(256, 128, 4, 2, 1): H = (8−1)×2 − 2 + 4 = 16 → (B, 128, 16, 16)Params: 256×128×4×4 = 524,288
ConvTranspose2d(128, 64, 4, 2, 1): H = 32 → (B, 64, 32, 32)Params: 128×64×4×4 = 131,072
ConvTranspose2d(64, 3, 4, 2, 1): H = 64 → (B, 3, 64, 64) — RGB image!Params: 64×3×4×4 = 3,072. Total G params: ~3.6M (+ BN params ~5K)

Build It — the discriminator (mirror architecture).

class DCGANDiscriminator(nn.Module):
    """Classify 64x64 images as real/fake."""
    def __init__(self, channels=3, feat=64):
        super().__init__()
        self.net = nn.Sequential(
            # (B, 3, 64, 64) → (B, 64, 32, 32) — no BN on first layer
            nn.Conv2d(channels, feat, 4, 2, 1, bias=False),
            nn.LeakyReLU(0.2, inplace=True),
            # → (B, 128, 16, 16)
            nn.Conv2d(feat, feat*2, 4, 2, 1, bias=False),
            nn.BatchNorm2d(feat*2), nn.LeakyReLU(0.2, inplace=True),
            # → (B, 256, 8, 8)
            nn.Conv2d(feat*2, feat*4, 4, 2, 1, bias=False),
            nn.BatchNorm2d(feat*4), nn.LeakyReLU(0.2, inplace=True),
            # → (B, 512, 4, 4)
            nn.Conv2d(feat*4, feat*8, 4, 2, 1, bias=False),
            nn.BatchNorm2d(feat*8), nn.LeakyReLU(0.2, inplace=True),
            # → (B, 1, 1, 1) — scalar output
            nn.Conv2d(feat*8, 1, 4, 1, 0, bias=False),
            nn.Sigmoid(),
        )

    def forward(self, img):
        return self.net(img).view(-1)  # (B,) probabilities

# D mirrors G: Conv2d (stride 2) downsamples where G's ConvTranspose2d upsamples
# LeakyReLU (not ReLU) — avoids dead neurons in D, critical for gradient flow to G
# No BN on first conv — applying BN to raw pixel input hurts quality
# No BN on D's output — would interfere with the sigmoid probability
# D params: ~3.6M (mirrors G). Total GAN: ~7.2M parameters

Failure case — checkerboard artifacts from ConvTranspose2d.

Artifact you'll see in practice

ConvTranspose2d with stride 2 produces checkerboard patterns.

When kernel_size (4) is not evenly divisible by stride (2), transposed convolution creates overlapping addition patterns in the output — visible as a grid of bright/dark spots. The fix: replace ConvTranspose2d with nn.Upsample(scale_factor=2, mode='bilinear') + nn.Conv2d(in, out, 3, 1, 1). This "upsample then convolve" pattern avoids overlap artifacts entirely. StyleGAN and most modern generators use this approach. If you see a faint grid pattern in your generated posters, checkerboard artifacts are the first thing to check.

DCGAN architectureQuestion 03 / 07
Why does the DCGAN generator use Tanh on its output layer instead of ReLU?
ATanh is faster to compute than ReLU.
BReal images are normalized to [−1, 1]. Tanh outputs exactly this range, so the generator's output matches the data distribution's support. ReLU only outputs [0, ∞), which can't represent negative pixel values in the normalized space.
CTanh has better gradient flow than ReLU in all cases.
DThe discriminator requires Tanh inputs.
04 — Mode collapse
Mode collapse — the GAN failure mode.

The most notorious GAN problem: the generator discovers one output that fools the discriminator and produces only that output (or a small set of similar outputs), ignoring the full diversity of the real data. Asked to generate movie posters, a mode-collapsed GAN might produce only dark-blue horror posters — they're realistic enough to fool D, so G has no incentive to explore other genres.

Mode collapse happens because the minimax game has no explicit diversity term. G is rewarded for fooling D, not for producing diverse outputs. If one region of image space is easy to fool D in, G concentrates there.

Mitigation strategies.

StrategyHow it works
Minibatch discriminationD sees statistics across the batch, not just individual samples — catches low diversity
Feature matchingG minimizes difference between real and fake feature statistics, not D's output directly
Wasserstein GAN (WGAN)Replace JS divergence with Wasserstein distance — provides gradients even when distributions don't overlap
Spectral normalizationNormalize D's weight matrices to control Lipschitz constant — stabilizes training
Mode collapseQuestion 04 / 07
Your CineMatch poster GAN generates beautiful horror posters but nothing else — every output looks like a dark-blue movie poster. What is happening and what's the fix?
AThe dataset only contains horror posters.
BThe latent dimension is too small to encode genre diversity.
CThe learning rate is too high.
DMode collapse. The generator found that dark-blue horror posters consistently fool the discriminator, so it collapsed to producing only those. The minimax objective has no diversity incentive. Fixes: minibatch discrimination (lets D detect low diversity), feature matching (G matches real data statistics, not just D's output), or switching to WGAN with gradient penalty (provides smoother gradients that prevent collapse).
05 — GANs vs diffusion
GANs vs diffusion models — where we are in 2026.

GANs dominated image generation from 2014 to ~2021. Then diffusion models (DDPM, Stable Diffusion, DALL-E 2/3) overtook them for most generation tasks. The key differences:

PropertyGANsDiffusion
Training stabilityFragile (mode collapse, G/D imbalance)Stable (single network, simple loss)
Sample qualitySharp, fast inferenceEqual or better quality, slower inference
DiversityProne to mode collapseFull distribution coverage by design
ControllabilityConditional GAN works but limitedText-conditioned generation (CLIP guidance) is native
Inference speedSingle forward pass (~10ms)Multiple denoising steps (~1s for 50 steps)

GANs remain relevant for real-time applications (super-resolution, video enhancement, game asset generation) where single-pass inference speed matters. For offline generation (marketing images, poster creation, data augmentation), diffusion models have largely won.

CineMatch Integration

GANs in the CineMatch stack.

Poster augmentation. The Phase 3.5 poster classifier was limited by 80K training posters. A conditional GAN (cGAN) conditioned on genre labels generates synthetic posters that augment the training set — effectively 10× the data for rare genres like foreign-language documentary.

User data augmentation. For cold-start users with very few watch events, a GAN trained on watch-sequence distributions can generate plausible synthetic histories, giving the transformer recommender more signal to work with.

Super-resolution. Many older movie posters in the catalog are low-resolution thumbnails. A super-resolution GAN (SRGAN) upscales them to 512×512 for the poster embedding pipeline, improving the CNN feature quality from Module 3.5.

06 — Training tricks
Training tricks — the practitioner's checklist.

GAN training is notoriously fragile. A checklist of tricks that collectively make it work:

TrickWhatWhy
Label smoothingReal labels = 0.9 instead of 1.0Prevents D from becoming too confident
Two timescale LRD's LR 2-4× higher than G'sKeep D ahead of G to provide useful gradients
Spectral normalizationNormalize D's weight matrices by spectral normControls Lipschitz constant, stabilizes training
Progressive growingStart at low resolution, add layers graduallyStabilizes training for high-res outputs
Non-saturating G lossmax log D(G(z)) instead of min log(1−D(G(z)))Better gradients when G is weak
Feature matchingG matches intermediate D features, not outputReduces mode collapse
The G/D balance problem

If D gets too good, G receives no gradient. If G gets too good, D receives no gradient.

GAN training is a balancing act. A perfectly accurate D gives G zero gradient (D(G(z)) = 0 for all z, so ∂loss_G/∂G ≈ 0). A perfectly fooled D gives itself zero gradient (it thinks everything is real). In practice, you train D for 1-5 steps per G step, using a learning rate that keeps D slightly ahead of G but never perfectly accurate. Monitoring both losses is essential — if either flatlines, the other can't learn.

Phase 4 — complete.
