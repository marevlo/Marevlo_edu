CNN architectures. A genealogy of modern vision.

Every image model between 2012 and the rise of transformers was built from the primitives in the last module — but organizing them into something that trained 50+ layers deep required several revolutions. AlexNet proved depth + GPUs + data works. VGG made depth a monotone virtue. Inception discovered multi-scale. ResNet solved the gradient-flow crisis with skip connections. DenseNet pushed connectivity to its logical extreme. Knowing the lineage is how you read modern architectures fluently.

01 — AlexNet
AlexNet (2012) — the moment deep learning took over.

The ImageNet classification challenge had existed since 2010. Every year, the winner improved the top-5 error rate (the fraction of test images where the true class wasn't in the model's top 5 guesses) by a percentage point or two, using hand-engineered features and classical classifiers. In 2011, the winning error rate was 25.8%. In 2012, AlexNet won with 15.3%. The second-place entry had 26.2%. A decade of classical computer vision was overtaken in one paper.

What AlexNet actually was.

Structurally, AlexNet was a deeper, wider LeNet trained with modern tricks. Eight learnable layers — five convolutional, three fully connected. About 60 million parameters. Key ideas that landed together:

  • ReLU activations instead of sigmoid / tanh — faster training, better gradient flow.
  • GPU training. Two GTX 580s. The model was explicitly split across them.
  • Dropout in the dense classifier head to combat overfitting on 1.2M images.
  • Data augmentation — random crops, horizontal flips, color jitter. Expanded effective training set by ~2048×.
  • Large receptive field kernels in early layers — the first conv was 11×11 with stride 4, the second 5×5. Subsequent architectures abandoned this.
import torch.nn as nn

class AlexNet(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, num_classes=1000):
        super().__init__()
        self.features = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Conv2d(3, 96, kernel_size=11, stride=4),    # 54x54x96
            nn.ReLU(inplace=True),
            nn.MaxPool2d(3, 2),                              # 26x26
            nn.Conv2d(96, 256, kernel_size=5, padding=2),    # 26x26x256
            nn.ReLU(inplace=True),
            nn.MaxPool2d(3, 2),                              # 12x12
            nn.Conv2d(256, 384, kernel_size=3, padding=1),   # 12x12x384
            nn.ReLU(inplace=True),
            nn.Conv2d(384, 384, kernel_size=3, padding=1),   # 12x12x384
            nn.ReLU(inplace=True),
            nn.Conv2d(384, 256, kernel_size=3, padding=1),   # 12x12x256
            nn.ReLU(inplace=True),
            nn.MaxPool2d(3, 2),                              # 5x5
        )
        self.classifier = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Flatten(),
            nn.Dropout(0.5),
            nn.Linear(256 * 5 * 5, 4096),
            nn.ReLU(inplace=True),
            nn.Dropout(0.5),
            nn.Linear(4096, 4096),
            nn.ReLU(inplace=True),
            nn.Linear(4096, num_classes),
        )
Why 2012 and not 1998

Three things finally aligned.

Yann LeCun had shown the same ideas work on MNIST in 1998. Why did it take fourteen more years to dominate ImageNet? Compute. The GTX 580s used for AlexNet had ~1.5 TFLOPs — enough to train the 60M-parameter model in about a week. Available GPUs in 1998 were not even close. Data. MNIST has 60K grayscale 28×28 digits; ImageNet has 1.2M 224×224 RGB photos of 1000 classes. Scaling required both capacity and examples. Tricks. ReLU, dropout, aggressive augmentation were each individually important; together they made it possible to train a network that size without collapsing.

02 — VGG
VGG (2014) — depth as a monotone good.

VGG was designed to answer a single question: does depth help? It kept everything simple. Every convolution was 3×3 with padding 1 and stride 1. Every pooling was 2×2 with stride 2. Blocks of 2 to 3 convolutions were followed by a pool to halve spatial dimensions, with channels doubling every block.

The VGG template.

# VGG-11 block definition (the simplest VGG)
cfg = [64, "M", 128, "M", 256, 256, "M", 512, 512, "M", 512, 512, "M"]

def make_vgg_features(cfg, in_channels=3):
    layers = []
    for v in cfg:
        if v == "M":
            layers += [nn.MaxPool2d(2, 2)]
        else:
            layers += [
                nn.Conv2d(in_channels, v, kernel_size=3, padding=1),
                nn.BatchNorm2d(v),
                nn.ReLU(inplace=True),
            ]
            in_channels = v
    return nn.Sequential(*layers)

VGG-16 and VGG-19 (16 and 19 weight layers) followed the same pattern with more convs per block. Both dramatically outperformed AlexNet — top-5 error dropped from 15% to 7%. The message: depth matters, and a simple template lets you explore it cleanly.

What VGG taught us.

  • 3×3 convs everywhere. Two stacked 3×3 convs have the same receptive field as one 5×5 and fewer parameters. Three stacked match a 7×7 with further savings. This insight made VGG possible and still holds.
  • Doubling channels at each stage. As spatial dimensions shrink, channel depth grows — the H·W·C volume stays roughly constant. 64 → 128 → 256 → 512 is the pattern every CNN has followed since.
  • Uniform template matters. A repeating pattern makes architectures easier to understand, debug, and modify. Architectural complexity should earn its keep, not be added for novelty.
VGG's real cost

The classifier head is ridiculous.

VGG-16 has 138M parameters. The convolutional feature extractor accounts for only 14M. The other 124M are in three fully-connected layers (Linear(25088, 4096), then Linear(4096, 4096), then Linear(4096, 1000)) sitting on top of flattened features. This is why modern CNNs use global average pooling into a small classifier — roughly 1/100th the parameters for equivalent or better accuracy.

03 — Inception
GoogLeNet / Inception (2014) — multi-scale features.

VGG asked "how deep?" Inception asked "how wide at each layer?" The observation: different objects and features appear at different scales. A large face needs large receptive fields to recognize; a texture needs small ones. Why pick one kernel size per layer when you can run multiple scales in parallel and concatenate?

The Inception module.

An Inception block runs four parallel paths — a 1×1 conv, a 3×3 conv, a 5×5 conv, and a max pool — and concatenates their outputs along the channel dimension. Each path can capture features at its scale; concatenation means the network can choose which scale to weight more in subsequent layers.

Figure 1. The Inception block. Four parallel paths at different scales, concatenated into one multi-scale output. The 1×1 convs before the 3×3 and 5×5 are 'bottlenecks' that reduce channel depth before the expensive convolutions — the efficiency trick that made Inception feasible.

The bottleneck trick.

Concatenating four parallel convs seems expensive. The critical efficiency move: use a 1×1 convolution before each costly path to reduce channel depth first. If the input has 256 channels and you want to apply a 3×3 conv producing 128 channels, a direct 3×3 conv would be 3·3·256·128 ≈ 295K params. But first compress 256→64 channels with a 1×1 (256·64 = 16K params), then apply 3×3 conv 64→128 (3·3·64·128 = 74K params) — total ~90K, 3× cheaper for approximately the same expressive power.

This 1×1 bottleneck pattern — reduce channels cheaply, do the expensive operation, optionally expand back — became ubiquitous. It's in ResNet bottleneck blocks, MobileNet, EfficientNet, and every deep architecture through today.

Inception bottleneck savings — concrete numbers
Without bottleneck: 256-ch input → 5×5 conv → 128-ch output.Params: 256 × 128 × 5 × 5 = 819,200. FLOPs on 28×28 feature map: 819K × 28² ≈ 642M
With 1×1 bottleneck: 256→32 (1×1), then 32→128 (5×5).Params: 256×32 + 32×128×25 = 8,192 + 102,400 = 110,592. That's 7.4× fewer.
FLOPs: (8K × 784) + (102K × 784) ≈ 6.3M + 80.3M = 86.6M7.4× fewer FLOPs too — the savings compound for deeper networks
Extra bonus: two ReLU activations instead of one → more nonlinearity for free.This is why Inception was 20× smaller than VGG with comparable accuracy
Two insights Inception contributed

First, that multi-scale parallel paths captured features a linear stack couldn't. Second, that 1×1 convolutions are a universal efficiency tool — any time you want to mix channels or reduce channel dimensionality cheaply, 1×1 is your friend. The first idea is not used in modern CNNs much; the second is everywhere.

04 — The crisis
The crisis — why deeper stopped working.

After VGG and Inception, the obvious next step was: go deeper. 50 layers. 100 layers. 200 layers. Surely if 16 layers was good, 50 must be better.

Except they weren't. By 2014-2015, multiple groups reported that very deep plain CNNs degraded: a 56-layer CNN had worse training error than a 20-layer CNN. Not overfit — undertrained. The deeper network couldn't match what a shallower one could do, even though it had strictly more capacity.

Figure 2. The degradation problem. A 56-layer plain CNN has worse training error than a 20-layer one — not a generalization gap, an optimization gap. Deeper networks were somehow unable to reach the same minima as shallower ones, even though they have strictly greater representational capacity. This was the problem ResNet solved.

Why this is a shocking result.

A 56-layer network can, in principle, replicate a 20-layer network exactly — just set the extra 36 layers to the identity function. So the 56-layer network has at least as much capacity as the 20-layer one. That it trains worse means the optimizer isn't finding the solution that exists in principle. The problem is with training, not with representational power.

The suspected cause: gradients struggling to flow through 50+ layers. Each layer, even with ReLU and decent initialization, shrinks the gradient signal slightly. Multiply many small numbers and the gradient at early layers becomes vanishingly small — the very-early-layer weights barely update.

Check your understandingQ4 / 07

Worse training error means the deeper model isn't even fitting the training data as well as the shallower one — despite having strictly more capacity. That's an optimization/gradient-flow failure, not a capacity or regularization issue. The deeper network can in principle represent whatever the shallower one does (identity layers), so if it can't reach that minimum, something's wrong with training itself.
DTraining error always matches validation error in practice.
05 — ResNet
ResNet (2015) — the skip connection revolution.

Kaiming He and colleagues posed the degradation problem and answered it with a remarkable insight. If the issue is that the deep network can represent "do nothing for 36 layers then do what the shallow network does," but optimization can't find that solution, then change the parameterization so "do nothing" becomes the default, not something the network has to learn.

The residual block.

A residual block computes:

y = F(x) + x (5.1)

where F is a short sequence of convolutions (typically two 3×3 convs with BN and ReLU in between). The key: the input x is added to the output F(x) via a skip connection (also called a residual connection). If F produces zero, the block becomes the identity — no change to x. The network starts near this identity-by-default state and learns to produce nonzero residuals only where they help.

Figure 3. The residual block. The input x splits into two paths: the learned residual F(x) (two 3×3 convs with BN and ReLU) and an identity skip connection. Their sum is passed through a final ReLU. The network learns how to modify x rather than what x should be — which turns out to be a vastly easier optimization target.

Why this fixes the degradation problem.

Three complementary explanations, all correct:

  1. Identity is the default. With skip connections, if F's weights are near zero (their initial state), the block computes approximately y ≈ x. Adding 50 such blocks to a shallow network doesn't hurt — it starts near identity and only learns residuals that help. The optimization target is "improve on the shallow network," which is strictly easier than "learn the whole function from scratch."
  2. Gradient highways. Because the output is the sum of F(x) and x, the gradient with respect to x is ∇F + I. That identity component means the gradient flowing backward through the block never multiplies by a tiny number — it always has the identity contribution. So gradients reach earlier layers intact, even through 100+ blocks.
  3. Ensemble-like behavior. A ResNet can be viewed as an exponential ensemble of shorter paths — the input can traverse any subset of blocks via skips. This gives it robustness and naturally flat minima, connecting to Module 2.5's generalization story.

Implementation.

import torch.nn as nn
import torch.nn.functional as F

class BasicBlock(nn.Module):
    """ResNet basic block (used in ResNet-18 and ResNet-34)."""
    expansion = 1

    def __init__(self, in_ch, out_ch, stride=1):
        super().__init__()
        self.conv1 = nn.Conv2d(in_ch, out_ch, 3, stride=stride, padding=1, bias=False)
        self.bn1 = nn.BatchNorm2d(out_ch)
        self.conv2 = nn.Conv2d(out_ch, out_ch, 3, padding=1, bias=False)
        self.bn2 = nn.BatchNorm2d(out_ch)

        # If stride != 1 or channels change, project the skip
        self.shortcut = nn.Identity()
        if stride != 1 or in_ch != out_ch:
            self.shortcut = nn.Sequential(
                nn.Conv2d(in_ch, out_ch, 1, stride=stride, bias=False),
                nn.BatchNorm2d(out_ch),
            )

    def forward(self, x):
        out = F.relu(self.bn1(self.conv1(x)))
        out = self.bn2(self.conv2(out))
        out += self.shortcut(x)
        return F.relu(out)

When the residual F(x) has a different shape than x (because stride > 1 reduced spatial size, or the channel count changed), the skip connection projects x to match via a 1×1 conv. That's the only real complexity.

What ResNet enabled

With skip connections, depth stopped being a failure mode. ResNet-152 beat VGG-19 by a wide margin in 2015. ResNet-1001 (a thousand layers!) was later trained without optimization collapse. Every modern architecture — transformers included — uses residual connections. It's arguably the single most important architectural innovation between 2012 and 2025.

Math Core — the gradient through L residual blocks.

Consider L stacked residual blocks. The output of block l is xl = Fl(xl-1) + xl-1. Unrolling recursively from block 1 to block L:

xL = x0 + Σl=1..L Fl(xl-1) (5.2)

The gradient of the loss with respect to input x0 is:

∂L/∂x0 = ∂L/∂xL · (1 + Σl ∂Fl/∂x0) (5.3)

The crucial "1" term — from the skip connection — ensures that even if every ∂Fl/∂x0 is small, the gradient to x0 is never zero. In a plain network, this would be ∂L/∂xL · Πl ∂Fl/∂xl-1 — a product that goes to zero exponentially if any factor is < 1. The skip connection replaces the product with a sum, which cannot vanish in the same way.

Parameters for ImageNet classifiers (224×224 input, 1000 classes)

Why does a residual block help gradient flow backward through a deep network?

06 — DenseNet
DenseNet (2017) — connecting every layer to every layer.

If one skip connection per block helps, why not more? DenseNet takes the ResNet idea to its extreme. In a dense block, every layer receives the concatenated outputs of all previous layers in the block. So layer 5 sees the outputs of layers 1, 2, 3, and 4 concatenated. Feature reuse is the central idea.

Sum vs concatenate.

The mathematical difference from ResNet is subtle but significant. ResNet sums the skip with the residual:

ResNet: xl = Fl(xl−1) + xl−1 (6.1)
DenseNet: xl = Fl([x0, x1, ..., xl−1]) (6.2)

Summing blends information and keeps channel count fixed. Concatenating preserves information (no blending) but grows the channel count linearly with depth. Each layer in DenseNet typically produces a small number of new channels — the growth rate k — so if the block has 4 layers with k=12, the output has the original channels plus 4·12 = 48 new ones.

Dense blocks and transitions.

DenseNet is organized into several dense blocks, each containing many densely-connected layers. Between blocks, transition layers reduce channel count (via 1×1 conv) and spatial size (via 2×2 average pool), preventing runaway channel growth.

class DenseLayer(nn.Module):
    """One layer within a dense block: BN → ReLU → 1×1 conv → BN → ReLU → 3×3 conv."""
    def __init__(self, in_ch, growth_rate, bn_size=4):
        super().__init__()
        self.bn1 = nn.BatchNorm2d(in_ch)
        self.conv1 = nn.Conv2d(in_ch, bn_size * growth_rate, 1, bias=False)
        self.bn2 = nn.BatchNorm2d(bn_size * growth_rate)
        self.conv2 = nn.Conv2d(bn_size * growth_rate, growth_rate, 3,
                               padding=1, bias=False)

    def forward(self, x):
        # x here is the concatenation of all prior layer outputs
        out = self.conv1(F.relu(self.bn1(x)))
        out = self.conv2(F.relu(self.bn2(out)))
        return torch.cat([x, out], dim=1)  # concat new features to input stack


class DenseBlock(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, num_layers, in_ch, growth_rate, bn_size=4):
        super().__init__()
        self.layers = nn.ModuleList([
            DenseLayer(in_ch + i * growth_rate, growth_rate, bn_size)
            for i in range(num_layers)
        ])

    def forward(self, x):
        for layer in self.layers:
            x = layer(x)
        return x

Why DenseNet matters (and why ResNet still wins in practice).

DenseNet's feature reuse means fewer parameters are needed for a given accuracy — DenseNet-121 has ~8M parameters vs ResNet-50's ~25M, for comparable accuracy. But in practice, ResNet-style architectures dominate deployment because:

  • Memory. Concatenation requires storing all intermediate feature maps until the end of the block — higher peak memory during training.
  • Hardware efficiency. ResNet's uniform structure (fixed channel counts within stages) maps cleanly to tensor-core optimized kernels.
  • Simplicity. ResNet has one idea (skip) executed consistently. DenseNet has interactions between growth rate, transition compression, and block sizes that make tuning more intricate.
Where these ideas went

DenseNet's feature reuse insight lives on. The encoder-decoder U-Net architectures that power image segmentation (and stable diffusion's backbone) use dense-style concatenation-based skip connections between encoder and decoder levels. Feature Pyramid Networks (FPN) for object detection similarly concatenate across scales. Every time you see torch.cat in a modern architecture, you're seeing DenseNet's legacy.

The modern picture — and where CNNs go from here.

The architectural lessons that survived.

  1. 3×3 convolutions are the primitive. Stacked 3×3s with BN + ReLU is the basic building block of every modern CNN.
  2. 1×1 convolutions for efficiency. Bottleneck compression (down-up) and channel mixing. Everywhere in deep architectures.
  3. Skip connections for gradient flow. Additive (ResNet) or concatenative (DenseNet). Essential for deep networks — and carried over to transformers and beyond.
  4. Global average pooling for classifier heads. Translation-invariant, parameter-free, strictly better than flatten-and-dense.
  5. Channel count grows as spatial size shrinks. Keeps activation volume bounded while increasing abstraction. 64 → 128 → 256 → 512 is the standard.

EfficientNet and compound scaling.

By 2019, the question wasn't which architecture family but how to scale one efficiently. EfficientNet introduced compound scaling: a single scaling coefficient that simultaneously multiplies network depth, width, and input resolution according to fixed ratios derived from a grid search. Scaling all three together turned out to beat scaling any one individually — you get predictably more accurate models at each compute budget.

The Vision Transformer era.

In 2020, a paper asked: what if we replace all the convolutions with attention? The Vision Transformer (ViT) splits an image into patches (16×16 pixels), linearly embeds each, and feeds the sequence through a standard transformer. On datasets with enough data (300M+ labeled images), ViT matches or beats the best CNNs. On smaller datasets, CNNs' inductive biases still win.

The debate "CNN or ViT" is roughly settled: use CNNs when you have limited data and need strong sample efficiency; use ViTs when you have lots of data or are using a pretrained backbone. For most production systems, a pretrained CNN or ViT (fine-tuned on your task) is the right answer. We'll return to transformers properly in Phase 4.

CineMatch poster classifier — what to actually use

A realistic 2026 recipe for CineMatch's genre classifier.

For a production movie-poster genre classifier, don't train from scratch. Take a pretrained ResNet-50 or ConvNeXt-Tiny (both available via torchvision.models), swap the final classifier layer for a 10-way head matching CineMatch's genre count, and fine-tune on your labeled poster dataset. Freeze early layers, unfreeze late layers, use a low learning rate (1e-4 or smaller), AdamW, cosine decay, standard augmentation. 50K labeled posters is usually enough to reach 85%+ top-1 accuracy this way. Training from scratch would need 500K+ and produce worse results.

Where this leads.

You now understand every CNN architecture of the last decade as variations on a few themes. Module 3.3 pivots to the other structured data type — sequences. Text, audio, user interaction histories all have temporal order that fully-connected and convolutional networks don't naturally capture. Recurrent neural networks were the dominant answer from 1990 through 2017, and though transformers have largely replaced them for language, understanding RNNs is essential for understanding why transformers were necessary and what they replaced.
