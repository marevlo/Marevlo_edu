Optimization in practice. SGD, momentum, Adam.

Plain SGD is the taxi of optimization — it works, but reluctantly. This module builds up the modern optimizer stack in sequence, showing what problem each innovation solved. By the end you'll know when to reach for SGD+momentum versus Adam versus AdamW, and why the "just use Adam" default isn't quite right.

01 — Goals
Optimization is not the same as learning.

Before diving into optimizers, a distinction that saves a lot of confusion: optimization and deep learning have different goals. The optimizer's job is to minimize the loss on the training data. Your job — as the person building the model — is to produce something that generalizes to new data. Those are related but not identical.

Optimization's goalDeep learning's goal
What's minimizedEmpirical risk — average loss on training setTrue risk — expected loss on unseen data
MetricTraining lossValidation/test loss, accuracy, F1, etc.
Access toTraining data onlyTraining + validation data (held out)

A model that minimizes training loss perfectly might still be useless (overfitting). A model that stops early before fully minimizing training loss might be the one you ship (better generalization). This tension — between what the optimizer wants and what you want — will come back in Module 2.6 on regularization. For now, keep the distinction in mind: the optimizer is a tool, not the objective.

The three problems every optimizer tries to solve.

In Phase 1 you saw that plain SGD works, but with caveats. Three specific pathologies emerge across problems and motivate every improvement we'll build:

  1. Ill-conditioned loss surfaces. Different parameters respond to different step sizes. Plain SGD with a single learning rate either diverges in the steep direction or crawls in the shallow one. Momentum and adaptive methods address this.
  2. Noisy gradients from mini-batching. Each mini-batch gradient is a noisy estimate of the true gradient. Plain SGD propagates every step of that noise. Momentum smooths it out.
  3. Sparse or variably-scaled features. Some parameters update often (frequent features), others rarely (sparse features). A single learning rate can't serve both. Adagrad/RMSProp/Adam adapt per-parameter.

02 — Baseline
SGD — the baseline, and the noise.

Stochastic gradient descent replaces the exact gradient of the full dataset with a noisy estimate from a mini-batch. The update rule is what you already know:

w ← w − η ∇w Lbatch(w) (2.1)

where Lbatch is the loss averaged over one mini-batch. The noise of the batch gradient is SGD's defining feature. It's annoying but also useful:

  • Good side. Noise helps escape sharp local minima; updates are cheap; memory use is low; works with streaming data.
  • Bad side. Loss oscillates during training; on ill-conditioned problems, oscillations can be severe; convergence to a tight minimum is slow.

The "bad side" of SGD is what all the following optimizers attempt to mitigate. The "good side" is why SGD, properly tuned, still outperforms fancier optimizers on some problems. This is the fundamental tension: more noise helps generalization, less noise helps convergence.

Learning rate scheduling — the first upgrade.

The simplest improvement over plain SGD is to decay the learning rate over time. Start with a large η to make fast progress, shrink it as training proceeds to converge to a tight minimum. Three common schedules:

ScheduleFormulaBehavior
Step decayηt = η0 · γ⌊t/step⌋Drops by factor γ every N steps
Cosineηt = η0 · 0.5(1 + cos(πt/T))Smooth drop to zero over T steps
Linear warmup + decayRamp up then decayModern default for transformers
import torch
from torch.optim import SGD
from torch.optim.lr_scheduler import CosineAnnealingLR

optimizer = SGD(model.parameters(), lr=0.1, momentum=0.9)
scheduler = CosineAnnealingLR(optimizer, T_max=100)   # 100 epochs

for epoch in range(100):
    for xb, yb in train_loader:
        optimizer.zero_grad()
        loss = criterion(model(xb), yb)
        loss.backward()
        optimizer.step()
    scheduler.step()   # update LR once per epoch

Warmup — starting from a tiny LR and linearly increasing to the target over the first few hundred steps — became essential for training large transformers. It prevents the first few updates (computed on random weights) from being disastrously large. If you're training any serious model, use warmup + decay rather than fixed LR.

CineMatch's default schedule

Warmup + cosine — set once, forget it.

For the CineMatch MLP, a linear warmup of 5 epochs followed by cosine decay over the remaining training epochs is a strong default. The warmup protects against early instability while the model's feature gate and normalization parameters settle. The cosine decay ensures the final epochs use tiny steps that refine the solution. In code: torch.optim.lr_scheduler.OneCycleLR does both of these together and is the one-stop shop for this pattern.

Warmup + cosine decay — actual LR values across 100 epochs (η₀ = 1e-3)
Epoch 0: LR = 1e-3 × (0/5) = 0. Epoch 1: 1e-3 × 0.2 = 2e-4. Epoch 5: 1e-3 × 1.0 = 1e-3 (peak)Linear warmup over 5 epochs — LR ramps from 0 to peak
Epoch 10: η = 1e-3 × 0.5(1 + cos(π×5/95)) = 1e-3 × 0.987 = 9.87e-4Just past peak — barely started decaying
Epoch 50: η = 1e-3 × 0.5(1 + cos(π×45/95)) = 1e-3 × 0.500 = 5.00e-4Halfway: LR is half the peak. Most learning is done.

Epoch 100: η = 1e-3 × 0.5(1 + cos(π)) = 1e-3 × 0 = 0LR reaches zero. Training effectively stops. This smooth ramp-down is why cosine outperforms step decay — no sudden drops that shock the optimizer.
03 — Momentum
Momentum — giving the optimizer memory.

The first big improvement on plain SGD: give the optimizer a velocity, so that successive gradients in the same direction reinforce each other, and opposing gradients in successive steps cancel out. The physical intuition is a ball rolling down a hill — it builds speed in consistent directions and doesn't bounce wildly when the slope wiggles.

vt = β vt−1 + ∇w L(wt)
wt+1 = wt − η vt
(3.1)

β ∈ [0, 1) is the momentum coefficient — typically 0.9. This means the velocity keeps 90% of its previous value and adds the new gradient. Over time, v becomes an exponentially-weighted average of recent gradients.

Why it helps on ill-conditioned problems.

Recall Module 1.2's loss surface geometry: when gradients zigzag across a stretched bowl, consecutive SGD steps point in different directions and waste effort on oscillation. Momentum's velocity averages consecutive gradients — the zigzag components cancel out, and the consistent direction (toward the minimum) accumulates. The result: faster and smoother progress through narrow valleys.

Figure 1. On an ill-conditioned loss surface, plain SGD zigzags across the narrow axis while making slow progress along the long axis. Momentum averages the oscillating components to cancellation while accumulating the consistent direction — producing a much smoother, faster trajectory.
# PyTorch: momentum is just a keyword argument to SGD
optimizer = torch.optim.SGD(model.parameters(), lr=0.01, momentum=0.9)

# Nesterov variant — looks ahead before computing the gradient
optimizer = torch.optim.SGD(
    model.parameters(), lr=0.01, momentum=0.9, nesterov=True
)

Nesterov momentum is a theoretical refinement that peeks ahead in the velocity direction before computing the gradient. In practice it gives a small improvement over vanilla momentum and is almost free to enable.

The default pairing that keeps coming back

SGD + momentum 0.9 + Nesterov, combined with a cosine or OneCycle LR schedule, is still the best optimizer for many vision tasks. It generalizes better than Adam on ImageNet-scale training of CNNs and ResNets. If you see a paper reporting state-of-the-art on ImageNet classification, it almost certainly uses SGD with momentum — not Adam. The lesson: "Adam is the default" is approximately true but not always true.

04 — Adaptive
Adaptive methods — per-parameter learning rates.

Momentum fixes oscillation. But there's a different problem it doesn't address: parameters that should move at different speeds. A feature that appears in 1% of examples and a feature that appears in every example cannot share a learning rate. Sparse feature updates get drowned out; dense feature updates dominate. The cure is per-parameter adaptive learning rates.

Adagrad — the first adaptive method.

Adagrad (Duchi et al., 2011) keeps a running sum of squared gradients for each parameter, and divides the learning rate by its square root:

st,j = st−1,j + gt,j2
wj ← wj − (η / √(st,j + ε)) · gt,j
(4.1)

The effect: parameters that have accumulated large gradients in the past get smaller updates now (s is large). Parameters that have had small gradients get larger updates (s is small). Each parameter effectively tunes its own learning rate. Great for sparse data — but has a fatal flaw.

The Adagrad flaw, and RMSProp's fix.

Because st,j only grows (it's a sum of squares), the effective learning rate η/√s eventually shrinks to zero. Late in training, no parameter can change anymore — learning halts. This is catastrophic in deep (non-convex) problems where we may need to keep learning for thousands of epochs.

RMSProp (Tieleman & Hinton, 2012) replaces the sum with an exponential moving average of squared gradients:

st,j = β · st−1,j + (1 − β) · gt,j2
wj ← wj − (η / √(st,j + ε)) · gt,j
(4.2)

Now the denominator doesn't grow without bound — it tracks recent gradient scale, decaying old information. RMSProp made deep-learning adaptive optimization actually work.

Adam — momentum plus RMSProp, with a bias fix.

Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2014) is the obvious fusion: combine momentum (for smoothness) with RMSProp's per-parameter scaling (for adaptivity). Plus one clever detail — bias correction for the early steps when the moving averages haven't "warmed up" yet.

mt = β1 mt−1 + (1 − β1) gt    # first moment — momentum-like
vt = β2 vt−1 + (1 − β2) gt2    # second moment — RMSProp-like
m̂t = mt / (1 − β1t)    v̂t = vt / (1 − β2t)    # bias correction
w ← w − η · m̂t / (√v̂t + ε)
(4.3)

Defaults: β1 = 0.9 (momentum), β2 = 0.999 (second moment decay), ε = 10−8, η = 10−3. Adam is the default optimizer in deep learning — robust across problems, forgiving of hyperparameter choices, and fast to converge. When in doubt, start with Adam and tune only if you have a reason.

Why bias correction matters

Without it, Adam takes tiny steps at the start.

At t = 1, m and v are both ≈ 0 (they were initialized to zero). The update is m̂/√v̂ ≈ 0 / 0. Without bias correction, the effective step size is biased toward zero in the first few iterations. The correction m̂t = mt/(1 − β1t) inflates the estimate to compensate — at t=1, β1t = 0.9, so we divide by 0.1, effectively scaling up 10×. After enough iterations, the correction factor approaches 1 and does nothing. But those early iterations matter enormously.

05 — AdamW
Weight decay, and why AdamW exists.

Weight decay is a regularization technique — a subject we'll cover fully in Module 2.6. For now, the quick version: it adds a penalty on the size of the weights to the loss, encouraging smaller weights. This improves generalization:

Ltotal(w) = Ldata(w) + (λ/2)||w||2 (5.1)

Differentiate: the gradient has an extra λw term. For plain SGD this means each step is:

w ← w − η(∇Ldata + λw) = (1 − ηλ)w − η∇Ldata (5.2)

The weight shrinks by a factor (1 − ηλ) each step — hence "decay." Simple enough. Now, the subtlety: when combined with Adam's per-parameter adaptive scaling, weight decay added to the gradient gets scaled by the same adaptive denominator. Large-second-moment parameters get their decay dampened; small-second-moment parameters get theirs amplified. This is almost never what you want.

AdamW — decoupled weight decay.

AdamW (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2019) decouples weight decay from the gradient update — it applies the decay directly to the weights, bypassing Adam's adaptive machinery:

w ← w − η · (m̂t / (√v̂t + ε) + λw) (5.3)

This small change produces substantially better generalization on many problems and has become the standard for transformer training. If you're using Adam with weight decay, use AdamW instead. In PyTorch:

# Old — broken weight decay behavior
opt = torch.optim.Adam(model.parameters(), lr=1e-3, weight_decay=0.01)

# New — decoupled weight decay. Use this.
opt = torch.optim.AdamW(model.parameters(), lr=1e-3, weight_decay=0.01)
What to use for CineMatch

AdamW + cosine scheduler is a great universal default.

For the CineMatch MLP and everything in Phase 3 (CNNs, RNNs), AdamW with lr=1e-3, weight_decay=0.01, plus a cosine or OneCycle scheduler, is the right starting point. Trial different values if training is unstable or plateaus, but don't change the optimizer unless you have a specific reason. The major exception: very large-scale vision training (ImageNet + ResNet) often still uses SGD+momentum+Nesterov because it generalizes slightly better there. For most problems, AdamW wins.

06 — Schedules
Learning rate schedules in detail.

Every modern training setup has an LR schedule — a fixed rule (or adaptive rule) for how the learning rate changes over training. Four patterns cover the vast majority of use cases:

Step decay.

Drop the learning rate by a factor (typically 0.1) at fixed epoch milestones. Simple, interpretable, still used in many vision pipelines.

scheduler = torch.optim.lr_scheduler.StepLR(
    optimizer, step_size=30, gamma=0.1
)
# LR = lr_initial at epochs 0-29, lr_initial*0.1 at 30-59, etc.

Cosine annealing.

Smoothly decay from initial LR to (near) zero following a cosine curve. The smooth decay is gentle on optimization and often yields slightly better final accuracy than step decay. Most transformer training uses cosine.

scheduler = torch.optim.lr_scheduler.CosineAnnealingLR(
    optimizer, T_max=100    # decays smoothly to zero over 100 epochs
)

Linear warmup.

Ramp up from a tiny LR to the target over the first few hundred steps, then apply whatever main schedule you want. Prevents the very first updates (on poorly-initialized weights) from being destructively large. Essential for training large transformers from scratch.

# Combine warmup + cosine
from torch.optim.lr_scheduler import LambdaLR
import math

def cos_warmup(step, warmup_steps=500, total_steps=10000):
    if step < warmup_steps:
        return step / warmup_steps
    progress = (step - warmup_steps) / (total_steps - warmup_steps)
    return 0.5 * (1 + math.cos(math.pi * progress))

scheduler = LambdaLR(optimizer, lr_lambda=cos_warmup)

OneCycleLR — batteries included.

Designed by Leslie Smith (2018), the one-cycle schedule warms up quickly, reaches a peak LR well above your "normal" starting LR, then decays smoothly. It often trains faster than other schedules and produces flatter minima (better generalization). PyTorch's OneCycleLR implements it directly:

scheduler = torch.optim.lr_scheduler.OneCycleLR(
    optimizer,
    max_lr=0.01,       # peak LR — often 5-10× your stable "flat" LR
    total_steps=5000,   # = epochs × batches per epoch
)
# step the scheduler *every batch* for OneCycle, not every epoch
for xb, yb in train_loader:
    # ... train step ...
    scheduler.step()
Figure 2. Four LR schedules side by side. Step decay is a coarse staircase. Cosine is smooth. OneCycle spikes above the nominal LR and decays. Warmup+cosine ramps up then decays — the pattern used for large transformer training.

07 — Decision
Which optimizer should you use? — and the checkpoint.

Here's a practical decision tree. Treat it as a starting point, not a rule.

ScenarioBest starting optimizerWhy
General MLPs, tabular data, most thingsAdamW, lr=1e-3Robust default; adapts to varying scales
Large CNNs on ImageNet, ResNet-style visionSGD+momentum 0.9, Nesterov, cosine LRGeneralizes better on vision; well-understood
Transformers from scratchAdamW + warmup + cosine, peak lr 1e-4 to 3e-4Essentially industry standard
Fine-tuning a pretrained modelAdamW, small lr (1e-5 to 5e-5)Gentle updates preserve pretrained knowledge
Sparse / NLP-ish problemsAdam or AdamW with weight_decayPer-parameter adaptation handles sparsity
Want maximum generalization, have patienceSGD+momentum+Nesterov, careful LR tuningSlightly better asymptotic generalization on some problems

Diagnostics for optimizer problems.

  • Loss spikes / diverges: LR too high, or missing warmup. Reduce LR by 3-10×; add warmup.
  • Loss plateaus early: LR too low, or need an LR schedule that drops it further. Try OneCycle.
  • Loss oscillates forever: ill-conditioned problem — add momentum or switch to Adam. Standardize features (Phase 1.2).
  • Adam trains fast but generalizes worse than SGD: try SGD+momentum. Or try Adam with strong weight decay via AdamW.

Where this leads.
