Optimization theory. Why any of this works.

Classical optimization theory is built for convex problems — clean, provable, reliable. Deep learning is wildly non-convex. And yet, SGD walks into a million-parameter saddle-point-infested landscape and trains a useful model. This module explains the theoretical reasons that shouldn't be possible, and the empirical reasons it is.

01 — The classical view
The classical view — convexity.

Classical optimization — the kind engineers used for decades before deep learning — is built around one idea. A function f is convex if the line segment joining any two points on its graph lies above the graph itself. Formally, for any x, y in its domain and any λ ∈ [0, 1]:

f(λx + (1 − λ)y) ≤ λf(x) + (1 − λ)f(y) (1.1)

The picture: bowl-shaped. No ripples, no second valleys, no sideways humps. Take any two points on the surface — the straight chord between them is never below the surface. The canonical example is f(w) = w2; the pathological counter-example is anything with multiple minima, like f(w) = w4 − 2w2.

Figure 1. A convex function lies below any chord drawn between two of its points. A non-convex function can have chords that dip below the curve — and, consequently, multiple local minima. Every loss surface in Phase 1 was convex; every loss surface in Phase 2 onward is not.

The guarantee that makes convex optimization beautiful.

For convex functions, a single property holds that shapes everything downstream:

The convex optimization guarantee

If f is convex, every local minimum is a global minimum. If gradient descent converges anywhere, it converges to the best possible answer — no matter where you start.

This is why all of Phase 1 "just worked" once the learning rate was reasonable. Linear regression with MSE? Convex. Logistic regression with cross-entropy? Convex. No matter the initialization, no matter the exact optimizer, they end up in the same valley — the global one. The algorithm designer's only worry was convergence speed, never quality of the final solution.

Additional convenient properties.

Convex functions come with a whole toolkit of guarantees. A sample:

  • Gradient = 0 ⇔ minimum. For convex f, any point where ∇f = 0 is a global minimum. No saddles, no maxima.
  • Sum of convex functions is convex. If individual training losses are convex, the total loss is convex.
  • Convergence rates are known. For strongly convex smooth problems, gradient descent has a known error bound that decays exponentially with iteration count. You can prove how long training will take.

Classical optimization textbooks — Boyd and Vandenberghe is the canonical one — are essentially a catalog of which real-world problems can be shoehorned into convex form, because once they are, everything becomes tractable. Linear programming, quadratic programming, SVMs, LASSO — all convex by design.

02 — Leaving convexity
Deep learning leaves convexity behind.

The moment you compose a linear layer with a non-linear activation and stack multiple layers, the loss as a function of the weights ceases to be convex. And not in a small, fixable way — in a thoroughly, hopelessly, unapologetic way.

Consider the simplest non-trivial case: a 2-layer MLP with weights W1, W2. One simple invariance alone kills convexity: you can permute the hidden units (swap the i-th and j-th columns of W1, swap the i-th and j-th rows of W2) and get the exact same function. So a network with 64 hidden units has at least 64! ≈ 1089 parameter configurations that all produce the same loss. Each is a local minimum. The loss surface is hopelessly multi-modal before you've even started.

And that's just one source of non-convexity. ReLUs, skip connections, batch norm — each adds more.

The surprise of the 2010s

Non-convex training works in practice — and nobody fully understood why.

From the 1960s through the 2000s, most of optimization theory held that trying to minimize a non-convex function in a million dimensions was hopeless. Textbooks warned specifically against the very setup that defines a modern neural network. Then the 2012 AlexNet result showed that with SGD + enough compute, trained networks reached astonishingly low loss and generalized to unseen data. The theoretical community is still, in 2026, catching up to explain exactly why. But a handful of big ideas — saddle points, flat minima, implicit regularization — form the consensus explanation, and this module walks through them.

So why isn't training doomed?

Two claims form the modern answer:

  1. Local minima aren't really the villain — saddle points are. In very high dimensions, pure local minima are rare; most critical points (where ∇f = 0) are saddles. SGD navigates around saddles surprisingly well.
  2. For over-parameterized networks, most local minima are good enough. When a network has more parameters than data points (almost always, now), the empirical loss surface has many equally good minima — and SGD's noise biases it toward the "flat" ones that generalize better.

Neither claim has a complete proof. Both have substantial empirical and partial theoretical backing. Both run against the classical warning that non-convex optimization is hard.

03 — Saddle points
Saddle points — and why high-dim is both easier and weirder.

A critical point is any point where ∇f = 0. In one dimension there are only two kinds: minima (curving up) and maxima (curving down). In higher dimensions a third kind appears: the saddle point, which curves up in some directions and down in others. Picture a Pringle or a horse saddle — locally flat in the gradient sense, but it's only a minimum along some directions and a maximum along others.

Figure 2. In 1D, critical points are either minima or maxima. In 2D, a saddle appears — curving down along the horizontal axis, up along the vertical. The gradient is zero there, but it's neither a minimum nor a maximum.

How common are saddles?

Here's the striking fact. At a random critical point of a generic non-convex function in d dimensions, each of the d curvature directions has roughly a 50/50 chance of curving up or down (intuition: the d eigenvalues of the Hessian are roughly random in sign). For the point to be a local minimum, all d directions must curve up — probability 2−d. For a 1M-parameter model, the probability a critical point is a true minimum is 2−1,000,000. Essentially zero.

In other words: almost every critical point in a deep network is a saddle. When you're worried about gradient descent "getting stuck," it's saddles you're worried about, not local minima.

The saddle-prevalence claim

In very high dimensions, local minima are exponentially rare compared to saddle points. The classical fear that gradient descent will get stuck in a suboptimal local minimum was mostly misplaced — what actually slows training is saddles and plateaus, not competing minima.

Why SGD mostly doesn't get stuck at saddles.

A pure saddle has zero gradient, so a deterministic gradient descent could in principle halt there. But in practice it almost never does, for two reasons:

  1. SGD's noise kicks you off. A saddle is unstable — any perturbation in a down-curving direction slides you away. Stochastic minibatches always provide enough perturbation.
  2. Momentum traverses flat regions. Near a saddle, the gradient is small but non-zero. Momentum accumulates that small signal over many steps and breaks out.
  3. Adaptive optimizers rescale. Adam and RMSProp amplify the tiny gradient components in flat directions by dividing by their historical magnitude, effectively giving them higher local learning rate.

So the trio of tricks from 2.4 — SGD stochasticity, momentum, adaptive rescaling — is exactly what gets you through the forest of saddles that characterizes a deep loss surface. None of them was designed for this problem originally. All of them happen to help.

04 — The Hessian
Second-order information — the Hessian.

The gradient ∇f tells us which way the function slopes. To know which way it curves, we need the second derivative. In one dimension that's f″(w). In d dimensions, the analog is the Hessian matrix H — a d × d matrix of all second partial derivatives:

Hij = ∂2f / ∂wi ∂wj (4.1)

The Hessian fully characterizes the local curvature. Its eigenvalues tell you, for each principal curvature direction, whether the surface is bowl-shaped (positive eigenvalue) or dome-shaped (negative eigenvalue) along that axis.

All eigenvalues of HGeometryWhat it means
All > 0Local minimumBowl-shaped in every direction
All < 0Local maximumDome-shaped in every direction
Mixed signsSaddleSome up, some down — unstable
Some = 0Degenerate / flatFlat along a subspace

The condition number, revisited.

Recall from Module 1.2 that the condition number of the loss landscape — the ratio of the largest to smallest eigenvalue of the Hessian — controls how hard gradient descent finds the problem. A high condition number means the loss is steep in some directions and gently sloped in others; gradient descent oscillates across the steep directions while creeping along the gentle ones.

For deep networks, the condition number of the Hessian is often in the millions. This is the fundamental reason plain SGD struggles, momentum helps moderately, and Adam (which approximates the curvature via running gradient squared statistics) helps a lot.

Why Adam was destined to win

Adam divides each gradient component by a running estimate of its own historical magnitude. This is a cheap, diagonal approximation to preconditioning by the inverse Hessian — the move classical optimization theory recommends for ill-conditioned problems. Adam effectively normalizes each parameter's update by how steep its local loss direction is. That's why it handles the terrible conditioning of deep networks better than plain SGD.

Newton's method — and why we don't use it.

If curvature information is this valuable, why not just use the full Hessian? The classical method that does is Newton's method:

wt+1 = wt − H−1∇f(wt) (4.2)

Newton's method takes exactly one step on a pure quadratic — it jumps directly to the minimum. On smooth convex problems, its convergence is quadratic — each step roughly squares the accuracy. It's glorious, when it's applicable.

For deep learning, Newton's method is useless. Three reasons:

  1. Memory cost. The Hessian is d × d. For a 1M-parameter model, that's 1012 entries — terabytes. You cannot store it.
  2. Compute cost. Inverting a d × d matrix is O(d3). Even approximating the inverse costs more than any practical budget.
  3. Non-convexity. In non-convex regions, the Hessian can have negative eigenvalues, meaning Newton's step points away from the minimum. Used naively on a saddle, it jumps to the saddle, not away from it.

So we settle for approximations. L-BFGS builds a low-rank approximation of H−1 from recent gradients. Adam/RMSProp approximate only the diagonal of H. Both give some second-order benefit without paying the full price.

05 — Why Adam works
Why Adam "works" — and what the theory still owes us.

Classical convex optimization theory has very little to say about why Adam should successfully train a ResNet to low loss. And yet every 2026 lab runs Adam-family optimizers daily with success. How? A few empirical regularities form the consensus explanation — partial proofs for some, intuitions for others.

Flatness and generalization.

When we care about a model, we don't care about the training loss — we care about the test loss. These two can differ. A model that fits the training data perfectly can be a catastrophe on test data; a slightly worse-fitting one can generalize much better.

An empirical regularity is that networks converged to flat minima — regions of weight space where the loss is low and changes slowly in all directions — generalize better than networks converged to sharp minima — narrow valleys where the loss is low but explodes as soon as you perturb the weights.

Figure 3. A schematic loss slice. The flat minimum (wide valley, left) has nearly equal train and test loss — the shift between curves doesn't move it meaningfully. The sharp minimum (narrow valley, middle) has low train loss but high test loss — a small shift between train and test distributions throws it out of the basin. Flatness correlates with generalization.

Why does flatness help? The intuition: the test distribution is the training distribution plus a small perturbation (different samples, maybe different noise). A sharp minimum fits the training perturbation exactly but not the test perturbation; a flat minimum tolerates both.

SGD's implicit regularization.

Here's the piece that started to click around 2017. Plain SGD with reasonable learning rates preferentially converges to flat minima, even without any explicit regularization. The reason is stochastic: SGD's mini-batch gradient is a noisy estimate of the true gradient, and this noise is larger near sharp minima than near flat ones. So as the optimizer bounces around, it effectively "escapes" sharp minima more easily than flat ones. Over many steps, it settles preferentially in the flat regions.

This is called the implicit regularization of SGD. No one designed this into the algorithm — it was discovered experimentally. The hyperparameters (batch size, learning rate) affect how strong the implicit regularization is. Smaller batch and higher LR = noisier SGD = more implicit regularization = flatter minima = better generalization, up to a point.

A surprising practical corollary

Very large batch training sometimes hurts generalization, even when train loss is identical. The reason: large batches reduce SGD's noise, which reduces implicit regularization, which makes the optimizer more willing to settle in sharp minima. This is why the modern recipe is to scale LR linearly with batch size (to preserve the noise-to-step-size ratio) and to use a warmup phase when scaling to very large batches.

What theory still owes us.

This is an incomplete story. Open questions as of 2026:

  • Why does Adam sometimes hurt generalization compared to SGD? The adaptive rescaling appears to bias Adam toward sharper minima than SGD, an effect without a crisp theoretical explanation.
  • How much of generalization is the optimizer vs the architecture? Skip connections, layer norm, and attention all seem to matter independently of the optimizer choice.
  • Why do learning-rate schedules help so much? Cosine and warmup work empirically but the "right" schedule isn't theoretically derivable from loss geometry.
  • When does optimization fail? Dead neurons, exploding gradients, training collapse — we have workarounds, not a unified theory.

All of this is active research. Practical deep learning remains a field where empirical tricks run ahead of theory — which means your default is to measure rather than predict, and to trust a validation run over an analytic calculation.

06 — Theory in the loop
Bringing theory back to the training loop.

We've covered a lot of ground — convexity, saddles, Hessians, flat minima, implicit regularization. What do you actually do differently in your CineMatch training code on Monday morning?

The theory-informed default recipe.

  1. Use a noise-preserving optimizer early. Start with SGD + momentum or Adam — both have enough noise/adaptivity to traverse saddles and escape sharp minima.
  2. Warm up, then decay. Use a small LR for the first few hundred steps (warmup) to avoid early blow-ups, then cosine-decay through training. This is directly supported by loss-geometry intuitions even if not formally derived.
  3. Don't use giant batches unless you need to. Large batches reduce SGD noise, which reduces implicit regularization, which can hurt generalization. If you must use large batches for compute reasons, scale LR linearly and use longer warmup.
  4. Plateau is usually saddle, not stuck. If training plateaus for hundreds of steps, the most likely cause is a saddle — not a dead run. Let it continue unless the plateau persists across thousands of steps.
  5. Measure generalization, not final loss. A run that reaches lower train loss has not necessarily found a better model. Validation metrics are the ground truth. Flat-minimum finders (plain SGD) often look slightly worse on train loss than adaptive methods but slightly better on test.

Everything in this module was about the optimizer. The next module is about the loss.

The other half is explicit regularization — weight decay, dropout, early stopping, data augmentation — all of which are design choices that shape the loss surface to favor good minima.
