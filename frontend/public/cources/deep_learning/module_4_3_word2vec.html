word2vec. Learning embeddings from context.

Every model we've built — from the Phase 1 linear baselines through the Phase 4 transformer — starts by converting discrete tokens (words, movie IDs) into dense vectors. Those vectors are the embeddings, and their quality dominates downstream performance. word2vec (Mikolov et al., 2013) was the paper that showed you could learn embeddings from raw co-occurrence data, no labels needed — and that the learned geometry encodes semantic meaning: king − man + woman ≈ queen. This module builds word2vec from scratch and applies the same idea to CineMatch movie embeddings.

01 — Representation
The problem — why not one-hot?

A vocabulary of V = 50,000 movies. One-hot representation: each movie is a 50,000-dimensional vector with a single 1 and 49,999 zeros. Two problems are immediately fatal:

No similarity structure. The one-hot for "The Godfather" is exactly as far from "Goodfellas" as it is from "Finding Nemo." The dot product between any two one-hots is zero. The representation contains no information about which movies are related.

Dimensionality explosion. Any weight matrix that takes one-hot input is V × d — for 50K movies that's 50K × d parameters in the first layer alone. For the LSTM and transformer, V × d is already the embedding table — but feeding one-hots directly into a dense layer means the input dimension is 50K, which is wasteful when 99.998% of the input is zero.

Dense embeddings fix both: a learned lookup table that maps each movie to a 256-dimensional vector where geometry reflects meaning. Similar movies end up nearby; dissimilar movies are far apart. The question is: how do you learn these vectors?

The distributional hypothesis

"You shall know a word by the company it keeps" (Firth, 1957). Words (or movies) that appear in similar contexts have similar meanings. word2vec turns this linguistic intuition into a training objective: predict context from a word, or a word from its context. The embeddings that emerge encode the distributional structure of the data.

02 — Skip-gram
Skip-gram — predict context from center.

word2vec has two variants. Skip-gram is the more commonly used one. The idea: given a center word (or movie), predict the words (movies) that appear nearby in the same context window.

For text: given the sentence "the cat sat on the mat", with center word "sat" and window size 2, the training pairs are: (sat, the), (sat, cat), (sat, on), (sat, the).

For CineMatch: given watch-history [..., Action₂, Comedy, Horror, SciFi, Drama, ...] with center movie Horror and window 2, the training pairs are: (Horror, Action₂), (Horror, Comedy), (Horror, SciFi), (Horror, Drama).

The objective: maximize the probability of seeing each context word given the center word:

P(wcontext | wcenter) = softmax(ucontextT · vcenter) (2.1)

Where vcenter is the center word's embedding and ucontext is the context word's embedding. Note: each word has two embeddings — one when it's the center, one when it's context. After training, we typically keep only the center embeddings v (or average both).

CBOW — the mirror variant.

Continuous Bag of Words inverts the direction: given the context words, predict the center word. The context embeddings are averaged and used to predict the center. CBOW is faster (one prediction per window instead of 2k); skip-gram works better for rare words and is more commonly used.

Skip-gramQuestion 02 / 06
In skip-gram with window size 2, center word "Horror", context is [Action₂, Comedy, SciFi, Drama]. What is the model being trained to do?
AClassify Horror into one of four genre categories.
BGiven Horror's embedding, produce high dot products with the embeddings of Action₂, Comedy, SciFi, and Drama (the actual context) and low dot products with all other movies in the catalog. This is four separate binary predictions — each one asks "is this movie in Horror's context window?"
CPredict Horror from the average of the four context embeddings (that's CBOW, not skip-gram).
DCompute the attention weights between Horror and the context movies.
03 — Negative sampling
Negative sampling — making training tractable.

The softmax in equation (2.1) requires computing uTv for every word in the vocabulary — all 50,000 movies. This is O(V) per training pair, and with millions of training pairs, it's prohibitively expensive.

Negative sampling replaces the V-way softmax with a much cheaper binary classification problem. For each positive pair (center, context), we also sample K negative movies (drawn from a noise distribution, typically the unigram distribution raised to the 0.75 power). The model learns to distinguish real context pairs from fake ones:

L = −log σ(uposT · vcenter) − Σk=1..K log σ(−uneg_kT · vcenter) (3.1)

Where σ is the sigmoid function. The first term pushes the positive pair's dot product up; the K negative terms push random pairs' dot products down. Typical K: 5–20 for small datasets, 2–5 for large ones.

Math Core — the skip-gram gradient with negative sampling.

The gradient of the negative sampling loss with respect to the center embedding vcenter is:

∂L/∂v = −(1 − σ(uposTv)) · upos + Σk σ(uneg_kTv) · uneg_k (3.2)

The first term pulls v toward upos (the true context movie). The second term pushes v away from each negative. The sigmoid controls the strength: when the positive score is already high (σ ≈ 1), the pull weakens. When a negative score is low (σ ≈ 0), the push weakens. The gradients are self-calibrating — they're largest where the model is most wrong.

Similarly, the gradient with respect to upos is: ∂L/∂upos = −(1 − σ(uposTv)) · v — the context embedding is pulled toward the center embedding. This symmetric pull is what creates co-occurrence structure in the embedding space.

import torch, torch.nn as nn, torch.nn.functional as F

class SkipGramNegSampling(nn.Module):
    """word2vec skip-gram with negative sampling."""
    def __init__(self, vocab_size, embed_dim):
        super().__init__()
        self.center_emb = nn.Embedding(vocab_size, embed_dim)
        self.context_emb = nn.Embedding(vocab_size, embed_dim)
        # Initialize small
        nn.init.uniform_(self.center_emb.weight, -0.5/embed_dim, 0.5/embed_dim)
        nn.init.zeros_(self.context_emb.weight)

    def forward(self, center, pos_context, neg_context):
        # center: (B,)  pos_context: (B,)  neg_context: (B, K)
        v = self.center_emb(center)             # (B, d)
        u_pos = self.context_emb(pos_context)   # (B, d)
        u_neg = self.context_emb(neg_context)   # (B, K, d)

        # Positive: log σ(u_pos · v)
        pos_score = torch.sum(v * u_pos, dim=-1)            # (B,)
        pos_loss = -F.logsigmoid(pos_score)                   # (B,)

        # Negative: Σ log σ(-u_neg · v)
        neg_score = torch.bmm(u_neg, v.unsqueeze(-1)).squeeze(-1)  # (B, K)
        neg_loss = -F.logsigmoid(-neg_score).sum(dim=-1)    # (B,)

        return (pos_loss + neg_loss).mean()
Subtle detail

The noise distribution matters more than you'd think.

Drawing negatives uniformly is suboptimal — frequent words (popular movies) get sampled too rarely as negatives, letting the model ignore them. The original paper uses P(w)^0.75 — raising the unigram frequency to the 0.75 power — which upsamples rare words and downsamples frequent words. For CineMatch, this means less popular but distinctive movies (indie horror, foreign art films) get sampled more often as negatives, which helps the model learn finer distinctions.

Negative samplingQuestion 03 / 06
Why does negative sampling replace the full softmax in word2vec?
ASoftmax produces probabilities that are too smooth.
BSoftmax requires a GPU while negative sampling works on CPU.
CSoftmax can only handle vocabularies under 10,000.
DThe full softmax denominator requires computing u^T·v for all V words per training pair — O(V) per step. With V=50K and millions of pairs, this is prohibitively expensive. Negative sampling replaces it with K+1 dot products per pair (1 positive + K negatives), reducing per-step cost from O(V) to O(K) where K is typically 5-20.
04 — item2vec
item2vec — word2vec for CineMatch movies.

The word2vec idea generalizes beyond text. Replace "word" with "item" and "sentence" with "user session" and you get item2vec (Barkan & Koenigstein, 2016) — the same skip-gram architecture applied to item co-occurrence in user behavior sequences.

For CineMatch: each user's watch-history is a "sentence." Each movie is a "word." We train skip-gram with negative sampling on millions of watch-histories. The resulting embeddings place movies near each other if they tend to be watched by the same users in similar contexts.

# CineMatch item2vec training data generation
# Each user's watch-history is a "sentence"
# Sliding window of size 5 generates (center, context) pairs

def generate_pairs(histories, window=5):
    """
    histories: list of lists of movie IDs
    yields: (center_id, context_id) pairs
    """
    for history in histories:
        for i, center in enumerate(history):
            # Random window size for variety (like original paper)
            w = random.randint(1, window)
            for j in range(max(0, i-w), min(len(history), i+w+1)):
                if j != i:
                    yield center, history[j]

CineMatch Integration

Three ways CineMatch uses item2vec embeddings.

1. Initialization. The CineMatchTransformer's nn.Embedding(50000, 256) can be initialized with pretrained item2vec vectors (padded from 128 to 256 dims, or retrained at 256). This gives the transformer a massive head start — the embedding table already knows that Godfather and Goodfellas are similar, so the transformer only needs to learn the sequence patterns on top.

2. Cold-start features. For new movies with no watch-history yet, item2vec trained on plot descriptions or metadata can provide a reasonable initial embedding. The transformer sees a new movie as "similar to the cluster of action movies" rather than as a random vector.

3. Nearest-neighbor retrieval. At serving time, the top-500 candidates from the transformer can be supplemented with nearest-neighbors from the item2vec embedding space — a simple and fast fallback that works even when the transformer hasn't seen a movie in its training data.

05 — Arithmetic
Embedding arithmetic — the geometry of meaning.

The most celebrated property of word2vec embeddings is that vector arithmetic encodes semantic relationships:

vec("king") − vec("man") + vec("woman") ≈ vec("queen") (5.1)

This works because the embedding space encodes "royal" and "gender" as roughly independent directions. Subtracting "man" removes the male component; adding "woman" adds the female component; the result lands near "queen."

For CineMatch's item2vec, the same principle holds with taste dimensions:

vec("Alien") − vec("horror") + vec("comedy") ≈ vec("Spaceballs") (5.2)

The embedding space learns "genre," "era," "pacing," "visual-style" as implicit directions. These are never labeled — they emerge purely from co-occurrence patterns in watch-histories.

Evaluation: intrinsic vs extrinsic.

Evaluation typeMethodWhat it measures
IntrinsicNearest neighbors — are similar items nearby?Embedding geometry quality
IntrinsicAnalogy completion (A:B::C:?)Linear structure in embedding space
ExtrinsicUse embeddings as features in downstream model (recommender, classifier)Task-level impact — the one that matters

Intrinsic evaluation is fast but unreliable — embeddings with great nearest-neighbors can still hurt a downstream task. Always evaluate extrinsically: does pretrained item2vec improve CineMatchTransformer's Hits@10?

Historical note

word2vec (2013) → GloVe (2014) → subword (2016) → contextual (2018).

word2vec gave each word a single vector — "bank" has one embedding regardless of whether it means "riverbank" or "financial institution." GloVe (2014) factored a global co-occurrence matrix for similar results. FastText (2016) added subword information, helping with morphology and rare words. Then BERT (2018) and GPT (2018) introduced contextual embeddings — each word gets a different vector depending on its surrounding sentence, produced by a transformer. Today, word2vec-style static embeddings are still used for item embeddings (where context doesn't change the item's meaning) and as initialization for transformer vocabularies.

06 — Synthesis
Connecting word2vec to the transformer stack.

word2vec and the transformer share a deep conceptual thread: both learn representations from context. word2vec predicts context words from a center word using a shallow model. A transformer predicts the next token from all preceding tokens using a deep model. The transformer's embedding table is, in a sense, a massively more powerful version of word2vec — it produces contextual embeddings that change depending on the surrounding sequence, not static embeddings that are the same regardless of context.

The evolution looks like this:

ModelYearEmbedding typeContext?CineMatch use
word2vec / item2vec2013Static (one vector per item)No — same vector regardless of surrounding itemsPretrain movie embeddings; cold-start; nearest-neighbor fallback
LSTM encoder2015Contextual (one vector per item per position)Yes — hidden state encodes left contextPhase 3 recommender
Transformer2017Contextual (one vector per item per position)Yes — attention encodes full bidirectional or causal contextPhase 4 recommender

The key insight: the transformer subsumes word2vec. If you train a 1-layer transformer with no FFN and a single attention head on the skip-gram objective, you approximately recover word2vec. The transformer is the generalization — deeper, contextual, and vastly more powerful — but word2vec remains useful as an efficient, simple baseline and as a pretraining step for the embedding table.
