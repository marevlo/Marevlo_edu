Likelihood Inference & MLE

Linear regression had "least squares." Logistic regression had "logistic loss." But behind both there's one common principle — the parameters that make the observed data most likely to have happened. Let's meet that principle.

Part 1 of Module 5.4 covers the first two ideas of likelihood-based inference. First, the likelihood function — a number that scores any candidate set of parameters by how well it explains the observed data. Second, negative log-likelihood (NLL) — the practical way we work with the likelihood, the universal "loss function" of statistics and machine learning. Together these explain why least squares is "likelihood for regression," logistic loss is "likelihood for classification," and a single principle ties almost every statistical model together. Part 2 then completes the picture with the probabilistic interpretation and MLE in action.

Topic 1
The Likelihood Function
What it is

What is Likelihood?

Picture this. You flip a coin 10 times and see 7 heads and 3 tails. Is this a fair coin (p = 0.5) or a biased one (say, p = 0.7)? Both are possible — but one of them fits the data better.

The likelihood function turns this idea into a number. For any candidate value of the parameter (call it θ), the likelihood is the probability of observing exactly the data we saw, if θ were the true value. Higher likelihood = better fit; lower likelihood = worse fit.

L(θ) = P(observed data | parameter is θ)
  • L(θ) is a function of the parameter, not of the data — the data is fixed at what we observed.
  • For each guess at θ, you get a single number. Plot the likelihood across all possible θ and you get a curve.
  • The peak of that curve identifies the parameter that explains the data best. That peak is called the maximum likelihood estimate (MLE).
Simple meaning: Likelihood is the score we give every possible parameter setting, saying "how well would this setting explain what actually happened?" The setting with the highest score wins.

Likelihood vs probability — the subtle but important difference

Beginners often confuse these two words. Both involve P(data | θ), but the spotlight shifts:

Probability

  • Parameter θ is fixed; we ask how likely different data outcomes are
  • "Given a fair coin, what's the chance of 7 heads in 10 flips?"
  • Function of data; sums to 1 over all possible data

Likelihood

  • Data is fixed at what we observed; we ask how well different θ fit it
  • "Given that we saw 7 heads in 10 flips, what θ best explains this?"
  • Function of θ; does NOT sum to 1 (it's not a probability distribution over θ)
Why we need it

Why Likelihood Is the Universal Scoring Rule

Why not just pick parameters by intuition or trial-and-error? Three good reasons:

1. One principle, every model

  • Linear regression — pick β to maximise likelihood (turns into least squares)
  • Logistic regression — pick β to maximise likelihood (turns into logistic loss)
  • Time-series, survival, neural networks — same idea

2. Statistical properties

  • Maximum-likelihood estimates are consistent (closer to truth with more data)
  • They are efficient (smallest possible variance among unbiased estimators)
  • Backed by deep theorems from statistics

3. Drives uncertainty estimates

  • The shape of the likelihood curve gives standard errors
  • Sharp peak → confident estimate; flat peak → uncertain
  • Confidence intervals, p-values — all built from likelihood
Big idea: Likelihood is the foundation of modern statistical inference. Almost every estimator you've used — including ordinary least squares — is really a maximum-likelihood estimator in disguise. Understanding likelihood means understanding the engine behind nearly every model.
Why it's used in practice

From One Data Point to Many — Why Multiplication Matters

When the data consists of multiple independent observations, the likelihood of the whole dataset is the product of the individual likelihoods:

L(θ) = P(y₁ | θ) × P(y₂ | θ) × P(y₃ | θ) × … × P(yₙ | θ)

Why a product? Because the probability of several independent events all happening is the product of their individual probabilities. If two independent events have 60% and 70% chance, both happening has 60% × 70% = 42% chance.

So for n observations, we string together n probabilities. The set of parameters that makes this whole product as large as possible is the maximum-likelihood estimate.

One subtlety: the product of many small numbers gets tiny very fast. For 100 data points each with P = 0.1, the product is 0.1¹⁰⁰ — vanishingly small. Computers struggle with this. Topic 2 introduces the standard fix: take logs.
How it works

The Likelihood Workflow — Step by Step

  1. Decide on the model. What family of distributions are you fitting? (Bernoulli for yes/no, Normal for measurements, etc.) The model determines the form of P(y | θ).
  2. Write the likelihood for one data point. Plug the observation into the chosen distribution. That gives you P(yᵢ | θ).
  3. Multiply across all observations. Build L(θ) = ∏ P(yᵢ | θ). Now L is a function of θ alone — the data is locked in.
  4. Find the θ that maximises L. Take the derivative, set it to zero, solve. (Or, in practice, let an optimiser like gradient ascent find it.)
  5. That θ is the MLE. Report it as your best estimate. The shape of L around its peak gives you the uncertainty.

This recipe is universal. Whether you're fitting a fair-coin probability, a regression slope, or a 100-million-parameter neural network, you are following these five steps.

⟁ Worked Example

Example: Coin Flips — How Biased Is It?

You flip a coin 10 times and see 7 heads. The unknown parameter is θ = P(heads). What's the most likely θ?

For one flip, the probability of heads is θ and the probability of tails is (1 − θ). The likelihood of the whole dataset (7 heads, 3 tails) is:

L(θ) = θ⁷ × (1 − θ)³

Formally: for n coin flips with k heads, the MLE of θ is always k/n — exactly the sample proportion. This matches everyday intuition. The likelihood framework recovers it as a consequence, not an assumption.

The lesson: The likelihood function turns "best guess" into a precise mathematical optimisation. For the coin, it gave us 0.7 = 7/10. For more complex models the answer is less obvious, but the procedure is identical.

⟁ The General Likelihood Function
L(θ) = ∏ᵢ₌₁ⁿ P(yᵢ | θ)

What each symbol means:

L(θ) — likelihood of parameter θ given the observed dataset
θ — parameter (or vector of parameters) being scored
yᵢ — the i-th observed data point
P(yᵢ | θ) — probability (or density) of yᵢ assuming parameter θ
∏ — product symbol; multiply across all i
n — number of independent observations

How to read it in words: "For each data point i from 1 to n, compute the probability that the model with parameters θ would have produced exactly that observation. Then multiply all those probabilities together. The result is a single number, the likelihood — how well the parameter θ explains the entire dataset."

Why this form? Independence of observations means the probability of seeing the whole dataset is the product of seeing each individual point. The likelihood function locks in the data and lets θ vary — so we can find which θ makes the product biggest.

For the coin example: n = 10 flips, k = 7 heads. Each flip has probability θ if heads, (1−θ) if tails. Multiplying gives L(θ) = θ⁷(1−θ)³. The peak is at θ = 0.7.

When to use it

When Likelihood Inference Is the Right Tool

  • You have a probabilistic model for how the data was generated (Bernoulli, Normal, Poisson, etc.).
  • You want a principled, automatic estimator of the model's parameters — not just an intuition.
  • You want uncertainty quantification alongside point estimates (standard errors, confidence intervals).
  • You're working in any branch of statistical modelling — regression, classification, time-series, mixed models, survival, neural networks.
What likelihood needs: a clearly specified probability model for the data. If you don't know the data-generating process even roughly, MLE has no foothold. In that case, model-free methods (bootstrap, decision trees, k-NN) are alternatives.
Real-life examples

Where the Likelihood Principle Shows Up

📊 Polling & Surveys

Estimating the proportion of voters supporting a candidate is a coin-flip likelihood problem at scale. Reported margin-of-error comes from the likelihood curve's width.

⚙️ Reliability Engineering

The mean time between failures for industrial equipment is estimated by maximum likelihood on lifetime distributions (exponential, Weibull).

🦠 Disease Spread

Epidemiologists estimate the reproductive number R0 by fitting infection counts with likelihood — same principle, different distribution.

🧬 Genomics

The probability that a DNA sequence comes from a particular gene family is computed as a likelihood, and used to score sequence matches.

📈 Finance

Risk models estimate volatility parameters by maximising the likelihood of observed daily returns under assumed distributions.

🤖 ML Foundations

Training a neural network for classification is maximum likelihood with cross-entropy loss. The loss function is just the negative log-likelihood.

Topic 2
Negative Log-Likelihood
What it is

From Likelihood to Negative Log-Likelihood

The likelihood as a product of many probabilities has a practical problem — it gets tiny very fast. With 100 data points, each with probability around 0.1, the product is 10⁻¹⁰⁰. Computers struggle to store such small numbers (they underflow to zero), and even if they could, comparing tiny values is awkward.

The standard fix is to take the natural logarithm. The log of a product becomes a sum of logs:

log L(θ) = Σᵢ₌₁ⁿ log P(yᵢ | θ)

This single trick turns multiplication into addition — much more numerically stable. The log-likelihood reaches its maximum at exactly the same θ as the original likelihood (because log is a monotonically increasing function), so the answer doesn't change.

One last step: by convention, optimisers minimise things rather than maximise them. So we flip the sign and define:

NLL(θ) = − log L(θ) = − Σᵢ₌₁ⁿ log P(yᵢ | θ)

This is the negative log-likelihood (NLL). Minimising NLL is identical to maximising L — same answer, easier maths, easier code.

Simple meaning: NLL is the same likelihood, dressed up in a numerically friendly outfit. Logs replace multiplication with addition. The negative sign converts "maximise" into "minimise" — which is what optimisation libraries everywhere expect.
Why we need it

Three Concrete Wins From Taking Logs and Negating

1. Numerical stability

  • Sums don't underflow like products do
  • Computers handle log P easily
  • Works for huge datasets (millions of points)

2. Cleaner math

  • Derivative of a sum = sum of derivatives
  • Optimisation algorithms work on sums
  • Calculus gets much simpler

3. Matches standard loss functions

  • Cross-entropy loss = NLL for classification
  • Mean Squared Error = NLL under Gaussian noise
  • Every "loss function" you know is NLL in disguise
Big idea: NLL is not a new idea — it's the same likelihood, just in a form that machine learning libraries can actually compute and optimise. Whenever you see "loss = ..." in a model training output, you're almost certainly looking at an NLL.
Why it's everywhere

NLL Is the Universal Loss Function

Here's the beautiful unification: most "loss functions" used in machine learning are special cases of NLL, derived from assuming a specific probability model for the data.

Regression with Normal noise

  • Assume yᵢ ~ Normal(μᵢ = β₀ + β₁xᵢ, σ²)
  • NLL = (1 / 2σ²) × Σ(yᵢ − ŷᵢ)² + constant
  • Minimising NLL ⟺ minimising Σ(yᵢ − ŷᵢ)² = Least Squares

Binary classification (logistic)

  • Assume yᵢ ~ Bernoulli(pᵢ = σ(β₀ + β₁xᵢ))
  • NLL = −Σ [ yᵢ log pᵢ + (1−yᵢ) log(1−pᵢ) ]
  • This is exactly the cross-entropy loss

So linear regression and logistic regression aren't fundamentally different algorithms — they are the same MLE procedure applied to different probability models for the data. The "loss function" changes because the assumed distribution changes. The principle stays the same: pick the parameters that make the observed data most likely.

How to use it

The NLL Workflow in Practice

  1. State your probabilistic model. What distribution generates each yᵢ? (Normal, Bernoulli, Poisson, etc.)
  2. Write the log-probability of one observation. log P(yᵢ | θ).
  3. Sum across all observations. Σᵢ log P(yᵢ | θ) is the log-likelihood.
  4. Negate. NLL = −Σᵢ log P(yᵢ | θ).
  5. Minimise NLL with respect to θ. Either analytically (closed-form solution exists, e.g. for least squares) or numerically (gradient descent for neural networks).
  6. The θ that minimises NLL is the MLE. Report it.
⟁ Worked Example

Example: Connecting NLL to Least Squares

Suppose we fit a linear regression and assume each observation has noise from a Normal distribution:

yᵢ = β₀ + β₁ · xᵢ + εᵢ, with εᵢ ~ Normal(0, σ²)

The density of a Normal value is:

P(yᵢ | β₀, β₁) = (1 / √(2πσ²)) × exp( −(yᵢ − ŷᵢ)² / (2σ²) )

Taking log of one observation:

log P(yᵢ | β₀, β₁) = −½ log(2πσ²) − (yᵢ − ŷᵢ)² / (2σ²)

Summing over n observations and negating:

NLL = (n/2) log(2πσ²) + (1 / 2σ²) × Σ (yᵢ − ŷᵢ)²

The first term doesn't depend on β. So minimising NLL with respect to β₀ and β₁ is the same as minimising:

Σ (yᵢ − ŷᵢ)²

That's Sum of Squared Errors — the exact quantity least squares minimises (Module 5.1, Part 2)!

The lesson: Least squares isn't a separate magic trick. It's MLE under the assumption of Normal noise. Two famous methods, one underlying principle.

⟁ Negative Log-Likelihood in Detail
NLL(θ) = − Σᵢ₌₁ⁿ log P(yᵢ | θ)

What each symbol means:

NLL(θ) — negative log-likelihood; a single non-negative scalar
log — natural logarithm (base e)
P(yᵢ | θ) — probability or density of yᵢ under parameter θ
Σ — sum across all observations
n — total number of observations

How to read it in words: "For each data point, take the model's predicted probability that the observation occurred. Take its log. Add all those logs together. Flip the sign. That's NLL — a single number that you want to make as small as possible."

Why it works as a loss function:

  • If the model assigns high probability to what actually happened, log P is close to 0 (since log 1 = 0); negated, NLL contribution is small. Good model.
  • If the model assigns very low probability to what happened, log P is very negative; negated, NLL contribution is very positive — a big penalty. Bad model.
  • Minimising NLL = pushing the model to assign higher probability to events that actually occurred = exactly what MLE wants.

Famous cases:

  • Normal noise → Mean Squared Error (the loss function used for regression).
  • Bernoulli outcome → Binary Cross-Entropy (the loss function used for binary classification).
  • Categorical outcome → Cross-Entropy (the loss function used for multi-class classification).

Each of these is just NLL with a different P(y | θ) plugged in. Same principle, different model.

When to use NLL

When NLL Is the Right Tool

  • Whenever you are training a probabilistic model by optimisation.
  • When you want a loss function with theoretical backing rather than something ad-hoc.
  • When you have large datasets where products would underflow numerically.
  • When you want to connect your model to standard inference (confidence intervals, model comparison via likelihood ratios).
Real-life examples

Where NLL Drives Real Systems

🧠 Neural Network Training

"Training loss" reported during training is almost always NLL. Cross-entropy = NLL for classification; MSE = NLL for regression with Gaussian noise.

🗣️ Language Models

Large language models train by minimising NLL of the next token under the model. The famous "perplexity" metric is exp(NLL per token).

🩺 Medical Risk Models

Survival models (Cox regression) are fit by maximising the partial likelihood — a special NLL designed for time-to-event data.

📡 Signal Processing

GPS positioning estimates your location by minimising NLL of the satellite signal model — same idea as least squares, derived from likelihood.

🦠 Epidemic Modelling

SIR-model parameters (transmission rate, recovery rate) are estimated by minimising NLL of observed case counts.

📊 Model Selection

Statistical criteria like AIC and BIC are based on NLL — they balance how well a model fits against how many parameters it uses.
