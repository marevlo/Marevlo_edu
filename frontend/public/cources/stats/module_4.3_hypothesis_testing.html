◆ 01
Null & Alternative Hypotheses
⟁ WHAT ARE HYPOTHESES?

In hypothesis testing, we start with two competing statements about a population. Think of them as two possible stories about reality:

  • The Null Hypothesis (H₀): This is the "default" story — the claim that nothing special is happening. There is no effect, no difference, no change from what we already know. It's the position we hold unless we find strong evidence against it.
  • The Alternative Hypothesis (H₁): This is the "challenger" story — the claim that something IS happening. There is an effect, a difference, or a change. This is what the researcher typically hopes to demonstrate.

The key insight: We never try to prove H₀ is true. Instead, we ask: "Is the data so unusual under H₀ that we should reject it in favor of H₁?"

Why This Structure?

This mirrors how courts work. A defendant is presumed innocent (H₀) unless the prosecution presents overwhelming evidence of guilt (H₁). We don't try to prove innocence; we ask if the evidence is strong enough to overturn the presumption.

Key Principle: H₀ always contains an equality (=), while H₁ contains an inequality (<, >, or ≠). This is why H₀ is called the "null" — it represents the neutral, no-effect position.
⟁ ONE-SIDED VS TWO-SIDED TESTS

After stating H₀, we must decide: does H₁ predict a direction, or just a difference?

TWO-SIDED TEST (≠)

  • H₁: μ ≠ μ₀
  • We test if the effect goes in EITHER direction
  • More conservative; splits α into both tails
  • Use when: You don't know which direction the effect will go
  • Example: "Does the new drug change blood pressure?" (up or down)

ONE-SIDED TEST (> or <)

  • H₁: μ > μ₀ (right-tailed) or μ < μ₀ (left-tailed)
  • We test if the effect goes in ONE specific direction
  • More powerful; concentrates all α in one tail
  • Use when: Theory or prior evidence suggests a direction
  • Example: "Does the new drug INCREASE blood pressure?" (directional)
⟁ EXAMPLE — FORMULATING HYPOTHESES

Scenario: A coffee shop claims their espresso shots average 30 mL. A customer suspects they're getting less. How do we set up the test?

Step 1: Identify the parameter

We're testing the mean volume: μ = average mL per shot

Step 2: State H₀ (the default claim)
H₀: μ = 30 mL

The shop's claim is correct; nothing unusual is happening.

Step 3: State H₁ (what we suspect)
H₁: μ < 30 mL

The shots are smaller than claimed. This is ONE-SIDED (left-tailed) because we suspect a specific direction.

◆ 02
Understanding P-Values
⟁ WHAT IS A P-VALUE?

The p-value is perhaps the most misunderstood concept in statistics. Let's be very precise:

Definition: The p-value is the probability of observing data at least as extreme as what we actually observed, assuming H₀ is true. It measures how "unusual" our sample is under the null hypothesis.

Think of it this way: If H₀ were true, how often would we see a result like ours (or more extreme) just by random chance?

Interpreting P-Values

  • Very small p-value (e.g., 0.001): Our data is extremely rare under H₀. This is strong evidence AGAINST H₀.
  • Small p-value (e.g., 0.03): Our data is fairly rare under H₀. This is moderate evidence against H₀.
  • Large p-value (e.g., 0.25): Our data is common under H₀. This is weak evidence against H₀ (or no evidence at all).
⚠ CRITICAL MISCONCEPTION: The p-value is NOT the probability that H₀ is true. It's the probability of the data given that H₀ IS true. These are very different things!
⟁ EXAMPLE — CALCULATING A P-VALUE

Scenario: A coin is flipped 100 times and comes up heads 65 times. Is the coin fair?

H₀: p = 0.5 (the coin is fair)

Under a fair coin, we expect about 50 heads in 100 flips.

What we observed: 65 heads (unusual, but possible)

The question: How often would we see 65 or more heads if the coin were actually fair?

The p-value calculation
p-value = P(X ≥ 65 | coin is fair) ≈ 0.0018

If the coin were fair, we'd see 65+ heads only about 0.18% of the time. This is very rare!

Interpretation

The small p-value (0.0018) suggests the coin is probably NOT fair. The data provides strong evidence against H₀.

⟁ COMMON P-VALUE MISCONCEPTIONS

❌ WRONG INTERPRETATION

  • "p = 0.03 means there's a 3% chance H₀ is true"
  • "p = 0.05 means we're 95% confident in our result"
  • "A small p-value proves the effect is large"
  • "p-value tells us the probability of replicating the result"

✓ CORRECT INTERPRETATION

  • "p = 0.03 means: IF H₀ were true, we'd see data this extreme 3% of the time"
  • "p = 0.05 means our data is in the rarest 5% under H₀"
  • "A small p-value means the effect is unlikely to be zero, but says nothing about size"
  • "p-value is computed from THIS sample under THIS H₀"
◆ 03
Significance Level & Decision Rules
⟁ THE SIGNIFICANCE LEVEL α

The significance level α (alpha) is a threshold we set BEFORE collecting data. It answers the question: "How rare must our data be before we reject H₀?"

Why Choose α Before Data Collection?

If we waited to see the p-value first, then chose α to match our result, we'd be cheating. We'd always find "significance." By committing to α in advance, we prevent this bias. It's like deciding the rules of a game before playing.

The Decision Rule: If p ≤ α, reject H₀. Otherwise, fail to reject H₀.

Common Choices for α

  • α = 0.05: The standard in most fields. We accept a 5% chance of a false positive.
  • α = 0.01: More stringent. Used in high-stakes decisions (medical trials, safety testing).
  • α = 0.10: More lenient. Used in exploratory research where false negatives are costly.
⚠ IMPORTANT: α is NOT the probability that H₀ is true. It's the probability of rejecting H₀ when it IS actually true (Type I error). We'll explain this next.
⟁ EXAMPLE — CHOOSING α

Scenario 1 (Medical Trial): Testing a new cancer drug. A false positive (approving a useless drug) is very costly.

Decision:
α = 0.01

We require very strong evidence (p ≤ 0.01) before approving the drug. We accept only a 1% risk of false approval.

Scenario 2 (Market Research): Testing if a new ad campaign increases engagement. A false positive is less costly.

Decision:
α = 0.10

We use a more lenient threshold (p ≤ 0.10). We're willing to accept a 10% risk of false positive to avoid missing real opportunities.

⟁ STATISTICAL SIGNIFICANCE VS PRACTICAL IMPORTANCE

A result can be statistically significant without being practically important. Here's why:

With a very large sample size, even tiny effects become statistically significant. For example, a new website design might increase click-through rate from 10.0% to 10.1%. With n = 100,000 visitors, this tiny difference might have p < 0.001 (highly significant). But is a 0.1% improvement worth the cost of redesigning?

Remember: Statistical significance means "unlikely to be due to chance." Practical importance means "large enough to matter in the real world." Always report the effect size (how big the effect is), not just the p-value.
◆ 04
Type I & Type II Errors
⟁ TWO TYPES OF ERRORS

When we make a decision based on a hypothesis test, we can be right or wrong in two different ways:

TYPE I ERROR (False Positive)

  • We reject H₀ when it's actually TRUE
  • We conclude there's an effect when there isn't one
  • Probability = α (the significance level)
  • Example: Convicting an innocent person
  • Example: Approving a drug that doesn't actually work

TYPE II ERROR (False Negative)

  • We fail to reject H₀ when it's actually FALSE
  • We conclude there's no effect when there is one
  • Probability = β (beta)
  • Example: Acquitting a guilty person
  • Example: Rejecting a drug that actually works

The Trade-Off

Here's the catch: we can't eliminate both errors simultaneously. If we make α smaller (fewer false positives), β gets larger (more false negatives). It's a seesaw.

The Solution: Choose α based on which error is more costly in your context. Then increase sample size to reduce both errors.
⟁ EXAMPLE — TYPE I VS TYPE II ERRORS

Scenario: A factory tests if a machine is producing parts within specification.

H₀: The machine is working correctly

H₁: The machine is broken and needs repair

TYPE I ERROR (False Positive):

We conclude the machine is broken when it's actually fine. Cost: Unnecessary downtime and repair costs.

TYPE II ERROR (False Negative):

We conclude the machine is fine when it's actually broken. Cost: Defective parts shipped to customers, reputation damage.

Decision:

Type II error is more costly (customer harm), so we choose α = 0.10 (more sensitive to detecting problems). We accept a higher false-positive rate to catch real problems.

◆ 05
Statistical Power
⟁ STATISTICAL POWER

Power is the probability that we correctly reject H₀ when it's actually false. In other words, it's our ability to detect a real effect.

Formula: Power = 1 − β

Where β is the Type II error rate (probability of missing a real effect).

Interpreting Power

  • Power = 0.80: If the effect is real, we have an 80% chance of detecting it. Standard target in most fields.
  • Power = 0.90: If the effect is real, we have a 90% chance of detecting it. Used when false negatives are costly.
  • Power = 0.50: If the effect is real, we have only a 50% chance of detecting it. Coin flip! Unacceptable.

What Affects Power?

INCREASES POWER

  • Larger sample size (n)
  • Larger true effect size
  • Larger significance level α
  • One-sided test (vs two-sided)
  • Less variability in the data

DECREASES POWER

  • Smaller sample size
  • Smaller true effect size
  • Smaller significance level α
  • Two-sided test (vs one-sided)
  • More variability in the data
Practical Tip: The most reliable way to increase power is to increase sample size. This is why researchers do power analysis BEFORE collecting data to determine how many subjects they need.
⟁ EXAMPLE — POWER IN PRACTICE

Scenario: A company wants to test if a new training program improves employee productivity. They want 80% power to detect a meaningful improvement.

Step 1: Define the effect size

Based on prior research, a meaningful improvement is a 10% increase in productivity.

Step 2: Set α and desired power
α = 0.05, Power = 0.80 (so β = 0.20)
Step 3: Calculate required sample size

Using power analysis software: n = 64 employees per group

Interpretation

With 64 employees in each group (trained vs control), if the training truly improves productivity by 10%, we have an 80% chance of detecting it with our test.

◆ 01
Null & Alternative Hypotheses
⟁ WHAT IS IT

The two competing statements

A hypothesis is a precise statement about a population that we want to test using sample data. In every hypothesis test we begin with two competing statements that together cover all possibilities.

The Null Hypothesis (H₀)

The null hypothesis, written H₀ and read as "H-naught," is the statement of no effect, no change, or no difference. It represents the default position — the claim that nothing interesting is happening in the population. The data must provide evidence against H₀ for it to be rejected.

Example. A manufacturer states that the mean weight of a coffee packet is 250 g.
H₀: μ = 250 — the mean weight equals 250 g (the manufacturer's claim holds).

The Alternative Hypothesis (H₁)

The alternative hypothesis, written H₁ or Hₐ, is the statement that opposes the null hypothesis H₀ ​ . It represents the effect, difference, or relationship that the researcher wants to test or find evidence for.

Continuing the example.
H₁: μ ≠ 250 — the true mean weight differs from 250 g.
⟁ WHY DO WE NEED IT

The problem hypothesis testing solves

In practice, sample data will almost always look slightly different from any specific claim about the population. The question is whether the observed difference is large enough to be considered real, or whether it can easily be explained by random sampling variation alone.

Hypothesis testing solves this problem by providing a structured, repeatable rule for separating signal from noise. Without such a framework, decisions based on data would rely on personal judgment, which is inconsistent across people and situations.

The "innocent until proven guilty" structure

Hypothesis testing follows the same logic as a court of law. The defendant is presumed innocent (H₀) and the prosecution must provide sufficient evidence to support guilt (H₁). The data act as evidence. H₀ is rejected only when the evidence is strong enough. Note that "failing to reject H₀" is not the same as proving H₀ true; it simply means the evidence was insufficient to overturn the default.

Formulation rule. H₀ always contains an equality (=, ≤, or ≥). H₁ states the strict inequality (≠, >, or <) that the test aims to detect.
⟁ TYPES OF ALTERNATIVE HYPOTHESES

Three forms of the alternative

The alternative hypothesis can take three forms, depending on the direction of the effect being tested. The shape of H₁ determines whether the test is one-sided or two-sided.

TWO-SIDED TEST

Detects a difference in either direction.

H₀: μ = 250
H₁: μ ≠ 250

RIGHT-TAILED TEST

Detects an increase only.

H₀: μ ≤ 250
H₁: μ > 250

LEFT-TAILED TEST

Detects a decrease only.

H₀: μ ≥ 250
H₁: μ < 250

◆ 02
P-values
⟁ WHAT IS IT

Definition

A p-value is a probability between 0 and 1. Formally, it is the probability of observing a result at least as extreme as the one obtained, computed under the assumption that H₀ is true.

A small p-value indicates that the observed data would be unusual if H₀ were true, which provides evidence against H₀. A large p-value indicates that the data are consistent with H₀, providing no evidence to reject it.

Interpretation scale

  • p < 0.01 — strong evidence against H₀
  • 0.01 ≤ p < 0.05 — moderate evidence against H₀
  • 0.05 ≤ p < 0.10 — weak or marginal evidence
  • p ≥ 0.10 — insufficient evidence against H₀

Illustrative example — testing a coin for fairness

Suppose we test whether a coin is fair. The null hypothesis is H₀: P(heads) = 0.5. We flip the coin 100 times and observe 80 heads. Under H₀, the expected number of heads is 50, with a standard deviation of about 5. Observing 80 is six standard deviations above the expected value — an extremely unlikely event. The p-value is approximately 1 × 10⁻⁹, far below any conventional threshold. We reject H₀ and conclude the coin is likely biased.

⟁ FORMAL DEFINITION OF P-VALUE
p-value = P( T ≥ t_observed | H₀ true )
The expression reads: "the probability that the test statistic T is at least as extreme as the value t_observed that we computed from our data, given that H₀ is true."
⟁ SYMBOL EXPLANATION
p
The p-value itself. A number between 0 and 1.
P( · )
"Probability of." The operator that returns the probability of the event inside the parentheses.
T
The test statistic — a random quantity computed from the sample (for example, a z-score or t-score).
t_observed
The specific numerical value of T that we actually obtained from our data.
≥
"At least as extreme as." For a right-tailed test this means greater than or equal to.
|
"Given" or "conditional on." The vertical bar separates the event from the condition under which it is evaluated.
H₀ true
The condition under which the probability is calculated — we assume the null hypothesis is correct.
Important clarification. The p-value is not the probability that H₀ is true. It is the probability of obtaining data this extreme assuming H₀ is true. These two probabilities are conceptually distinct and should not be confused.

◆ 03
Statistical Significance
⟁ THE DECISION THRESHOLD

The significance level α

To convert a p-value into a clear yes-or-no decision, we must specify a threshold in advance. This threshold is called the significance level, denoted by α (the Greek letter alpha). Standard choices are α = 0.05, 0.01, or 0.10. The most widely used default is α = 0.05.

Decision rule.
If p ≤ α — reject H₀; the result is declared statistically significant.
If p > α — fail to reject H₀; the result is not statistically significant.

Interpretation of α

The significance level α equals the probability of rejecting H₀ when it is actually true. If α = 0.05, the procedure will incorrectly reject a true null in about 5% of repeated experiments. The choice of α controls how often we are willing to make this specific type of error.

Statistical significance is not the same as practical importance. With a sufficiently large sample, even tiny effects can become statistically significant. A significant result indicates that an effect exists in the population — it does not, on its own, tell us whether that effect is large enough to matter in practice. Always report the effect size alongside the p-value.
⟁ DECISION RULE
Reject H₀   if   p ≤ α
Reject the null hypothesis whenever the computed p-value is less than or equal to the pre-specified significance level α.
⟁ SYMBOL EXPLANATION
p
The p-value computed from the sample data, between 0 and 1.
α
The Greek letter alpha (pronounced "AL-fa"). The significance level — the maximum chance of a false positive that we are willing to accept. Common values: 0.05, 0.01, 0.10.
≤
"Less than or equal to." If p is at this threshold or below, we reject H₀.
H₀
The null hypothesis we are testing.
◆ 04
Type I & Type II Errors
⟁ THE TWO POSSIBLE ERRORS

Four possible outcomes

A hypothesis test produces one of four outcomes, determined by the actual truth of H₀ and the decision made. Two outcomes are correct; the other two represent errors.

H₀ is TRUEH₀ is FALSE
Reject H₀ Type I Error
Probability = α		 Correct Decision
Probability = 1 − β (power)
Fail to Reject H₀ Correct Decision
Probability = 1 − α		 Type II Error
Probability = β

Type I Error (α) — False Positive

A Type I error occurs when H₀ is rejected even though H₀ is true. The test detects an effect that does not actually exist. The significance level α directly controls this error rate.

Example. A diagnostic test indicates the presence of a disease in a patient who is actually healthy.

Type II Error (β) — False Negative

A Type II error occurs when we fail to reject H₀ even though H₀ is false. The test misses a real effect. Its probability is denoted by β (the Greek letter beta).

Example. A diagnostic test returns negative for a patient who actually has the disease.
Trade-off. For a fixed sample size, lowering α (reducing false positives) increases β (more false negatives), and vice versa. The only general way to reduce both errors at the same time is to increase the sample size.
⟁ TYPE I ERROR RATE
α = P( Reject H₀ | H₀ true )
α is the probability of rejecting the null hypothesis when it is actually true — that is, the probability of a false positive.
⟁ SYMBOL EXPLANATION
α
Greek letter alpha. The Type I error rate. Set by the researcher before the data are analyzed (commonly 0.05).
P( · )
Probability operator.
Reject H₀
The event in which the test concludes against the null hypothesis.
|
"Given." The probability is computed under the stated condition.
H₀ true
The condition assumed when computing this probability — that the null hypothesis is actually correct.
⟁ TYPE II ERROR RATE
β = P( Fail to reject H₀ | H₀ false )
β is the probability of failing to reject H₀ when H₀ is actually false — that is, the probability of a false negative.
⟁ SYMBOL EXPLANATION
β
Greek letter beta (pronounced "BAY-ta"). The Type II error rate. Depends on the sample size, the true effect size, the population variability, and α.
Fail to reject H₀
The event in which the test does not reject the null hypothesis.
H₀ false
The condition assumed when computing this probability — that the null hypothesis is actually incorrect.
◆ 05
Test Power
⟁ SENSITIVITY OF THE TEST

Definition

The power of a hypothesis test is the probability that the test correctly rejects H₀ when H₀ is false. Equivalently, it is the probability of avoiding a Type II error.

Power = 1 − β
Power is the complement of the Type II error rate.

Why power matters

A test with low power may fail to detect real effects, leading to incorrect conclusions of "no effect" even when one is present. A study that lacks sufficient power can produce misleading results and waste resources. Power analysis is a standard step in experimental design — researchers calculate the required sample size before running a study so that the test will have enough sensitivity to detect effects of practical interest.

Factors that affect power

  • Sample size (n). Larger samples reduce variability and increase power. This is the most reliable lever.
  • Effect size. Larger true differences are easier to detect, leading to higher power.
  • Significance level (α). A larger α raises power but also raises the Type I error rate.
  • Population variability (σ). Less variable data produce higher power.
Conventional target. A power of at least 0.80 is the standard benchmark in research and industry. This corresponds to an 80% chance of detecting a real effect when one exists.
⟁ POWER FUNCTION
Power = 1 − β = P( Reject H₀ | H₁ true )
Power is the probability of correctly rejecting H₀ when a specific alternative hypothesis H₁ is the true state of the world.
⟁ SYMBOL EXPLANATION
Power
The sensitivity of the test — the chance of correctly detecting a real effect. A number between 0 and 1.
1 − β
"One minus beta." The complement of the Type II error rate.
β
The Type II error rate (probability of a false negative).
Reject H₀
The event of correctly concluding against the null hypothesis.
H₁ true
The condition under which power is evaluated — that the alternative hypothesis is correct.

◆ 06
Standard Testing Procedure
⟁ THE SIX-STEP PROCESS

Every hypothesis test follows the same structured procedure, regardless of the specific statistical method used.

STEP 1
State the hypotheses. Write H₀ and H₁ in clear mathematical form. Decide whether the test is one-sided or two-sided.
STEP 2
Choose the significance level. Fix α before collecting or examining the data. Common choices: 0.05 or 0.01.
STEP 3
Select the test statistic. Choose a quantity (such as z, t, χ², or F) whose sampling distribution is known under H₀.
STEP 4
Compute the test statistic and the p-value using the sample data.
STEP 5
Apply the decision rule. Reject H₀ if p ≤ α; otherwise fail to reject H₀.
STEP 6
State the conclusion in the context of the original problem, including the effect size where appropriate.
⟁ WORKED EXAMPLE — A/B TEST FOR CONVERSION RATE

Problem. A product team launches a new checkout page design. Historically the conversion rate is 12%. They run an experiment on n = 400 new visitors and observe a sample conversion rate of p̂ = 0.15. Is the new design significantly better? Use α = 0.05.

Step 1 — State the hypotheses

H₀: p = 0.12 (the new design has no effect)
H₁: p > 0.12 (the new design is better) — right-tailed test

Step 2 — Significance level
α = 0.05
Step 3 — Test statistic (z for proportions)
z = (p̂ − p₀) / √(p₀(1−p₀)/n)

Where p̂ is the sample proportion, p₀ is the value claimed under H₀, and n is the sample size.

Step 4 — Compute
Standard error = √(0.12 × 0.88 / 400) = √0.000264 ≈ 0.01625
z = (0.15 − 0.12) / 0.01625 ≈ 1.846
p-value (right-tailed) = P(Z > 1.846) ≈ 0.0324
Step 5 — Decision rule

Since p = 0.0324 ≤ 0.05 = α, we reject H₀.

Step 6 — Conclusion

At the 5% significance level, there is statistically significant evidence that the new checkout page improves the conversion rate above the historical baseline of 12%.

◆ 07
Real-World Applications

Machine Learning Model Comparison

Determining whether a new model architecture yields a statistically significant improvement in accuracy over a baseline on held-out test data.

A/B Testing in Product Analytics

Evaluating whether a redesigned interface produces a meaningful change in user conversion rate while controlling false-positive deployments.

Clinical Trials

Testing whether a new treatment produces a clinically meaningful change in a primary outcome. Both Type I and Type II error rates must be carefully managed.

Manufacturing Quality Control

Detecting whether production has drifted from specification limits, triggering recalibration when statistically significant deviations occur.

Quantitative Finance

Testing whether a trading strategy generates returns reliably different from zero after accounting for transaction costs and noise.

Scientific Research

The standard inferential framework across the empirical sciences. The 0.05 threshold has shaped a century of published research.

Correct. Statistical significance indicates the effect is unlikely to be due to chance; the magnitude of the effect must be assessed separately.

// Module 4.3 · Compiled Summary

Hypothesis testing is the formal framework for drawing inferences about populations from sample data. The procedure begins with two competing statements — H₀ and H₁ — and uses the p-value to quantify the strength of evidence against H₀. A pre-specified significance level α determines the threshold for rejection. Two errors are possible: Type I (rejecting a true H₀, rate α) and Type II (failing to reject a false H₀, rate β). The power of a test, equal to 1 − β, measures its ability to detect real effects.

01
H₀ vs H₁. H₀ contains the equality and represents no effect; H₁ specifies the effect of interest.
02
P-value. Probability of observing data at least as extreme as the sample, assuming H₀ is true.
03
Significance level α. Pre-specified decision threshold. Reject H₀ when p ≤ α.
04
Type I error. Rejecting a true H₀ — controlled by α.
05
Type II error. Failing to reject a false H₀ — rate denoted by β.
06
Power = 1 − β. Probability of correctly detecting a true effect; standard target is 0.80.
07
Significance vs importance. A statistically significant result does not necessarily imply a practically meaningful effect.
