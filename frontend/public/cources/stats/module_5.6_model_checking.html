Model Checking & Goodness of Fit

A fitted model returns numbers. The next question is: are those numbers any good? In this module we meet the four most-used scorecards that data scientists and analysts use to answer that question — for regression, for classification, and in everyday work.

After fitting a model we always need to ask: how well does it actually fit the data? This module covers four tools that turn that vague question into precise numbers. R² and adjusted R² measure how much of the variation in y the model explains. RMSE and MAE measure the typical size of the prediction error in the units of the outcome. We will see what each one tells us, when to prefer one over another, and how to pair them with the residual-plot diagnostics from earlier modules. By the end you will be able to read a model report card the way a senior analyst does.

Topic 1
R² — The Coefficient of Determination
What it is

What is R²?

Imagine you have no predictors at all. Your best guess for any new y value would just be the average of y in your data. You would still be wrong sometimes — y varies around that average — but it's the safest blind guess.

Now suppose you fit a regression model that uses one or more predictors. Hopefully the model's predictions are better than the blind average. R² (the coefficient of determination) measures how much of that "variation around the mean" your model managed to capture.

R² = 1 − ( SS_res / SS_tot )
  • SS_tot — the total variation in y around its mean. The size of the problem.
  • SS_res — the leftover variation after the model's predictions. The size of the residuals squared.
  • R² = 1 means SS_res = 0 — the model explains everything perfectly.
  • R² = 0 means SS_res = SS_tot — the model is no better than guessing the mean.
  • R² can even go negative on test data — meaning the model is worse than the simple mean.
Simple meaning: R² is the fraction of the "wiggle" in y that the model successfully explains. An R² of 0.78 means the model explains 78% of the variation in y; the remaining 22% is residual noise the predictors couldn't account for.
Why we need it

Why R² Exists

Raw error numbers like "the average residual was 4.7" don't tell you whether the model is good. Good compared to what? A residual of 4.7 might be tiny for predicting house prices (in lakhs) but massive for predicting test scores (out of 10). R² gives a unit-free comparison: the fraction of variation explained. That makes it portable across problems and easy to communicate.

Compares across problems

  • A 0–1 scale works for any units
  • "78% of variation explained" travels well
  • Useful for talking to non-technical stakeholders

Compares two models on same data

  • Higher R² means more variation captured
  • Used to decide between candidate models
  • Beware: more predictors always raise it (see Topic 2)

Provides reality check

  • R² near 0 → the model isn't learning anything useful
  • R² near 1 → either a great fit or possible overfit
  • Pair with diagnostics before trusting it
How to read it

Reading R² Without Getting Fooled

R² is famous and easy to misread. Three things to keep in mind:

  • Different fields have different baselines. Physics often achieves R² above 0.95. Social science models are often happy with 0.30. Comparing R² values across fields without context is meaningless.
  • A high R² doesn't prove the model is correct. A nonsense model with many predictors can hit a high R² by chance. Always combine R² with residual plots and out-of-sample evaluation.
  • R² can fall on new data. The 0.85 you saw on training might become 0.62 on held-out test data. Report both.
Common mistake: "My R² is 0.99 — the model is perfect." Often this is overfitting (too many parameters relative to data points), data leakage, or a hidden bug. A suspiciously high R² is worth investigating, not celebrating.

⟁ R² — formula in detail
R² = 1 − ( Σ (yᵢ − ŷᵢ)² / Σ (yᵢ − ȳ)² )

What each symbol means:

yᵢ — observed value of the response for observation i
ŷᵢ — model's prediction for that observation
ȳ — simple mean of all y values
Σ (yᵢ − ŷᵢ)² — sum of squared residuals (SS_res)
Σ (yᵢ − ȳ)² — sum of squared deviations from the mean (SS_tot)

How to read it: "Take the total variation in y. Subtract the part the model couldn't explain. Divide by total. That's the fraction explained." If a model leaves zero residuals, R² hits 1. If the model is no better than the mean, the numerator equals the denominator and R² is 0.

Quick check: in a simple linear regression with one predictor, R² also equals the square of the Pearson correlation between x and y. Both numbers are equivalent in this special case, which is why R² is sometimes loosely called "R squared".

When to use it

When R² is the right tool

  • You need a single, unit-free score to summarise how well a regression fits.
  • You want to communicate model quality to non-technical readers.
  • You are comparing models with the same number of predictors on the same data.
  • You are working in a field where R² values have an established interpretation.
When R² alone is not enough: when comparing models with different numbers of predictors (use adjusted R²); when the outcome is classification rather than regression (use accuracy, AUC, log-loss); when you care about the absolute size of errors in real units (use RMSE or MAE).
Real-life examples

Where R² shows up in everyday work

🏠 Real-estate models

House-price models in real-estate platforms typically achieve R² between 0.7 and 0.9. Lower than that and the model becomes unreliable for individual valuations.

📊 Business dashboards

Sales-forecast dashboards report R² as a quality indicator. A drop in R² over time triggers an investigation into model drift.

🧪 Lab calibration

Chemistry calibration curves are required to have R² above 0.99 — otherwise the analytical method is rejected for use.

📈 Finance

Factor models for asset returns rarely exceed R² of 0.4; that's normal for noisy markets and not a sign of model failure.

🩺 Public health

Models linking lifestyle variables to health outcomes often have R² below 0.3, since human health depends on many unmeasured factors.

🤖 ML benchmarks

Regression benchmarks on Kaggle and academic leaderboards regularly report R² as the headline metric alongside RMSE.

Topic 2
Adjusted R²
What it is

What is Adjusted R²?

Plain R² has a subtle problem: it never decreases when you add a new predictor, even if the new predictor is pure noise. The model will always find some tiny way to use that extra column to fit a bit better — and R² will inch up. Without a fix, R² rewards more complex models even when they aren't really better.

Adjusted R² is the fix. It starts with R² but pays a penalty for each predictor you add. If a new predictor truly helps, adjusted R² rises. If the predictor adds nothing useful, adjusted R² falls — even though plain R² may still creep upward.

Adjusted R² = 1 − [ (1 − R²) × (n − 1) / (n − p − 1) ]
  • n — number of observations
  • p — number of predictors in the model (not counting the intercept)
  • R² — the plain R² of the same model
Simple meaning: Adjusted R² answers the question "is this extra predictor pulling its weight?" If yes, the number goes up. If the predictor is just noise, the penalty wins and the number goes down. It's R² with an honesty filter for model complexity.
Why we need it

Why a Penalty for Complexity Matters

Without a complexity penalty, model selection becomes a race to the bottom: throw in every column you can find, R² will rise, and you'll declare victory. But that "victory" is overfitting in disguise — the model has memorised noise that won't repeat on new data.

Plain R²

  • Always ≥ R² of any subset model
  • Rewards adding predictors
  • Useless for choosing between models of different sizes

Adjusted R²

  • Penalises useless predictors
  • Can go down when a bad predictor is added
  • Helps choose between models of different sizes

Two simple rules of thumb:

  • To describe how well a single model fits — use R².
  • To compare models with different numbers of predictors — use adjusted R² (or use cross-validation from Module 5.5).
⟁ Adjusted R² — formula in detail
Adj. R² = 1 − ( (1 − R²) · (n − 1) / (n − p − 1) )

What each symbol means:

R² — plain R² of the model
n — number of observations
p — number of predictors in the model (intercept not counted)
n − p − 1 — degrees of freedom remaining

How to read it: The fraction (n−1)/(n−p−1) grows as p grows. So the term (1 − R²) gets multiplied by a bigger and bigger number, pulling adjusted R² downward. A new predictor only "earns" its place if it raises R² enough to overcome that pull.

One useful property: adjusted R² can be negative even when plain R² is positive. That happens when you've added so many useless predictors that the penalty exceeds whatever fit you gained. It's a clear signal to drop predictors.

Example: should we add "day of week" to our sales model?

A sales-forecasting team has a 100-observation dataset and tests two model versions:

  • Model A: sales ~ ad_spend + price (p = 2). R² = 0.620.
  • Model B: sales ~ ad_spend + price + day_of_week (p = 3). R² = 0.625.

The new predictor only nudged R² up by 0.005. Worth keeping? Compute adjusted R² for both:

A — without day20.6200.380 × 99 / 97 = 0.3880.612

Real-life examples

Where Adjusted R² Drives Real Decisions

📰 Academic publishing

Journals in economics, psychology and medicine routinely require adjusted R² in published regression tables, not just plain R².

🏗️ Engineering models

Structural-load models with many sensor inputs use adjusted R² to decide which sensors carry real signal versus redundant noise.

📊 Marketing-mix models

When deciding whether to include another media channel in an attribution model, marketers compare adjusted R² before and after.

🧬 Bioinformatics

Gene-expression models with thousands of candidate features use adjusted R² (alongside cross-validation) to flag which features add real predictive power.

Topic 3
RMSE & MAE — Error in Real Units
What they are

Two Ways to Summarise Prediction Errors

R² is unit-free — useful for comparison but useless if a stakeholder asks "how far off are the predictions on average, in rupees?" Two metrics answer that question directly:

RMSE — Root Mean Squared Error

  • Square the residuals, average them, take the square root
  • Result is in the same units as y
  • Penalises large errors heavily (because of squaring)

MAE — Mean Absolute Error

  • Take the absolute value of each residual, then average
  • Also in the units of y
  • Treats all errors equally, regardless of size
RMSE = √ ( (1 / n) · Σ (yᵢ − ŷᵢ)² )
MAE = (1 / n) · Σ |yᵢ − ŷᵢ|
Simple meaning: Both numbers tell you how far off the predictions are, on average, in the original units. RMSE punishes large errors more harshly because it squares them first. MAE is a calmer, more democratic average — every error is treated by its size, no extra penalty for being big.
Why both exist

RMSE vs MAE — Two Personalities

If you only had to pick one error number, RMSE is the most common default. But RMSE and MAE answer subtly different questions, and the gap between them tells you something useful about the data.

When RMSE makes sense

  • Large errors are especially costly
  • You want the model to avoid big misses even at the cost of a few extra small ones
  • You're working with Normal-noise assumptions (RMSE is the natural metric)
  • Common in physics, engineering, finance

When MAE makes sense

  • Outliers exist and you don't want them to dominate the metric
  • You want a robust, easy-to-explain average error
  • You're working with heavy-tailed or skewed errors
  • Common in delivery-time, demand forecasting, household-income models

The gap is informative: RMSE is always at least as large as MAE. If RMSE is much bigger than MAE (say, RMSE = 12 but MAE = 4), you have a few large errors dragging RMSE up — investigate those outliers. If they're close together, errors are fairly uniform.

⟁ RMSE & MAE — full formulas
RMSE = √ ( (1 / n) Σᵢ (yᵢ − ŷᵢ)² )
MAE = (1 / n) Σᵢ | yᵢ − ŷᵢ |

What each symbol means:

yᵢ — observed response for observation i
ŷᵢ — model prediction for observation i
n — number of observations
(yᵢ − ŷᵢ)² — squared residual (always positive, large errors blown up)
|yᵢ − ŷᵢ| — absolute residual (always positive, errors counted by their size)

How to read them:

  • RMSE: "Square the residuals so large ones count more, average them, then square-root back to original units."
  • MAE: "Drop the sign of each residual, take the plain average."

Useful fact: for residuals from a Normal distribution, RMSE ≈ 1.25 × MAE. If your model gives RMSE = 8 but MAE = 5 (ratio 1.6), the errors have heavier-than-Normal tails — a few big residuals are stretching RMSE. Useful diagnostic information.

Example: comparing two delivery models

Two competing models predict delivery time (in minutes) for 5 orders. Their residuals are:

Compute MAE and RMSE for each:

  • Model A: MAE = (3 + 2 + 4 + 1 + 5) / 5 = 3.0 min; RMSE = √((9 + 4 + 16 + 1 + 25) / 5) = √(11) ≈ 3.32 min
  • Model B: MAE = (1 + 1 + 2 + 1 + 25) / 5 = 6.0 min; RMSE = √((1 + 1 + 4 + 1 + 625) / 5) = √(126.4) ≈ 11.24 min

The verdict: Model A is the more reliable choice. Model B is great for 4 out of 5 orders but completely misses one. RMSE makes that one bad miss visible (11.24 vs 3.32). For a delivery system where one severely late order ruins a customer's day, Model A's steadier behaviour wins.

The lesson: RMSE and MAE together tell a richer story than either alone. Always report both — the gap between them flags the presence of outlier errors, which is information you don't want to lose.
When to use each

Quick Decision Guide

  • Use RMSE when large errors are disproportionately costly (e.g. flight-time predictions, dosage prediction), or when you're following least-squares conventions.
  • Use MAE when outliers exist and you want a robust, easy-to-explain average (e.g. delivery times, income predictions, demand forecasts).
  • Report both when communicating to stakeholders — the contrast itself is information.
Real-life examples

Where RMSE and MAE drive real decisions

🚚 Delivery time predictions

Food-delivery apps optimise for MAE because outlier delays hurt customer trust more than the metric itself; rare extreme delays are tracked separately.

📈 Sales forecasting

Retail forecast accuracy is often reported as RMSE; planners care about big misses because they cause stockouts or overstock.

🌡️ Weather forecasting

Numerical weather models are scored with RMSE on temperature, pressure, and rainfall to compare global forecast systems.

🏥 Hospital readmission models

Length-of-stay predictions report MAE — extreme cases exist and shouldn't dominate the headline number.

📺 Recommendation systems

Netflix's famous prize used RMSE as the official score — the contest was won by reducing it by about 10%.

⚡ Energy demand

Electricity-load forecasters watch both RMSE and MAE; the gap signals how often unusual events (heat waves, storms) are dominating errors.

Topic 4
The Complete Model Report Card
What it is

Combining Metrics With Residual Diagnostics

R², adjusted R², RMSE, and MAE are summaries. Each is a single number trying to tell you whether a model is any good. None of them alone is enough. A complete model report card combines these numerical scores with the residual-plot diagnostics from Module 5.2 to give a 360° view.

Think of it like a medical check-up: the headline numbers (blood pressure, weight) are quick summaries, but the doctor also looks at trends, X-rays, and history before declaring you healthy. A model is the same.

Simple meaning: A trustworthy model report combines (1) summary scores like R² and RMSE, (2) cross-validation estimates from Module 5.5, and (3) visual diagnostics like residual plots from Module 5.2. Any single source can mislead; together they paint an honest picture.
How to do it

The Five-Step Reporting Workflow

  1. Fit the model on your training data. Compute R² (or adjusted R² if comparing models with different predictor counts).
  2. Compute RMSE and MAE on the same data. Report them with units. Note the gap between them — a wide gap signals outliers in the residuals.
  3. Run cross-validation from Module 5.5. Report mean CV-RMSE and its standard deviation across folds.
  4. Inspect residual plots from Module 5.2: residuals-vs-fitted, Q-Q plot, scale-location, residuals-vs-leverage. Look for patterns the headline numbers can't see.
  5. Compare training vs CV scores. If CV-RMSE is much worse than training-RMSE, the model is overfitting. If they're similar, the model generalises well.
The output: a one-page report card with a model R², an honest cross-validated RMSE, an MAE for an outlier check, plus four small diagnostic plots. Anyone with even modest stats background can read it and form a trustworthy opinion.

Real-life examples

Where Complete Report Cards Are Required

🏦 Banking model risk

Regulators require model validation reports with R², RMSE, cross-validated equivalents, and diagnostic plots — never a single metric in isolation.

🏥 Clinical decision tools

FDA submissions for AI medical devices require a multi-metric report card including calibration plots, decision curves, and held-out cohort performance.
