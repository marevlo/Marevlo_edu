◉ MODULE 4.6 · ANALYSIS OF VARIANCE

ANOVA

A method for comparing the means of three or more groups simultaneously, while controlling the overall false-positive rate.

When we want to compare more than two groups, running multiple t-tests one pair at a time is unreliable — the chance of a false positive grows with every additional test. ANOVA (Analysis of Variance) solves this by partitioning the total variability in the data into two parts: the variation between group means and the variation within each group. The ratio of these two — the F-statistic — tells us whether any of the group means differ. This module covers the intuition behind variance decomposition, the complete one-way ANOVA procedure with every formula explained symbol by symbol, the F-distribution and its degrees of freedom, two interactive simulations, multiple-choice questions, and an AI-evaluated descriptive section.

◆ 01
Why ANOVA — The Problem It Solves
⟁ THE PROBLEM WITH MULTIPLE T-TESTS

Why not just run many t-tests?

Suppose we want to compare the average exam scores of four schools — A, B, C, and D. One approach is to run a separate two-sample t-test for every possible pair: A vs B, A vs C, A vs D, B vs C, B vs D, and C vs D. With four groups this means six tests.

The multiple testing problem. Each t-test has its own probability of a false positive (α). When we run many tests, the probability that at least one of them gives a false positive grows much higher than α. With six tests at α = 0.05, the chance of a false alarm somewhere is approximately 1 − (1 − 0.05)⁶ ≈ 0.265 — about 27%.

What ANOVA does instead

ANOVA (Analysis of Variance) replaces those many pairwise tests with one single test. The single test has the following hypotheses:

H₀: μ₁ = μ₂ = μ₃ = ... = μ_k  (all group means are equal)
H₁: At least one μᵢ differs from the others

If ANOVA rejects H₀, we conclude that the groups are not all the same — but we then use a follow-up procedure to identify which specific groups differ. This is covered in Module 4.8 (multiple testing corrections).

⟁ THE CORE IDEA

Variance is the key

Despite its name, ANOVA tests for differences in means, but it works by analyzing variances. Here is the intuition.

If all groups truly have the same mean, then the variation we see between the group averages should be similar to the variation we see within each group — both reflect natural noise. If, however, the groups have genuinely different means, the variation between the group averages will be larger than the variation within groups.

ANOVA compares these two sources of variation using a ratio called the F-statistic. A large F means the between-group variation is much larger than the within-group variation, which is evidence that the group means truly differ.

◆ 02
Variance Decomposition
⟁ SPLITTING THE TOTAL VARIATION

Three quantities to measure

ANOVA breaks the total variability in the data into two pieces:

  • SST — Total Sum of Squares: the total variation across all observations.
  • SSB — Sum of Squares Between Groups: how far each group mean lies from the grand mean.
  • SSW — Sum of Squares Within Groups: how much observations vary around their own group mean.

These three quantities are linked by a clean identity:

⟁ FUNDAMENTAL ANOVA IDENTITY
SST = SSB + SSW
The total variation in the data equals the variation between groups plus the variation within groups. This decomposition is the mathematical heart of ANOVA.
⟁ SYMBOL EXPLANATION
SST
Total Sum of Squares. Measures the overall variation of every observation around the grand mean.
SSB
Sum of Squares Between groups (also called SS-Treatment). Measures variation explained by group differences.
SSW
Sum of Squares Within groups (also called SS-Error or SS-Residual). Measures unexplained variation — the noise within each group.
⟁ TOTAL SUM OF SQUARES
SST = Σᵢ Σⱼ ( xᵢⱼ − x̄ )²
Add up the squared difference between every individual observation and the grand mean.
⟁ SYMBOL EXPLANATION
xᵢⱼ
The j-th observation in the i-th group. For example, x₂,₅ is the 5th value in group 2.
x̄
Pronounced "x-bar." The grand mean — the average of ALL observations across ALL groups combined.
Σᵢ Σⱼ
A double sum. Outer sum runs over all k groups; inner sum runs over all observations within each group.
(xᵢⱼ − x̄)²
The squared deviation between an individual observation and the grand mean.
⟁ BETWEEN-GROUPS SUM OF SQUARES
SSB = Σᵢ nᵢ ( x̄ᵢ − x̄ )²
For each group, compute the squared difference between that group's mean and the grand mean, weighted by the group's sample size. Add these across all groups.
⟁ SYMBOL EXPLANATION
nᵢ
The sample size of group i — the number of observations in that group.
x̄ᵢ
The mean of group i — the average of just that group's observations.
x̄
The grand mean (same as in SST).
(x̄ᵢ − x̄)²
The squared deviation between a group's mean and the grand mean.
⟁ WITHIN-GROUPS SUM OF SQUARES
SSW = Σᵢ Σⱼ ( xᵢⱼ − x̄ᵢ )²
Within each group, sum the squared deviations between each observation and that group's own mean. Then add across all groups.
⟁ SYMBOL EXPLANATION
xᵢⱼ
Individual observation in group i, position j.
x̄ᵢ
Mean of group i.
(xᵢⱼ − x̄ᵢ)²
The squared deviation between an observation and its own group's mean. This captures noise within each group.
◆ 03
The F-Statistic and One-Way ANOVA
⟁ MEAN SQUARES AND THE F-RATIO

From sums of squares to mean squares

Sums of squares depend on how many observations contributed to them. To make them comparable, we divide each one by its degrees of freedom to obtain a mean square.

⟁ MEAN SQUARE BETWEEN AND MEAN SQUARE WITHIN
MSB = SSB / (k − 1)      MSW = SSW / (N − k)
Each sum of squares is converted to a mean square by dividing by the appropriate degrees of freedom.
⟁ SYMBOL EXPLANATION
MSB
Mean Square Between (also written MS-Treatment). The average between-group variation per group.
MSW
Mean Square Within (also written MS-Error). The average within-group variation per observation. This is also an estimate of the common variance σ².
k
The number of groups being compared.
k − 1
Degrees of freedom for the between-groups term (df₁).
N
The total sample size across all groups (sum of all nᵢ).
N − k
Degrees of freedom for the within-groups term (df₂).
⟁ THE F-STATISTIC
F = MSB / MSW
The F-statistic is the ratio of between-group variation to within-group variation. Under H₀ (all means equal), MSB and MSW estimate the same quantity, so F should be close to 1. If F is much larger than 1, this is evidence against H₀.
⟁ SYMBOL EXPLANATION
F
The F-statistic. Named after Sir Ronald Fisher. Always non-negative because MSB and MSW are non-negative.
MSB
Mean Square Between (numerator).
MSW
Mean Square Within (denominator).
F-distribution
The reference distribution. It has two parameters: df₁ = k − 1 (numerator df) and df₂ = N − k (denominator df).
⟁ DECISION RULE

How to draw a conclusion

The decision rule is the same as for any hypothesis test:

Reject H₀ if p-value ≤ α
The p-value is the area to the right of the observed F under the F-distribution with df₁ = k − 1 and df₂ = N − k.

Rejecting H₀ means we conclude that at least one group mean differs from the others. ANOVA does not tell us which specific groups differ — that requires a follow-up step like Tukey's HSD or the Bonferroni correction (Module 4.8).

⟁ THE ANOVA SUMMARY TABLE

Standard reporting format

Software typically reports ANOVA results in a standardized table:

⟁ ASSUMPTIONS

When ANOVA is appropriate

For the standard one-way ANOVA to be valid, three assumptions should hold:

  • Independence. Observations within and across groups are independent of each other.
  • Normality. The data within each group are approximately normally distributed (less critical for large samples due to the Central Limit Theorem).
  • Equal variances (homoscedasticity). The groups share a common variance. Mild violations are acceptable, but large differences invalidate standard ANOVA. In that case, use Welch's ANOVA.
⟁ WORKED EXAMPLE — COMPARING THREE TEACHING METHODS

Problem. Three classes are taught using three different teaching methods. Their final exam scores (each class has n = 5 students) are:

Method A: 78, 82, 85, 80, 75 → mean = 80, group sum = 400
Method B: 88, 90, 92, 85, 95 → mean = 90, group sum = 450
Method C: 70, 72, 75, 68, 70 → mean = 71, group sum = 355

Test at α = 0.05 whether the three methods produce different average scores.

Step 1 — Hypotheses

H₀: μ_A = μ_B = μ_C   vs   H₁: At least one mean differs.

Step 2 — Grand mean
x̄ = (400 + 450 + 355) / 15 = 1205 / 15 ≈ 80.33
Step 3 — SSB (between groups)
SSB = 5·(80 − 80.33)² + 5·(90 − 80.33)² + 5·(71 − 80.33)²
SSB = 5·(0.109) + 5·(93.51) + 5·(87.07) ≈ 903.33
Step 4 — SSW (within groups)

Group A: (78−80)² + (82−80)² + (85−80)² + (80−80)² + (75−80)² = 4 + 4 + 25 + 0 + 25 = 58

Group B: (88−90)² + (90−90)² + (92−90)² + (85−90)² + (95−90)² = 4 + 0 + 4 + 25 + 25 = 58

Group C: (70−71)² + (72−71)² + (75−71)² + (68−71)² + (70−71)² = 1 + 1 + 16 + 9 + 1 = 28

SSW = 58 + 58 + 28 = 144
Step 5 — Degrees of freedom and mean squares
df₁ = k − 1 = 3 − 1 = 2
df₂ = N − k = 15 − 3 = 12
MSB = 903.33 / 2 ≈ 451.67
MSW = 144 / 12 = 12.0
Step 6 — F-statistic
F = MSB / MSW = 451.67 / 12.0 ≈ 37.64
Step 7 — p-value and decision

For F = 37.64 with (df₁=2, df₂=12), p-value < 0.001. Since p-value < 0.05, reject H₀. There is strong evidence that the three teaching methods produce different average scores.

◆ 04
Multiple Group Comparison Intuition
⟁ WHAT ANOVA DOES AND DOES NOT TELL YOU

An overall test

ANOVA gives a single answer: either there is evidence that at least one group differs (reject H₀) or there isn't (fail to reject H₀). It does not identify which pair of groups is responsible for the difference. For that, we need post-hoc tests — follow-up procedures that compare individual pairs while controlling the overall false-positive rate.

Common post-hoc procedures

  • Tukey's HSD (Honestly Significant Difference). Compares all pairs of group means with simultaneous confidence intervals.
  • Bonferroni correction. Adjusts the significance level for each pairwise test by dividing α by the number of comparisons.
  • Scheffé's method. Used when arbitrary contrasts (combinations) of group means are tested.

These corrections are the topic of Module 4.8.

Family-wise error rate

When many comparisons are made, the probability that at least one of them gives a false positive is called the family-wise error rate. ANOVA followed by a post-hoc procedure keeps the family-wise error rate at the desired level (typically 0.05), unlike running multiple unadjusted t-tests.

◆ 05
Real-World Applications

Clinical Trials with Multiple Treatments

Comparing average recovery times across patients receiving Treatment A, Treatment B, Treatment C, and a placebo. ANOVA tests whether any treatment differs.

Agricultural Studies

Comparing crop yields across different fertilizer formulations. ANOVA was originally developed by Fisher for this kind of agricultural experiment.

Machine Learning Model Comparison

Comparing the cross-validation accuracy of several model architectures simultaneously, rather than running multiple pairwise comparisons.

Marketing — Multi-Arm A/B Tests

Comparing conversion rates or session times across multiple website variants (A, B, C, D) at once.

Manufacturing Process Comparison

Comparing the mean output quality of products from several different machines or production lines.

Educational Research

Comparing student performance across multiple teaching methods, curricula, or school types.
