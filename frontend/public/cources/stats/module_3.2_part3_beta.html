From Time to Proportion
A different kind of question

A New Domain — Quantities in [0, 1]

Up to now, the continuous distributions in Module 3.2 have lived on either an unbounded interval (Exponential and Gamma cover [0, ∞)) or any bounded interval (Uniform on [a, b]). But many natural quantities live on a very specific bounded interval: [0, 1].

Anything that's a proportion, a probability, or a fraction belongs there:

  • The success rate of a manufacturing process
  • The probability of heads on a possibly-biased coin
  • The fraction of voters who support a candidate
  • The click-through rate of an online ad
  • The conversion rate of a website
  • The recovery rate of a treatment

For all of these, the value must be between 0 and 1. We need a flexible distribution that lives exactly on [0, 1] and can take many shapes — symmetric, skewed left, skewed right, U-shaped, flat, or peaked. That distribution is the Beta distribution.

The big idea: Beta is to proportions what Gamma is to waiting times. Both have two parameters that control shape. Both can morph between many forms. Beta lives strictly on [0, 1]; outside that range, density is zero.
2
The Beta Distribution
Definition

What Beta Models

The Beta distribution, written Beta(α, β), is a continuous distribution on the interval [0, 1]. It has two shape parameters, both positive:

  • α (alpha) — pulls the distribution toward 1. Larger α → more mass on the right side.
  • β (beta) — pulls the distribution toward 0. Larger β → more mass on the left side.

The interplay of α and β controls everything: the location of the peak, the symmetry, the spread, even whether the distribution is bell-shaped, U-shaped, or flat.

Mental model — Beta as a tug-of-war: imagine α and β as two opposing forces. α pulls the mass right (toward 1); β pulls it left (toward 0). Their ratio determines where the mass settles. Their sum determines how concentrated it is. Big α and β together → tight peak. Small α and β → spread out.
⟁ Beta PDF
f(x) = [Γ(α + β) / (Γ(α) · Γ(β))] · xα−1 · (1 − x)β−1   for 0 ≤ x ≤ 1

The PDF looks complex but each piece tells a story. Outside [0, 1], the density is zero.

xα−1 — pulls density up near x = 1 as α grows
(1 − x)β−1 — pulls density up near x = 0 as β grows
Γ(α+β) / [Γ(α)Γ(β)] — normalising constant, makes total area = 1
α, β > 0 — both shape parameters must be positive

The constant in front is called 1/B(α, β), where B is the "Beta function" — that's where the distribution gets its name. You almost never compute it by hand — libraries handle it. What matters is the kernel: xα−1(1−x)β−1. This shape is the heart of the distribution.

⟁ Beta Mean & Variance
E[X] = α / (α + β)
Var(X) = α·β / [(α + β)² · (α + β + 1)]

Mean intuition: the mean is simply the ratio of α to the total α + β. If α = β, mean is 0.5. If α > β, mean is greater than 0.5 (right-leaning). If α < β, mean is less than 0.5 (left-leaning).

Variance intuition: the larger α + β grows (with their ratio held constant), the smaller the variance. The "concentration" comes from the sum α + β. This is why α + β is sometimes called the "effective sample size" in Bayesian contexts.

Beta's many faces

How Different (α, β) Values Reshape the Curve

α = 1, β = 1 → Uniform!

  • PDF = 1 everywhere on [0, 1]
  • Beta(1, 1) is the same as Uniform(0, 1)
  • Most common "uninformative prior" in Bayesian work

α = β > 1 → Symmetric Bell

  • Mean is 0.5
  • Bell-shaped around the middle
  • Bigger α + β → tighter peak
  • Example: Beta(5, 5) is a clear bell at 0.5

α > β > 1 → Right-Skewed Peak

  • Peak shifted toward 1
  • Example: Beta(7, 2) — heavy on the high side
  • Models "usually high but with variability"

β > α > 1 → Left-Skewed Peak

  • Peak shifted toward 0
  • Example: Beta(2, 7) — heavy on the low side
  • Models "usually low but with variability"

α < 1 AND β < 1 → U-Shaped

  • Density rises near both 0 and 1
  • Dip in the middle
  • Models "polarized" outcomes
  • Example: Beta(0.5, 0.5) — the Jeffreys prior

α = 1, β > 1 → Decreasing

  • Strictly decreasing from x = 0 to x = 1
  • Maximum density at x = 0
  • A bounded analog of exponential decay

Beta in Bayesian Inference
The deep Bayesian connection

Beta Is the Bayesian Twin of Binary Outcomes

The most important application of Beta is in Bayesian updating for proportions. If you're estimating a probability or success rate from binary data (success/failure trials), Beta is the natural prior — and the posterior stays in the Beta family. This property is called conjugacy.

The setup: you have an unknown probability p (e.g., conversion rate of an ad). You start with a Beta(α, β) prior. You observe n trials with k successes. The posterior is then a new Beta:

⟁ Beta-Binomial Bayesian Update
Prior: p ∼ Beta(α, β)
Data: k successes in n trials
Posterior: p | data ∼ Beta(α + k, β + n − k)

Updating is trivial: add successes to α, add failures to β. That's it. No integrals, no normalising constants to compute. The posterior is just a new Beta with updated parameters.

α + k — new alpha (prior plus successes)
β + (n − k) — new beta (prior plus failures)
Posterior mean = (α + k) / (α + β + n)
α + β — "effective prior sample size"
Why this is huge: in general, computing a Bayesian posterior requires integration that may have no closed form. Beta-Binomial conjugacy means you skip all that — just bump the parameters. This is why Beta dominates A/B testing, click-rate estimation, and survey analysis in modern data science.
Worked Bayesian example

A/B Testing with Beta

You're testing a new website design. You start with prior Beta(2, 2) — slightly informative, centered at 0.5 with moderate uncertainty. You show the new design to 50 visitors; 30 click through.

  • Step 1 — Posterior: α' = 2 + 30 = 32, β' = 2 + 20 = 22. Posterior ∼ Beta(32, 22).
  • Step 2 — Posterior mean: 32 / 54 ≈ 0.593 → estimated click-through rate ≈ 59%.
  • Step 3 — Posterior variance: 32 × 22 / [54² × 55] ≈ 0.00444. SD ≈ 0.067.
  • Step 4 — Conclusion: we have an estimated CTR of about 59% ± 7%. The Beta posterior captures both our point estimate and our uncertainty cleanly.

Compare with frequentist: point estimate = 30/50 = 60%, no built-in uncertainty unless you compute a confidence interval separately. Beta gives you the entire posterior distribution as a single object — much richer information for decision-making.

How to Use the Beta Distribution
Practical workflow

Step-by-Step

  1. 01 Confirm support is [0, 1]. Are you modeling a proportion, probability, or fraction? If yes, Beta is a natural fit.
  2. 02 Choose the shape you want. Pick α and β based on prior knowledge: the desired mean is α/(α+β), and the concentration grows with α+β.
  3. 03 Use Beta(1, 1) when you know nothing. This is the uninformative Uniform prior — every proportion equally likely.
  4. 04 Update with data. Add successes to α, add failures to β. The posterior is automatically a new Beta.
  5. 05 Extract what you need. Use the posterior mean as your point estimate. Use percentiles for credible intervals: e.g., scipy.stats.beta.ppf(0.025, α, β) and the 0.975 quantile.
  6. 06 Sample from the posterior if needed — for Monte Carlo simulations or Thompson sampling. Libraries: scipy.stats.beta.rvs(α, β, size=N).
Real-world Beta

Where Beta Lives in the Wild

📊 A/B Testing

Every modern A/B testing framework uses Beta posteriors for conversion rates. Bayesian variants of platforms like Optimizely and statsig run Beta-Binomial under the hood.

🎯 Click-Through Rate Estimation

Online advertising platforms estimate per-ad CTR using Beta posteriors. The α + β "effective sample size" reflects how much data we've collected on each ad.

🏥 Drug Approval Trials

Bayesian clinical trials use Beta priors for binary outcomes (recovery yes/no). Adaptive trials stop early when the posterior shows clear winners.

🎮 Multi-Armed Bandits

Thompson sampling — a popular bandit algorithm — samples from per-arm Beta posteriors. The arm sampled with the highest probability gets played next.

🔬 Survey Polling

Election polling estimates use Beta priors to combine new polls with historical baselines. Pollsters update parameters as new survey results arrive.

🤖 Reinforcement Learning

Bayesian RL agents maintain Beta posteriors over action success rates. Used in exploration-exploitation strategies and safe policy learning.

🌐 Quality Control

Manufacturing lines monitor defect rates as Beta posteriors. Anomaly detection triggers when the posterior shifts far from the historical baseline.

🧠 Bayesian ML Models

Beta distributions appear inside Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA), Beta-Bernoulli mixtures, and many topic models. Foundational in modern Bayesian deep learning.

Common Mistakes
Mistake 1 — Using Beta outside [0, 1]. Beta is defined only on [0, 1] — for proportions, probabilities, and rates. If your data spans [0, 10] or [−1, 1], you need a transformation first (rescale to [0, 1]) or a different distribution.
Mistake 2 — Confusing Beta with Binomial. Binomial is discrete (counts successes). Beta is continuous (a proportion / probability). They live in different spaces but go hand in hand — Beta is the natural prior for Binomial's success probability.
Mistake 3 — Forgetting Beta(1, 1) = Uniform(0, 1). This is a common surprise. Plug α = 1, β = 1 into the PDF and the kernel x⁰·(1−x)⁰ = 1 — pure constant. Many "noninformative priors" are simply this.
Mistake 4 — Misreading α and β. α + β controls concentration. α / (α + β) is the mean. People sometimes flip the roles — α pulls toward 1, β pulls toward 0. Big α → mass on the right side.
Mistake 5 — Computing Beta CDF by hand. Beta's CDF (the "regularised incomplete beta function") has no elementary closed form. Use libraries — scipy.stats.beta.cdf — don't try to integrate by hand.
Mistake 6 — Using a Beta prior where data goes negative. Beta strictly requires support [0, 1]. If your "probability" estimate has noise that pushes it negative (e.g., a regression coefficient interpreted as a probability), use a different model — maybe Gaussian or Logistic.

M-01
Continuous probability is fundamentally different. P(X = x) = 0, density is per-unit-length rate, and probability lives in intervals via integration.
M-02
Uniform(a, b) — equally likely everywhere in [a, b]. The default "no information" distribution. Mean (a+b)/2, Variance (b−a)²/12. Powers every random number generator via inverse-transform sampling.
M-03
Exponential(λ) — continuous waiting time until the next event. Mean = SD = 1/λ. The only continuous memoryless distribution. Two sides of the Poisson process.
M-04
Gamma(k, λ) — waiting time for the k-th event. Sum of k independent Exponentials. Mean = k/λ, Variance = k/λ². Reduces to Exponential when k = 1; equals Chi-Squared when k = d/2 and λ = 1/2.
M-05
Beta(α, β) — proportions on [0, 1]. Mean = α/(α+β), Variance = αβ/[(α+β)²(α+β+1)]. Six canonical shapes governed by α and β. Reduces to Uniform when α = β = 1.
M-06
Two Bayesian conjugacy gems: Gamma is conjugate for Poisson/Exponential rates; Beta is conjugate for Binomial proportions. Updating stays in the family — no integrals.
M-07
The decision rule: ask what the support is. [a, b] equally likely → Uniform. [0, ∞), one event → Exponential. [0, ∞), k events or sum → Gamma. [0, 1] proportion → Beta.
M-08
Memorylessness is rare. Only Exponential (continuous) and Geometric (discrete) have it. Real-world systems with aging effects need Gamma or Weibull.
