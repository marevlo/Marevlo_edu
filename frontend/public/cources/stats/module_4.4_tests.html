◆ 01
One-Sample Tests
⟁ WHAT IS IT

The one-sample setting

A one-sample test compares a sample mean against a single known or hypothesized value. We collect data from one group and ask whether its mean differs from a specific reference number.

Example. A factory states that the mean weight of a chocolate bar is 100 g. We weigh a random sample of bars and want to test whether the actual mean differs from this stated value.
H₀: μ = 100  vs  H₁: μ ≠ 100

z-test vs t-test — which one to use

The choice depends on whether we know the population standard deviation σ.

SituationUseReason
σ KNOWNz-testStandard error uses σ directly; sampling distribution is exactly normal.
σ UNKNOWN, large n (n ≥ 30)t-test (or z-test as approximation)Sample standard deviation s replaces σ; t-distribution applies, close to normal for large n.
σ UNKNOWN, small n (n < 30)t-testUse Student's t-distribution to account for extra uncertainty from estimating σ with s.
⟁ ONE-SAMPLE Z-TEST STATISTIC
z = ( x̄ − μ₀ ) / ( σ / √n )
The z-statistic measures how many standard errors the sample mean is away from the value claimed in H₀. Used when σ is known.
⟁ SYMBOL EXPLANATION
z
The test statistic. A standardized distance — how many standard errors the sample mean lies from μ₀.
x̄
Pronounced "x-bar." The sample mean — the average of the observations in our sample.
μ₀
Pronounced "mu-naught." The value of the population mean stated under H₀ (the reference value we are testing against).
σ
Pronounced "sigma." The known population standard deviation — a measure of spread in the population.
n
The sample size — the number of observations.
√n
The square root of n. For example, √64 = 8 because 8 × 8 = 64.
σ/√n
The standard error of the sample mean.
⟁ ONE-SAMPLE T-TEST STATISTIC
t = ( x̄ − μ₀ ) / ( s / √n )
Identical in structure to the z-test, except the unknown σ is replaced by the sample standard deviation s. The statistic follows a Student's t-distribution with n − 1 degrees of freedom.
⟁ SYMBOL EXPLANATION
t
The t-statistic. Like z, but accounts for the additional uncertainty of estimating σ from the sample.
x̄
The sample mean.
μ₀
The hypothesized population mean under H₀.
s
The sample standard deviation — computed from the data when σ is unknown.
n
The sample size.
s/√n
The estimated standard error of the sample mean.
df
Degrees of freedom = n − 1. Used to identify which t-distribution to refer to.
⟁ WORKED EXAMPLE — ONE-SAMPLE T-TEST

Problem. A coffee machine should pour 200 ml of coffee on average. A technician measures n = 16 cups and finds x̄ = 196 ml with sample standard deviation s = 6 ml. Test at α = 0.05 whether the machine is under-pouring.

Step 1 — Hypotheses

H₀: μ = 200  vs  H₁: μ < 200 (left-tailed)

Step 2 — Choose the test

σ is unknown and n is small, so use a one-sample t-test with df = 15.

Step 3 — Standard error
SE = s/√n = 6/√16 = 6/4 = 1.5
Step 4 — Test statistic
t = (196 − 200) / 1.5 = −4/1.5 ≈ −2.667
Step 5 — p-value

For a left-tailed test with t = −2.667 and df = 15, p-value ≈ 0.0089.

Step 6 — Decision

Since 0.0089 ≤ 0.05, reject H₀. There is significant evidence that the machine is under-pouring.

◆ 02
Two-Sample Tests
⟁ COMPARING TWO INDEPENDENT GROUPS

The two-sample setting

A two-sample test compares the means of two independent groups to determine whether they differ. The two samples must be drawn independently — measurements in one group cannot influence those in the other.

Example. Compare the average study time of students in two different schools.
H₀: μ₁ = μ₂ (no difference)  vs  H₁: μ₁ ≠ μ₂ (the means differ)

Equal variances vs unequal variances

Two versions of the test exist, depending on whether the two populations have the same variance.

  • Pooled t-test (equal variances assumed). Combines both samples into a single estimate of variance. Slightly more powerful when the assumption holds.
  • Welch's t-test (unequal variances). Does not assume equal variances. More robust and is the default in most statistical software today.
⟁ TWO-SAMPLE T-TEST (WELCH'S VERSION)
t = ( x̄₁ − x̄₂ ) / √( s₁²/n₁ + s₂²/n₂ )
Measures how many standard errors the difference between the two sample means is away from zero. Does not require equal variances.
⟁ SYMBOL EXPLANATION
t
The t-statistic for comparing two means.
x̄₁
Sample mean of group 1.
x̄₂
Sample mean of group 2.
s₁²
Sample variance of group 1 (the square of the sample standard deviation).
s₂²
Sample variance of group 2.
n₁
Sample size of group 1.
n₂
Sample size of group 2.
√(...)
The square root of the combined standard error term inside the parentheses.
⟁ POOLED TWO-SAMPLE T-TEST (EQUAL VARIANCES)
t = ( x̄₁ − x̄₂ ) / ( s_p · √(1/n₁ + 1/n₂) )
Uses a pooled standard deviation that combines information from both samples. Use only when the two population variances are believed to be equal.
⟁ SYMBOL EXPLANATION
s_p
The pooled standard deviation: s_p = √[ ((n₁−1)s₁² + (n₂−1)s₂²) / (n₁+n₂−2) ]
n₁ + n₂ − 2
Degrees of freedom for the pooled t-test.
1/n₁ + 1/n₂
Weighting factor reflecting both sample sizes.

◆ 03
Paired Tests
⟁ MATCHED PAIRS DESIGN

What is a paired test?

A paired test is used when the two samples are not independent but are linked observation-by-observation. The most common case is when the same subject is measured twice — for example, before and after a treatment.

Example. Measure the blood pressure of 20 patients before they take a new drug and 4 weeks after. Each "before" value pairs naturally with one "after" value from the same patient.

Why pairing matters

Pairing removes between-subject variability. If we naively used a two-sample t-test, the natural variability across patients would mask the effect of the treatment. By focusing on the differences within each pair, we isolate the effect of interest.

The key idea

A paired test reduces to a one-sample t-test on the differences. For each pair i, compute d_i = (value after) − (value before). Then test whether the mean difference is zero.

⟁ PAIRED-DIFFERENCE T-TEST
t = ( d̄ − 0 ) / ( s_d / √n )
A one-sample t-test applied to the differences d_i. The hypothesized mean difference under H₀ is usually 0 (no effect).
⟁ SYMBOL EXPLANATION
t
The paired t-statistic.
d̄
Pronounced "d-bar." The mean of the n paired differences.
d_i
The difference for pair i, computed as (second measurement) − (first measurement).
s_d
The sample standard deviation of the differences.
n
The number of pairs.
s_d/√n
The standard error of the mean difference.
df
Degrees of freedom = n − 1.
⟁ WORKED EXAMPLE — PAIRED T-TEST

Problem. A nutritionist measures the cholesterol level of 10 patients before and after a 3-month diet program. The mean of the 10 differences (after − before) is d̄ = −12 mg/dL with s_d = 8 mg/dL. Test at α = 0.05 whether the diet reduces cholesterol.

Step 1 — Hypotheses

H₀: μ_d = 0  vs  H₁: μ_d < 0 (left-tailed, since a reduction is expected)

Step 2 — Test choice

Paired t-test with df = 9.

Step 3 — Standard error
SE = s_d / √n = 8 / √10 ≈ 2.530
Step 4 — t-statistic
t = (−12 − 0) / 2.530 ≈ −4.743
Step 5 — p-value

For t = −4.743 with df = 9, the left-tailed p-value ≈ 0.0005.

Step 6 — Decision

Since 0.0005 ≤ 0.05, reject H₀. There is strong evidence that the diet program reduces cholesterol.

◆ 04
Proportion Tests
⟁ TESTING PROPORTIONS

What is a proportion test?

A proportion test is used when the data are binary — every observation is a yes/no, success/failure, or click/no-click outcome. Instead of a mean, we test the proportion (the fraction of successes).

Example. A website redesign claims to improve the click-through rate from the baseline of 10%. We test n = 500 visitors with the new design and observe 65 clicks (a sample proportion of 13%). Is this evidence that the true click-through rate has increased?

One-proportion vs two-proportion

  • One-proportion test. Compares the sample proportion p̂ against a single claimed value p₀.
  • Two-proportion test. Compares the proportions of two independent groups.

Required assumptions

For these tests to be valid, the sample size must be large enough that the sampling distribution of the proportion is approximately normal. The rule of thumb is that both np₀ and n(1 − p₀) should be at least 10.

⟁ ONE-PROPORTION Z-TEST
z = ( p̂ − p₀ ) / √( p₀(1−p₀) / n )
Measures how many standard errors the sample proportion lies away from the value claimed under H₀.
⟁ SYMBOL EXPLANATION
z
The z-statistic for the proportion test.
p̂
Pronounced "p-hat." The sample proportion = (number of successes) / n.
p₀
The proportion stated under H₀ (the reference value being tested).
n
The sample size.
p₀(1−p₀)
The variance of a single Bernoulli observation under H₀.
√(p₀(1−p₀)/n)
The standard error of the sample proportion, computed using p₀.
⟁ TWO-PROPORTION Z-TEST
z = ( p̂₁ − p̂₂ ) / √( p̂(1−p̂) · (1/n₁ + 1/n₂) )
Tests whether two independent groups have different population proportions. Uses a pooled proportion p̂ that combines both samples.
⟁ SYMBOL EXPLANATION
p̂₁
Sample proportion in group 1 = x₁/n₁ (where x₁ is the number of successes in group 1).
p̂₂
Sample proportion in group 2 = x₂/n₂.
p̂
The pooled proportion = (x₁ + x₂) / (n₁ + n₂). Used as the best estimate of the common proportion under H₀.
n₁, n₂
Sample sizes of the two groups.
1/n₁ + 1/n₂
Combined size factor that reflects both group sizes.
⟁ WORKED EXAMPLE — TWO-PROPORTION Z-TEST (A/B TEST)

Problem. An e-commerce team runs an A/B test on the checkout button. Version A: n₁ = 1000 visitors, 80 conversions (p̂₁ = 0.08). Version B: n₂ = 1000 visitors, 110 conversions (p̂₂ = 0.11). Is the difference in conversion rates statistically significant at α = 0.05?

Step 1 — Hypotheses

H₀: p₁ = p₂  vs  H₁: p₁ ≠ p₂ (two-tailed)

Step 2 — Pooled proportion
p̂ = (80 + 110) / (1000 + 1000) = 190 / 2000 = 0.095
Step 3 — Standard error
SE = √( 0.095 × 0.905 × (1/1000 + 1/1000) ) = √( 0.0001720 ) ≈ 0.01311
Step 4 — z-statistic
z = (0.08 − 0.11) / 0.01311 = −0.03 / 0.01311 ≈ −2.288
Step 5 — p-value

For |z| = 2.288, two-tailed p-value ≈ 0.0221.

Step 6 — Decision

Since 0.0221 ≤ 0.05, reject H₀. There is significant evidence that Version B converts at a higher rate than Version A.

◆ 06
Real-World Applications

Pharmaceutical Trials

Paired t-tests compare patient outcomes before and after treatment; two-sample t-tests compare treatment groups against placebo.

A/B Testing in Tech Products

Two-proportion z-tests determine whether a new feature changes the conversion rate, click-through rate, or retention rate.

Manufacturing Process Control

One-sample z-tests check whether the mean dimension of produced parts has drifted away from the specification target.

Machine Learning Model Comparison

Paired t-tests compare model accuracies across the same set of cross-validation folds, controlling for fold-to-fold variability.

Educational Research

Two-sample tests compare student performance between two teaching methods or between schools.

Survey Analysis

One-proportion tests check whether the fraction of respondents holding a certain view differs from a claimed baseline.

// Module 4.4 · Compiled Summary

This module applies the hypothesis testing framework to four common situations: one-sample tests (z when σ is known, t otherwise), two-sample tests for comparing independent groups, paired tests for matched observations, and proportion tests for binary outcomes. Each test follows the same six-step procedure introduced in Module 4.3 but uses a different test statistic and a different reference distribution.

01
One-sample z-test. Used when σ is known. z = (x̄ − μ₀) / (σ/√n).
02
One-sample t-test. Used when σ is unknown. Uses sample SD s and df = n − 1.
03
Two-sample t-test. Compares two independent groups. Welch's version (unequal variances) is the modern default.
04
Paired t-test. A one-sample t-test on the within-pair differences. Used when the same subjects are measured twice.
05
One-proportion z-test. Tests a sample proportion p̂ against a claimed value p₀.
06
Two-proportion z-test. Tests whether two groups have different proportions; the foundation of A/B testing.
07
Right test, right context. Choosing the appropriate test is the most important decision in applied inference.
