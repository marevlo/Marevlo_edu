Topic 3
The Sigmoid Function
What it is

What is the Sigmoid Function?

In Part 1 we converted probabilities into log-odds — a value that can be any real number. That worked beautifully for the model's interior: the log-odds form a clean linear equation.

But when the model needs to output a prediction, we want a probability back, not a log-odds value. The function that takes any real number and squeezes it into a probability between 0 and 1 is called the sigmoid function:

σ(z) = 1 / (1 + e^(−z))

It is the exact inverse of the logit. The logit takes p (in [0, 1]) and returns z (anywhere in ℝ). The sigmoid takes z (anywhere in ℝ) and returns p (in [0, 1]). They are two directions of the same translation.

Simple meaning: The sigmoid is an S-shaped curve. Feed it a very negative number, you get out a probability near 0. Feed it a very positive number, you get out a probability near 1. Feed it 0, you get exactly 0.5.

Three key values to memorise

  • σ(0) = 0.5 — at log-odds = 0, the model is exactly 50/50
  • σ(+∞) → 1 — very confident yes
  • σ(−∞) → 0 — very confident no
  • σ(2) ≈ 0.88, σ(−2) ≈ 0.12 — useful checkpoints either side of zero

Shape facts

  • The curve is symmetric around the point (0, 0.5).
  • It is steepest at z = 0 and flattens at both ends.
  • It is monotonically increasing — bigger z always gives bigger σ(z).
  • It never quite touches 0 or 1, just approaches them.
Why we need it

Why the Sigmoid Solves the Classification Problem

Three reasons it's the standard choice:

1. Range match

  • Linear input z can be any real number
  • Probability output must be in [0, 1]
  • The sigmoid maps ℝ → [0, 1] exactly
  • No predictions out of range

2. Smooth transitions

  • The S-shape changes gradually
  • Near the boundary, a small change in z = noticeable change in probability
  • Far from the boundary, the curve flattens — confidence saturates
  • Natural calibration

3. Easy gradient

  • Its derivative is σ(z) × (1 − σ(z))
  • Beautiful for training algorithms
  • This is why it became the original neural-network activation
  • Gradient descent works smoothly
Big idea: The sigmoid is the mathematical "translator." It turns the unbounded linear score (log-odds) into a bounded probability — without losing the rank order of cases. Whoever has the highest log-odds also has the highest probability of being class 1.
Why it's everywhere

The Sigmoid Outside Logistic Regression

The sigmoid is one of the most reused functions in all of machine learning:

  • Neural networks (historically): the sigmoid was the original activation function. Every neuron applied σ(z) to its weighted input. Modern networks often prefer ReLU for hidden layers, but the sigmoid still rules the output of binary classifiers.
  • Population growth models: bacterial growth, market saturation, and viral spread all follow S-shaped curves that are basically sigmoids.
  • Psychology & psychophysics: human response to stimulus intensity often follows a sigmoid — sometimes called the "psychometric function."
  • Reinforcement learning: policies that output a probability of taking an action use sigmoids for binary choices.
  • Recommendation systems: click-through rates and engagement scores are often produced by sigmoids at the final layer of large models.
How the shape responds to β

How β₀ and β₁ Reshape the Sigmoid

In a one-predictor logistic regression, the full curve becomes:

P(y=1 | x) = σ(β₀ + β₁ · x) = 1 / (1 + e^−(β₀ + β₁x))

Two parameters, two visible effects on the curve:

β₀ — Intercept

  • Shifts the curve left or right
  • Specifically: the "midpoint" of the curve (where P = 0.5) sits at x = −β₀ / β₁
  • More negative β₀ → midpoint shifts right (need bigger x to reach P = 0.5)
  • More positive β₀ → midpoint shifts left

β₁ — Slope on the log-odds scale

  • Controls steepness of the curve
  • Large positive β₁ → very sharp transition near the midpoint
  • Small positive β₁ → gentle, gradual transition
  • Negative β₁ → the curve flips (more x → lower probability)

⟁ The Sigmoid in Detail
σ(z) = 1 / (1 + e^(−z))

What each symbol means:

σ — Greek letter sigma (lowercase), standard name for the sigmoid function
z — any real number (the log-odds output of the linear part)
e — Euler's number, ≈ 2.71828
e^(−z) — e raised to the power negative z
σ(z) — the output probability, between 0 and 1

How to read it: "Take z, negate it, exponentiate it, add 1, then divide 1 by that result." When z is very large positive, e^(−z) is tiny, so σ(z) ≈ 1/(1+0) = 1. When z is very large negative, e^(−z) is huge, so σ(z) ≈ 1/(huge) = 0. When z = 0, e^0 = 1, so σ(0) = 1/2.

Worked numbers:

  • σ(0) = 1/(1+1) = 0.500
  • σ(1) = 1/(1+e^−1) ≈ 1/(1+0.368) = 0.731
  • σ(2) = 1/(1+e^−2) ≈ 1/(1+0.135) = 0.881
  • σ(−1) ≈ 0.269   ·   σ(−2) ≈ 0.119
  • σ(4) ≈ 0.982   ·   σ(−4) ≈ 0.018

Useful property: σ(−z) = 1 − σ(z). The sigmoid is point-symmetric around (0, 0.5). This matches our intuition: if z is the log-odds of "yes," then −z is the log-odds of "no," and the two probabilities must add to 1.

⟁ Worked Example

Example: Loan Approval Sigmoid

A bank's logistic regression for loan approval uses one predictor: a credit score on a 300-900 scale. The fitted equation is:

log-odds(approve) = −18 + 0.025 · credit_score

So the probability of approval is σ(−18 + 0.025 · credit_score).

Apply it to three applicants:

ApplicantCredit ScoreLog-odds zσ(z) = P(approve)
Low500−18 + 0.025·500 = −5.5σ(−5.5) ≈ 0.004 (almost no chance)
Borderline720−18 + 0.025·720 = 0σ(0) = 0.500 (exactly 50/50 — the midpoint)
High820−18 + 0.025·820 = 2.5σ(2.5) ≈ 0.924 (very likely approved)

What the midpoint tells us: at credit score 720, the model is exactly 50/50. That score is the "boundary" applicants need to cross to be more likely approved than rejected. It comes directly from solving −18 + 0.025 · x = 0, giving x = 720.

The lesson: The sigmoid converts a continuous "score" into a probability. The midpoint where P = 0.5 is the natural threshold for binary decisions and is determined entirely by the fitted coefficients.
When to use it

When Sigmoid is the Right Choice

  • You need to output a single probability in [0, 1] for a binary outcome.
  • You want smooth, calibrated probabilities, not just hard labels.
  • You're building the output layer of a binary classification model (logistic regression, neural network's final layer for binary tasks).
  • You want the monotonic mapping from a linear score to a probability — same ranking, same direction.
When sigmoid is NOT the right choice: for multi-class problems (cat / dog / bird), use the softmax function instead — a multi-output generalisation. For hidden layers of modern deep networks, ReLU and its cousins outperform sigmoid because they avoid the "vanishing gradient" issue at the saturated extremes.
Real-life examples

Where Sigmoid Curves Appear in Daily Tech

🧠 Neural Network Outputs

Every binary-classification neural network ends with a sigmoid in the last layer to produce the probability of the positive class.

📱 App Recommendation

"Probability you'll install this app" — sigmoids produce the score that app stores rank suggestions by.

🛒 Cart-Abandonment Models

E-commerce sites model P(customer completes checkout) with sigmoids and trigger nudges below a threshold.

🦠 Epidemic Models

The S-shaped logistic growth curve of an epidemic is mathematically the same as the sigmoid — saturation is a natural feature.

🏏 Win Probability Models

Live win probability charts in cricket and football turn a "lead" or "form" score into a sigmoid-shaped probability curve.

🎯 Ad Click Prediction

Modern online ad bidding outputs CTR estimates through a final sigmoid layer over a deep score.

Topic 4
Decision Boundaries
What it is

What is a Decision Boundary?

So far the model outputs a probability for every case. But practical systems must make hard choices — approve the loan, or don't. Flag the email as spam, or send it to the inbox. To go from probability to decision, we pick a threshold (commonly 0.5) and split cases into two groups.

The decision boundary is the set of predictor values where the classifier is exactly at the threshold — the line in input space that separates "predicted yes" from "predicted no." Cross the boundary, and the verdict flips.

Simple meaning: The decision boundary is the dividing line between the two classes in the predictor space. On one side, the model says "yes." On the other, it says "no." On the boundary, the model is exactly 50/50.

Why the boundary is linear in logistic regression

For the threshold P = 0.5, the sigmoid output equals 0.5 exactly when its input z equals 0. So the boundary in input space is:

β₀ + β₁ · x₁ + β₂ · x₂ + … + βₚ · xₚ = 0

That is a linear equation in the predictors — a line if there are two predictors, a plane if there are three, a hyperplane in general. Logistic regression always produces a linear boundary in the original predictor space, regardless of how curved the sigmoid looks in 1D.

Why we need it

Why a Boundary Matters in Practice

Three reasons every classifier eventually faces a boundary decision:

1. Real decisions are discrete

  • Banks must accept or reject a loan
  • Email must be sent to inbox or spam
  • Doctors must order more tests or not
  • "Probability" alone doesn't act

2. Threshold encodes cost

  • The default 0.5 treats both kinds of mistakes equally
  • If false negatives cost more (missing fraud), lower the threshold
  • If false positives cost more (annoying inbox), raise it
  • Threshold = business decision

3. Boundary reveals model shape

  • Linear classifiers produce straight boundaries
  • If the true boundary is curved, a linear model will under-fit
  • This motivates non-linear features or different models
How it works

How to Read a 2D Decision Boundary

The simplest case: two predictors x₁ and x₂. Plot the data with x₁ on one axis and x₂ on the other; colour each point by its class. The fitted boundary is a straight line drawn through this plane. Points on one side are predicted "no"; points on the other side are predicted "yes."

Reading the equation visually

If β₀ + β₁·x₁ + β₂·x₂ = 0 is the boundary, then we can rewrite it as:

x₂ = − (β₀ / β₂) − (β₁ / β₂) · x₁

That is the familiar "intercept + slope · x" form. The line on the 2D plot has:

  • Intercept: −β₀ / β₂ (where the line crosses the x₂ axis)
  • Slope: −β₁ / β₂ (how steep the line is)

What changing the threshold does

The boundary at P = 0.5 corresponds to z = 0. If we change the threshold to P = 0.7, we need z = ln(0.7/0.3) ≈ 0.847. The boundary equation becomes:

β₀ + β₁·x₁ + β₂·x₂ = 0.847

That's the same line, just shifted. Threshold changes shift the boundary parallel to itself — they don't tilt it. The model still produces the same probabilities; we're just choosing a different cut-off.

How to use it

How to Choose a Threshold in Practice

Setting the threshold isn't a math problem — it's a business problem. Different mistakes cost different amounts, and the right threshold depends on those costs.

  1. List the four outcomes: true positives (correctly flagged yes), false positives (wrongly flagged yes), true negatives (correctly classed as no), false negatives (missed yeses).
  2. Assign cost / benefit to each. What does a false positive cost the user? What does a false negative cost the business?
  3. Plot the ROC curve. Sweep the threshold from 0 to 1 and plot true positive rate vs false positive rate. This shows the tradeoff visually.
  4. Pick the threshold that minimises expected total cost. This is sometimes called the "operating point" of the classifier.
  5. Re-evaluate periodically. Class proportions, costs, and the model itself drift over time. Thresholds need refreshing.
Common mistake: "Default 0.5 threshold" sounds safe but is often wrong. If only 1% of cases are positive, predicting "no" for everyone gets 99% accuracy and is useless. Calibrate threshold to context, not to the default.
⟁ Worked Example

Example: Fraud Detection Threshold

A payment network builds a logistic regression for credit-card fraud detection. The model outputs P(fraud | transaction). At default threshold 0.5, the model flags 100 transactions per day. Of these:

  • 30 are genuine fraud (true positives)
  • 70 are honest customers triggered for review (false positives)

But the model is missing fraud too — there are 20 fraud cases per day below the 0.5 threshold (false negatives). Each missed fraud costs the bank ₹50,000. Each false positive costs only ₹50 in customer-service review time.

Cost analysis at threshold 0.5:

  • False positive cost: 70 × ₹50 = ₹3,500/day
  • False negative cost: 20 × ₹50,000 = ₹10,00,000/day
  • Total cost = ₹10,03,500/day

The bank lowers the threshold to 0.2 — more transactions flagged. Now:

  • True positives: 45 (was 30) — caught more fraud
  • False positives: 220 (was 70) — many more reviews
  • False negatives: 5 (was 20) — much less missed fraud

Cost at threshold 0.2:

  • FP cost: 220 × ₹50 = ₹11,000/day
  • FN cost: 5 × ₹50,000 = ₹2,50,000/day
  • Total cost = ₹2,61,000/day
The lesson: Threshold tuning saved the bank ₹7.4 lakh per day. The default 0.5 was almost four times more expensive than the cost-optimal 0.2. Threshold choice is one of the highest-leverage decisions in deploying a classifier.
When boundaries get interesting

Linear vs Non-linear Boundaries

Logistic regression always produces a linear boundary. That's fine when the two classes can be separated by a straight line. But what if they can't?

✔ Linear works when

  • Classes can be separated by a single straight cut
  • Predictors have additive effects
  • Most real tabular datasets — surprisingly often

✘ Linear struggles when

  • The yes-region looks like a ring around the no-region
  • Classes form spirals or interlocking shapes
  • Image and text data with raw pixels/tokens
  • Fix: feature engineering (x², x₁·x₂) or non-linear models (trees, neural networks)

A common technique: add transformed predictors (like x₁², x₁·x₂) to a logistic regression. The boundary in the original space becomes curved, even though the math is still "linear in the coefficients." This trick — called basis expansion — connects logistic regression to more advanced methods like polynomial regression and kernel SVMs.

Real-life examples

Where Decision Boundaries Drive Daily Outcomes

📧 Spam Threshold

Gmail's spam folder uses a tuned threshold below 0.5 because users hate missing legitimate mail in their spam folder more than they dislike a little spam in their inbox.

🏥 Medical Screening

Mammogram AI systems use very low thresholds because missing cancer is far more costly than over-referral to a radiologist.

💳 Card Fraud

Banks tune thresholds based on transaction size — large transactions get a stricter "yes-it-is-fraud" cut-off than small ones.

🚪 Building Access

Face-recognition entry systems use very high thresholds (P > 0.95) because the cost of letting in the wrong person is much greater than a brief inconvenience.

📰 Content Moderation

Social platforms use different thresholds for "flag for review" (low) vs "auto-remove" (high). Boundary choice is a policy decision.

🚗 Self-Driving Brake

Autonomous-vehicle obstacle detectors use very low thresholds for braking — a false brake is a comfort issue, but a missed obstacle is a safety event.
