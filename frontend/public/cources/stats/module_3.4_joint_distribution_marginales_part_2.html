Conditioning & Independence

Two of the most powerful ideas in probability — what happens to X when we already know Y, and the rare case when knowing Y tells us nothing about X at all.

Part 1 gave us the full picture (joint) and individual slices (marginals). Part 2 introduces two transformative tools. First, conditional distributions — the math of "given that I already know Y, what is X likely to be?" This single idea drives Bayesian inference, medical diagnosis, recommendation systems, and almost all of modern AI. Then, independence — when two variables truly have no influence on each other. The line between dependent and independent variables shapes how we model the world. Everything in beginner-friendly language with live visuals.

Why We Need Conditioning
A new kind of question

When You Already Know Something

Up to now, we've asked questions like: "What's the chance of X?" or "What's the chance of X and Y together?"

But often in real life, you already know something. You're not asking from scratch. You're asking from a position of partial information. For example:

  • "Given that someone studied a lot, what's the chance they got an A?"
  • "Given that today is rainy, what's the chance they brought an umbrella?"
  • "Given that the test came back positive, what's the chance the patient is sick?"
  • "Given that I bought a coffee, what's the chance I also bought a muffin?"
  • "Given a user just watched a thriller, what's the chance they'll watch another?"

The word "given" is the magic keyword. It signals that we are conditioning on something — narrowing our world to only the cases where the known information holds true.

Big idea: Conditional distributions answer questions of the form "What is the distribution of X, restricted to the world where Y already equals some specific value?" They are how we use one piece of evidence to update our beliefs about another.
A simple analogy

The Spotlight Analogy

Imagine the full joint distribution is a giant room filled with people, where each person is a possible (X, Y) pair.

  • The joint distribution describes everyone in the room.
  • The marginal of X ignores Y — like asking "what's the X distribution overall?" — averaging across all Y values.
  • The conditional of X given Y = y is like shining a spotlight on only the people with that specific Y value, then describing only those people.

The spotlight gives you a focused, narrower view. The conditional distribution describes only the subset of the world that matches your given condition.

Conditional Distributions
The formal definition

What is a Conditional Distribution?

The conditional distribution of X given Y = y, written p(x | y) or f(x | y), gives the probability distribution of X restricted to the cases where Y equals y.

Reading the notation:

  • p(x | y) — read as "probability of X equals x, GIVEN that Y equals y"
  • The vertical bar | means "given" — it separates what we want to know from what we already know
  • What's on the left of | is the unknown; what's on the right is the known
⟁ Conditional PMF — Discrete Case
p(x | y) = p(x, y) / pY(y)

In words: "The conditional probability of X given Y = y is the joint probability of (x, y), divided by the marginal probability of Y = y." You take the joint, then divide by the marginal of what you've conditioned on.

p(x, y) — joint probability of seeing both x and y
pY(y) — marginal probability of y (it must be > 0)
p(x | y) — conditional probability of x given y

Why divide? Dividing by pY(y) rescales the slice so that the conditional probabilities add up to 1 — making it a valid probability distribution within the restricted world where Y = y.

⟁ Conditional PDF — Continuous Case
f(x | y) = f(x, y) / fY(y)

In words: exact same idea for continuous variables — divide the joint density by the marginal density. The conditional density gives the distribution of X within the slice where Y = y.

A concrete walkthrough

Computing Conditionals from the Study-Hours Table

Let's go back to our joint table from Part 1:

X \\ Y Grade Y pX(x)
A B C
High study 0.25 0.10 0.02 0.37
Medium study 0.10 0.20 0.08 0.38
Low study 0.02 0.08 0.15 0.25
pY(y) 0.37 0.38 0.25 1.00
Highlighted row = "given High study"

Question: Among students who studied a lot, what's the distribution of grades?

Apply the conditional formula. We want p(Y | X = High). For each grade y:

  • p(A | High) = p(High, A) / pX(High) = 0.25 / 0.37 ≈ 0.676 → 67.6%
  • p(B | High) = p(High, B) / pX(High) = 0.10 / 0.37 ≈ 0.270 → 27.0%
  • p(C | High) = p(High, C) / pX(High) = 0.02 / 0.37 ≈ 0.054 → 5.4%

Sanity check: 0.676 + 0.270 + 0.054 = 1.000 ✓ — it's a valid distribution.

Reading the conditional: Among high-study students, 68% get an A, 27% get a B, and only 5% get a C. Compare to the unconditional marginal of Y (37% A, 38% B, 25% C). Knowing that someone studied a lot shifts the grade distribution dramatically toward A's. This is exactly the kind of insight conditioning unlocks.
Same idea, other direction

Reversing the Conditional — p(X | Y)

You can condition either way. Let's compute the conditional of X given Y = A — that is, among students who got an A, what's the distribution of their study habits?

  • p(High | A) = p(High, A) / pY(A) = 0.25 / 0.37 ≈ 0.676 → 67.6%
  • p(Medium | A) = p(Medium, A) / pY(A) = 0.10 / 0.37 ≈ 0.270 → 27.0%
  • p(Low | A) = p(Low, A) / pY(A) = 0.02 / 0.37 ≈ 0.054 → 5.4%

Interpretation: Among students who got an A, 67.6% studied a lot, 27% studied moderately, and only 5.4% studied little. Most A students studied — which makes intuitive sense.

Critical: p(X | Y) and p(Y | X) are NOT the same thing! In our example they happened to give the same numbers because the table is symmetric. But in general, p(X | Y = y) and p(Y | X = x) are completely different distributions. (This subtle point causes more statistical confusion than almost any other.)

Why this matters

The Power of Conditioning — Real-World Reasoning

Conditional distributions are the backbone of how we update our beliefs in light of evidence. Some real applications:

🏥 Medical Diagnosis

  • P(disease | test positive)
  • How a positive test updates disease probability
  • Bayes' theorem in action

🛒 Recommendation Systems

  • P(buy item B | bought item A)
  • Powers "customers who bought X also bought Y"
  • Heart of Amazon, Netflix, Spotify

🤖 Machine Learning

  • P(label | features)
  • Every classifier outputs a conditional
  • Logistic regression, neural networks

🎯 Sports Analytics

  • P(win | leading by 10 at halftime)
  • Win probability charts
  • Real-time betting odds

Independence Between Variables
When two variables don't talk

What Does Independence Mean?

Two random variables X and Y are independent if knowing the value of one tells you nothing new about the other.

For example:

  • A coin flip and a die roll — knowing the coin result doesn't change the die distribution.
  • Tomorrow's weather in Tokyo vs today's lottery numbers in New York — no relationship.
  • The roll of one fair die vs the roll of another fair die — fully independent.

By contrast, dependent variables do influence each other:

  • Study hours and grade — knowing study hours shifts the grade distribution.
  • Weather and umbrella — knowing it's rainy makes umbrellas much more likely.
  • Height and weight — taller people tend to weigh more.
The clean test: X and Y are independent if and only if knowing Y gives you exactly the same probabilities for X as you'd have without that knowledge. Formally: the conditional p(X | Y) is identical to the marginal pX(X), for every possible Y.
⟁ Independence — Definition
p(x, y) = pX(x) · pY(y)   (discrete)
f(x, y) = fX(x) · fY(y)   (continuous)

In words: The joint probability factors into the product of the marginals — for every possible (x, y) pair. If this holds, X and Y are independent. If even one cell breaks the rule, they are NOT independent.

Symbol: Independence is written as X ⊥ Y or X ⫫ Y — "X is independent of Y."

⟁ Equivalent Definitions of Independence
p(x | y) = pX(x)   for all y
p(y | x) = pY(y)   for all x

Both say the same thing: if X and Y are independent, conditioning on Y doesn't change the distribution of X, and vice versa. The conditional just collapses to the marginal — "knowing Y tells you nothing about X."

Testing for independence

Example 1 — A Truly Independent Pair

Recall the coin × 4-sided die from Part 1:

X \\ Y Die Y pX(x)
1234
X = 0 1/81/81/81/8 1/2
X = 1 1/81/81/81/8 1/2
pY(y) 1/4 1/4 1/4 1/4 1.00
Independent joint: every p(x, y) = pX(x) × pY(y)

Test: for every cell, does p(x, y) = pX(x) × pY(y)?

  • p(0, 1) = 1/8. Marginal product: (1/2)(1/4) = 1/8. ✓ Match!
  • p(1, 3) = 1/8. Marginal product: (1/2)(1/4) = 1/8. ✓ Match!
  • All 8 cells match → X and Y are independent.

This makes intuitive sense — the coin flip and the die roll have nothing to do with each other.

A dependent pair

Example 2 — A Clearly NOT Independent Pair

Now compare with the study-hours-vs-grades table:

Test the same way. Does p(x, y) = pX(x) × pY(y) for every cell?

  • p(High, A) = 0.25. Marginal product: 0.37 × 0.37 ≈ 0.137. 0.25 ≠ 0.137
  • p(Low, C) = 0.15. Marginal product: 0.25 × 0.25 ≈ 0.0625. 0.15 ≠ 0.0625
  • Even one mismatch breaks independence → X and Y are NOT independent.

Again, intuitive — study hours and grades are clearly related. More study generally means better grades.

The signature of dependence: when the joint table's diagonal (or anti-diagonal) shows much higher probabilities than would arise by chance, the variables are dependent. The "concentration on the diagonal" pattern is the visual fingerprint of a positive relationship.

Properties of independence

Key Properties of Independent Variables

When X and Y are independent, many nice things happen:

1. Joint Factors

  • p(x, y) = pX(x) · pY(y)
  • Joint determined by marginals alone
  • Multiplying marginals reconstructs the joint

2. Conditioning Has No Effect

  • p(X | Y) = pX(X)
  • p(Y | X) = pY(Y)
  • Knowing Y doesn't update X (and vice versa)

3. Expectations Factor

  • E[X · Y] = E[X] · E[Y]
  • Powerful simplification
  • True ONLY when independent

4. Covariance is Zero

  • Cov(X, Y) = 0
  • (See Part 3)
  • Variables don't move together
Critical caution — Correlation does not imply causation. Even if X and Y are dependent, that doesn't mean X causes Y. It just means they're related. Both could be influenced by a hidden third variable (ice cream sales and drownings both rise in summer — not because one causes the other, but because of the hot weather). Dependence is a statistical pattern; causation is a separate (and harder) question.
Caution #2 — Zero covariance ≠ independence. Independence implies zero covariance, but zero covariance does not always imply independence. There are weird distributions where Cov(X, Y) = 0 but X and Y are still dependent in non-linear ways. (For example: Y = X² with X symmetric around 0 — they're tightly linked but have zero covariance!) We'll see covariance in Part 3.
When is independence reasonable?

Real-World Examples of Independence

  • Sensor noise readings from two well-separated sensors — independent.
  • Two coin flips in succession — independent (memoryless).
  • Daily lottery numbers in different countries — independent.
  • Random samples drawn one at a time with replacement — independent.
  • Quantum particle measurements on entangled-free systems — independent.

And examples where independence is a simplifying assumption (used even when not quite exact):

  • Naive Bayes classifier assumes features are independent given the class — usually wrong, but works well in practice.
  • Independent and Identically Distributed (IID) samples — the workhorse assumption of statistics.
  • Treating customer arrivals as independent in queueing models — often approximately true.
