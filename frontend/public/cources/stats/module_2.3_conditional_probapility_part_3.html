2.3.9
Bayes' Theorem Basics
The core inversion problem

The Problem Bayes Solves

Often in the real world, we know how a cause produces an effect, but we want to reason in the opposite direction — from effect back to cause:

  • We know P(positive test | disease) — sensitivity. But we want P(disease | positive test).
  • We know P(word "lottery" | spam). But we want P(spam | word "lottery").
  • We know P(observation | hypothesis). But we want P(hypothesis | observation).

This inversion — going from P(B | A) to P(A | B) — is exactly what Bayes' theorem accomplishes. It's the mathematical engine that lets us reverse the direction of conditional probability.

Big idea: Bayes' theorem is the formal rule for updating our beliefs about a hypothesis (A) after observing some evidence (B). It is the law of learning from data.
⟁ Bayes' Theorem — The Foundation of Belief Updating
P(A | B) = P(B | A) · P(A) P(B)
P(A | B) — Posterior
What we want: the probability of hypothesis A after observing evidence B. The updated belief.
P(A) — Prior
What we believed about A before seeing any evidence. The base rate, background frequency, or initial guess.
P(B | A) — Likelihood
How likely is the evidence B if hypothesis A is true? The "forward" or generative probability — usually the easy one to estimate.
P(B) — Evidence (Marginal)
The total probability of observing B, summing over all hypotheses. Computed via Law of Total Probability.
Derivation — where Bayes comes from

Building Bayes from What You Already Know

Bayes' theorem isn't a new axiom — it falls out of the multiplication rule from Part 1. Here's the two-line derivation:

P(A ∩ B) = P(A | B) × P(B)    ① (from Part 1)

P(A ∩ B) = P(B | A) × P(A)    ② (from Part 1, symmetric form)

Set ① equal to ② (both equal P(A ∩ B)):

P(A | B) × P(B) = P(B | A) × P(A)

Divide both sides by P(B):

P(A | B) = P(B | A) × P(A) / P(B)    ✓

That's it. Bayes' theorem is just the multiplication rule rearranged. The reason it feels so powerful isn't that it's a deep new fact — it's that it captures the direction of inference we actually care about in the real world.

⟁ Expanded Form — With Law of Total Probability in the Denominator
P(A | B) = P(B | A) × P(A) / [ P(B | A) × P(A) + P(B | ¬A) × P(¬A) ]

In practice, the denominator P(B) is almost never given directly. We compute it from a partition of the sample space (Part 2's Law of Total Probability). The most common partition is {A, ¬A} — the hypothesis is either true or false — which gives the expanded form above. This is the form you'll use 90% of the time.

2.3.10
Bayesian Thinking Intuition
Learning from evidence

The Bayesian Mindset

Bayesian thinking is not just a formula — it's a worldview. It treats beliefs as probabilities, evidence as updates, and reasoning as a continuous flow of revision. Three operating principles:

  • Start with a prior. You always begin with some background belief, even if it's just "uniform — I don't know." Honest reasoning requires naming your prior.
  • Update with evidence. When new data arrives, your belief shifts in proportion to the likelihood of that data under each hypothesis.
  • Today's posterior is tomorrow's prior. Beliefs are never "final." Each round of evidence updates them; the updated belief is the starting point for the next round.
Bayesian thinking treats certainty as a degenerate case of probability. We rarely know; we mostly estimate with confidence. The Bayesian asks not "is X true?" but "given everything I know, how probable is X?"
Why posterior often surprises us

The Strength-vs-Rarity Tug-of-War

The posterior is a battle between two forces:

  • Prior (base rate): How rare is the hypothesis in general? Rare hypotheses get a low starting belief.
  • Likelihood ratio: How much more probable does the evidence become under the hypothesis vs. its negation? Strong evidence (high ratio) overcomes a low prior.

For a hypothesis A vs. ¬A, define the Bayes factor:

BF = P(B | A) / P(B | ¬A)

This is the "strength of evidence." When the prior is tiny (rare disease, rare event), the Bayes factor must be enormous to push the posterior to a meaningful level. This is why a 99%-accurate test for a 0.1% disease still produces mostly false alarms — the Bayes factor (~99) cannot overcome the (0.001 / 0.999) prior odds.

Medical diagnosis case study

The Medical Test Paradox Explained

This is the most famous Bayesian example in the world. A disease affects 1 in 1000 people. A test for it has 99% sensitivity and 99% specificity. You test positive. What's your probability of having the disease?

Most people guess: 99%, or close to it.

Actual Bayesian answer: About 9%.

P(D | +) = (0.99 × 0.001) / (0.99 × 0.001 + 0.01 × 0.999) = 0.00099 / 0.01098 ≈ 0.090

Why? Out of 1000 people, 1 has the disease (likely to test positive). Of the 999 healthy, about 10 will test positive falsely. So among ~11 positives, only 1 is truly sick. That's 1/11 ≈ 9%.

Lesson: Doctors who don't think Bayesianly often overestimate diagnostic confidence — sometimes catastrophically. Genetic testing companies and screening programs grapple with this every day. Mastery of Bayes is literally life-saving knowledge.
Spam detection intuition

How Naive Bayes Built the Modern Inbox

Before 2002, spam was crushing the internet. Then Paul Graham popularized Bayesian spam filtering, which roughly works as follows:

  1. Start with a prior P(spam) based on the overall fraction of emails that are spam (say 50%).
  2. For each word in the email, look up its likelihood under "spam" vs "not spam" from training data. (P("lottery" | spam) is high, P("lottery" | not spam) is low.)
  3. Apply Bayes' theorem repeatedly — once per word — multiplying the evidence. Each word nudges the posterior up or down.
  4. If the final posterior P(spam | all words in email) crosses a threshold, mark as spam.

The "naive" assumption is that words are conditionally independent given the class — which is technically false but works astonishingly well in practice. This single application made email usable again.

AI belief updating

Bayesian Thinking in Modern AI

Bayesian reasoning permeates AI in deep ways:

  • Bayesian neural networks place a probability distribution over the network's weights — every prediction comes with a calibrated uncertainty.
  • Reinforcement learning with exploration bonuses uses posterior beliefs over rewards.
  • Probabilistic programming languages (Stan, PyMC, Pyro) let scientists encode prior knowledge and update it with data.
  • Bayesian optimization uses Gaussian processes — explicit posteriors over functions — to optimize expensive black-box objectives.
  • Hidden Markov models, Kalman filters, particle filters — all are real-time Bayesian update engines.

Whenever an AI system must reason under uncertainty and update beliefs over time, Bayes is somewhere underneath.

2.3.11
Conditional Probability Trees
Trees as visual Bayes

The Geometric Way to Compute Bayes

A probability tree visualizes a sequence of conditional probabilities as a branching diagram. Each branch is labeled with a conditional probability, and any path from root to leaf gives a joint probability via the multiplication rule.

Trees are the most powerful tool for solving Bayes problems by hand. They make the partition explicit and reduce Bayes' theorem to simple arithmetic.

How to read this tree: Multiply along each path to get joint probabilities. The posterior P(D | +) = (pink leaf) / [(pink leaf) + (amber leaf)] — the true positives divided by all positives. That single division is Bayes' theorem in action.

Why Trees Work So Well

Trees expose every quantity Bayes needs:

  • The prior is the first level of branches.
  • The likelihoods are the second level.
  • The joint probabilities are the leaf labels.
  • The evidence P(B) is the sum of leaves that match the observation.
  • The posterior is the matching leaf divided by the sum of matching leaves.

For two-step Bayes problems, drawing the tree is often easier than memorizing the formula.

2.3.12
Real-World Applications

Medical Testing MED

Every clinical decision involving a test — pregnancy, COVID, cancer screening, genetic disease — is a Bayes problem. Posteriors guide treatment.

Fraud Detection FIN

Credit card systems compute P(fraud | transaction features). Bayes combines low base rates of fraud with strong feature signals to block fraudsters without flooding customers with false alarms.

Recommendation Systems REC

P(user likes item | history) drives Netflix, Spotify, Amazon. Bayesian collaborative filtering and matrix factorization underpin most of modern personalization.

ML Classification ML

Naive Bayes, Logistic Regression, and every probabilistic classifier output P(class | features). The softmax of a neural network is a Bayes-style posterior over labels.

Bayesian AI Systems AI

Bayesian neural networks, probabilistic programming, variational inference, and MCMC sampling all rest on Bayes' theorem applied at scale.

Search & SEO WEB

Search engines compute P(relevant | query, page features). Click models, ranking models, and ad auctions all use Bayesian posteriors.

Forensic DNA LAW

Match probability + prior = posterior probability of guilt. The prosecutor's fallacy — confusing P(match | innocent) with P(innocent | match) — has overturned real convictions.

A/B Testing BIZ

Bayesian A/B testing computes P(variant B is better | observed data) — interpretable, doesn't require pre-fixed sample sizes, and enables early stopping.

Self-Driving Perception AV

P(pedestrian present | LiDAR + camera + radar). Sensor fusion is sequential Bayes updating: each modality refines the posterior over the surrounding scene.

2.3.13
Common Mistakes
01
Confusing dependence with causation. If A and B are dependent, it does not mean A causes B. Both might be driven by a hidden third variable. Correlation is not causation — dependence is mathematical, causation is mechanistic.
02
Mixing independence and exclusivity. The fatal confusion from Part 2. Mutually exclusive (cannot co-occur) and independent (don't influence each other) are different — and for events with nonzero probability, mutually exclusive ⟹ dependent.
03
Wrong denominator. When computing P(A | B) = P(A ∩ B) / P(B), people often plug P(A) or P(Ω) into the denominator. Always verify the denominator is the conditioning event's probability.
04
Bayes inversion error (prosecutor's fallacy). Confusing P(B | A) with P(A | B). "If you have the disease, the test is 99% accurate" tells you P(+ | D) — NOT P(D | +). Without the prior, you cannot invert.
05
Ignoring the base rate. Even strong evidence cannot rescue you from a tiny prior. A rare condition with a 99%-accurate test still yields mostly false positives. Base rates are not optional.
06
Forgetting to compute P(B). Bayes' denominator requires summing over all hypotheses (Law of Total Probability). Skipping this step gives joint probabilities, not posteriors.
07
Treating the prior as "subjective junk." Some students think priors are arbitrary. They're not — they encode background knowledge. With enough data, the posterior is robust to reasonable priors, but ignoring the prior entirely is the mistake.
08
Conjunction fallacy. Believing P(A ∩ B) > P(A). Mathematically impossible: a joint event cannot be more probable than either of its components. (Famous "Linda the bank teller" problem.)

Conditional Probability & Bayesian Reasoning — Complete
01
Conditional probability is the mathematics of belief updating. P(A | B) measures the probability of A inside the restricted sample space where B has happened.
02
Multiplication rule: P(A ∩ B) = P(A | B) × P(B). The chain rule generalizes this for any number of sequential events — the foundation of probabilistic models in ML.
03
Independence: P(A ∩ B) = P(A) × P(B). Knowing B tells you nothing about A. Mutually exclusive events are dependent, not independent — this distinction is critical.
04
Law of Total Probability: P(A) = Σᵢ P(A | Bᵢ) × P(Bᵢ) over a partition. The denominator of Bayes' theorem.
05
Bayes' theorem: P(A | B) = P(B | A) × P(A) / P(B). The single most powerful equation for reasoning under uncertainty. Posterior = (likelihood × prior) / evidence.
06
The base-rate fallacy: A "99% accurate" test for a 0.1% disease still produces mostly false positives. Always weight evidence against the prior — the Bayesian mindset prevents life-altering misjudgments.
07
Bayesian thinking powers spam filters, medical diagnosis, fraud detection, recommendation engines, autonomous driving, and the entire field of probabilistic machine learning. You now have the foundational vocabulary.
08
Today's posterior is tomorrow's prior. Belief is never final — it is always being updated. This is the deepest lesson of Bayesian reasoning and the engine of all learning systems.
