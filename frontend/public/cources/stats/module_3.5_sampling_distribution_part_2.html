TOPIC 3
Standard Error
What it is

What is the Standard Error?

The standard error (often shortened to SE) is a number that tells you how much your sample average is likely to differ from the true population average.

Put very simply: if you took a sample and got an average of 170, the standard error tells you how far that 170 might be from the truth — maybe 2 cm off, maybe 5 cm off, maybe 10 cm off.

  • Small SE → your sample average is probably very close to the truth. You can trust it.
  • Big SE → your sample average could be quite far from the truth. Be careful.
Easy way to remember: The standard error is the "typical wiggle" of a sample average. It's the answer to the question: "If I took another sample, how different would my average be?"
Important: SE vs SD

Standard Error vs Standard Deviation — Don't Confuse Them!

These two sound similar but mean different things. Here's the simple way to tell them apart:

TermWhat it measuresExample
Standard Deviation (σ) How spread out the individual values are in the population "People's heights vary by 10 cm"
Standard Error (SE) How much the sample average wiggles from sample to sample "Average of 100 people varies by 1 cm"
Quick rule: Standard deviation describes individuals. Standard error describes averages. SE is always smaller than σ (because averages are more stable than single values).
⟁ The Standard Error Formula
SE = σ / √n

Recognize this formula? Yes — it's the exact same formula we used in Part 1 for the "spread of sample means." The standard error of the mean is just a special name for that spread.

SE — standard error (how much sample averages wiggle)
σ — population standard deviation (how much individuals vary)
n — sample size (how many people in each sample)
√n — the square root of n (n=4 → √n=2; n=100 → √n=10)

What the formula says in plain English:

"To find the typical wiggle of a sample average, take how much individuals wiggle (σ) and shrink it by √n. Bigger samples shrink the wiggle more — making sample averages more reliable."

⟁ When You Don't Know σ — Use the Sample Estimate
SE ≈ s / √n

The real-world version: Most of the time, you don't actually know the population standard deviation σ. Instead, you estimate it from your sample. Just replace σ with s (the sample standard deviation), and you get a usable formula.

s — sample standard deviation (computed from your data)
n — sample size

In real projects, this is the version you'll use 99% of the time. Software (Excel, Python, R) computes both s and SE for you with one click.

How to use it

How to Use the Standard Error in Practice

Once you have your sample average x̄ and the standard error SE, you can build a "confidence interval" — a range you can be fairly sure contains the true answer:

68% Range

  • x̄ − SE to x̄ + SE
  • "Plus or minus 1 SE"
  • About 68% sure truth is here

95% Range (Most Common)

  • x̄ − 2·SE to x̄ + 2·SE
  • "Plus or minus 2 SE"
  • About 95% sure truth is here
  • This is the standard in news/research

99.7% Range

  • x̄ − 3·SE to x̄ + 3·SE
  • "Plus or minus 3 SE"
  • Almost certain truth is here

This comes directly from the empirical rule (68-95-99.7) we learned in Module 3.3.

Example: A survey found average customer satisfaction = 8.2 out of 10, with SE = 0.15. The "95% confidence interval" is 8.2 ± 0.30, which is the range from 7.9 to 8.5. You can say with 95% confidence that the true satisfaction is somewhere between 7.9 and 8.5.
When to use it

When You Should Compute Standard Error

  • Any time you report an average from a sample and want to express how trustworthy it is.
  • Whenever you create a chart with error bars — those bars are usually 1 or 2 SE wide.
  • When running A/B tests — SE tells you whether the difference between A and B is real or just noise.
  • For all polls, surveys, and studies — SE is the basis of the "margin of error."
  • In scientific publications — almost every research result is reported with SE or a confidence interval.
⟁ Worked Example

Example: Coffee Shop Customer Wait Times

A coffee shop owner times how long customers wait. They time 36 random customers and find:

  • Sample average wait time: x̄ = 4.0 minutes
  • Sample standard deviation: s = 1.8 minutes
  • Sample size: n = 36

Step 1 — Calculate the Standard Error.

SE = s / √n = 1.8 / √36 = 1.8 / 6 = 0.30 minutes

The standard error is 0.30 minutes — meaning if we repeated the survey, the average would typically wiggle about 0.30 minutes (18 seconds) from this value.

Step 2 — Build the 95% confidence interval.

95% range = x̄ ± 2·SE = 4.0 ± 2(0.30) = 4.0 ± 0.60

So we're 95% confident that the true average wait time is between 3.4 minutes and 4.6 minutes.

Step 3 — Make a business decision.

The owner's goal is "average wait under 5 minutes." Since the 95% range is 3.4 to 4.6 — entirely under 5 — the owner can confidently say: "Our average wait is under 5 minutes." 🎉

Why this works: The standard error gave us a precise way to turn one sample into a trustworthy statement about all customers — without having to time every single customer.

Real-life examples

Where Standard Error Shows Up Every Day

📊 News Polls

"Candidate A: 52% support, ±3%." That ±3% is roughly 2 standard errors. The pollster used SE to give you a trustworthy range.

🧪 Drug Trials

"Drug X reduces blood pressure by 12 mmHg (SE = 1.2)." Researchers report SE so others know how reliable the result is.

📈 Stock Returns

"Average return: 8% per year (SE = 0.5%)." Investors use SE to judge whether a fund's returns are genuinely good or just luck.

🎯 A/B Testing

"Version B converts 5% better, SE = 0.8%." If the difference is 6+ times bigger than SE, it's almost certainly real.

🏭 Quality Control

"Average product weight: 500g (SE = 2g)." Factories use SE to decide if a batch meets specifications.

🌡️ Climate Science

"Global temperature anomaly: +1.1°C (SE = 0.1°C)." Climate reports use SE to show how confident the estimate is.

Real-life use

How Standard Error Drives Real Decisions

SE isn't just a textbook number — it determines real outcomes:

  • Election predictions: "Too close to call" happens when the gap between candidates is smaller than 2·SE.
  • Medical approval: The FDA requires new drugs to show benefits much larger than the SE — to prove the effect is real, not random.
  • Product launches: Companies pull failing tests when their A/B difference is smaller than SE — meaning "no clear winner."
  • Scientific publishing: Journals require all reported numbers to come with SE so readers can judge reliability.

Understanding standard error is one of the most useful real-world stats skills. It's the difference between blindly trusting numbers and reading them critically.

TOPIC 4
Why Samples Differ from Populations
What's going on

Why Don't Samples Match the Real Population?

You take a sample. You compute an average. It's almost never exactly the same as the true population average. Why?

There are three big reasons. Two are unavoidable, and one is your enemy:

1. Random Wiggle ✓

  • Natural and unavoidable
  • Different samples have different people by chance
  • Measured by the Standard Error
  • Shrinks as sample size grows

2. Measurement Error ⚠

  • Sometimes avoidable, sometimes not
  • Scales might be slightly off
  • People mis-report their weight
  • Better tools and methods reduce it

3. Sampling Bias ✗

  • The dangerous one!
  • Sample doesn't represent the population
  • Bigger samples DON'T fix bias
  • Can completely ruin your results
Reason 1 in detail

Random Wiggle (Sampling Error) — The Friendly Kind

This is the most basic reason samples differ. Every time you take a sample, you grab a slightly different mix of people just by chance.

Imagine you have a bag with 1,000 marbles: 500 red, 500 blue. Reach in and grab 10.

  • You might get 5 red, 5 blue (truth!) — but probably not.
  • You might get 6 red, 4 blue (close-ish).
  • You might get 3 red, 7 blue (more off, but not crazy).
  • Rarely, you might get 9 red, 1 blue (very off, but possible).

None of this is anyone's fault. It's just chance. This kind of difference is what the standard error measures. And as we learned, bigger samples make this wiggle smaller.

The good news: Random wiggle is predictable and controllable. You can shrink it by taking bigger samples. It's the "honest" kind of error.
Reason 2 in detail

Measurement Error — Small Mistakes Add Up

Sometimes the difference comes from how you collect data, not who you measure.

Examples:

  • A scale that always reads 1 kg too heavy → all weights are off by 1 kg.
  • A survey question that's worded confusingly → people answer wrong.
  • People mis-remember their income on a survey.
  • A faulty thermometer reads 2°C too cool.
  • An interviewer pushes people toward certain answers.
The fix: better tools, clearer questions, careful methods, double-checking data. Measurement error can usually be reduced with good design — but never fully eliminated.
Reason 3 in detail

Sampling Bias — The Silent Killer

This is the most dangerous reason — and the most common cause of bad statistics.

Sampling bias happens when your sample doesn't really represent the population. Some kinds of people are over-represented, others are under-represented. The sample is systematically off.

Worst part? Bigger samples don't fix it. If your method is biased, sampling 10,000 people gives just as wrong an answer as sampling 100.

Real Examples of Sampling Bias That Caused Big Failures:

🗳️ The 1936 Election Disaster

  • A magazine surveyed 2.4 MILLION people
  • Predicted Landon would beat Roosevelt
  • Roosevelt won in a landslide
  • Why? They polled phone owners (rich people)
  • Phone owners didn't represent all voters

📱 Online-Only Surveys

  • Only reaches internet users
  • Misses elderly, rural, poor populations
  • Results favor opinions of online crowd
  • Common modern bias

⭐ Restaurant Review Bias

  • Only the most pleased or angry write reviews
  • Average customers don't bother
  • Reviews don't represent typical experience
  • Selection bias in action

🏥 Hospital Patient Studies

  • Only sick people come to hospitals
  • Can't conclude things about healthy folks
  • Famous bias in medical research
The bias trap: Bias is invisible. Your sample average looks fine. Your standard error looks small. But if your sample is biased, everything is wrong — and you might never know.
How to fix it

How to Get Good Samples — Practical Tips

  • Random selection: Every person in the population must have an equal chance of being picked. This is the gold standard.
  • Reach everyone: Don't just use one method (phone, online, mail). Use multiple methods to reach different groups.
  • Account for non-response: People who don't respond may be different from those who do. Try to follow up or adjust.
  • Pre-test your survey: Try questions on a few people first to catch confusing wording.
  • Stratified sampling: Deliberately ensure each group (age, gender, region) is properly represented.
  • Be honest about limits: Report what your sample covers and what it doesn't.

When to watch for sample differences

When to Be Most Careful

  • Polls during elections: who answered the phone or filled out the form?
  • Health studies: Did the study only include certain age groups or genders?
  • Customer feedback: Are you hearing only from the very happy or very angry?
  • Online surveys: Who has internet and time to fill these out?
  • Self-reported data: Are people being honest? (Income, weight, exercise, etc.)
  • Volunteer studies: Who volunteers may be different from the general population.
⟁ Worked Example

Example: Why Two Surveys Gave Different Answers

A company wants to know: "How many hours per day do people use social media?"

Survey A: Posted on Instagram, asks followers to respond.

  • 2,000 responses, average reported usage: 4.5 hours/day
  • Standard error: 0.05 hours
  • Looks very precise! 95% range: 4.4 to 4.6 hours

Survey B: Randomly selects 500 phone numbers, calls people of all ages.

  • 500 responses, average reported usage: 2.1 hours/day
  • Standard error: 0.12 hours
  • Less precise, but 95% range: 1.86 to 2.34 hours

Which is right? They give wildly different answers — 4.5 vs 2.1 hours. Why?

  • Survey A asked Instagram users (heavy social media users by definition) → sampling bias
  • Survey B reached a much more diverse group → less bias
  • Survey A's bigger sample size did NOT save it. Bigger sample with bias still gives biased results.
The lesson: Don't be fooled by big sample sizes or small standard errors. If your method is biased, your numbers are wrong — and nobody can tell from the report alone. Always ask: who is in this sample, and who is missing?
Real-life impact

How Sample Differences Affect Decisions

🗳️ Election Surprises

Polls that miss certain voter groups can predict the wrong winner. The 2016 US election surprised many because polls underestimated certain rural voters.

💊 Medical Disasters

Drugs tested mainly on young white men have sometimes harmed women or older patients in ways researchers missed.

🤖 AI Bias

Facial recognition models trained mostly on light-skinned faces fail on dark-skinned faces. Biased samples create biased AI.

📱 Product Mistakes

Companies that only listen to their power users miss how regular users actually behave. Bad product decisions follow.
