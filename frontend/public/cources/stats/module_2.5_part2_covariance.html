Covariance & Correlation

When two random variables share a story — how do we measure the way they move together? Covariance captures joint motion; correlation strips out the scale.

Part 2 extends the single-variable summaries of Part 1 into the world of pairs. We build covariance from first principles, see why its scale-dependence is a problem, then derive correlation as the clean, scale-free fix. We end with the famous warning: correlation is not causation — and an interactive demo of how nonlinear patterns can have zero correlation despite being perfectly structured.

From One Variable to Two
The new question

When Random Variables Travel Together

In Part 1 we summarized a single random variable with two numbers: a center (E[X]) and a spread (Var(X)). That's enough when we only care about one quantity.

But real data almost never comes alone. Height and weight. Study hours and test scores. Temperature and electricity demand. Stock A and stock B. Income and education. In every case the question shifts from "what does X do?" to "how do X and Y move together?"

Part 2's central question: If X goes up, does Y tend to go up, down, or wander randomly? We need a mathematical tool that captures the joint dynamics of two variables — not just their individual behaviors.
Three possible relationships

The Three Flavors of Joint Behavior

Positive Relationship

  • X up → Y also tends up
  • X down → Y also tends down
  • Example: study hours & test scores
  • Example: height & weight

Negative Relationship

  • X up → Y tends down
  • X down → Y tends up
  • Example: price & demand
  • Example: exercise & resting heart rate

No (Linear) Relationship

  • X says nothing about Y
  • Cloud has no slope
  • Example: shoe size & IQ
  • Example: dice rolls in two casinos

We need a single number that captures which of these three patterns we have — and how strong the pattern is. That number is covariance.

2
Covariance
Building the idea

The Core Intuition — Synchronized Deviations

Recall variance: Var(X) = E[(X − μ_X)²] — the average squared deviation of X from its own mean.

Covariance generalizes this to two variables: instead of squaring one deviation, we multiply the deviation of X by the deviation of Y. The covariance is the average of that product.

Mental model: For each data point, ask "is X above its mean? Is Y above its mean?" If both deviate in the same direction (both above, or both below), the product is positive. If they deviate in opposite directions, the product is negative. Average all the products → covariance.
⟁ Covariance — Definition
Cov(X, Y) = E[(X − μ_X)(Y − μ_Y)]

For each pair of outcomes, multiply the deviation of X from its mean by the deviation of Y from its mean, then average across the joint distribution. The sign tells you the direction of the relationship; the magnitude tells you the strength — but the magnitude depends on the scales of X and Y.

μ_X = E[X] — mean of X
μ_Y = E[Y] — mean of Y
(X − μ_X)(Y − μ_Y) — joint deviation product
Cov(X, Y) — average over the joint distribution
⟁ Covariance — Computational Shortcut
Cov(X, Y) = E[XY] − E[X]·E[Y]

Just like variance has a shortcut formula (E[X²] − μ²), covariance has one too. To compute Cov(X, Y): compute the expected product E[XY], then subtract the product of the expectations. Much easier than tracking deviations directly.

Proof: Expand E[(X−μ_X)(Y−μ_Y)] = E[XY − μ_Y X − μ_X Y + μ_X μ_Y] = E[XY] − μ_Y μ_X − μ_X μ_Y + μ_X μ_Y = E[XY] − μ_X μ_Y. ✓

Reading the sign

What the Sign of Covariance Tells You

  • Cov(X, Y) > 0 — on average, when X is above its mean, Y is also above. Positive linear association.
  • Cov(X, Y) < 0 — on average, when X is above its mean, Y is below. Negative linear association.
  • Cov(X, Y) = 0 — no linear association on average. Important: this doesn't mean independent! It only means no linear pull.
Critical gotcha: Zero covariance does not imply independence. Two variables can have Cov = 0 yet still be perfectly related through a nonlinear pattern (we demonstrate this with an interactive demo below).
A complete worked computation

Computing Covariance Step by Step

Given paired data: (X, Y) = (1, 2), (2, 4), (3, 5), (4, 7), (5, 8). Compute Cov(X, Y).

  • Step 1 — Means: μ_X = (1+2+3+4+5)/5 = 3. μ_Y = (2+4+5+7+8)/5 = 5.2.
  • Step 2 — Deviations from means:
    • (X − μ_X): −2, −1, 0, 1, 2
    • (Y − μ_Y): −3.2, −1.2, −0.2, 1.8, 2.8
  • Step 3 — Products of deviations: 6.4, 1.2, 0, 1.8, 5.6
  • Step 4 — Average: Cov(X, Y) = (6.4 + 1.2 + 0 + 1.8 + 5.6) / 5 = 15.0 / 5 = 3.0.

Positive covariance → X and Y rise together. But is 3.0 a "big" number? That depends entirely on the scales of X and Y. This scale-dependence is what makes covariance hard to interpret in absolute terms — and motivates correlation.

⟁ Covariance — Algebraic Properties
Cov(X, X) = Var(X)
Cov(X, Y) = Cov(Y, X)
Cov(aX + b, cY + d) = ac · Cov(X, Y)
Var(X + Y) = Var(X) + Var(Y) + 2·Cov(X, Y)

Variance is self-covariance. Covariance is symmetric in its arguments. Linear scaling pulls constants outside (and adding constants has no effect — shifts don't change joint motion). The fourth equation is the crucial generalization of Var(X+Y) = Var(X) + Var(Y) from Part 1 — that simple form only holds when Cov(X, Y) = 0.

Why scale dependence is a problem

The Achilles Heel of Covariance

Suppose Cov(X, Y) = 3.0. Is that a "strong" relationship? You cannot tell. The number 3.0 means something very different depending on the scales:

  • If X is in millimeters and Y is in kilograms, Cov is in mm·kg — odd units.
  • If you re-measure X in meters (divide by 1000), the covariance drops by 1000× — to 0.003. Same data, same relationship, very different number.
  • Cov(X, Y) values from different datasets cannot be directly compared.

We need a measure that captures the same information as covariance, but is independent of scale. Enter correlation.

3
Pearson Correlation
The fix to covariance's scale problem

Standardizing the Relationship

Pearson correlation (denoted ρ, rho) is covariance divided by the product of standard deviations. This division removes the units entirely — what remains is a pure, scale-free number between −1 and +1.

The key insight: By dividing by σ_X · σ_Y, we're measuring covariance in units of standard deviation. The result is unitless. A correlation of 0.7 means the same thing whether X is in meters, miles, or microseconds.
⟁ Pearson Correlation Coefficient
ρ(X, Y) = Cov(X, Y) / (σ_X · σ_Y)

Take the covariance and normalize it by the product of standard deviations. The result is always in the range [−1, +1]. Often written as ρ_{XY}, Corr(X, Y), or simply r when computed from sample data.

Cov(X, Y) — captures direction & raw magnitude
σ_X = √Var(X) — std dev of X (units of X)
σ_Y = √Var(Y) — std dev of Y (units of Y)
ρ ∈ [−1, +1] — unitless, comparable across datasets
Interpreting the magnitude

The Correlation Scale

ρ = +1 — Perfect Positive

  • All points lie exactly on a line with positive slope
  • Y is a perfect linear function of X
  • No scatter at all
  • Rare in real data

ρ ≈ +0.7 to +0.9 — Strong Positive

  • Clear upward trend with some scatter
  • Example: height & weight in adults
  • Example: education years & income

ρ ≈ ±0.3 to ±0.5 — Moderate

  • Trend visible but lots of noise
  • Example: stress level & sleep quality
  • Useful but not predictive on its own

ρ ≈ 0 — None

  • No linear pattern
  • Either truly independent or nonlinear relationship
  • Watch out for misleading nonlinear cases

ρ ≈ −0.7 to −0.9 — Strong Negative

  • Clear downward trend with some scatter
  • Example: car age & resale price
  • Example: exercise & resting heart rate

ρ = −1 — Perfect Negative

  • All points on a line with negative slope
  • Y is a perfect inverse linear function of X
  • Also rare in real data

⟁ Why ρ Lies in [−1, +1] — The Cauchy-Schwarz Bound
|Cov(X, Y)| ≤ σ_X · σ_Y

This is a deep result from linear algebra (the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality applied to random variables). Dividing both sides by σ_X · σ_Y gives |ρ| ≤ 1. Equality (ρ = ±1) holds if and only if Y is a perfect linear function of X.

This is why ρ = 1 means "perfectly linearly predictable." Anything less leaves residual scatter.

Independence vs. Uncorrelated
A subtle but critical distinction

The Two Concepts Are Not the Same

Many students collapse "uncorrelated" and "independent" into the same idea. They are not. The relationship is asymmetric:

Independence (Strong)

  • P(X, Y) = P(X) · P(Y) for all values
  • Knowing X tells you nothing about Y
  • The strongest form of "no relationship"
  • Implies uncorrelated (ρ = 0)

Uncorrelated (Weak)

  • Cov(X, Y) = 0, hence ρ = 0
  • No linear association
  • Does NOT imply independent
  • Curved or other patterns can survive
The implication chain: Independent ⟹ Uncorrelated, but Uncorrelated ⇏ Independent. The reverse implication fails because correlation is blind to nonlinear structure.
The famous counterexample

The Parabola — Zero Correlation, Total Dependence

Let X be a random variable symmetric around zero (say uniform on [−1, +1]). Let Y = X². Then:

  • X completely determines Y. Knowing X = 0.5 means Y must be 0.25 exactly. They are maximally dependent.
  • E[X] = 0 (symmetry). E[XY] = E[X·X²] = E[X³] = 0 (odd moment of a symmetric distribution).
  • Cov(X, Y) = E[XY] − E[X]E[Y] = 0 − 0 = 0.
  • Therefore ρ(X, Y) = 0 / (σ_X σ_Y) = 0.

So ρ = 0 even though Y is a deterministic function of X. The correlation coefficient simply cannot see the parabolic structure — it only looks for linear trends.

Practical warning: Always plot your data. Never trust ρ ≈ 0 alone to conclude "no relationship." Curved trends, periodic patterns, and threshold effects all produce ρ ≈ 0 while being highly informative.
⟁ Independence — Algebraic Consequences
If X ⊥ Y, then E[XY] = E[X] · E[Y]
If X ⊥ Y, then Cov(X, Y) = 0 ⟹ ρ(X, Y) = 0
If X ⊥ Y, then Var(X + Y) = Var(X) + Var(Y)

Independence has stronger algebraic consequences than just zero correlation. The expected product factors, the covariance vanishes, and the variance of sums adds cleanly. These are the formulas that power the central limit theorem, error analysis, and most of inferential statistics.

5
Correlation ≠ Causation
The most famous warning in statistics

What Correlation Cannot Tell You

A high correlation between X and Y is consistent with several very different underlying realities. Correlation alone cannot distinguish between them:

  • X causes Y — smoking → lung cancer rates
  • Y causes X — high temperature → AC unit sales (looks like AC → temp if you only see the correlation)
  • A third variable Z causes both — ice cream sales and drowning deaths both rise with summer temperature
  • Selection bias — measured only in a non-representative slice of data
  • Pure coincidence — random alignment in a small sample
Famous example: Number of pirates worldwide has been declining for two centuries. Global average temperature has been rising. ρ is strongly negative. Conclusion? Pirates don't cause global cooling. Both trends are driven by separate, unrelated causes.
When can we conclude causation?

What Establishing Causation Actually Requires

To move from correlation to causation, you need more than data — you need either:

  • Randomized controlled experiments — assign X randomly so it cannot depend on hidden factors. The gold standard.
  • Quasi-experimental designs — natural experiments where assignment to X is effectively random (e.g., draft lotteries, policy changes by birth date).
  • Instrumental variables — using an exogenous "instrument" Z that affects X but not Y directly (covered in Track 6, Module 6.5).
  • Causal graph analysis with strong assumptions — Pearl's do-calculus and DAGs (also Module 6.5).

Observational correlation, no matter how strong, does not get you there alone. This is the single most-violated rule in published claims about data.

Where correlation lives in practice

Real-World Uses of Correlation

📈 Finance — Portfolio Diversification

Two stocks with low/negative correlation reduce overall portfolio variance when combined. Modern portfolio theory is essentially correlation engineering.

🧬 Genomics — GWAS

Genome-wide association studies look for correlations between genetic variants and traits/diseases — generating hypotheses for follow-up causal experiments.

🤖 ML — Feature Selection

Highly correlated input features cause multicollinearity, inflating coefficient variance in regression. Removing redundant features is correlation-driven.

🌐 Signal Processing

Autocorrelation (a variable correlated with its own past) is the basis for radar, sonar, and time-series prediction (Track 6, Module 6.4).

🏥 Epidemiology

Initial associations between exposures and outcomes are detected as correlations — then causal inference methods refine them into actionable claims.

📊 A/B Testing

Random assignment of users to variants converts a correlation question into a causal question. The randomization is what licenses the causal interpretation.

Common Mistakes
Mistake 1 — "Cov = 0 means independent." No. Cov = 0 means no linear relationship. Variables can be perfectly nonlinearly dependent (Y = X²) and still have zero covariance.
Mistake 2 — Comparing covariances across datasets. Covariance is unitful and scale-dependent. Cov = 5 in one dataset and Cov = 50 in another tells you nothing about which has the stronger relationship. Convert to correlation first.
Mistake 3 — Confusing ρ with R². Correlation ρ ranges from −1 to +1. R² (coefficient of determination in regression) is ρ² and ranges from 0 to 1. R² represents the proportion of variance explained.
Mistake 4 — "ρ = 0.5 is twice as strong as ρ = 0.25." No. The squared values are more meaningful for "shared variance." ρ = 0.5 → R² = 0.25 (25% of variance explained). ρ = 0.25 → R² = 0.0625 (only 6.25%). The ratio is 4×, not 2×.
Mistake 5 — Computing correlation from outliers-contaminated data. Pearson ρ is sensitive to outliers. A single bad point can flip the sign. For robust analysis, use Spearman rank correlation or robust estimators.
Mistake 6 — Treating ρ as "causal strength." The single biggest sin. ρ measures linear association, not causation. Even ρ = 0.99 in observational data cannot tell you whether X → Y, Y → X, or Z → both.
Mistake 7 — Adding variances of correlated variables. Var(X + Y) = Var(X) + Var(Y) only when Cov(X, Y) = 0. Otherwise add 2·Cov(X, Y). This is critical for portfolio risk and error propagation.
