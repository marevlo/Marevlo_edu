Topic 3
Least Squares Intuition
What it is

What is the Least Squares Rule?

In Part 1 we said the best-fit line "sits as close as possible to all the dots." Least squares is the precise rule that makes this idea exact. It is a recipe in three steps:

  • Step 1: For every data point, take the residual (the gap between the dot and the line).
  • Step 2: Square each residual — multiply it by itself.
  • Step 3: Add up all those squared residuals. The total is one number that scores the line.

This total is called the Sum of Squared Errors, written SSE. Smaller SSE = better line. The least squares line is whichever line makes SSE as small as it can possibly be — no other line scores lower on the same data.

Simple meaning: Least squares is the rule that says — out of every line we could draw, the best one is the line that gives the smallest total of squared misses. Find that line, and you've found the least-squares fit.

The SSE formula

SSE = Σ (yᵢ − ŷᵢ)² = Σ eᵢ²
  • Σ means "add up all of them" — sum over every data point i from 1 to n.
  • yᵢ is the actual y for point i.
  • ŷᵢ is what the line predicts at point i.
  • eᵢ = yᵢ − ŷᵢ is the residual we met in Part 1.
Why we need it

Why Squaring? Why Not Just Add the Residuals?

This is a fair question. We could simply add residuals together — why complicate things by squaring? Three solid reasons:

1. Stops cancellation

  • Positive residuals and negative residuals cancel out
  • A terrible line could add to zero and look perfect
  • Squaring makes every miss count positively

2. Penalises big misses

  • A residual of 10 contributes 100 to SSE
  • A residual of 1 contributes only 1
  • The line works hard to avoid large mistakes

3. Has a clean solution

  • The squared sum is a smooth curve in parameter space
  • Calculus gives one exact answer
  • No iterative search needed
Big idea: Squaring solves three problems at once — it removes the sign, weights big errors more, and gives us a smooth function we can solve exactly. That's why least squares became the standard method, and why it still drives most of statistics and machine learning today.
Why it's used everywhere

The Reach of Least Squares

Once you understand least squares, you understand the engine behind a surprisingly large chunk of data science:

  • Many classical methods - including regression, ANOVA, and related inference tools — are closely connected to least-squares ideas.
  • Machine learning: the "Mean Squared Error" (MSE) loss used to train countless models — including the output of neural networks for regression tasks — is just SSE divided by n.
  • Signal processing: noise reduction, curve fitting, and Kalman filters all use the least-squares principle.
  • GPS & navigation: your phone's location is computed by least-squares fitting against satellite signals.
  • Physics & engineering: fitting models to measurements of physical systems.
How it works

The Error Landscape — A Visual Story

Here is the geometric picture. We are choosing two numbers: β̂₀ (intercept) and β̂₁ (slope). Each pair of numbers gives a different line, and each line has a different SSE. So SSE is really a function of those two numbers.

If we plotted SSE in 3D, with β̂₀ on one floor axis and β̂₁ on the other floor axis, SSE would rise upward. The resulting shape would look like a smooth bowl that opens upward. Somewhere at the bottom of that bowl is one lowest point. That point — the unique pair (β̂₀, β̂₁) where SSE is smallest — is the least-squares solution.

This bowl shape is important. It means:

  • For ordinary linear regression with valid data, there is one least-squares solution.
  • You can find it by many optimisation methods work by moving toward lower error values
  • Once you reach the bottom, no small change in either coefficient can make SSE smaller.

⟁ The Closed-Form Solution

For one predictor, the best β̂₀ and β̂₁ can be written directly from the data — no iteration needed:

β̂₁ = Σ (xᵢ − x̄)(yᵢ − ȳ) / Σ (xᵢ − x̄)²
β̂₀ = ȳ − β̂₁ × x̄

What every symbol means:

x̄ — the mean (average) of all x values
ȳ — the mean of all y values
xᵢ − x̄ — how far point i's x is from the mean of x
yᵢ − ȳ — how far point i's y is from the mean of y
Σ — add up across all data points
β̂₁, β̂₀ — the resulting best slope and intercept

How to read the slope formula in plain words: for each point, multiply "how much x is above its mean" by "how much y is above its mean." Add these products across all points (top). Then divide by the total of the x-deviations squared (bottom). The top measures how x and y move together; the bottom rescales into x-units.

How to read the intercept formula: once you know the slope, the intercept is forced by one fact — the fitted line must pass through the centre of the data, the point (x̄, ȳ). Plug x = x̄ into ŷ = β̂₀ + β̂₁ x̄ and set it equal to ȳ. Solving gives β̂₀ = ȳ − β̂₁ x̄.

Where this comes from: Take SSE as a function of β̂₀ and β̂₁. Use calculus to find where it's smallest — take the partial derivative with respect to each, set both to zero. The two resulting equations (called the normal equations) solve to exactly the two formulas above.

The lesson: Two short formulas turn raw data into a complete prediction equation. This is exactly what every regression library does behind the scenes when you call lm() or LinearRegression().fit().
When to use it

When Least Squares Works Well — and When It Doesn't

✔ Works well when

  • The relationship between x and y is roughly straight
  • The noise in the data is symmetric around the line
  • There are no extreme outliers pulling the line
  • All x values are not identical (otherwise the denominator is zero)

✘ Struggles when

  • The data is curved — a straight line will systematically miss
  • A few extreme points dominate (squaring amplifies their pull)
  • The output is categorical (yes/no) — use logistic regression instead
  • You need predictions outside the observed range of x
Outlier alert: A single bad data point can move the least-squares line a lot, because its squared residual is huge. Always check for outliers before trusting the fit. Robust alternatives (like least absolute deviations) exist for noisy data.
Real-life examples

Where Least Squares Powers Everyday Tech

📡 GPS Positioning

Your phone receives signals from multiple satellites with small errors. GPS often uses least-squares methods to estimate location from multiple satellite signals.

🎯 ML Training Loss

The MSE (Mean Squared Error) loss in neural networks is the average squared residual. Many regression models use mean squared error, which is closely related to least squares.

🌐 A/B Testing

Least squares can be used to model how user metrics change across experiments or features. Least squares gives the cleanest estimate of how much each change moves the metric.

🏗️ Engineering Calibration

Bridges, buildings and aircraft are checked by fitting measured strain against load. Least squares estimates the structural response curve.

💹 Asset Pricing

The "beta" of a stock — how strongly it follows the market — is the least-squares slope of stock returns against market returns. That's the CAPM model.

🔬 Lab Curve Fitting

Scientists fit lines through experimental measurements to estimate reaction rates, dose-response curves, decay constants — all least squares.

Topic 4
Correlation vs Regression
What it is

Two Cousins, Two Different Questions

Correlation and regression are closely related but answer different questions about the same data.

Correlation (r)

  • One single number, between −1 and +1
  • Tells you strength and direction of a linear relationship
  • r near +1 → strong positive (move together)
  • r near −1 → strong negative (move opposite)
  • r near 0 → no straight-line pattern
  • Has no units — pure shape information
  • Swapping x and y does not change r

Regression slope (β̂₁)

  • Has real units (y per unit of x)
  • Tells you how much y changes per 1-unit change in x
  • Can be any real number, not just between −1 and +1
  • Swapping x and y gives a different number
  • Used for prediction, not just description
The short version: Correlation answers "how tightly do the dots hug a line?" Regression answers "what is that line?" — and the slope of that line is β̂₁.

The Pearson correlation formula

r = Σ(xᵢ − x̄)(yᵢ − ȳ) / √[ Σ(xᵢ − x̄)² × Σ(yᵢ − ȳ)² ]
  • Top: same "joint movement" term we saw in the slope formula.
  • Bottom: a square-root combination of the spread of x and the spread of y.
  • The denominator forces the answer to lie between −1 and +1.
Why we need both

Why Strength and Magnitude Are Not the Same Thing

This is the most common confusion in applied statistics. Strength (how tight the cloud is) and magnitude (how steep the line is) can disagree in surprising ways:

  • You can have strong correlation (r ≈ 0.95) but a tiny slope, if y barely varies. Example: body temperature plotted against age — the dots may hug a line tightly, but the line is almost flat.
  • You can have modest correlation (r ≈ 0.4) but a huge slope, if y varies wildly. The cloud is loose, but its lean is steep and real.
The danger of using only one: Reporting only r tells the reader nothing about the practical size of the effect. Reporting only β̂₁ tells nothing about how reliable the line is. Real analyses report both.
How they connect

The Bridge Formula

Correlation and the regression slope are not independent — they are connected by one clean equation:

β̂₁ = r × (s_y / s_x)
  • r — Pearson correlation
  • s_y — standard deviation of y (how spread out y is)
  • s_x — standard deviation of x (how spread out x is)
  • β̂₁ — regression slope (the one we computed with least squares)

In words: the slope equals the correlation, scaled by the ratio of the spreads. The correlation gives the "shape" (strength and direction); the spread ratio puts it into real units.

What this tells us

  • If y varies far more than x (s_y/s_x is large), even a moderate correlation produces a big slope.
  • If y barely varies (s_y/s_x is small), even a near-perfect correlation produces a tiny slope.
  • This is why we always need both numbers to understand a relationship.

R² — the third related number

If you square the correlation, you get a number between 0 and 1 called R² (the coefficient of determination):

R² = r²

It has a clean meaning — the fraction of variation in y that the line explains. R² = 0.8 means the line accounts for 80% of the up-and-down variation in y; the remaining 20% is left in the residuals. R² is the most common number reported when summarising a regression. We will study it more carefully in Module 5.6.

⟁ Worked Example

Example: Same Data, Two Numbers

Returning to our café data:

  • Slope β̂₁ ≈ 3.68 cups per °C (from Topic 3)

Now let's also compute the correlation. From the table:

  • Σ(x − x̄)(y − ȳ) = 250
  • Σ(x − x̄)² = 68
  • Σ(y − ȳ)² = (−17)² + (−12)² + (−2)² + 13² + 18² = 289 + 144 + 4 + 169 + 324 = 930

Plug into the Pearson formula:

r = 250 / √(68 × 930) = 250 / √63240 ≈ 250 / 251.5 ≈ 0.994

And R²:

R² = r² ≈ 0.988

Reading both numbers:

  • r ≈ 0.99 — temperature and cold-coffee sales are very tightly linked. The dots hug a line almost perfectly.
  • β̂₁ ≈ 3.68 cups per °C — every extra degree adds about 3.68 cups of expected sales.
  • R² ≈ 0.99 — the fitted line explains roughly 99% of the day-to-day variation in cups sold. Only 1% is "left over" in residuals.

Bridge check

Let's verify the bridge formula:

  • s_x = √(68/5) ≈ 3.69 °C
  • s_y = √(930/5) ≈ 13.64 cups
  • r × (s_y / s_x) = 0.994 × (13.64 / 3.69) ≈ 0.994 × 3.70 ≈ 3.68 ✓
The lesson: r tells the café that temperature is a powerful predictor of cold-coffee sales. β̂₁ tells them the practical rule: "for each degree warmer, prep about 4 more cups." Both numbers together give the full picture.
When to use which

A Short Decision Guide

  1. Summarise a relationship in one number? Use correlation r. It is comparable across studies and free of units.
  2. Predict y from x? Use the regression equation. Correlation alone cannot produce predicted values in the original units.
  3. Quantify the effect size in real units? Use the regression slope β̂₁. The correlation cannot answer this.
  4. Compare two datasets on different scales? Use correlation. The slope depends on units, so direct comparison is risky.
  5. Report quality of fit? Use R² (= r² in simple linear regression). It tells the reader what fraction of variation the line captures.
A famous warning

Correlation Is Not Causation

This deserves its own paragraph. A high correlation between x and y — or a steep regression slope — does not prove that x causes y. Both variables could be driven by a third unseen factor.

Classic example: ice-cream sales and drowning deaths are positively correlated. Ice cream does not cause drowning. Both rise in summer — when people swim more, and when they eat more ice cream. Heat is the hidden cause of both.

Regression measures association, not causation. To establish causation you need experimental design (randomised trials) or careful causal inference methods. We'll cover this in Module 6.6.

Real-life examples

Where Both Numbers Show Up Together

📊 Research Papers

Scientific papers almost always report both r (or R²) and the regression slope. r tells the reader how reliable the relationship is; the slope tells them how big the effect is.

📉 Stock Market Beta

"Beta" of a stock = the regression slope of its returns against the market. Correlation tells investors how closely the stock follows the market overall.

🧬 Genetics & Heritability

Researchers compute correlations of traits between parents and children. The slope translates this into a heritability estimate in real units.

🔍 Feature Screening in ML

Data scientists compute correlations of every feature with the target. Features with strong correlation get selected. Then a regression model fits the actual prediction equation.

🏃 Sports Performance

Coaches compute correlation between training metrics and game performance. The slope tells them how much each extra training session translates into real-game gains.

🌡️ Climate Science

Correlation of CO₂ levels with global temperature tells the strength of the link. The regression slope quantifies the actual temperature rise per ppm of CO₂.
