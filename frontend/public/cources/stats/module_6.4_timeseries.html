Time Series & Forecasting

Most real-world data does not arrive all at once. It arrives in order, one observation per moment — sales per day, sensor readings per second, temperatures per hour. Time series methods turn that ordered stream into structure, and structure into forecasts.

This module covers five core ideas: trend and seasonality (the predictable patterns), stationarity (the property most forecasting models need), autocorrelation (how past values relate to present ones), ARIMA intuition (the classical workhorse of forecasting), and forecast evaluation (how you know your prediction is any good). These are the foundations every data scientist working with time-stamped data needs.

Topic 1
Trend & Seasonality
What it is

The Two Most Visible Patterns in a Series

Look at almost any real time series — monthly sales, daily temperatures, weekly website visits — and two things usually jump out before any modelling begins:

  • Trend — the long-term direction. Is the series drifting up, down, or staying flat over months and years?
  • Seasonality — repeating cycles tied to a calendar. Ice cream sales spike every summer. Email volume drops every weekend. These are seasonal.

Whatever is left after trend and seasonality is called the residual or noise — the random part of the series that isn't explained by the predictable patterns.

One-line definition: A time series can usually be broken into three parts — trend (long-term direction), seasonality (calendar-driven repetition), and residual (everything else).
Additive vs Multiplicative Decomposition
Additive:   Yₜ = Tₜ + Sₜ + Rₜ
Multiplicative:   Yₜ = Tₜ × Sₜ × Rₜ
The observed value at time t is the sum (or product) of trend, seasonal component, and residual. Use additive when seasonal swings are about the same size over time. Use multiplicative when seasonal swings grow with the level of the series.
Yₜ — observed value at time t
Tₜ — trend component (slow drift)
Sₜ — seasonal component (cyclical pattern)
Rₜ — residual (random noise)
Why we care

Why Decomposition Matters Before Forecasting

1. Reveals What Drives the Series

  • Is the series mostly trending or mostly seasonal?
  • How big are the seasonal swings relative to the trend?
  • How noisy is the underlying signal?

2. Guides Model Choice

  • Strong seasonality → use a seasonal model (SARIMA, Prophet)
  • Steady trend → linear or exponential growth model
  • Pure noise → simple baseline is enough

3. Helps Spot Anomalies

  • Once trend and seasonality are removed, what's left should look random
  • Big residuals = unusual events worth investigating
  • Useful for fraud detection, equipment failures, demand spikes
⟁ Real-Life Example

Ride-Hailing Demand

A ride-hailing company looks at hourly ride requests in a city. Three layers are clear in the data:

  • ▸ Trend: requests have grown roughly 3% per month as the city expands
  • ▸ Daily seasonality: peaks at 8 AM and 6 PM commutes
  • ▸ Weekly seasonality: Friday and Saturday nights are bigger than weekday nights

Once these patterns are subtracted out, the residual contains the interesting stuff — sudden weather effects, concerts, sports events. A forecaster who ignores the seasonal layer would consistently under-predict Friday nights and over-predict early Sunday mornings.

Topic 2
Stationarity
What it is

When the Statistical Behaviour Doesn't Change Over Time

A time series is stationary if its statistical properties — mean, variance, and autocorrelation — stay the same no matter which slice of time you look at. The series wiggles, but it doesn't drift, doesn't grow, doesn't lose or gain volatility.

Most real series are not stationary in their raw form. A stock price drifts up over years. Daily temperatures swing seasonally. Most classical forecasting models, including ARIMA, require stationarity to work properly. So the first job is usually to transform the series until it becomes stationary.

One-line definition: A series is stationary when its mean and variance do not depend on time, and the correlation between two observations depends only on the gap between them, not on where in the series they are.
First Differencing
ΔYₜ = Yₜ − Yₜ₋₁
The most common technique for removing trend: take the difference between consecutive observations. If the original series drifts up steadily, its differences hover around a constant — often zero. Differencing once is usually enough; differencing twice handles series that accelerate.
Yₜ — original series at time t
Yₜ₋₁ — value one step earlier
ΔYₜ — first difference (the change)
Δ²Yₜ — second difference (the change in the change)
Why models need it

Why Most Forecasting Models Assume Stationarity

If the statistical properties keep changing, the "rules" of the series keep changing too. A model fit on the past would not apply to the future. Stationarity is the assumption that the same rules hold over time — only then can we learn the rules from history and extrapolate.

  • Mean stability — the average level stays constant. No trend.
  • Variance stability — the size of fluctuations is roughly the same throughout.
  • Autocorrelation stability — the relationship between Yₜ and Yₜ₋ₖ depends on k, not on t.
Common pitfall: fitting ARIMA to a clearly trending series without differencing first. The model will produce forecasts that look plausible short-term but drift badly over longer horizons. Always make the series stationary first.
How to use it

The Practical Workflow

  1. Plot the series. Look for visible trend or seasonality. Stationarity rules them out.
  2. Try differencing. Compute Yₜ − Yₜ₋₁ and plot it. Often this removes the trend.
  3. Try seasonal differencing. If a yearly cycle dominates, compute Yₜ − Yₜ₋₁₂ (for monthly data).
  4. Stabilise variance. If swings grow with the level, take logs first: log(Yₜ).
  5. Test stationarity formally. The Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) test gives a yes/no answer.
⟁ Real-Life Example

From Stock Price to Returns

Apple's daily closing price has drifted upward for decades — clearly not stationary. But take the daily percent change (which is a kind of differencing on log-prices) and you get a series that hovers around zero with roughly constant variance. The transformed series — daily returns — is much closer to stationary, and is the input to most financial forecasting models.

Topic 3
Autocorrelation
What it is

How Past Values Relate to Present Ones

In an ordinary dataset, observations are unrelated to each other. In a time series, neighbouring observations usually are related — today's temperature is similar to yesterday's; this month's sales are similar to last month's. The strength of that relationship is called autocorrelation.

Autocorrelation at lag k measures how strongly Yₜ is related to Yₜ₋ₖ. Lag 1 is the relationship between today and yesterday. Lag 12 (for monthly data) is the relationship between this month and the same month last year.

One-line definition: Autocorrelation at lag k is the correlation between the series and a copy of itself shifted by k time steps.
Autocorrelation Function (ACF)
ρ(k) = Cov( Yₜ, Yₜ₋ₖ ) / Var( Yₜ )
The autocorrelation at lag k is the covariance between Yₜ and Yₜ₋ₖ, divided by the variance of the series. It is a number between −1 and +1. Plotting ρ(k) against k gives the ACF plot — one of the most useful diagnostics in time series analysis.
ρ(k) — autocorrelation at lag k
k — the lag (1, 2, 3, …)
Cov — covariance between Yₜ and its shifted version
ρ(0) = 1 — perfect correlation with itself at lag 0
Why it matters

What the ACF Plot Tells You

Slow Decay

  • ACF drops off gradually over many lags
  • Signals trend or non-stationarity
  • Often: differencing will fix it

Fast Decay

  • ACF drops to ≈ 0 within a few lags
  • Signals a stationary series with short memory
  • Good candidate for ARMA models

Spikes at Regular Lags

  • Large ACF at lag 12 (monthly data) → yearly seasonality
  • Large ACF at lag 7 (daily data) → weekly cycle
  • Tells you the period of repetition
⟁ Real-Life Example

Daily Temperature ACF

For daily temperatures in a city, the autocorrelation looks like this:

  • ▸ Lag 1: ~0.95 — today is very similar to yesterday
  • ▸ Lag 7: ~0.6 — last week's same day is still informative
  • ▸ Lag 180: ~−0.8 — six months ago was the opposite season (winter ↔ summer)
  • ▸ Lag 365: ~0.85 — same day last year is back to similar weather

The ACF plot would show a clear sinusoidal pattern with period 365 — that's the seasonality jumping out.

Topic 4
ARIMA Intuition
What it is

The Classical Workhorse of Forecasting

ARIMA stands for AutoRegressive Integrated Moving Average. It is the most widely used classical forecasting model, and it combines three simple ideas into one framework. Specifying an ARIMA model means picking three integers: (p, d, q).

  • AR(p) — AutoRegressive: predict the next value from the last p values.
  • I(d) — Integrated: difference the series d times to make it stationary first.
  • MA(q) — Moving Average: include the last q forecast errors as predictors.
One-line definition: ARIMA(p, d, q) forecasts by combining recent observations (AR), recent forecast errors (MA), and differencing (I) to handle non-stationarity.
ARIMA(1, 1, 1) — The Simplest Useful Case
ΔYₜ = φ · ΔYₜ₋₁ + θ · εₜ₋₁ + εₜ
d=1: work with differences ΔYₜ = Yₜ − Yₜ₋₁ (handle trend by differencing once).
p=1: today's change depends on yesterday's change (the AR part, weight φ).
q=1: today's change depends on yesterday's forecast error (the MA part, weight θ).
εₜ is fresh random noise. Bigger models use more lags on each side.
ΔYₜ — first difference of the series
φ — autoregressive coefficient (memory of past changes)
θ — moving-average coefficient (memory of past errors)
εₜ — random shock at time t
How to use it

Picking p, d, and q

  1. Choose d. Difference the series until it looks stationary. Usually d = 0, 1, or 2.
  2. Plot the ACF and PACF of the differenced series. Their shapes guide p and q.
  3. Pick small p and q. Common choices: (0, 1, 0), (1, 1, 0), (0, 1, 1), (1, 1, 1). Bigger is rarely better.
  4. Fit the model. Libraries like statsmodels, pmdarima, or auto.arima will estimate coefficients and report fit quality.
  5. Diagnose residuals. If model residuals look like random noise, the model is doing its job.
⟁ Real-Life Example

Monthly Airline Passengers

The classic Box-Jenkins example: monthly airline passenger counts from the 1950s. The raw series has a clear upward trend and strong yearly seasonality.

  • ▸ Take logs to stabilise the growing variance
  • ▸ Apply first difference (d=1) to remove the trend
  • ▸ Apply seasonal difference at lag 12 to remove the yearly cycle
  • ▸ Fit a SARIMA(0,1,1)(0,1,1)₁₂ — gives accurate forecasts

This exact approach is still used today, often as a strong baseline before reaching for neural models.

Where it is used

ARIMA in the Real World

Demand Forecasting

Retailers use ARIMA and its seasonal cousin SARIMA to forecast weekly product demand, feeding inventory and staffing decisions.

Macroeconomics

Central banks rely on ARIMA-style models to forecast GDP, inflation, and unemployment for monetary policy decisions.

Energy Load

Utility companies forecast electricity demand hour by hour using ARIMA + weather covariates to plan generation.

Cloud Capacity

Cloud providers forecast server load to scale capacity up or down. Classical ARIMA still beats many fancier models here.

Topic 5
Forecasting Evaluation Basics
What it is

How Do You Know a Forecast Is Any Good?

Building a forecast is half the job. Knowing whether it's any good is the other half — and the easier one to get wrong. The standard practice is to hold out the most recent slice of the series, train the model on the older part, forecast the held-out part, and compare predictions to actual values.

Three common error metrics summarise how close the forecasts are to reality.

One-line definition: Forecast evaluation compares predicted values against actual values on a held-out portion of the data and summarises the gap with an error metric.
Three Standard Error Metrics
MAE = (1/n) · Σ |Yₜ − Ŷₜ|
RMSE = √[ (1/n) · Σ (Yₜ − Ŷₜ)² ]
MAPE = (100/n) · Σ |Yₜ − Ŷₜ| / |Yₜ|
MAE (Mean Absolute Error) — the average size of the mistake, in the same units as the data.
RMSE (Root Mean Squared Error) — like MAE but punishes big errors more heavily.
MAPE (Mean Absolute Percentage Error) — expresses error as a percentage of the actual value, useful for comparing across scales.
Yₜ — actual value at time t
Ŷₜ — forecasted value at time t
n — number of forecast points
|…| — absolute value (ignore sign)
How to use it

The Evaluation Workflow

  1. Split the series chronologically. Older 80% = training set; recent 20% = test set. Never shuffle randomly — that leaks future into the past.
  2. Train the model on the training set only.
  3. Produce forecasts for the test period.
  4. Compare predictions to actuals. Plot them together. Eyeball first, then compute metrics.
  5. Compare to a baseline. "Yesterday equals today" or "same as last week" are surprisingly hard baselines to beat. If your fancy model can't beat them, the model isn't earning its complexity.
Common pitfall: training on the whole series including the test period leaks future information and gives unrealistically good numbers. Always keep the test set untouched until the very end.
⟁ Real-Life Example

Evaluating Energy Demand Forecasts

A utility company trains an ARIMA model on three years of hourly electricity data, then forecasts the next month. The actual values come in day by day, and the team computes daily MAE and MAPE.

If the average MAPE is 2%, that means the forecast is typically off by 2% of the actual demand — good enough for planning. If MAPE jumps to 8% during heatwaves, the team knows the model isn't capturing extreme temperature effects and needs to add weather as a covariate.
