Bell Curve Intuition
The most famous shape in statistics

What Does a Bell Curve Look Like?

Imagine you measure the heights of 1,000 random adult men. You'd find:

  • A few very short ones (around 160 cm)
  • A few very tall ones (around 200 cm)
  • But most people clustered around 175 cm — the average

If you plotted those heights on a graph, you'd see a bell-shaped curve — high in the middle, low on both sides. That's the Normal distribution.

Simple idea: The Normal distribution says, "Most things are average. Some are slightly above or below average. Very few are extreme."
Why "Normal"?

The Name Tells You the Story

It's called "Normal" because this shape shows up so often in nature that it became the norm. When statisticians first noticed it appearing in heights, weights, exam scores, measurement errors, and countless other places, they called it the "Normal" distribution.

Other names you'll see in books — all the same thing:

  • Gaussian distribution (named after mathematician Carl Friedrich Gauss, who studied it deeply)
  • Bell curve (because of its shape)
  • Normal curve
Two numbers control everything

The Two Parameters: μ and σ

A Normal distribution is fully described by just two numbers:

μ (mu) — The Mean

  • The center of the bell
  • The "average" value
  • Where the peak sits
  • Most common single value
  • Slides the curve left/right

σ (sigma) — The Standard Deviation

  • How spread out the bell is
  • Big σ → wide, short bell
  • Small σ → tall, narrow bell
  • Controls "typical distance from mean"
  • Squeezes or stretches the curve

We write a Normal distribution as N(μ, σ²) — where σ² is the variance. So N(175, 49) means a Normal distribution with mean 175 and variance 49 (which means standard deviation 7, since σ = √49 = 7).

The 4 key shape properties

What Every Bell Curve Always Looks Like

No matter the values of μ and σ, every Normal distribution has these four features:

  • Symmetric — the left half is a mirror image of the right half
  • Single peak (unimodal) — exactly one hump in the middle
  • Mean = Median = Mode — all three "averages" land at the center μ
  • Tails approach but never touch zero — extreme values are very rare but always possible
Practical takeaway: If a real-world dataset is roughly symmetric, has one peak, and the mean is close to the median, the Normal distribution is probably a good model for it.
⟁ The Normal Distribution Formula (For Reference)
f(x) = (1 / (σ√(2π))) · e−(x − μ)² / (2σ²)

Don't panic at this formula! You almost never need to use it directly — we use tables, software, and the standardization trick (in Part 2) instead. But here's what it says, in plain English: "The probability density at point x depends on how far x is from the mean μ, scaled by σ."

μ — center / mean of the distribution
σ — standard deviation (spread)
(x − μ)² — squared distance from center
e−... — exponential decay (makes tails fade)
1/(σ√(2π)) — normalization (makes total area = 1)

The Standard Normal Distribution
A special, universal bell curve

What is the "Standard" Normal?

There are infinitely many Normal distributions — one for every choice of μ and σ. To make things simple, statisticians picked one special version as the reference:

The Standard Normal Distribution is the Normal distribution with mean μ = 0 and standard deviation σ = 1. Written as N(0, 1) or just Z.

Think of it as the master key. If you can compute probabilities for the standard normal, you can compute probabilities for ANY normal distribution — using a simple trick called standardization (we cover it in Part 2).

Properties of the standard normal

The N(0, 1) Cheat Sheet

Center

  • Mean = 0
  • Peak sits at x = 0
  • The "neutral" point

Spread

  • Standard deviation = 1
  • Variance = 1
  • One unit = "one σ"

Symbol

  • Random variable: Z
  • "Z ∼ N(0, 1)"
  • Values are called Z-scores

Shape

  • Bell-shaped, symmetric
  • Tails extend to ±∞
  • Peak height ≈ 0.399
Why does it matter?

The Power of N(0, 1)

Imagine three friends comparing their scores on different tests:

  • Alex scored 80 on a math test (class average 70, σ = 5)
  • Beth scored 85 on a physics test (class average 80, σ = 10)
  • Cal scored 92 on a biology test (class average 90, σ = 4)

Who did relatively best? You can't tell just by looking at raw scores — they're on different scales. But by converting each score to a Z-score (using N(0, 1) as the reference), you can compare them fairly. We'll solve this exact puzzle in the next section.

The big insight: The standard normal gives us a universal scale. Every normal distribution can be translated into the same standard reference frame — and then directly compared.

Z-Scores — Measuring Distance From the Mean
The most useful number in basic statistics

What is a Z-Score?

A Z-score tells you how many standard deviations a value is away from the mean. That's it. It's a measure of "how unusual" a value is.

  • Z = 0 → exactly at the mean (perfectly average)
  • Z = +1 → one standard deviation above the mean
  • Z = −1 → one standard deviation below the mean
  • Z = +2 → two standard deviations above (pretty unusual!)
  • Z = −3 → three standard deviations below (very rare)
In plain English: "I scored 2 standard deviations above the mean" means much more than "I scored 80." Z-scores tell you where you stand, not just what number you got.
⟁ The Z-Score Formula
Z = (X − μ) / σ

Step 1: Subtract the mean from your value (X − μ). This tells you how far you are from the average — in raw units.

Step 2: Divide by the standard deviation σ. This rescales the distance so one unit equals one σ — a universal measure.

Result: a unit-free number telling you how many σ's above or below the mean you are.

X — your raw value
μ — the mean of the distribution
σ — the standard deviation
Z — the Z-score (signed, unit-free)
Worked Example — comparing test scores

Who Did Best — Alex, Beth, or Cal?

Remember our three friends? Let's compute their Z-scores:

  • Alex: Z = (80 − 70) / 5 = 10 / 5 = +2.00 → 2 σ above mean
  • Beth: Z = (85 − 80) / 10 = 5 / 10 = +0.50 → half a σ above mean
  • Cal: Z = (92 − 90) / 4 = 2 / 4 = +0.50 → half a σ above mean

Alex did relatively best! Even though Cal had the highest raw score (92), Alex's performance was 2 standard deviations above her class — far more impressive in context. Beth and Cal both did the same relative to their classmates.

Z-scores level the playing field. They strip away units and scale, leaving only one question: "how far from average?"

Z-Score quick reference

How "Unusual" is Each Z-Score?

A simple feel for the Z-score scale:

|Z| < 0.5 — Typical

  • Close to the mean
  • About 38% of values
  • "Pretty average"

|Z| in [0.5, 1] — Common

  • Within 1 σ of mean
  • About 68% live in ±1σ
  • "Not surprising"

|Z| in [1, 2] — Uncommon

  • Between 1σ and 2σ
  • Noticeable but possible
  • "A bit unusual"

|Z| ≥ 2 — Unusual / Rare

  • About 5% of values beyond ±2σ
  • Beyond ±3σ → only 0.3%
  • "Worth investigating"
