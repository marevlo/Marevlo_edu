Gamma Distribution

The natural extension of Exponential — when you don't wait for one event but for several. Gamma is the universal waiting-time distribution, and its shape morphs from sharp decay to a smooth bell as you wait for more events.

Part 2 starts with a simple but profound question: what if you need to wait for the k-th arrival, not just the first? The answer is the Gamma distribution. We meet the famous Gamma function Γ(k) — the continuous extension of the factorial — see how Gamma generalizes both Exponential (k = 1) and the Erlang distribution (integer k), and explore the deep links to Poisson processes, Chi-Squared distributions, and Bayesian inference. The shape parameter k controls everything: small k looks like exponential decay; large k starts to look like a bell.

1
From Exponential to Gamma
A simple question, a bigger family

What If You Wait for More Than One Event?

In Part 1, Exponential answered: "How long until the next event?" One single event. That was clean and useful — but real life often asks something deeper.

Suppose you're running a customer support line. You don't just care about the next call — you want to know:

  • How long until the 3rd call comes in?
  • How long until the 10th failure occurs in this hardware test?
  • How long until the 5th patient with a rare condition arrives at this hospital?

Each of these is a waiting-time question — but for the k-th arrival, not the first. The distribution that answers them is the Gamma distribution.

The big idea: If event inter-arrival times are independent Exponential(λ), then the total time to see k events is the sum of k Exponentials. That sum follows the Gamma distribution. Gamma is "stacked Exponentials."
Why this matters

Why a Single Exponential Is Not Enough

Exponential is wonderfully simple — but its memoryless property and single-event focus make it too narrow for many tasks. Consider three problems:

Problem 1 — Aggregated Wait

  • You wait for 4 trucks to deliver supplies
  • Each truck arrives independently
  • Total wait ≠ Exponential
  • It IS Gamma with k = 4

Problem 2 — Service Total Time

  • A service has multiple stages
  • Each stage takes random time
  • Total = sum of times
  • Often modeled as Gamma

Problem 3 — Aging Components

  • Real devices fail with rising rate
  • Exponential's memoryless can't capture this
  • Gamma allows non-constant hazard rate
  • k controls how "aged" the failure shape is

Gamma fixes all three. It is the natural step up from Exponential — adding one more degree of freedom that controls shape.

2
The Gamma Function Γ(k)
A small detour, big rewards

What Is the Gamma Function?

Before we can write the Gamma distribution's PDF, we need one piece of math: the Gamma function Γ(k). Don't worry — it sounds scary but the idea is simple.

You already know the factorial: 5! = 5 × 4 × 3 × 2 × 1 = 120. But factorial only works for positive integers. What's 2.5! ? The plain definition doesn't say.

The Gamma function fills the gap. It's the continuous extension of the factorial — defined for all positive real numbers (and most complex ones too).

One key property to remember: for any positive integer n, Γ(n) = (n − 1)! That is — Γ(5) = 4! = 24. The function "shifts" the factorial by one position. Beyond that, Γ(2.5), Γ(0.7), and so on are all defined too — by an integral.
⟁ The Gamma Function
Γ(k) = ∫₀^∞ tk−1 · e−t dt   for k > 0

An integral that produces a single number for each k. For integer values, it equals (k − 1)! For non-integer values, it gives a smooth interpolation. You don't need to compute it by hand — every scientific calculator and library knows Γ(k).

Γ(1) = 0! = 1
Γ(2) = 1! = 1
Γ(3) = 2! = 2
Γ(4) = 3! = 6
Γ(0.5) = √π ≈ 1.7725
Γ(k+1) = k · Γ(k) — recursion property

Why we need it: the Gamma distribution's density formula has Γ(k) in the denominator. It is the normalizing constant that makes the total area equal 1. We can't write the PDF without it.

3
The Gamma Distribution
Definition

What Gamma Models

The Gamma distribution, written Gamma(k, λ), describes the waiting time until the k-th event in a Poisson process with rate λ.

It has two parameters:

  • k — the shape parameter. Controls how many events we are waiting for, or more generally, how "spread out vs peaked" the distribution is. Larger k → more bell-shaped.
  • λ — the rate parameter (events per unit time). Same role as in Exponential.

When k = 1, Gamma collapses back to Exponential. When k = 2, 3, 4, …, Gamma is sometimes called the Erlang distribution (named after Danish telecom engineer Agner Krarup Erlang, who used it for telephone traffic). When k is non-integer (say k = 2.5), it is still well-defined — that's the whole point of using the Gamma function.

Mental model: Gamma is "the sum of k independent Exponential(λ) waits." Roll k Exponential dice; add up the times; you get a Gamma sample.
⟁ Gamma PDF
f(x) = [λk / Γ(k)] · xk−1 · e−λx   for x ≥ 0

The PDF looks complex but each piece is doing a clear job. Let's break it down:

xk−1 — pulls the density up for small x as k grows. For k = 1, this is 1 (constant). For k = 3, it grows like x².
e−λx — the same exponential decay as before. Controls how fast probability dies off for large x.
λk / Γ(k) — the normalizing constant. Makes the area under the curve exactly 1.
k > 0, λ > 0 — both parameters must be positive.

Shape intuition: the xk−1 term pulls the curve up for small x (more events take longer to all arrive), while e−λx pulls it down for large x. The competition between these two creates the famous Gamma peak — somewhere in the middle. As k grows, the peak shifts right and the shape smooths out.

⟁ Gamma Mean & Variance
E[X] = k / λ
Var(X) = k / λ²
σ = √k / λ

Mean intuition: if each Exponential wait has mean 1/λ, then waiting for k of them takes on average k × (1/λ) = k/λ. Pure linearity of expectation from Module 2.5.

Variance intuition: the variance of a single Exponential is 1/λ². Sum of k independent Exponentials has variance k × (1/λ²) = k/λ². Variances add for independent variables — Module 2.5 Part 2.

Special case: when k = 1, mean = 1/λ and variance = 1/λ² — exactly Exponential. The whole framework is self-consistent. Gamma extends Exponential without contradicting it.

How to Use the Gamma Distribution
A practical workflow

Step-by-Step: Picking and Using Gamma

  1. 01 Identify the question. Are you waiting for the k-th event, or for a total of k random durations to add up? Both are Gamma.
  2. 02 Estimate the rate λ. Use historical data: if N events occurred in time T, then λ ≈ N/T.
  3. 03 Set k. If you want the k-th event, k is your target number. If fitting Gamma to data with rising hazard rate, fit k from the sample (method of moments or maximum likelihood — covered in inference modules).
  4. 04 Compute probabilities. Use libraries: scipy.stats.gamma.cdf(x, k, scale=1/λ) or equivalent. No closed-form CDF for general k.
  5. 05 Sanity-check with mean and variance. E[X] = k/λ should match your real-world expectation. If the data spreads much more or less, Gamma may not be the right model.
Important note about parameterizations: Some references use Gamma(k, θ) where θ = 1/λ is called the scale. In that form, PDF = xk−1·e−x/θ / [θk·Γ(k)] and mean = kθ. Always check whether your library expects "rate" or "scale" — mixing them is a frequent bug. SciPy uses scale by default; R's dgamma accepts both.

Where Gamma Lives in the Real World
Real applications

Eight Places You'll Meet Gamma

📞 Call Center — k-th Call Wait

If calls arrive at λ = 6/hour, waiting time for the 3rd call has Gamma(3, 6). Used to plan agent scheduling and predict batch arrivals.

☔ Rainfall Totals

Monthly rainfall amounts are often modeled as Gamma. The shape k captures how variable the season is; λ controls the average intensity.

🔧 Component Aging

For devices with rising failure rates (most real-world products), Gamma with k > 1 fits well. Reliability engineers use it for warranty modeling.

💰 Insurance Claim Sizes

Aggregated claim totals (not single claims) are commonly modeled as Gamma. Actuaries use it for portfolio risk and premium pricing.

🤖 Bayesian Priors on Rates

Gamma is the conjugate prior for the rate parameter of a Poisson or Exponential likelihood. After observing data, the posterior is also Gamma — a clean, closed-form update.

📡 Queueing Service Times

In multi-stage service systems (e.g., 3-step manufacturing line), total service time is Gamma. The basis of advanced queueing theory beyond M/M/1.

🧪 Biology — Tumor Growth Times

Time for a tumor to reach a target size, given multiple sub-stages of growth, is often Gamma-distributed. Used in oncology biostatistics.

📊 Chi-Squared = Gamma in Disguise

The Chi-Squared distribution with d degrees of freedom is exactly Gamma(d/2, 1/2). Foundation of hypothesis testing — covered in inference modules.

Common Mistakes
Mistake 1 — Confusing rate and scale parameterizations. Gamma(k, λ) with rate has mean k/λ. Gamma(k, θ) with scale has mean kθ. Always check which form your library or textbook uses before plugging in numbers.
Mistake 2 — Using Gamma with k < 1 carelessly. When k < 1, the PDF actually diverges at x = 0 (goes to infinity). The total area is still finite (= 1), but it looks alarming. This is mathematically valid but signals a peculiar use case — usually not "waiting for k events" but a special model.
Mistake 3 — Assuming Gamma is memoryless. Only Exponential (Gamma with k = 1) is memoryless. For k ≠ 1, past waiting time gives you information about the future. Don't accidentally apply the memoryless rule to general Gamma.
Mistake 4 — Trying to write the CDF by hand. Gamma's CDF doesn't have an elementary closed form for general k. You need either the "incomplete gamma function" or a numerical library. Don't waste time integrating by hand — let scipy.stats.gamma.cdf do the work.
Mistake 5 — Forgetting Gamma's relationship to other distributions. Gamma(1, λ) = Exponential(λ). Gamma(n, λ) = Erlang for integer n. Gamma(d/2, 1/2) = Chi-Squared with d degrees of freedom. Knowing these connections lets you reuse Gamma machinery in many seemingly unrelated problems.
Mistake 6 — Misreading the shape parameter k. k is not a probability and not bounded by 1. It can be any positive real number: 0.5, 1, 2.7, 100. Large k just means a more concentrated, bell-like shape.
