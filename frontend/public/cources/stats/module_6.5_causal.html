Causal Inference Fundamentals

Correlation is what data shows. Causation is what data wants to mean. Causal inference is the discipline that gets you from one to the other — carefully, transparently, and without lying to yourself.

This module covers six core ideas: DAGs (the visual language of cause), confounding and the backdoor criterion (the main reason naive analysis goes wrong), counterfactual intuition (the "what would have happened" thinking), the potential outcomes framework (the formal structure), propensity score matching (a practical tool when randomisation is impossible), and instrumental variables (one of causal inference's most-used industry techniques). This is the missing piece in most data-science curricula.

Topic 1
DAGs — The Language of Cause
What it is

Drawing What You Think Causes What

A DAG — Directed Acyclic Graph — is a diagram with one node per variable and an arrow from A to B if you believe A is a direct cause of B. "Directed" means arrows have a direction. "Acyclic" means you can't follow arrows in a loop and return to where you started.

DAGs are not statistical objects — they encode assumptions about how the world works. Two researchers analysing the same dataset can draw different DAGs because they have different beliefs about causation. Causal inference forces those beliefs out into the open.

One-line definition: A DAG is a picture of causal beliefs — nodes are variables, arrows go from causes to effects, and no loops are allowed.
Why we need it

Three Basic Structures Everything Is Built From

Every DAG, no matter how complex, is built from three elementary three-variable patterns. Understanding these is most of what's needed for causal reasoning.

1. Chain   X → M → Y

  • X causes M, which causes Y
  • M is a mediator
  • Conditioning on M blocks the X→Y effect
  • Example: smoking → tar → cancer

2. Fork   X ← Z → Y

  • Z causes both X and Y
  • Z is a confounder
  • Makes X and Y look related when they aren't directly
  • Example: hot weather → ice cream sales & drownings

3. Collider   X → C ← Y

  • X and Y both cause C
  • C is a collider
  • Conditioning on a collider creates a fake link between X and Y
  • Example: skill + luck → award; given award, skill and luck appear anti-correlated
The shocking part of causal inference: "controlling for more variables" is not always a good idea. Conditioning on a collider creates bias rather than removing it. The DAG tells you which variables to adjust for and which to leave alone.
⟁ Real-Life Example

Ice Cream and Drowning

Cities with high ice cream sales also have more drownings. Naively, eating ice cream looks dangerous. But the real story is a fork: hot weather (Z) causes both ice cream sales (X) and swimming (which causes drownings, Y).

Hot weather is a confounder. Ignore it and you get a strong but meaningless correlation. Adjust for it (compare ice cream sales and drownings within days of the same temperature) and the apparent effect vanishes.

Topic 2
Confounding & the Backdoor Criterion
What it is

The Most Common Cause of Wrong Conclusions

Confounding happens when a third variable secretly causes both your suspected cause and your outcome, creating a misleading correlation. It is the single most common reason a real-world analysis reaches the wrong conclusion.

The backdoor criterion (Judea Pearl, 1995) is a precise rule for identifying which variables you must adjust for in order to recover the true causal effect of X on Y.

The backdoor criterion in one line: Adjust for a set Z of variables that blocks every "backdoor path" from X to Y — paths that go from X back through some cause and then forward into Y — while not including any descendant of X.
The Backdoor Adjustment Formula
P( Y | do(X = x) ) = Σz P( Y | X = x, Z = z ) · P( Z = z )
The do-operator means "set X to x by intervention, not by observation." If Z is a valid backdoor adjustment set, the right-hand side — which uses only observational data — gives the true causal quantity on the left. This is the foundation of "controlling for" variables.
do(X = x) — intervene to force X = x
Z — the backdoor adjustment set
P(Y | X, Z) — conditional probability from data
Σ_z … P(Z=z) — average over Z to get the true effect
How to use it

Practical Approach

  1. Draw the DAG. Write out what you believe causes what, including unobserved variables if relevant.
  2. Find all backdoor paths from X to Y — paths that start with an arrow into X and connect to Y.
  3. Identify a set Z that blocks every backdoor path. Often a single confounder, sometimes several.
  4. Check Z does not include descendants of X. Adjusting for downstream variables (mediators or colliders) can introduce new bias.
  5. Adjust for Z using stratification, regression, or matching (Topic 5).
Common pitfall: "throwing everything into the regression" sounds safer but can be worse. Including a collider or mediator creates bias that wasn't there before. The DAG tells you which variables to use and which to leave alone.
⟁ Real-Life Example

Does Coffee Cause Heart Disease?

Suppose observational studies show coffee drinkers have higher rates of heart disease. Is coffee causing it? Maybe not — smoking is a common confounder (smokers tend to drink more coffee and have higher heart disease risk).

The DAG: Smoking → Coffee and Smoking → Heart Disease. To find coffee's true causal effect, you must adjust for smoking. Studies that did this found the apparent coffee–heart disease link mostly disappears. That correction is what the backdoor criterion makes formal.

Topic 3
Counterfactual Intuition
What it is

The "What If" Question at the Heart of Causality

The cleanest way to define a causal effect is by comparing what did happen with what would have happened under a different choice. That alternative reality is a counterfactual.

Example: a patient was given drug A and recovered in 7 days. The counterfactual question is: how long would the same patient have taken to recover if they had been given a placebo instead? The difference between these two outcomes — actual minus counterfactual — is the causal effect of the drug for this patient.

One-line definition: A counterfactual is what would have happened under a different intervention than the one actually applied. Causal effects are differences between actual and counterfactual outcomes.
Why it matters

The Fundamental Problem of Causal Inference

For any individual person, we observe exactly one outcome — either treated or untreated, not both. The counterfactual is unobserved by definition. This is called the fundamental problem of causal inference and it's why all causal methods are, at heart, ways of constructing plausible substitutes for the missing counterfactual.

Solution 1: Randomisation

  • Randomly assign treatment
  • Treated and untreated groups are similar on average
  • The untreated group's outcome stands in for the treated group's counterfactual
  • This is what RCTs (randomised controlled trials) do

Solution 2: Adjustment

  • When you can't randomise, you adjust
  • Find observed differences between treated and untreated and statistically correct for them
  • Backdoor criterion + propensity matching + IV are all flavours of this

Solution 3: Quasi-experiments

  • Exploit natural variation that mimics randomisation
  • Policy rollouts, lotteries, regression discontinuities
  • Whenever assignment is "as good as random" within a subpopulation
⟁ Real-Life Example

"Did the Email Campaign Work?"

A marketing team sends an email blast to 50,000 customers and observes that 5% bought the product within a week. Did the email cause those sales?

You cannot tell from this alone. The counterfactual question is: how many of those same customers would have bought without the email? Maybe 4%. Maybe 5%. Maybe 6%. Without a control group (a comparable set who did not receive the email), the counterfactual is invisible — and so is the true effect.

Topic 4
Potential Outcomes Framework
What it is

The Formal Language of Counterfactuals

The potential outcomes framework (Neyman, 1923; Rubin, 1974) gives counterfactuals a precise mathematical home. For each unit (person, store, country, …) we imagine two potential outcomes:

  • Y(1) — the outcome that would occur if this unit were treated
  • Y(0) — the outcome that would occur if this unit were not treated

The individual treatment effect is simply Y(1) − Y(0). But we only ever observe one of these for each unit — never both. So causal inference is fundamentally about estimating averages of (Y(1) − Y(0)) across populations where you can only see one side at a time.

Average Treatment Effect (ATE)
ATE = E[ Y(1) − Y(0) ] = E[ Y(1) ] − E[ Y(0) ]
The average treatment effect is the expected difference between the treated-world outcome and the untreated-world outcome, averaged across the whole population. Under randomisation, ATE simplifies to the observed mean outcome in the treated group minus the observed mean in the control group.
Y(1) — potential outcome if treated
Y(0) — potential outcome if not treated
ATE — population-average causal effect
ATT — average effect on the treated (a related quantity)
When does it work

The Identification Assumptions

To estimate ATE from data, three classical assumptions are needed:

  1. SUTVA (Stable Unit Treatment Value Assumption). One unit's treatment doesn't affect another unit's outcome. No "interference" between units.
  2. Ignorability / Unconfoundedness. Given the observed covariates, treatment assignment is as good as random. This is the key assumption — and it's untestable from data alone. You need a DAG to argue for it.
  3. Positivity / Overlap. Every unit has a non-zero probability of being treated and untreated. If a subgroup is always treated, you can't estimate their counterfactual.
Why this framework is powerful: it gives you a precise vocabulary. Instead of waving your hands at "controlling for" things, you can write down exactly which counterfactual you're estimating, which assumptions make that possible, and which assumptions you'd need to verify in practice.
Topic 5
Propensity Score Matching
What it is

Building Comparable Treated and Untreated Groups

When randomisation is impossible — most of the time in practice — treated and untreated groups usually differ in dozens of observable ways. Adjusting for all of them through regression is messy. Propensity score matching (Rosenbaum & Rubin, 1983) collapses this into one number per unit.

The propensity score e(X) is the probability that a unit gets treated, given its observed covariates X. Once you have it, you match each treated unit to an untreated unit with a similar score, building two groups that are balanced on the observed factors. Compare outcomes across the matched pairs — that's your estimate of the treatment effect.

One-line definition: The propensity score is the probability of receiving treatment given observed covariates. Matching on propensity scores creates comparable groups out of observational data.
The Propensity Score
e(X) = P( Treatment = 1 | X )
Estimated by fitting a logistic regression (or any classifier) with treatment as the target and covariates X as predictors. Each unit gets a score between 0 and 1 — its estimated probability of being treated. Two units with similar e(X) are similar overall on the observed dimensions, even if individual covariates differ.
e(X) — propensity score for a unit with covariates X
X — observed pre-treatment covariates
Treatment = 1 — unit received treatment
Treatment = 0 — unit did not
How to use it

The Matching Workflow

  1. List your observed covariates — everything you think might affect treatment and outcome.
  2. Fit a model (typically logistic regression) predicting treatment from covariates. The predicted probability is the propensity score.
  3. Match treated units to untreated with similar scores — nearest-neighbour matching is most common.
  4. Check balance. Within the matched sample, treated and untreated should now have similar covariate distributions.
  5. Compute the average outcome difference across matched pairs. That's the estimated treatment effect on the treated.
Key limitation: propensity matching only balances observed covariates. If a confounder is unmeasured, matching cannot fix the bias. The DAG should make these gaps explicit.
⟁ Real-Life Example

Did the Training Program Improve Earnings?

A government wants to evaluate a job-training program. Workers were not randomly selected — those who joined were typically younger, less skilled, and more motivated.

Researchers compute a propensity score for each worker based on age, prior earnings, education, and previous unemployment. Each trained worker is then matched to a non-trained worker with a similar score. Comparing post-program earnings within matched pairs gives a much fairer estimate of the program's effect than simply comparing all trained vs all untrained — which would be confounded by who self-selected into training.

Topic 6
Instrumental Variables (Intuition)
What it is

Borrowing Randomness From the World

What if a confounder exists that you cannot measure? Propensity matching fails. Regression fails. The backdoor criterion can't help because you can't condition on what you didn't observe.

An instrumental variable (IV) is a way out. An instrument Z is something that:

  1. Affects the treatment X (relevance)
  2. Has no direct effect on the outcome Y except through X (exclusion)

If you can find such a Z, variation in Z gives you a slice of "as-if-random" variation in X — separate from the confounder. The causal effect of X on Y can then be backed out from how Z, X, and Y move together.

One-line definition: An instrument is a variable that nudges treatment without directly touching the outcome. It supplies a fragment of randomisation that lets you escape unmeasured confounding.
The IV Estimator (Wald form)
Effect of X on Y ≈ Cov(Z, Y) / Cov(Z, X)
Intuition: the instrument Z influences Y only through X. So the part of Y that moves with Z must come from the part of X that moves with Z. Dividing the Z–Y movement by the Z–X movement isolates the true effect of X on Y — even when an unobserved confounder is distorting the direct X–Y correlation.
Z — the instrument
X — treatment (possibly confounded)
Y — outcome
Cov — covariance between two variables
When to use it

Real Instruments in Real Research

Distance to College

To estimate the effect of college education on earnings: distance from home to the nearest college affects whether someone enrols (relevance) but is unlikely to affect their wages otherwise (exclusion).

Vietnam Draft Lottery

To estimate the effect of military service on later earnings: the random draft number affected service likelihood but was unrelated to ability. This is one of the most famous IVs in economics.

Random Encouragement

To estimate the effect of a new feature on user engagement: randomly send some users an email encouraging them to try the feature. The email is the instrument; usage is the treatment.

Rainfall as Income Shock

In agricultural economies: rainfall variation drives farm income (relevance) but is otherwise random. Used to study effects of income on health, schooling, conflict.

The hardest part: finding a valid instrument. The exclusion restriction is untestable from data alone — it relies on domain knowledge and careful argument. A weak or invalid instrument produces biased estimates, sometimes worse than ignoring the problem.
⟁ Real-Life Example

Does Going to Hospital Help You?

Naive comparison: patients who visit hospitals have worse health outcomes than those who don't. Does that mean hospitals harm you? Obviously not — sicker people go to hospital, so the comparison is hopelessly confounded.

One classic IV approach: distance to the nearest hospital. Distance affects whether you go (relevance) but presumably has no direct effect on your health outcome conditional on getting care or not (exclusion). Using distance as an instrument, researchers have estimated genuinely causal effects of hospital care on patient outcomes — typically positive, despite the misleading naive correlation.
