Logistic Regression & Binary Outcomes

What if the thing we're predicting is not a number, but a yes-or-no answer? Will this email be spam? Will this loan default? Will this patient survive? Linear regression can't help here. Logistic regression is what we need.

Part 1 of Module 5.3 covers the first two ideas that make logistic regression work. First, binary classification intuition — what makes yes/no outcomes special, why linear regression fails on them, and the cleaner framing of "predicting a probability." Second, odds and log-odds — the two number scales that turn a bounded probability into something a linear model can comfortably handle. By the end of Part 1 you'll know what binary classification is, why probabilities behave like a sliding scale, and how the log-odds transformation makes the math click. Part 2 then introduces the sigmoid function and decision boundaries.

Topic 1
Binary Classification Intuition
What it is

What is Binary Classification?

In Modules 5.1 and 5.2 our response variable y was always a continuous number — exam marks, house prices, sales. But many real questions are not about numbers. They're about yes or no:

  • Is this email spam — yes or no?
  • Will this customer buy the product — yes or no?
  • Does this patient have the disease — yes or no?
  • Will this student pass the exam — yes or no?

The response y takes only two possible values, typically coded as 0 (no, negative class) and 1 (yes, positive class). The task is to predict which class a new observation belongs to, based on one or more predictor variables. This task is called binary classification.

Simple meaning: Binary classification is the job of looking at the features of something and deciding which of two categories it belongs to. The output is not a number — it's a label (or, more usefully, a probability of being in the positive class).

The shift from "number" to "probability"

A good binary classifier doesn't just shout "yes" or "no." It returns a probability — a number between 0 and 1 that says how confident the model is that this case belongs to class 1.

  • P(y=1 | x) = 0.95 — very confident it's a yes
  • P(y=1 | x) = 0.55 — leaning toward yes but unsure
  • P(y=1 | x) = 0.20 — probably a no
  • P(y=1 | x) = 0.02 — almost certainly a no

This probability is the real output. The yes/no label comes later by applying a threshold (most often 0.5).

Why we need it

Why Linear Regression Fails on Yes/No Data

You might wonder — can't we just code y as 0/1 and run linear regression? Technically yes, but the results are wrong in three concrete ways:

1. Predictions escape [0, 1]

  • A straight line keeps rising forever
  • For large x, the line predicts ŷ = 1.4 or 2.3
  • A "probability" of 1.4 has no meaning
  • For small x, the line predicts ŷ = −0.3 — negative probabilities

2. Residuals are not symmetric

  • y can only be 0 or 1, not in between
  • Residuals are forced into specific shapes
  • The LINE assumptions (Module 5.2) are violated
  • Standard errors and p-values become unreliable

3. The linear shape is wrong

  • The relationship between predictor and P(y=1) is typically S-shaped
  • A straight line can't capture how probability flattens at the edges
  • The model under-fits at extremes and over-fits in the middle
Big idea: Linear regression assumes y is a continuous number on an open range. Probabilities are bounded between 0 and 1 and behave non-linearly near the boundaries. We need a model whose output naturally lives in [0, 1] — that model is logistic regression.
Why it's used in practice

Two Things Binary Classifiers Have to Do Well

🎯 Rank the cases

  • Sort all cases by P(y=1), highest first
  • Critical for fraud detection — focus attention on top scores
  • Critical for ad targeting — show ad to highest-probability buyers

⚖️ Decide on a single case

  • Pick a probability threshold (often 0.5)
  • Above threshold → predict class 1; below → class 0
  • Threshold is tuned based on real-world cost of mistakes

A good binary classifier produces well-calibrated probabilities. "Well-calibrated" means that, among all cases the model rates around 0.7, roughly 70% really do belong to class 1. Logistic regression is the simplest model that produces sensible calibrated probabilities, which is why it remains a baseline in machine learning even decades after fancier methods were invented.

How it works

The Binary Classification Workflow

  1. Define your two classes clearly. What is "yes" and what is "no"? In medical tests, "yes" might be "disease present." In banking, "yes" is "loan defaulted." Be explicit.
  2. Collect labelled data. Every row has predictor values (x₁, x₂, …) AND a known class label (0 or 1).
  3. Choose a classifier model. Logistic regression is our focus, but other options (decision trees, SVMs, neural networks) follow the same idea.
  4. Train the model. The model learns to produce a probability P(y=1 | x) that fits the data well.
  5. Pick a threshold. Decide the cut-off where you flip a prediction from "no" to "yes." Default is 0.5, but real applications often tune it.
  6. Evaluate. Check accuracy, but also precision, recall, and the confusion matrix — measures we'll meet later. These tell you what kinds of mistakes the classifier makes.

⟁ Probability Output of a Classifier
P(y = 1 | x) = some function of x that lies in [0, 1]

What each symbol means:

P(y = 1 | x) — probability that y is 1 given the predictor value x
y — the actual class label, either 0 or 1
x — predictor variable(s) for one observation
| — read as "given" or "conditional on"

Why this form? The model's job is to output a number between 0 and 1 for every input. That number is interpreted as "how confident are we that this case is a yes?" Two cases with different x values get different probabilities — the model has learned how the predictors influence the chance of class 1.

Why this is useful: A single output P(y=1|x) is more informative than just a yes/no label. It tells you the model's certainty. A doctor with P(disease|symptoms) = 0.95 acts very differently from one with P = 0.55, even though both might cross a 0.5 threshold and trigger a "positive" diagnosis.

⟁ Worked Example

Example: Email Spam Filtering

An email service builds a binary classifier to flag spam. Every email is labelled 1 (spam) or 0 (not spam). The model learns from features like:

  • Number of all-caps words
  • Number of links in the body
  • Whether the sender is in the user's contact list
  • Frequency of certain trigger phrases

For three incoming emails, the trained classifier returns:

EmailP(spam | features)Threshold (0.5)Action
From mom, no links, 0 caps0.03BelowInbox ✅
Newsletter, 5 links, calm tone0.42BelowInbox ✅
"FREE PRIZE!!! Click NOW", 8 links0.97AboveSpam folder 🚫

Key insight: the classifier doesn't just label — it ranks. The newsletter at P = 0.42 is "borderline." The user can train the system further by manually marking it as spam or not, which adjusts the model.

The lesson: Binary classifiers produce probabilities, which are then converted to labels by threshold. The probability itself is what you should look at when calibrating real systems — labels alone hide the model's uncertainty.
When to use it

When Binary Classification Is the Right Tool

  • The outcome is naturally two-valued — yes/no, pass/fail, click/no-click, churn/stay.
  • You want a probability in addition to a label.
  • You can tune the threshold based on the relative cost of false positives vs false negatives.
  • The predictors are reasonably numeric or one-hot encoded.
  • The classes are not extremely imbalanced (e.g., 99.99% no, 0.01% yes — needs special handling).
When binary classification doesn't fit: if the outcome has more than two categories (cat / dog / bird), you need multi-class classification. If the outcome has natural order (low / medium / high), you need ordinal regression. If you need to predict a continuous number, go back to linear regression.
Real-life examples

Where Binary Classification Powers Daily Decisions

📧 Spam Filtering

Gmail, Outlook, and Yahoo all use binary classifiers (often based on logistic regression as a baseline) to flag billions of emails per day.

🏦 Loan Default Prediction

Banks predict P(default | applicant features) and use the probability — not just a label — to decide loan terms, interest rate, and collateral.

💊 Medical Diagnosis

Cancer screening tools output P(malignant | imaging features). Doctors use the probability to decide whether to recommend a biopsy.

📱 Click-Through Prediction

Online ad systems estimate P(click | user + ad). This drives auction bids in real time — higher predicted CTR = higher bid.

👥 Customer Churn

Telecom companies predict P(customer leaves next month | usage features) to target retention offers at the highest-risk users.

🚗 Fraud Detection

Credit card networks score every transaction with P(fraud). Above a threshold triggers a security check; below it the transaction passes silently.

Topic 2
Odds & Log-Odds
What it is

What Are Odds?

You already know probability. Odds is a different way of expressing the same information — a way that gamblers, doctors, and statisticians have used for centuries.

Odds is the ratio of "how likely yes" to "how likely no":

odds = P(y = 1) / P(y = 0) = p / (1 − p)

Three quick examples:

  • p = 0.5 (50/50 coin flip) → odds = 0.5 / 0.5 = 1 (often called "1 to 1" or "even odds")
  • p = 0.75 → odds = 0.75 / 0.25 = 3 ("3 to 1 in favour")
  • p = 0.10 → odds = 0.10 / 0.90 ≈ 0.11 ("about 1 to 9 against")
Simple meaning: Odds = how many times more likely "yes" is than "no." A probability of 0.75 says "yes happens 75% of the time"; odds of 3 say "yes happens 3 times for every 1 time no happens." Same information, different framing.

The range of odds

  • When p is near 0 → odds are tiny (close to 0)
  • When p = 0.5 → odds = 1
  • When p is near 1 → odds shoot to infinity

Odds live on the range (0, ∞) — always positive, never negative. That's a step toward what a linear model can output, but not all the way there.

Why we use odds

Why Switch From Probability to Odds?

Probabilities are bounded between 0 and 1, which makes them awkward for linear models. Odds expand that range to (0, ∞), which is better — but still not enough for a model that wants to output any real number.

The deeper reason to use odds is interpretation. In logistic regression, the coefficient β₁ has a clean meaning when expressed in odds: "for every 1-unit increase in x, the odds of y = 1 multiply by e^β₁." This is the famous odds ratio, used in medical and social-science research everywhere.

Probability — bounded

  • Lives in [0, 1]
  • Hard to model with linear coefficients
  • "+10% probability" doesn't mean the same thing at p=0.5 vs p=0.95

Odds — half-bounded

  • Lives in (0, ∞)
  • Multiplicative effects make sense
  • "Odds doubled" is a clean statement at any starting point
What log-odds is

From Odds to Log-Odds (the "Logit")

Odds are still bounded on one side — they can never be negative. To unbox them completely, we take the natural logarithm of the odds. The result is called the log-odds or the logit:

logit(p) = ln(odds) = ln( p / (1 − p) )

The logit transformation has a beautiful property — it maps the bounded interval (0, 1) onto the entire real line (−∞, +∞):

  • p = 0.5 → logit = ln(1) = 0
  • p = 0.75 → logit = ln(3) ≈ +1.10
  • p = 0.10 → logit = ln(0.111) ≈ −2.20
  • p → 1 → logit → +∞
  • p → 0 → logit → −∞
Simple meaning: The logit takes a probability (locked in 0 to 1) and stretches it onto the full number line, where positive values mean "more likely yes" and negative values mean "more likely no." Now a linear model can naturally produce any logit value, and we can convert it back to a probability when needed.
Why we need log-odds

Why the Log Step Matters

This is the magic ingredient that makes logistic regression work. By predicting log-odds instead of probability directly, the model can be built as a familiar linear combination:

log-odds of y=1 = β₀ + β₁·x₁ + β₂·x₂ + … + βₚ·xₚ

The right-hand side is exactly the same linear function we've used in regression. The left-hand side is no longer probability — it's log-odds, which can take any value. The two ranges now match perfectly.

Three things log-odds gives us

  • Range match: linear combinations can be any real number, and so can log-odds
  • Clean interpretation: each βⱼ has a meaning in terms of odds ratios
  • Symmetric scale: +1 log-odd means same magnitude as −1 log-odd, just opposite direction

The coefficient interpretation

  • β₁ is the change in log-odds per 1-unit increase in x₁
  • e^β₁ is the multiplicative change in odds per 1-unit increase in x₁ (the "odds ratio")
  • e^β₁ = 2 means each extra unit of x₁ doubles the odds of y = 1
  • e^β₁ = 0.5 means each extra unit halves the odds

⟁ The Two Directions — Logit and Inverse Logit
logit(p) = ln( p / (1 − p) ) → probability to log-odds
p = 1 / (1 + e^(−z)) → log-odds (z) back to probability

What each symbol means:

p — probability of y = 1, between 0 and 1
1 − p — probability of y = 0
ln — natural logarithm (base e ≈ 2.718)
z — log-odds; any real number from −∞ to +∞
e^(−z) — e raised to negative z
1 / (1 + e^(−z)) — the famous sigmoid function

How to read both directions:

  • Forward: "Take p, divide by (1−p) to get odds, take its natural log to get the unbounded log-odds."
  • Inverse: "Take z, plug into 1/(1+e^(−z)) to convert back to a clean probability in [0, 1]."

Why both formulas matter: Logistic regression operates internally on the log-odds scale — that's where the model is linear and the math is clean. But humans want probabilities at the end. The inverse logit (= the sigmoid) handles the conversion. We'll meet the sigmoid as a topic of its own in Part 2.

⟁ Worked Example

Example: A Hiring Decision in Log-Odds Form

A company fits a logistic regression to predict whether candidates pass the technical interview. Suppose the fitted log-odds equation is:

log-odds(pass) = −2.5 + 0.6 · Years_Experience + 1.2 · Coding_Score

Where Coding_Score is on a 0-5 scale.

Candidate A: 3 years experience, coding score 4.

  • log-odds = −2.5 + 0.6 × 3 + 1.2 × 4 = −2.5 + 1.8 + 4.8 = +4.1
  • odds = e^4.1 ≈ 60.3 → about 60 times more likely to pass than fail
  • probability = 1 / (1 + e^(−4.1)) = 60.3 / 61.3 ≈ 0.984 → very confident pass

Candidate B: 1 year experience, coding score 2.

  • log-odds = −2.5 + 0.6 × 1 + 1.2 × 2 = −2.5 + 0.6 + 2.4 = +0.5
  • odds = e^0.5 ≈ 1.65 → slightly more likely to pass than fail
  • probability = 1 / (1 + e^(−0.5)) ≈ 0.622 → leaning pass but with uncertainty

Reading the coefficients:

  • β₁ = 0.6 for Experience → each extra year multiplies the odds of passing by e^0.6 ≈ 1.82. About 82% higher odds per year.
  • β₂ = 1.2 for Coding_Score → each extra coding point multiplies the odds by e^1.2 ≈ 3.32. More than triple the odds per point.
  • β₀ = −2.5 means at zero experience and zero coding score, log-odds = −2.5, so odds ≈ 0.08 and probability ≈ 0.075 (very low).
The lesson: Logistic regression keeps the familiar linear sum on the inside, but the output is a probability of yes. Each coefficient β tells you how strongly its predictor pulls toward "yes" on the log-odds scale — and e^β translates that into the odds ratio everyone uses to report results.
When odds matter most

Where Odds & Log-Odds Are Used Daily

  • Medical research papers nearly always report odds ratios — "smokers have 5× the odds of lung cancer" — derived from logistic regression coefficients.
  • Sports betting quotes odds directly (e.g., "+150 odds") — bookmakers' core language.
  • Risk modelling in insurance and banking uses log-odds because it linearises the relationship between predictors and the probability of an event.
  • Machine learning interpretability tools often summarise classifier behaviour using odds-ratio language — easier to communicate to non-technical stakeholders.
Real-life examples

How Odds Show Up Outside Statistics Class

🎲 Sports Betting

"5 to 1 odds" against a team means the bookmaker thinks the team wins about 1/6 of the time. Odds are the native language of betting markets.

🚬 Smoking & Cancer Studies

Landmark studies report "smokers have 25× the odds of lung cancer compared to non-smokers." That single number summarises a logistic regression coefficient.

🏥 Disease Risk Scores

Scores like the Framingham heart risk score use log-odds equations under the hood, then convert back to "10-year risk" probabilities for patients.

📨 Spam Filter Internals

Each spam feature adds or subtracts from a log-odds score. Final score is converted via sigmoid to a spam probability.

📊 A/B Test Reporting

"Variant B increased the odds of conversion by 1.4×" — a typical product analytics statement, computed via logistic regression.

💳 Credit Scoring

FICO and similar credit scores are mathematically equivalent to log-odds of default. Higher score = more negative log-odds of default.
