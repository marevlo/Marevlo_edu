Beyond Mean and Variance
When two summaries aren't enough

Same Mean, Same Variance, Different Worlds

By the end of Part 1, we knew mean and variance. By Part 2, we knew how pairs of variables move together. But there's still a gap. Consider two distributions:

Distribution A — Symmetric Bell

  • Mean = 0, Variance = 1
  • Symmetric around the mean
  • Thin tails — outliers very rare
  • Example: classical Gaussian

Distribution B — Heavy-Tailed

  • Mean = 0, Variance = 1
  • Symmetric around the mean
  • Heavy tails — extreme events common
  • Example: Student-t with low degrees of freedom

Both have the same mean and variance. Yet B's extreme outcomes (X > 5) might occur thousands of times more often than A's. Mean and variance miss the tail behavior entirely. A risk manager who only knew (μ, σ) would treat these two as identical — and underestimate B's catastrophic risk.

The gap: Mean tells us location. Variance tells us spread. But what about asymmetry? Tail weight? These require higher-order summaries — and that's exactly what moments give us.
A four-number fingerprint

What This Part Will Build

We will assemble the full shape fingerprint of a distribution — four numbers, each capturing one aspect:

  • 1st moment (mean μ) — where the distribution centers (covered in Part 1)
  • 2nd moment (variance σ²) — how spread out it is (Part 1)
  • 3rd standardized moment (skewness γ₁) — its asymmetry
  • 4th standardized moment (kurtosis γ₂) — how heavy its tails are

Then we'll meet a single object — the Moment Generating Function (MGF) — that packages all moments into one mathematical entity. This is the deeper structure beneath the entire framework.

2
Moments of a Distribution
The general definition

What Is a Moment?

A moment is an expected value of a power of X (or of a centered version of X). Moments come in two flavors:

  • Raw moments μ'_k = E[X^k] — taken about the origin (zero).
  • Central moments μ_k = E[(X − μ)^k] — taken about the mean.

Central moments are usually what we want, because they describe the shape around the center — independent of where the center happens to be.

⟁ The k-th Central Moment
μ_k = E[(X − μ)^k]

Take the deviation from the mean. Raise it to the k-th power. Take the expected value. The result is the k-th central moment. Different k values reveal different aspects of the distribution's shape.

μ_1 = 0 — first central moment always zero by definition
μ_2 = Var(X) — variance (the 2nd central moment)
μ_3 — captures asymmetry (skewness numerator)
μ_4 — captures tail weight (kurtosis numerator)
Why standardize

From Raw Moments to Standardized Ones

Just like covariance had a scale problem (fixed by dividing by σ_X · σ_Y to get correlation), the 3rd and 4th central moments have scale problems too. A μ_3 of 100 in one dataset and 1 in another tells us nothing about which is "more skewed" — it depends on the scales.

The fix: divide by σ raised to the appropriate power. This is called standardization, and it gives us scale-free shape measures.

⟁ Standardized Moments — The Scale-Free Forms
Skewness γ₁ = μ_3 / σ³ = E[(X − μ)³] / σ³
Kurtosis γ₂ = μ_4 / σ⁴ = E[(X − μ)⁴] / σ⁴

Skewness divides the 3rd central moment by σ³, making it dimensionless. Kurtosis divides the 4th by σ⁴. Both are pure, scale-free numbers — comparable across datasets just like correlation.

A frequently used alternative is excess kurtosis: γ₂ − 3. Subtracting 3 makes a normal distribution's kurtosis equal to zero — a convenient reference point.

3
Skewness — The Asymmetry Measure
What asymmetry looks like

Three Possible Asymmetry Patterns

Skewness γ₁ measures the asymmetry of a distribution. Its sign tells you which side has the long tail.

γ₁ > 0 — Right-Skewed (Positive Skew)

  • Long tail extends to the right
  • Most mass on the left, a few large values on the right
  • Mean > Median > Mode (typically)
  • Examples: income, wait times, insurance claims, file sizes

γ₁ ≈ 0 — Symmetric

  • Distribution is roughly mirror-image around the mean
  • Mean ≈ Median ≈ Mode
  • Examples: heights, test scores, measurement errors
  • The normal distribution has γ₁ = 0 exactly

γ₁ < 0 — Left-Skewed (Negative Skew)

  • Long tail extends to the left
  • Most mass on the right, a few small values on the left
  • Mean < Median < Mode (typically)
  • Examples: age at death (mostly old, a few young), exam scores with a low ceiling
Why the cube

Why Skewness Uses the Third Power

Why cube the deviations? Why not just take the absolute value or some other measure?

  • Cubing preserves sign. A deviation of +5 gives +125; a deviation of −5 gives −125. The two cancel symmetrically — exactly what we want for measuring asymmetry.
  • Cubing amplifies large deviations. Big outliers in one direction (say +20) contribute 8,000 to the sum, dominating small balanced deviations. So one big right tail can produce significant positive skewness.
  • It's the natural next moment. After variance (squaring), the next natural power is cubing. It's the simplest function that distinguishes left from right.
Mental model: Imagine standing on the balance point (mean) of a seesaw. Variance asks "how much wiggle is there overall?" Skewness asks "is the wiggle balanced left and right, or does it lean?"
⟁ Skewness — Rough Magnitudes
|γ₁| < 0.5 → nearly symmetric
0.5 ≤ |γ₁| < 1 → moderately skewed
|γ₁| ≥ 1 → highly skewed

These thresholds are rules of thumb — not formal rules. In finance, even γ₁ = 0.2 can be material for risk; in social sciences, γ₁ = 2 is common for income data. Always interpret in context.

A real-world example

Why Income Is the Textbook Right-Skewed Example

Consider income in any country. Most people earn moderate amounts; a few earn millions; the very richest earn billions. The right tail extends arbitrarily far, while the left is bounded near zero. Result: strongly right-skewed.

Practical consequence: the mean overstates the typical person. If 99 people earn ₹50,000 and one person earns ₹100 million, the mean income is ₹1,050,000 — but only 1 person actually has anywhere near that. The median (₹50,000) is far more representative.

Skewness warning: Whenever a distribution is heavily skewed, reporting only the mean is misleading. Always inspect the median, the standard deviation, and the skewness together. This is why median income (not mean) appears in policy reports.
4
Kurtosis — The Tail Risk Measure
What tail weight looks like

Skinny Tails vs. Fat Tails

Kurtosis γ₂ measures how heavy the tails of a distribution are — specifically, how frequently extreme deviations occur relative to typical ones.

For historical reasons, the reference point is the normal distribution, which has γ₂ = 3. By convention, we often report excess kurtosis = γ₂ − 3, so a normal distribution becomes the "zero" baseline.

Mesokurtic (γ₂ ≈ 3, excess ≈ 0)

  • Same tail weight as a normal distribution
  • Extreme events are rare but possible
  • Example: heights, IQs (approximately)

Leptokurtic (γ₂ > 3, excess > 0) — Fat Tails

  • Higher peak, heavier tails
  • Extreme events much more frequent
  • Example: stock returns, earthquakes, internet traffic
  • The "black swan" world

Platykurtic (γ₂ < 3, excess < 0) — Thin Tails

  • Flatter peak, lighter tails
  • Extreme events very rare
  • Example: uniform distribution (γ₂ = 1.8)
Why this matters in finance

The 2008 Crisis Was a Kurtosis Story

Pre-2008, many risk models assumed normal distributions for asset returns. Normal → γ₂ = 3 → extreme events extremely rare. Under that model, a 25%-in-a-day market drop should occur about once every 10²² years.

Real stock returns are strongly leptokurtic. Daily S&P returns have excess kurtosis on the order of 5–15. Extreme moves happen vastly more often than the normal would predict — about once a decade rather than once in cosmic time.

The 2008 financial crisis was, in part, a failure to account for tail weight. Risk models calibrated to a normal world catastrophically underestimated tail events. Nassim Taleb's book The Black Swan is essentially a 300-page essay about kurtosis.

The lesson: Variance alone can't tell you about catastrophic risk. Two assets can have identical variances but vastly different kurtosis. The one with higher kurtosis is the one that will surprise you.
⟁ Kurtosis Reference Values
Normal distribution: γ₂ = 3, excess = 0
Uniform distribution: γ₂ = 1.8, excess = −1.2
Laplace distribution: γ₂ = 6, excess = 3
Student-t (df = 5): γ₂ = 9, excess = 6

These reference values help calibrate intuition. Anything with positive excess kurtosis has tails heavier than normal — and tail risk that simple Gaussian models underestimate.

5
Moment Generating Functions (MGFs)
A single object that holds everything

The Idea — Packaging All Moments into One Function

So far we've computed moments one at a time: E[X], E[X²], E[X³], E[X⁴], and so on. Each moment is a number.

What if we could package all of them into a single object — one function from which any moment can be extracted by a simple operation? That object is the Moment Generating Function (MGF).

The trick: Multiply X by a dummy variable t inside an exponential, take the expectation, and the result is a function M(t). Taking k derivatives of M(t) and evaluating at t = 0 gives you the k-th raw moment of X. It's a moment factory.
⟁ Moment Generating Function — Definition
M(t) = E[e^(tX)]

The MGF is the expected value of e^(tX), treated as a function of t. For discrete X: M(t) = Σ e^(tx) · P(X = x). For continuous X: M(t) = ∫ e^(tx) · f(x) dx. The MGF exists wherever the expectation converges (a neighborhood around t = 0).

⟁ Extracting Moments — The Magic Step
E[X^k] = M^(k)(0) = d^k M(t) / dt^k |_{t=0}

Take the k-th derivative of M(t) with respect to t, then evaluate at t = 0. The result is the k-th raw moment of X. This is why M is called the moment generating function — each derivative pulls out one moment.

M'(0) = E[X] — first derivative gives the mean
M''(0) = E[X²] — second gives the 2nd raw moment
M^(k)(0) = E[X^k] — pattern continues for all k
Why this works — the Taylor expansion view

The Hidden Engine

The trick becomes obvious once we expand e^(tX) as a Taylor series:

e^(tX) = 1 + tX + (tX)²/2! + (tX)³/3! + (tX)⁴/4! + ...

Taking the expectation of both sides (linearity!):

M(t) = E[e^(tX)] = 1 + t·E[X] + t²·E[X²]/2! + t³·E[X³]/3! + ...

The MGF is literally a power series whose coefficient of t^k/k! is E[X^k]. Differentiating k times and evaluating at zero strips out exactly the k-th coefficient. The "magic" is just calculus applied to a generating function.

Two killer properties

Why MGFs Are So Powerful

Property 1 — Uniqueness

  • If two RVs have the same MGF (where they exist), they have the same distribution
  • An MGF uniquely fingerprints a distribution
  • Show two MGFs are equal → conclude two distributions are identical

Property 2 — Sums Factor

  • If X ⊥ Y (independent), then M_{X+Y}(t) = M_X(t) · M_Y(t)
  • The MGF of a sum is the product of MGFs
  • This makes sums of independent RVs trivial to analyze
Why this is huge: The Central Limit Theorem — the most important result in statistics — is proved using MGFs. So is the closed form for sums of independent normals, Poissons, gammas, and dozens of other families. MGFs are the algebraic backbone of probability theory.
⟁ MGFs of Common Distributions
Bernoulli(p): M(t) = (1−p) + p·e^t
Binomial(n, p): M(t) = ((1−p) + p·e^t)^n
Poisson(λ): M(t) = exp(λ(e^t − 1))
Normal(μ, σ²): M(t) = exp(μt + σ²t²/2)
Exponential(λ): M(t) = λ / (λ − t), t < λ

Each named distribution has a known MGF. To compute moments, differentiate the MGF and evaluate at zero. Many textbook formulas (e.g., Var(Poisson) = λ) are derived in 30 seconds via MGF differentiation.

A worked computation

Using MGF to Get Mean and Variance of Poisson

For X ~ Poisson(λ), the MGF is M(t) = exp(λ(e^t − 1)).

  • Step 1 — First derivative: M'(t) = exp(λ(e^t − 1)) · λe^t. (Chain rule.)
  • Step 2 — Evaluate at t = 0: M'(0) = exp(0) · λ · 1 = λ. So E[X] = λ.
  • Step 3 — Second derivative: M''(t) = exp(λ(e^t − 1))·(λe^t)² + exp(λ(e^t − 1))·λe^t = M(t)·λe^t·(λe^t + 1).
  • Step 4 — Evaluate at t = 0: M''(0) = 1·λ·(λ + 1) = λ² + λ. So E[X²] = λ² + λ.
  • Step 5 — Variance: Var(X) = E[X²] − μ² = (λ² + λ) − λ² = λ.

That's the famous Poisson identity Mean = Variance = λ — derived cleanly from the MGF.

Beyond MGFs — Characteristic Functions

MGFs have one limitation: they don't always exist. For distributions with very heavy tails (like the Cauchy), E[e^(tX)] diverges and the MGF is undefined.

The fix is the characteristic function φ(t) = E[e^(itX)] — same idea but using imaginary exponents. This always exists for any distribution. Characteristic functions are the "professional grade" tool used in advanced probability theory; MGFs are the friendlier introductory cousin that suffices for almost all practical cases.

Real-world MGF usage

Where MGFs Live in Practice

🧠 Central Limit Theorem

The proof that sums of independent variables approach the normal distribution uses MGFs — specifically, that the MGF of a properly scaled sum converges to the MGF of the normal.

📊 Queueing & Reliability

Sums of independent exponential service times have MGFs that multiply — yielding closed forms for total wait times and lifetimes in operations research.

💰 Finance — Cumulants

The logarithm of the MGF is the cumulant generating function. Cumulants are how higher-moment risk (skew, kurt) is decomposed in option pricing models like SVI.

📡 Information Theory

Large deviation theory — used in coding theory and rare-event simulation — is built entirely on MGF analysis (Chernoff bounds, Cramér's theorem).

🔬 Physics — Partition Functions

In statistical mechanics, the partition function is essentially an MGF over energy states. Free energy and entropy are derived as MGF derivatives.

🤖 ML — Concentration Bounds

Probabilistic guarantees on ML algorithms (Hoeffding, Bennett, Bernstein inequalities) all derive from MGF bounds on bounded random variables.

Common Mistakes
Mistake 1 — Treating raw moments as final summaries. Raw moments depend on the origin and on scale. Always standardize (divide by σ^k) before comparing skewness or kurtosis across datasets.
Mistake 2 — Reading kurtosis = 3 as "high." Kurtosis = 3 is the normal baseline — the reference point, not "heavy." Heavy tails mean excess kurtosis > 0 (i.e., γ₂ > 3).
Mistake 3 — Confusing skewness with bias. Skewness measures distribution asymmetry. Bias in statistics means systematic estimator error. They are different concepts that share none of the same mathematics.
Mistake 4 — Trusting MGF in heavy-tailed worlds. Cauchy, Lévy, and other heavy-tailed distributions don't have MGFs. If your data is heavy-tailed, MGF-based methods (including CLT-derived confidence intervals) can fail spectacularly.
Mistake 5 — Assuming MGF factoring works for dependent sums. M_{X+Y}(t) = M_X(t) · M_Y(t) holds only when X and Y are independent. For dependent variables, you cannot factor the MGF.
Mistake 6 — Forgetting to evaluate at t = 0. The k-th derivative of the MGF gives a function of t. To extract the moment as a number, you must set t = 0 after differentiating. A common exam slip.
Mistake 7 — Using moments to fully characterize fat-tailed data. For some distributions (like the Cauchy), moments beyond the mean don't even exist. "Compute the variance" fails — the integral diverges. In such cases, use robust statistics (median, IQR, quantiles).

Six Tools to Summarize Any Random System

Across three parts, Module 2.5 has built the complete vocabulary for compressing random variables into meaningful numbers. Together, these six tools form the language of statistics, finance, ML, and quantitative science.

From Part 1 — Single Variable

  • E[X] — long-run average (center of mass)
  • Var(X) — typical spread around the center
  • σ = √Var(X) — same as variance but in original units

From Part 2 — Two Variables

  • Cov(X, Y) — joint motion (scale-dependent)
  • ρ(X, Y) — scale-free linear association in [−1, +1]

From Part 3 — Shape Fingerprints

  • γ₁ (skewness) — asymmetry direction
  • γ₂ (kurtosis) — tail weight
  • M(t) — MGF, the complete moment encyclopedia

The Hierarchy

  • 1st moment → center
  • 2nd moment → spread
  • 3rd moment → asymmetry
  • 4th moment → tail weight
  • All moments → MGF
