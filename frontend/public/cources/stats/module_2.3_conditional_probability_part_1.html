2.3.1
Why Conditional Probability Exists
Uncertainty with additional information

The Problem of Updating Beliefs

Imagine you wake up and ask: "What's the chance it will rain today?" Without looking outside, maybe you'd guess 30%. But then you glance at the window — the sky is dark, the wind is strong. Now what's your estimate? Probably 70%, maybe higher.

Nothing in the physical world changed. The clouds were already there. What changed is what you know. Your probability shifted because new information arrived.

This is the central insight of probability theory: probabilities are not fixed numbers attached to events — they are statements about our state of knowledge. When knowledge changes, probabilities must change with it. The mathematical tool that lets us do this rigorously is conditional probability.

The big idea: Conditional probability answers the question "what is the probability of A, given that I now know B is true?" Written: P(A | B).
Probability updating intuition

The "Given That" Phrase — Your New Best Friend

Every conditional probability problem hides behind one of these phrasings:

  • "Given that the test was positive, what's the chance the patient is sick?"
  • "Knowing the user clicked, what's the chance they buy?"
  • "If today is rainy, what's the chance tomorrow is too?"
  • "Among students who passed, what fraction got an A?"

Each of these is asking the same kind of question: narrow the world down to the cases where B is true, then ask what fraction of that narrowed world also has A.

Translation guide: Whenever you see "given," "knowing," "if … then," "among," or "out of those who…" — you are in conditional probability territory.
Real-world examples — why it matters

Where Updating Beliefs Becomes Critical

Conditional probability is not abstract math — it is the engine behind every system that learns from evidence:

🩺 Medical Diagnosis

"Given a positive HIV test, what's the actual probability the patient has HIV?" This number is often far lower than people expect — and the gap has killed reputations and ruined lives.

📧 Spam Detection

"Given that the email contains the word 'lottery', what's the probability it's spam?" Naive Bayes spam filters made the internet usable in the early 2000s.

🛒 Recommendation Engines

"Given the user watched The Matrix, what's the probability they'll like Inception?" Netflix, Spotify, and YouTube are built on conditional probabilities like this.

💳 Fraud Detection

"Given the card was used in two cities in 10 minutes, what's the probability of fraud?" Banks block transactions when this conditional crosses a threshold.

🧠 Deep Learning

Every classifier output is a conditional: P(class = "cat" | image pixels). The softmax layer of a neural net is a conditional probability distribution.

⚖️ Legal Reasoning

"Given the DNA match, what's the probability the suspect is guilty?" The prosecutor's fallacy is a famous misuse of conditional probability that has overturned convictions.

2.3.2
Conditional Probability Fundamentals
Definition

The Formal Definition

The conditional probability of event A given event B, written P(A | B), is the probability that A occurs under the assumption that B has occurred.

Read P(A | B) aloud as: "probability of A, given B." The vertical bar means "given." Whatever appears to the right of the bar is treated as known / certain; whatever appears to the left is the unknown we are asking about.

The formal mathematical definition is:

⟁ Definition of Conditional Probability
P(A | B)  =  P(A ∩ B) / P(B)   (when P(B) > 0)
P(A | B) — the conditional probability of A given B
P(A ∩ B) — joint probability: both A and B occur
P(B) — the marginal probability of B

Why this formula? Once we know B has happened, the only outcomes still "alive" are those inside B. Out of those, the ones we care about are the ones that also lie inside A — that is, in A ∩ B. So the conditional probability is the fraction of B's probability that lies inside A.

Condition: The formula requires P(B) > 0. We cannot condition on an event of probability zero — that would be asking "given the impossible…" which has no meaning.

Event restriction intuition

The Restriction View — Zooming In

Think of P(A | B) as a "zoom-in" operation. Originally, the universe of possibilities is the full sample space Ω. The total probability is 1.

When you condition on B, you essentially throw away everything outside B. The universe shrinks. Now only outcomes inside B exist — and inside that smaller universe, probabilities must still total 1. So we rescale.

That rescaling is exactly the division by P(B). We are saying: "The piece of A that lives inside B has size P(A ∩ B), but inside this new shrunken world of total size P(B), the new probability is P(A ∩ B) / P(B)."

Mental image: A magnifying glass placed over event B. Everything outside the lens vanishes. Inside the lens, probabilities re-normalize so they still sum to 1.
A first concrete example

Worked Walk-through — Rolling a Die

Roll a fair six-sided die. Ω = {1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6}, so P(any outcome) = 1/6.

Let A = "the roll is a 4" = {4}. Without any extra information, P(A) = 1/6 ≈ 16.7%.

Now suppose someone tells you "the roll was even." Let B = "even" = {2, 4, 6}. What is P(A | B)?

Restriction view: The new universe is just {2, 4, 6}. Only one of those three is a 4. So P(A | B) = 1/3 ≈ 33.3%.

Formula view: P(A ∩ B) = P({4}) = 1/6. P(B) = 3/6 = 1/2. So P(A | B) = (1/6) / (1/2) = 1/3. ✓

Notice: knowing that the roll was even doubled the probability that it was a 4. Information has measurable, mathematical impact.

2.3.3
Conditional Sample Spaces
Original vs. filtered sample space

What "Sample Space" Becomes Under a Condition

Recall from Module 2.1: the sample space Ω is the universe of all possible outcomes of an experiment. Under classical probability, P(A) = |A| / |Ω|.

When we condition on B, we essentially swap our sample space. The new effective sample space is B itself. Inside this new universe, we re-compute everything.

  • Original probability: P(A) = |A| / |Ω|
  • Conditional probability: P(A | B) = |A ∩ B| / |B|

This is why conditional probability is sometimes called "probability with a restricted sample space." You're not doing different math — you're doing the same math on a smaller, filtered universe.

Subspace reasoning

The Subspace Mindset

Once you internalize the subspace view, conditional probability stops being mysterious. Here's how to apply it to any problem:

  1. Identify the "given" event (B). Read the problem and find the words "given," "knowing," "if … then," or "among."
  2. Mentally erase everything outside B. All outcomes outside B are now irrelevant. They do not exist for this problem.
  3. Count what's left inside B. That becomes your new denominator.
  4. Inside B, count how many outcomes also satisfy A. That becomes your new numerator.
  5. Divide. The result is P(A | B).
Pro tip: If you ever feel lost in a conditional probability problem, just ask: "What is the new sample space after the 'given' clause kicks in?" Counting in that smaller universe is almost always easier than wrestling with formulas.
2.3.4
The Multiplication Rule
Product rule intuition

From Conditional to Joint — The Reverse Direction

The conditional formula P(A | B) = P(A ∩ B) / P(B) can be rearranged to express P(A ∩ B) directly. Multiply both sides by P(B):

⟁ Multiplication Rule (Product Rule)
P(A ∩ B)  =  P(A | B) × P(B)  =  P(B | A) × P(A)

What it says: The probability that A and B both happen equals the probability of one of them times the conditional probability of the other given the first. Both factorizations are valid — pick whichever conditional is easier to compute.

Why two forms? Symmetry. A ∩ B = B ∩ A (set intersection is commutative), so the joint probability can be decomposed starting from either event.

Sequential events

When Events Happen One After Another

The multiplication rule is the natural language for sequential experiments. "First, draw a card. Then, draw another." Two events, occurring in order:

  • A = "first card is a King"
  • B = "second card is a King" — but the probability of B depends on whether A happened.

The probability that both happen is:

P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B | A)

Without replacement: P(A) = 4/52. Once a King is drawn, only 3 Kings remain in a 51-card deck, so P(B | A) = 3/51. Therefore:

P(both Kings) = (4/52) × (3/51) = 12 / 2652 ≈ 0.0045 (about 0.45%)

Generalization to many events

Chain Rule — Multiplication for Long Sequences

The multiplication rule extends naturally to three, four, or any number of events. This generalization is called the chain rule of probability:

⟁ Chain Rule of Probability
P(A₁ ∩ A₂ ∩ A₃ ∩ … ∩ Aₙ) = P(A₁) × P(A₂ | A₁) × P(A₃ | A₁ ∩ A₂) × … × P(Aₙ | A₁ ∩ … ∩ Aₙ₋₁)

Each factor conditions on everything that came before. This rule is the workhorse of probabilistic graphical models, sequence models (RNNs, transformers), and any system reasoning over time-ordered events.
