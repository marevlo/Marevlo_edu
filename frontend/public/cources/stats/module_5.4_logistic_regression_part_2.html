Topic 3
Probabilistic Interpretation of Models
What it is

What is the Probabilistic View of a Model?

So far we've often thought of a model as a function: feed it x, it returns ŷ. That's a useful shortcut, but it hides something important. Real data is noisy. Two students with the same study hours rarely get exactly the same marks. Two houses of identical size rarely sell for the same price. The line goes through the middle of the cloud, but the cloud is real.

The probabilistic view says: a model doesn't predict a single number — it predicts a distribution over possible outcomes. The prediction ŷ is the centre of that distribution; the spread of the distribution describes how noisy the world is.

y | x ~ some distribution whose centre depends on x

For a regression model with one predictor and Normal noise:

y | x ~ Normal( β₀ + β₁ · x , σ² )

Read this as: "Given predictor value x, the actual response y is a Normal random variable. Its mean is β₀ + β₁x (the regression line). Its variance is σ²." The line is no longer just a prediction — it's the expected value of a distribution, and σ² captures how much real observations wobble around that expected value.

Simple meaning: The probabilistic view turns "the model gives me a number" into "the model gives me a distribution." The point estimate is the centre; the spread tells you how much uncertainty surrounds it. This is the foundation for confidence intervals, prediction intervals, and Bayesian thinking.
Why we need it

Three Things the Probabilistic View Unlocks

1. Honest uncertainty

  • A pure function gives a single number — you can't tell how confident it is
  • A distribution gives a centre AND a width
  • Prediction intervals, confidence intervals — all come from the spread

2. Single principled estimator

  • Once you write y as a distribution, MLE applies directly
  • Different models = different distributions, same procedure
  • Linear regression and logistic regression become two faces of MLE

3. Composability

  • Distributions can be combined to form complex models
  • Hierarchical models, mixed-effects models, Bayesian networks
  • All of probabilistic programming starts here
Big idea: Treating predictions as distributions is one of the deepest shifts in modern statistics. It transforms a model from "a calculator" into "a probability machine," which is exactly what Bayesian inference, deep generative models, and modern uncertainty quantification all need.
How it works for our models

The Two Familiar Models, Rewritten Probabilistically

🟢 Linear Regression

  • Old form: ŷ = β₀ + β₁ · x
  • Probabilistic form: y | x ~ Normal(β₀ + β₁ · x, σ²)
  • Centre of the bell curve = the line
  • Width of the bell = noise level σ
  • Squared error from least squares = NLL of Normal distribution

🟣 Logistic Regression

  • Old form: P(y=1 | x) = σ(β₀ + β₁ · x)
  • Probabilistic form: y | x ~ Bernoulli(σ(β₀ + β₁ · x))
  • Output is a single number p between 0 and 1
  • y is then a yes/no draw from a Bernoulli with success probability p
  • Cross-entropy loss = NLL of Bernoulli distribution

In both cases, the linear formula β₀ + β₁x produces a number that parameterises a distribution. For Normal regression, that number is the mean. For logistic regression, the linear formula's output is squashed by the sigmoid into the success probability of a Bernoulli. The two models share an identical structure — just different distributions for the response.

Why this view matters in practice

The Generative Story Behind Every Model

The probabilistic view gives every model a generative story — a description of how the data could have been produced. Knowing this story matters because:

  • Checking assumptions: if the noise around the line is clearly not Normal (heavy-tailed, skewed), the Normal-noise model is mis-specified. Diagnostic plots (from Module 5.2) test exactly this assumption.
  • Sampling synthetic data: you can simulate new data from a fitted model by drawing from the distribution. Useful for testing, bootstrapping, and Bayesian methods.
  • Switching distributions easily: if Normal noise doesn't fit, swap it for Laplace (robust regression), Student-t (heavy tails), or Poisson (count outcomes). Same MLE recipe, different distribution — that's the whole framework of generalised linear models.
  • Predicting whole distributions: for new x, the model returns the full distribution of plausible y values, not just a point estimate. This is exactly what risk managers, weather forecasters, and probabilistic ML systems output.

⟁ The Probabilistic View of Our Two Models
Linear regression: y | x ~ Normal( β₀ + β₁ · x , σ² )
Logistic regression: y | x ~ Bernoulli( σ( β₀ + β₁ · x ) )

What each piece means:

y | x — the actual response y, given the predictor value x
~ — read as "is distributed as"
Normal(μ, σ²) — bell-shaped distribution with mean μ and variance σ²
Bernoulli(p) — yes-or-no distribution with success probability p
σ( · ) — the sigmoid function squashing into (0, 1)
β₀, β₁ — model parameters to be estimated

How to read in words:

  • "For linear regression, given an x, the response y comes from a Normal distribution whose mean is the line and whose spread σ describes the noise."
  • "For logistic regression, given an x, the response y is a yes/no draw from a Bernoulli whose success probability is the sigmoid of the linear score."

Why this matters: Both forms express y as a random variable whose distribution depends on x through the linear combination β₀ + β₁x. The linear function is no longer the prediction itself — it's the input to a probability distribution. This single shift makes MLE apply uniformly to both situations.

⟁ Worked Example

Example: A Probabilistic Prediction for One Person

A regression model predicts blood pressure (mm Hg) from age (years):

BP | age ~ Normal( 90 + 0.5 · age , σ² = 8² )

For a 50-year-old patient:

  • Mean predicted BP: 90 + 0.5 × 50 = 115 mm Hg
  • 68% interval (±1σ): 115 ± 8 = 107 to 123 mm Hg
  • 95% interval (±2σ): 115 ± 16 = 99 to 131 mm Hg

The point prediction "115" was always available from the linear formula. What the probabilistic view adds is the interval: where is the patient's true BP likely to fall? The Normal-noise assumption with σ = 8 gives a full distribution of plausible outcomes — much more useful than a single number.

The same idea for a classifier: for spam classification with P(spam | x) = 0.8, the probabilistic view says "this email is a Bernoulli outcome with success probability 0.8." We're not just labelling it spam — we're describing a coin flip biased 80% toward spam. If we needed to draw a synthetic email, we'd sample yes/no with that probability.

The lesson: Every fitted model has a hidden distribution. Making it explicit gives you uncertainty estimates, simulation capability, and the foundation for MLE.
When the probabilistic view matters

When to Adopt the Probabilistic Mindset

  • Whenever you need prediction intervals, not just point predictions.
  • Whenever you are doing inference — confidence intervals, hypothesis tests, p-values.
  • Whenever you might switch the noise distribution (Normal → Laplace → Student-t) without rebuilding everything.
  • Whenever you want to sample from the fitted model (bootstrapping, Bayesian posterior, generative AI).
  • Whenever you want to combine models in a principled way — Bayesian networks, hierarchical models, probabilistic programming.
Real-life examples

Where the Probabilistic View Drives Decisions

🌦️ Weather Forecasts

Modern forecasts don't say "it will rain 12 mm" — they say "70% chance of rain, with a distribution of possible amounts." This is the probabilistic view of regression in action.

📈 Risk Management

Value-at-Risk (VaR) used by banks asks: "What's the 5th percentile of tomorrow's possible portfolio losses?" Computed from the predicted distribution, not a point estimate.

🧪 Drug Trials

Trial outcomes are reported with confidence intervals — "drug X reduces disease by 30% (95% CI: 22-38%)." The interval comes from the distribution implied by the model.

🎯 A/B Testing

Modern A/B platforms compute posterior distributions of the lift, not just point estimates. Decisions are made by comparing the distributions, not the means.

🤖 Generative AI

LLMs are explicitly probabilistic: they output a distribution over the next token. Sampling from this distribution is what "generating text" actually means.

🛰️ Sensor Fusion

Self-driving cars combine sensor readings as random variables with known distributions — the probabilistic view powers Kalman filters and modern object tracking.

Topic 4
MLE in Action — Two Models, One Procedure
What it is

The Universal MLE Recipe

From Part 1 we know the principle: pick the parameters that make the observed data most likely under the assumed model. Now we apply this concretely, twice — first to linear regression, then to logistic regression — and watch the same recipe produce two different methods that we used to think of as totally separate.

  1. Step 1: Specify the probabilistic model. Write y | x as a distribution whose parameters depend on the predictors.
  2. Step 2: Write the likelihood of one observation. P(yᵢ | xᵢ, θ).
  3. Step 3: Multiply across all observations. L(θ) = ∏ P(yᵢ | xᵢ, θ).
  4. Step 4: Take log and negate. NLL(θ) = − Σ log P(yᵢ | xᵢ, θ).
  5. Step 5: Minimise NLL with respect to θ. Either with closed-form formulas or with gradient descent.
Simple meaning: Pick a probability model for y. Write down the negative log-likelihood. Minimise it. Done. The same five steps cover linear regression, logistic regression, Poisson regression, and every neural network.
Why it matters

Why the Same Procedure Yields Different Algorithms

The five-step recipe is identical. But because step 1 uses different distributions, step 4 ends up with different formulas — and so does step 5:

Linear regression — closed form

  • NLL = (1 / 2σ²) Σ(yᵢ − β₀ − β₁xᵢ)² + constant
  • Minimising means setting the gradient to zero
  • Two linear equations, two unknowns — solvable exactly
  • The closed-form formulas from Module 5.1 fall out

Logistic regression — no closed form

  • NLL = − Σ [yᵢ log pᵢ + (1−yᵢ) log(1−pᵢ)] where pᵢ = σ(β₀+β₁xᵢ)
  • Setting gradient to zero gives equations involving sigmoids
  • No clean algebraic solution exists
  • Need iterative optimisation — gradient descent or Newton's method

This is one of the deep reasons logistic regression is computationally more involved than linear regression — not because it's a fundamentally different idea, but because the resulting NLL doesn't have a clean closed-form minimum.

How it works numerically

Gradient Descent — Walking Down the NLL Surface

For models without a closed form (logistic regression, neural networks), we minimise NLL by walking downhill on its surface:

  1. Start somewhere. Pick initial parameter values — random or zero.
  2. Compute the gradient. Calculate how NLL changes with respect to each parameter at the current point.
  3. Take a small step downhill. Update each parameter in the direction opposite to its gradient — by a small "learning rate" amount.
  4. Repeat. Recompute the gradient at the new point, take another step, and so on.
  5. Stop when changes become tiny. The algorithm has converged to a local minimum.

For logistic regression the NLL surface is "convex" (bowl-shaped) — there is only one minimum, and gradient descent always finds it. For deep neural networks the surface is much more complex, but the same idea drives training: each step makes the model assign higher probability to the observed data.

The gradient update for one parameter β in logistic regression is:

β ← β − η · Σᵢ ( pᵢ − yᵢ ) · xᵢ
  • η — learning rate, a small positive number
  • pᵢ − yᵢ — the "prediction error": how far the model's probability is from the actual label
  • xᵢ — the predictor value for observation i

So the update direction is "the error" times "the input." This same form drives neural-network back-propagation — logistic regression is the prototype.

⟁ The Two NLLs Side-by-Side
Linear (Normal noise): NLL = (1 / 2σ²) Σ (yᵢ − β₀ − β₁xᵢ)² + const
Logistic (Bernoulli): NLL = − Σ [ yᵢ log pᵢ + (1 − yᵢ) log(1 − pᵢ) ]

What each symbol means:

yᵢ — actual response for observation i (number for linear, 0/1 for logistic)
xᵢ — predictor value for observation i
β₀, β₁ — model parameters being estimated
σ² — variance of the Normal noise (also estimated)
pᵢ — sigmoid(β₀ + β₁xᵢ), the model's probability for observation i
const — terms that don't depend on β, irrelevant to optimisation

How to read them in words:

  • Linear NLL: "Sum the squared misses, divide by twice the variance, add a constant. To minimise this, just minimise the sum of squared errors — which is exactly least squares."
  • Logistic NLL: "For each data point, take the log of the probability the model assigned to its actual label. (If y=1, use log p; if y=0, use log(1−p).) Sum these logs, then negate."

The unification: Both are MLE under different distributions. The least-squares formula and the cross-entropy formula are not two separate inventions — they are the same idea applied to Normal and Bernoulli respectively.

⟁ Worked Example

Example: Fitting Linear and Logistic Regressions Side-by-Side

Compare the workflow for two tasks on the same dataset of student records (study hours + outcome):

TaskStep 1: modelStep 4: NLLStep 5: minimise via
Predict marks (continuous) marks | hours ~ Normal(β₀+β₁·hours, σ²) Σ(yᵢ − ŷᵢ)² / (2σ²) + const Closed-form least squares
Predict pass / fail (binary) pass | hours ~ Bernoulli(σ(β₀+β₁·hours)) − Σ [yᵢ log pᵢ + (1−yᵢ) log(1−pᵢ)] Gradient descent (no closed form)

Same recipe, different distributions:

  • Both start by writing y | x as a distribution with a linear-in-β structure.
  • Both turn the likelihood into NLL and minimise.
  • Both produce a fitted β₀ and β₁ at the end.
  • Only the optimisation method differs — and that's because of how the NLL looks, not because of a different underlying philosophy.
The lesson: Once you adopt the probabilistic view, MLE becomes a single, universal procedure. Linear and logistic regression are siblings, not strangers. The same idea continues into Poisson regression (count data), survival models (time-to-event), and even modern deep learning.
When to use it

When MLE is the Method of Choice

  • You have a specified probability model for the data-generating process.
  • You want theoretically grounded parameter estimates (consistent, efficient, with standard errors).
  • You want a unified approach across regression, classification, count models, survival analysis.
  • You are comfortable with numerical optimisation if a closed form doesn't exist.
When MLE struggles: with very small samples (estimates can be unstable), in the presence of complete separation in logistic regression (MLE diverges to infinity), or when the assumed distribution is severely wrong. Bayesian methods, regularised MLE, or robust methods are the typical fixes.
Real-life examples

Where the MLE Recipe Powers Real Pipelines

🧠 Deep Learning

Training a neural network is MLE under whatever output distribution you choose — softmax + cross-entropy for classification, Normal + MSE for regression.

🩺 Survival Analysis

Cox proportional hazards models estimate hazard ratios via maximum partial likelihood — MLE adapted for censored time-to-event data.

📈 Time-Series Modelling

ARIMA, GARCH, state-space models — all fitted by maximum likelihood, often via Kalman-filter-based likelihood computation.

🌐 Recommender Systems

Matrix-factorisation systems treat ratings as probabilistic outcomes (Gaussian or Poisson) and fit by MLE — same recipe, much bigger model.

🔬 Genomics

Population genetics estimates allele frequencies and selection coefficients by MLE under multinomial or beta-binomial models.

🗣️ Language Models

GPT-class models minimise the NLL of the next token across trillions of training examples. The fancy architecture changes; the principle does not.
