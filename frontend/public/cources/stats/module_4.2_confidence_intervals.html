A method for reporting an estimate together with a calibrated range that quantifies its uncertainty.

We build intuition for what a confidence interval represents, define the margin of error and explain every term in its formula, examine how the confidence level controls the trade-off between precision and reliability, and construct intervals for a population mean. The module concludes with two interactive simulations and a complete worked example.

◆ 01
Confidence Interval Intuition
⟁ WHAT IS IT

The interval estimate

A confidence interval is a range of plausible values for an unknown population parameter, computed from sample data. Instead of reporting a single number as our estimate, we report a lower bound and an upper bound. The interval communicates two things at once — the estimate and the uncertainty around it.

Intuition. A point estimate states "the population mean is 50." A confidence interval states "the population mean is plausibly between 47 and 53." The second statement is more informative because it conveys how precise the estimate is.

Formal definition

A confidence interval is a range (L,U) calculated from sample data to estimate an unknown population value. If we repeatedly take samples and build intervals using the same method, the confidence level tells us how often those intervals would contain the true parameter.

Population vs sample — terminology recap

  • Population. The complete group we want to learn about (for example, all packets produced by a factory in a year).
  • Sample. A subset of the population that we actually measure.
  • Parameter. A numerical summary of the population (denoted with Greek letters, such as μ for the population mean).
  • Statistic. A numerical summary of the sample (denoted with Latin letters, such as x̄ for the sample mean).
⟁ WHY DO WE NEED IT

The problem confidence intervals solve

In practice we cannot measure the entire population. We can only measure a sample. Because of natural sampling variability, the sample mean x̄ will almost never exactly equal the population mean μ. A single number therefore creates a false impression of precision.

A confidence interval addresses this in three ways:

  • It honestly reports the uncertainty associated with the estimate.
  • It allows comparison across studies and methods on a common scale.
  • It supports informed decision-making by giving a range that decisions can be checked against.

What happens without confidence intervals

If we only report point estimates, two studies that disagree on the value of μ can look more contradictory than they actually are. Their intervals might overlap substantially. Without intervals, sample size and precision are also invisible to the reader.

⟁ WHERE IT IS USED

Practical importance

Confidence intervals appear in nearly every quantitative discipline:

  • Machine learning. Reporting model accuracy with an interval rather than a single point estimate.
  • Clinical research. Stating the effect of a treatment along with the precision of that effect.
  • Polling and surveys. Reporting candidate support with a margin of error.
  • Industrial quality control. Estimating mean dimensions of produced parts and flagging departures from specification.
  • Finance. Estimating expected returns and the precision of those estimates.

Where intervals should not be used

  • When the entire population has been measured (no estimation is needed).
  • When the sample is not representative — a non-random sample produces a non-valid interval.
  • When the sample is very small and the data is highly non-normal, the formulas presented here may not apply without modification.
⟁ HOW IT WORKS

The core formula structure

All confidence intervals share the same building blocks: a point estimate at the center, and a margin of error that extends symmetrically on each side.

Confidence Interval = Point Estimate ± Margin of Error
Confidence Interval — the range of values computed from sample data that is believed to contain the unknown population parameter.
Point Estimate — a single value (such as the sample mean x̄) used as the best guess for the unknown population parameter.
Margin of Error — the amount added and subtracted from the point estimate to create the interval, accounting for sampling variability.

For the population mean μ with known population standard deviation σ:

CI for μ = x̄ ± z × (σ / √n)
CI for μ — the confidence interval for the population mean.
x̄ (x-bar) — the sample mean, the point estimate of μ.
z — the critical value from the standard normal distribution corresponding to the chosen confidence level.
σ (sigma) — the population standard deviation.
n — the sample size.
√n — the square root of the sample size.
σ / √n — the standard error of the mean, measuring the typical variability of the sample mean.
Correct interpretation of "95% confidence." The 95% refers to the procedure, not to any single computed interval. If the experiment were repeated many times and an interval was constructed each time using the same procedure, approximately 95% of those intervals would contain the true value μ. Once we compute a specific interval, μ is either inside it or not — the 95% does not apply to that one interval as a probability statement.

◆ 02
Margin of Error
⟁ WHAT IS IT

Definition

The margin of error is the half-width of the confidence interval. It is the quantity added to and subtracted from the point estimate to produce the lower and upper bounds.

A small margin of error indicates a precise estimate. A large margin of error indicates that the estimate is imprecise — the true value could lie anywhere within a wide range.

Why it exists

The margin of error captures the inherent randomness of sampling. Two different samples drawn from the same population will produce two different sample means. The margin of error quantifies how far apart those sample means are expected to be from the true population mean.

⟁ MARGIN OF ERROR (KNOWN σ)
E = z × (σ / √n)
This formula is used when the population standard deviation σ is known and the sample is sufficiently large or the underlying distribution is approximately normal.
E — margin of error.
z — critical value from the standard normal distribution corresponding to the chosen confidence level.
σ (sigma) — the population standard deviation, a measure of how spread out the population values are.
n — sample size, the number of observations collected.
√n — the square root of n. Recall √16 = 4 because 4 × 4 = 16.
σ / √n — the standard error of the mean, denoted SE. It measures the typical distance between a sample mean and the population mean.
⟁ DRIVERS OF THE MARGIN

What controls the margin of error

The formula E = z × (σ / √n) shows three controllable factors:

  • Confidence level (z). A higher confidence level uses a larger z value, which widens the interval.
  • Variability (σ). More variable data leads to less precise estimates. We cannot usually change σ, but selecting a more homogeneous population can reduce it.
  • Sample size (n). Increasing n shrinks the margin. Because n appears under a square root, doubling the sample size does not halve the margin — it reduces it by a factor of √2 ≈ 1.414.

When not to use this formula

  • When the population standard deviation σ is unknown, we use the sample standard deviation s instead. We also replace the z-value with a t-value taken from the t-distribution (also called Student’s t-distribution). A t-value is a number that helps decide how wide the confidence interval should be.
  • When the sample is very small (n < 30) and the underlying distribution is highly non-normal.

◆ 03
Confidence Levels
⟁ DEFINITION

What a confidence level represents

The confidence level, often written as (1 − α) × 100%, specifies the long-run proportion of intervals (constructed using the same procedure) that will contain the true parameter. The most commonly used levels are 90%, 95%, and 99%.

Critical z-values

Each confidence level corresponds to a specific critical value from the standard normal distribution, denoted z₍α/2₎ for a two-sided interval. These values mark the points beyond which (α/2) of the probability lies in each tail.

⟁ STANDARD CRITICAL VALUES
Confidence Level α z₍α/2₎ Interval Width
90% 0.10 1.645 Narrowest
95% 0.05 1.960 Standard default
99% 0.01 2.576 Widest
⟁ THE TRADE-OFF

Precision versus reliability

Increasing the confidence level does not improve the estimate — it only widens the interval. A 99% interval is wider, and therefore less precise, than a 95% interval based on the same sample. Choosing a confidence level is a deliberate decision about how much imprecision can be tolerated in exchange for reliability.

WHEN TO USE 90%

  • Exploratory analysis where speed matters
  • Marketing experiments with low risk
  • Internal dashboards and monitoring

WHEN TO USE 95%

  • Standard research and reporting
  • Most published scientific work
  • Default in industry practice

WHEN TO USE 99%

  • Drug approvals and safety claims
  • High-stakes engineering decisions
  • Regulatory compliance
◆ 04
Confidence Interval for the Population Mean
⟁ COMPLETE FORMULA
CI for μ = x̄ ± z × (σ / √n)
This interval is appropriate when the population standard deviation σ is known and either the sample size is large (n ≥ 30) or the underlying population is approximately normal.
μ (mu) — the unknown population mean we are estimating.
x̄ (x-bar) — the sample mean, computed by averaging the observations.
z — the critical value for the chosen confidence level.
σ / √n — the standard error of the mean.
⟁ CONSTRUCTION PROCEDURE

Step-by-step procedure

STEP 1
Collect the sample using a random sampling method, and record the n observations.
STEP 2
Compute the sample mean x̄ by adding all observations and dividing by n.
STEP 3
Select the confidence level (90%, 95%, or 99%) and identify the matching critical value z.
STEP 4
Compute the standard error SE = σ / √n.
STEP 5
Compute the margin of error E = z × SE.
STEP 6
Construct the interval as (x̄ − E, x̄ + E).
STEP 7
State the conclusion in the context of the problem, identifying the interval and the confidence level used.
⟁ WORKED EXAMPLE — AI INFERENCE LATENCY

Problem. An engineering team measures the response time of a deployed AI model on a random sample of n = 64 requests. The sample mean is x̄ = 220 ms. Historical data indicate that the population standard deviation σ = 32 ms. Construct a 95% confidence interval for the true mean response time μ.

Step 1 — Identify the given values
x̄ = 220,   σ = 32,   n = 64,   Confidence = 95%
Step 2 — Critical value
z = 1.96   (from the standard normal table for 95% confidence)
Step 3 — Standard error
SE = σ / √n = 32 / √64 = 32 / 8 = 4 ms
Step 4 — Margin of error
E = z × SE = 1.96 × 4 = 7.84 ms
Step 5 — Construct the interval
CI = 220 ± 7.84 = (212.16, 227.84) ms
Step 6 — Interpretation

We are 95% confident that the true mean response time of the AI model lies between 212.16 ms and 227.84 ms. Equivalently, if the team were to repeat this sampling procedure many times, approximately 95% of the constructed intervals would contain the true mean μ.

◆ 05
Real-World Applications

Machine Learning Model Reports

Reporting test-set accuracy as 91.4% with a 95% CI of (89.8%, 93.0%) — communicates how reliable the score is, not just its point value.

A/B Testing

Conversion lift estimated at 2.3% with CI (1.6%, 3.0%). Engineering teams use the interval, not just the mean, to decide on launches.

Clinical Trials

A drug reduces blood pressure by 10 mmHg, CI (8, 12). Regulators evaluate the entire interval, especially its lower bound.

Manufacturing Quality Control

The mean weight of a biscuit packet is 100 g with a 95% CI of (98.5, 101.5). Out-of-specification intervals trigger process recalibration.

Financial Forecasting

Expected quarterly return = 6.2% with 95% CI (4.1%, 8.3%). Risk officers plan against the lower bound to be conservative.

Polling and Surveys

"42% of voters support the proposal, with a margin of error of ±3 percentage points at 95% confidence." A direct interval estimate.
