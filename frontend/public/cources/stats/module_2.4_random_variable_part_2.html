Continuous Random Variables
What "continuous" really means

The Uncountable World

A random variable X is continuous when its possible values form an uncountable set — typically an interval of the real line. Between any two values, no matter how close, there are infinitely many other values. There's no "next" value to list. You can't enumerate them, even with infinite time.

Examples of inherently continuous quantities:

  • Height of a person: 162.347... cm — could keep adding digits forever.
  • Time between earthquakes: any positive real number.
  • Temperature: 23.7891°C is just as valid as 23.7890°C.
  • Stock price: any real number with arbitrary precision.
  • Bullet's landing position on a target: any point in 2D space.
  • Weights output by a neural network: any real numbers in ℝⁿ.
The deep distinction: Discrete RVs are like sand grains — you can count them. Continuous RVs are like water — between any two drops, there's always more water. This isn't pedantry; it forces us to use completely different mathematical tools.
The shocking truth about continuous probabilities

Why P(X = exactly x) = 0 — Always

Here is the single fact that confuses every student of probability the first time they meet it:

For any continuous random variable X and any specific value x,   P(X = x) = 0.

Yes — the probability that a random adult is exactly 175.0000000000... cm tall is zero. Not "very small." Not "approximately zero." It is mathematically, identically zero.

Why? Think about it: there are uncountably many real numbers in any interval. If each individual point had any positive probability, no matter how tiny, summing over infinitely many of them would explode past 1. The only consistent assignment is that every single point gets probability zero.

This breaks discrete intuition: For a discrete variable, P(X = 7) can be 1/36 — meaningful and positive. For a continuous variable, P(X = 7) = 0, and so is P(X = 7.001), and P(X = 7.0001). Yet somehow, P(X is between 7 and 8) can be perfectly positive. How? Through density.
Probability lives in intervals, not points

The Reframing — From Point to Region

For continuous random variables, we abandon the question "what's P(X = exactly this)?" — it's always zero and gives us nothing.

Instead, we ask: "What's P(X falls in this range)?" — and this question is meaningful and useful. The probability assigned to intervals can be positive, even when each individual point gets zero.

Think of probability for a continuous RV like spreading butter on a piece of bread. The butter has a total mass of 1, and it's smeared across the bread. The amount of butter at any infinitely thin point is zero — but the amount of butter inside a finite slice is positive. Density measures how thick the butter is at each location.

The key idea: For continuous RVs, probability is no longer "mass at a point" — it's "mass over a region." The mathematical tool that captures this is the probability density function (PDF), and computing real probabilities requires integrating the density over the region of interest.

Probability Density Function (PDF)
What a PDF actually is

The Density Function — Carefully Defined

The probability density function, or PDF, written f(x) or fₓ(x), is the continuous analog of the PMF. But here's the critical distinction: f(x) is NOT a probability. It's a density — probability per unit length on the x-axis.

To get an actual probability, you have to integrate the density over a region:

⟁ PDF Definition — How to Get Real Probabilities
P(a ≤ X ≤ b)  =  ∫ₐᵇ f(x) dx

The probability that X falls in the interval [a, b] equals the area under the PDF curve between a and b. The PDF tells you the shape; the integral tells you the probability.

A PDF must satisfy two non-negotiable conditions:

①   f(x) ≥ 0   for every x
②   ∫₋∞^∞ f(x) dx  =  1

Condition ① says density is non-negative (no "anti-density"). Condition ② says the total area under the curve is exactly 1 — the total probability over all possible outcomes is 1.

f(x) — density at point x (probability per unit length)
∫ₐᵇ f(x) dx — area under curve from a to b = P(a ≤ X ≤ b)
f(x) can be > 1 — yes, density is not capped at 1, only the integral is
Why f(x) can exceed 1

The Mind-Bending Property of Density

Here's another fact that confuses students: the PDF value f(x) at a point can be greater than 1. For instance, a uniform distribution on [0, 0.5] has f(x) = 2 everywhere inside that interval — well above 1.

Why is this OK? Because f(x) is not a probability. It's density. The probability is the area, and as long as the total area equals 1, the height can be anything — narrow-and-tall, wide-and-short, or anywhere in between.

Mental model: Imagine a heavy weight (1 kg of mass) spread over a thin metal rod. If the rod is 1 meter long, the density is 1 kg/m everywhere. But if you compress the same mass into a 50 cm rod, the density doubles to 2 kg/m. The total mass is still 1 kg. PDFs work the same way.

Reading a PDF — what its shape tells you

Visual Interpretation of Density Curves

The shape of a PDF tells you everything about the distribution. Three universal patterns:

Peaks & Modes

  • High f(x) values = "popular" regions, where outcomes cluster
  • The peak of the PDF is the most-likely region (the mode)
  • One peak → unimodal. Two peaks → bimodal. Useful for detecting mixed populations

Tails

  • Low-but-nonzero f(x) at extremes = rare events still possible
  • "Heavy tails" → extremes are more frequent than Gaussian predicts (finance, networks)
  • "Thin tails" → extremes are very rare (Gaussian itself)

Symmetry & Skew

  • Symmetric PDF → mean = median = mode (Gaussian)
  • Right-skewed → long tail to the right (income, response times)
  • Left-skewed → long tail to the left (exam scores when most do well)
Computing real probabilities from a PDF

The Five Probability Questions — All Become Integrals

For a continuous random variable X with PDF f(x), every probability you'll ever compute is one of these:

  • P(X ≤ a) = ∫₋∞^a f(x) dx   — area under f from −∞ to a (this is the CDF — Part 3)
  • P(X ≥ a) = ∫ₐ^∞ f(x) dx   — area from a to ∞
  • P(a ≤ X ≤ b) = ∫ₐᵇ f(x) dx   — area between a and b
  • P(X = a) = 0   — zero-width interval, zero area, zero probability
  • P(X < a) = P(X ≤ a) — because the single point has probability 0 (a key consequence!)
Subtle but important: For continuous RVs, strict and non-strict inequalities give the same probability! P(X < 5) = P(X ≤ 5). For discrete RVs, this is generally false.
A worked example — the uniform PDF

Walk-Through: The Uniform Distribution on [0, 10]

Let X be uniformly distributed on [0, 10]. This means every value in the interval is "equally likely" — and outside the interval, density is zero. The PDF is constant inside the interval:

f(x) = 1/10 = 0.1   for 0 ≤ x ≤ 10,   f(x) = 0 otherwise

Check it's a valid PDF: f(x) ≥ 0 ✓. Total area = base × height = 10 × 0.1 = 1 ✓.

P(X = 5): = 0 (a single point has zero width).

P(2 ≤ X ≤ 6): = ∫₂⁶ 0.1 dx = 0.1 × (6 − 2) = 0.4. Equivalently, "interval length / total length" = 4/10 = 0.4.

P(X ≥ 7): = ∫₇¹⁰ 0.1 dx = 0.1 × 3 = 0.3.

P(X = 5.0001 OR X = 7.0001): = 0 + 0 = 0. Individual points carry zero probability.

Famous continuous distributions — preview

The Continuous Distribution Zoo (Preview)

In later modules we'll formally meet famous continuous distributions — each defined by its PDF formula. Here's a sneak peek:

  • Uniform(a, b) — constant density on [a, b]. The simplest. Used in random sampling, simulations.
  • Exponential(λ) — f(x) = λe^(−λx). Models waiting times. "Memoryless" property.
  • Gaussian / Normal(μ, σ²) — the famous bell curve. f(x) = (1/√(2πσ²)) e^(−(x−μ)²/(2σ²)). Central to the CLT, machine learning, physics.
  • Beta(α, β) — supported on [0, 1]. Models proportions, probabilities. Foundation of Bayesian inference.
  • Gamma(k, θ) — generalization of exponential. Models survival times, waiting for k events.
  • Chi-squared(k) — sum of squared Gaussians. Used in hypothesis testing.
  • Student's t — heavy-tailed bell. Used when sample size is small.

All of these are continuous RVs, each with its own PDF formula and unique application area. Once you grasp the PDF framework in this part, every one of these becomes "just a different f(x)."

Real-world PDFs

Continuous Densities in the Real World

📊 Stock Returns

Daily log-returns are modeled with Gaussian (or heavier-tailed) PDFs. Portfolio risk, options pricing, and Value-at-Risk all rest on these density functions.

📐 Manufacturing Tolerances

Part dimensions follow Gaussian PDFs centered on the design spec. Quality control uses tail probabilities (∫ outside tolerance) to set rejection rates.

⏱️ Customer Service Times

Service durations often follow exponential or gamma PDFs. Operations research uses these for staffing, queueing, and wait-time forecasts.

🧬 Gene Expression Levels

RNA-seq measurements are continuous. Lognormal and gamma PDFs model expression — informing biology, medicine, and drug discovery.

🤖 Neural Network Weights

Trained weights have empirical PDFs. Regularization (L1/L2) imposes prior PDFs on weights — Laplace (sparse) or Gaussian (smooth).

🌡️ Physical Measurements

Temperatures, voltages, particle positions — all measured continuously, all described by PDFs. Quantum mechanics is literally a PDF theory.

📞 Signal Noise

Background noise in audio, video, or sensor data is modeled with Gaussian (or other) PDFs. Filtering algorithms rely on this density structure.

🛡️ Cybersecurity Anomaly

Normal traffic forms a "typical" PDF; anomalies live in the rare tails. Intrusion detection sets thresholds by integrating density over suspicious regions.

When to use PDFs

Decision Guide — Reach for a PDF When…

  • The variable is measured on a continuous scale (time, length, weight, temperature, voltage, monetary value).
  • Between any two values, intermediate values are physically possible.
  • You care about the probability of falling in a range, not the exact value.
  • You need to work with calculus tools — optimization, gradients, integrals, derivatives.
  • You're building parametric models (Gaussian regression, mixture models, generative networks, Bayesian inference).

Reach instead for a PMF (Part 1) when:

  • The variable is naturally discrete or categorical (counts, classes, integer-valued).
  • You need positive probability at specific values.
Common mistakes

Pitfalls to Avoid

Mistake 1: Thinking f(x) is a probability. It is NOT — it is a density. The probability of a region is the integral of f(x), not f(x) itself.
Mistake 2: Worrying that f(x) > 1 means something is wrong. It's perfectly fine. Density can be any non-negative number — only the integral must equal 1.
Mistake 3: Saying P(X = 5) = "some small positive number." For continuous RVs, P(X = exactly 5) is identically zero. No exceptions.
Mistake 4: Forgetting to verify ∫f(x) dx = 1 when defining a PDF. The constant out front (the "normalization constant") is determined by this requirement.
Mistake 5: Mixing PMF and PDF logic. You sum a PMF; you integrate a PDF. Don't try to "sum" a continuous density — that doesn't work.
Mistake 6: Assuming a "uniform PDF" is unique. It depends on the interval! Uniform on [0, 1] has f = 1; Uniform on [0, 10] has f = 0.1. The shape is constant, the height differs.
