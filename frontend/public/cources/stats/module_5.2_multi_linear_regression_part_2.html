Topic 3
Multicollinearity
What it is

What is Multicollinearity?

So far we treated each predictor as if it contributed a separate piece of information. In practice, the predictors we collect often overlap. Two columns may carry the same story told in different units.

Take a salary dataset. Suppose you include both "years of experience" and "age." For most working adults these two are tightly linked — older usually means more experienced. The data points (age, experience) lie close to a straight line. The two columns are not identical, but they carry almost the same information.

Multicollinearity is the name for this overlap between predictors. It happens when one predictor can be predicted well from a combination of the others.

Simple meaning: Multicollinearity is when two or more predictors say roughly the same thing about the data. The model has trouble deciding which one to credit for the effect, so the coefficients become shaky.

Two flavours of the same problem

  • Perfect multicollinearity — one predictor is an exact linear combination of others (for example, including height in cm and height in inches). The model literally cannot fit; software will refuse or drop a column.
  • High multicollinearity — predictors are strongly correlated but not identical (age and experience). The model fits, but the coefficients become unstable and hard to interpret.
Why it matters

Why Overlapping Predictors Make the Model Wobble

When two predictors carry nearly the same information, the least-squares method cannot tell which one deserves the credit. The result is three visible symptoms:

1. Unstable coefficients

  • Coefficient values jump around if a single data point is added or removed
  • Same data, slight reshuffle — completely different β values
  • Trust in the numbers drops

2. Inflated standard errors

  • Confidence intervals become very wide
  • p-values look insignificant even when the variable truly matters
  • Hard to declare any predictor "important"

3. Strange coefficient signs

  • Coefficients may flip sign from what you'd expect
  • "More experience → lower salary" — counterintuitive
  • The model is just balancing redundant variables
What it does NOT hurt: Multicollinearity does not damage predictions for new cases (within the range of the training data). If you only care about ŷ accuracy, you can largely ignore it. But if you care about interpreting each coefficient — and most scientific and policy work does — multicollinearity is a serious problem.
Why we check for it

Why Every Serious Regression Workflow Checks for Multicollinearity

  • Honest interpretation: publishing "the effect of age on salary is X" while age is heavily entangled with experience is misleading without a flag.
  • Policy decisions: if a model says "education has no effect" because it's collinear with experience, removing one or the other can flip the conclusion entirely.
  • Model maintenance: highly collinear models are fragile — small data updates can change conclusions. Identifying multicollinearity lets you build a sturdier model.
  • Auditing & regulation: regulated industries (insurance, lending, healthcare) require diagnostic reports including multicollinearity checks.
How to detect it

The VIF Diagnostic — How to Spot Multicollinearity

The standard diagnostic is the Variance Inflation Factor (VIF). Each predictor gets its own VIF number that tells you how much that predictor's variance is "inflated" by overlap with the other predictors.

How to read VIF

  • VIF = 1: the predictor is completely uncorrelated with the others. No multicollinearity issue.
  • VIF 1 – 5: mild correlation. Usually safe — coefficients are reliable.
  • VIF 5 – 10: moderate concern. Worth investigating, especially if you care about interpretation.
  • VIF > 10: serious multicollinearity. Coefficients are likely unstable; fix needed.

How VIF is computed

For each predictor xⱼ, fit a regression of xⱼ on all the other predictors. Get the R² of that fit, call it R²ⱼ. Then:

VIFⱼ = 1 / (1 − R²ⱼ)
  • If xⱼ is unrelated to the other predictors, R²ⱼ is near 0, so VIFⱼ ≈ 1.
  • If xⱼ is well-explained by the others (R²ⱼ near 1), then 1 − R²ⱼ is tiny, and VIFⱼ explodes.
  • VIF measures how predictable each predictor is from the others — a direct quantification of overlap.

Other quick checks

  • Correlation matrix: compute pairwise correlations between predictors. Any |r| above ~0.8 between two predictors is a red flag.
  • Drop-one-predictor test: refit the model after removing a suspect predictor; if other coefficients change dramatically, multicollinearity is likely.

How to fix it

Three Practical Fixes for Multicollinearity

  1. Drop one of the overlapping predictors. If experience and age both say roughly the same thing, keep the one that is more interpretable or more reliably measured. The model becomes simpler and the remaining coefficient becomes stable.
  2. Combine the predictors into one composite. If two predictors are conceptually related (e.g., height and weight → BMI), build a single combined variable. Principal Component Analysis (PCA) is a formal version of this for many predictors.
  3. Use regularisation (Ridge / Lasso). These modified regression methods accept multicollinearity gracefully — they shrink unstable coefficients toward zero in a controlled way. Module 5.7 covers these in depth.

The right fix depends on the goal. For interpretation, dropping or combining is cleaner. For prediction, regularisation often gives the best results without losing information.

When to worry vs not

How Strict Should You Be?

Worry strongly when

  • You need to interpret individual coefficients
  • You're publishing or making policy decisions
  • The model is regulated or audited
  • VIF > 10 for any predictor

You can mostly ignore it when

  • You only care about predicting ŷ for new cases
  • You're using regularised methods (Ridge / Lasso)
  • Predictions for new data stay inside the training range
  • VIF below 5 for all predictors
⟁ Worked Example

Example: A Salary Model With and Without Age

An analyst fits a model of monthly salary against three predictors: years of experience, age in years, and years of education.

Run 1 — All three predictors included:

Salary = 18,000 + 1,200·Exp + 800·Age + 4,500·Edu

Looks reasonable at first. But the diagnostic report shows:

  • VIF for Experience = 9.2 (high)
  • VIF for Age = 8.7 (high)
  • VIF for Education = 1.3 (fine)
  • Standard error on Experience = ±900 (huge)
  • p-value for Experience = 0.18 (not significant!)

The high VIFs for Age and Experience flag the problem — these two are saying nearly the same thing. The analyst drops Age and refits.

Run 2 — Age removed:

Salary = 22,500 + 2,100·Exp + 4,400·Edu

Now:

  • VIF for Experience = 1.2 (fine)
  • VIF for Education = 1.2 (fine)
  • Standard error on Experience = ±150 (tight)
  • p-value for Experience = 0.001 (highly significant)

The new coefficient on Experience is larger (₹2,100 vs ₹1,200) and much more reliable. The previous Run 1 was splitting credit between two overlapping predictors.

The lesson: Multicollinearity hides the true effect of each predictor by splitting it across the overlapping pair. Removing or combining redundant predictors restores stability and reveals the real relationship.
Real-life examples

Where Multicollinearity Hides in Real Data

📣 Marketing Channels

TV, digital and out-of-home ad spend often move together — campaigns ramp up across channels simultaneously. Multicollinearity makes it hard to attribute sales to a single channel.

🧬 Genetic Studies

Genes in the same pathway are highly correlated. Naive regression of disease risk on many genes produces unstable coefficients. Specialised methods (Lasso, group regularisation) are used.

📐 Body Measurements

Height, weight, BMI, waist, hip — all carry overlapping info about body size. Health studies must carefully choose which to include to avoid multicollinearity.

📉 Economic Indicators

GDP, employment, consumer spending, retail sales all move together. Macroeconomic models often face severe multicollinearity, hence Ridge regression is common.

🏠 Real-Estate Features

House size, number of rooms, and number of bathrooms are linked. Including all three blindly creates multicollinearity. Often analysts combine into "total liveable area."

🤖 ML Feature Engineering

Engineered features (ratios, differences, lags) are often heavily correlated with their parents. Feature-selection pipelines explicitly remove highly collinear features.

Topic 4
Residual Analysis
What it is

What is Residual Analysis?

In Part 1 we met residuals — the gaps between actual y and predicted ŷ. Residual analysis is the practice of inspecting these residuals systematically to check whether the fitted model is trustworthy.

The fitted coefficients alone do not tell you whether a model is good. Even a poorly chosen model will return some β values — those are just numbers from the least-squares formula. The real question is: did the model capture the structure of the data, or did it leave hidden patterns in the residuals?

If residuals look random and shapeless, the model has done its job. If residuals show structure — a curve, a fan, a clustered tail — the model has missed something. Residual analysis is how we find out.

Simple meaning: Residual analysis is the after-fit inspection. Once the line (or hyperplane) is drawn, you look at what's left over. Random leftovers = a good fit. Patterned leftovers = a hidden problem.
Why we do it

Why Coefficients Without Diagnostics Are Half the Story

Three reasons every fitted model deserves a diagnostic check before being shared:

  • Assumption verification: the four LINE assumptions (Linearity, Independence, Normality, Equal variance) all show up in residual plots. Without checking, you cannot trust standard errors, p-values, or confidence intervals.
  • Outlier detection: a single unusual point can dominate a fit. Residuals are the natural place to spot such points and decide whether to keep, transform, or remove them.
  • Model improvement: patterns in residuals tell you what's missing. A curve in residuals suggests adding a non-linear term. A fan suggests log-transforming y. Diagnostics drive the next iteration of the model.
Big idea: Diagnostics are not a bureaucratic step. They are the feedback loop that turns "ran the regression" into "actually understand the relationship." Skipping them is like writing code without testing it.
Why it's used in practice

The Four Core Diagnostic Plots

1. Residuals vs Fitted

  • x-axis: predicted ŷ, y-axis: residual e
  • Detects: linearity, equal variance, outliers
  • Healthy = shapeless cloud around zero

2. Q-Q Plot of Residuals

  • Plots sorted residual values vs theoretical bell-curve values
  • Detects: normality of residuals
  • Healthy = points lie on a straight diagonal

3. Scale-Location Plot

  • x-axis: fitted ŷ, y-axis: √|standardised residual|
  • Detects: equal variance more sensitively
  • Healthy = flat horizontal trend

4. Residuals vs Leverage

  • x-axis: leverage, y-axis: standardised residual
  • Detects: influential points
  • Healthy = no points outside Cook's distance lines
How to use them

Outliers · Leverage · Influence — Three Different Things

Beginners often lump these together. They are related but distinct concepts:

Outlier

  • A point with a large residual
  • The line missed it badly
  • Not all outliers move the line — only the ones with leverage do

Leverage

  • A point with extreme predictor values
  • Sits far from the average x
  • Has potential to pull the line, but might still be on it

Influence

  • Outlier + Leverage = real influence
  • Removing the point would change the coefficients significantly
  • Measured by Cook's distance

A high-leverage point that sits perfectly on the line is fine — it stabilises the fit. An outlier with low leverage adds noise but doesn't pull the line. Only an outlier with leverage is dangerous — that's the combination that distorts coefficients.

Standardised vs raw residuals

Diagnostic plots usually show standardised residuals — residuals divided by their estimated standard deviation. This makes them comparable across observations regardless of leverage. As a rule of thumb, any standardised residual with absolute value above 2 deserves a look, above 3 is concerning.

Cook's distance — the single influence number

Cook's distance combines residual size and leverage into one number per observation. It measures how much the fitted line would shift if that single point were removed. Points with Cook's distance above 1 (or above 4/n for smaller samples) are flagged as influential.

⟁ Key Diagnostic Quantities
Standardised residual rᵢ = eᵢ / (s × √(1 − hᵢᵢ))

What each symbol means:

eᵢ — raw residual for observation i
s — estimated standard deviation of residuals (overall noise level)
hᵢᵢ — leverage of observation i (between 0 and 1)
rᵢ — standardised residual, comparable across all observations

The standardised version puts every residual on the same scale. Without this scaling, a small residual at a high-leverage point can look harmless but actually be unusual relative to what we'd expect there.

Cook's distance Dᵢ = (rᵢ²/p) × (hᵢᵢ / (1 − hᵢᵢ))

What each symbol means:

p — total number of predictors (plus intercept)
rᵢ — standardised residual for observation i
hᵢᵢ — leverage of observation i
Dᵢ — Cook's distance — a single influence score per observation

How to read it: Cook's distance multiplies "how big is the residual" (rᵢ²) by "how leveraged is the point" (hᵢᵢ / (1 − hᵢᵢ)). Both factors must be substantial to produce a large Cook's distance. A point can have a huge residual but tiny leverage and still be harmless; or huge leverage but tiny residual and still be fine. Only when both are present does the point pull the line significantly.

Threshold: Dᵢ > 1 is a strong warning. Dᵢ > 4/n is a milder flag for smaller samples. Software typically marks any flagged point on the residuals-vs-leverage plot.

How to use them

The Diagnostic Workflow — Step by Step

  1. Fit the model. You now have β coefficients and residuals.
  2. Plot Residuals vs Fitted. Look for shapeless cloud around zero. Flag any curve or fan.
  3. Plot Q-Q. Look for points on the diagonal. Heavy tails or S-shapes signal non-normal residuals.
  4. Plot Scale-Location. Look for flat trend. Rising trend signals growing variance.
  5. Plot Residuals vs Leverage. Look for points outside Cook's distance lines.
  6. Investigate flagged points. For each outlier or influential point, ask: data error, genuine unusual case, or hint at a missing variable?
  7. Take action. Transform variables, add terms, drop bad data, or use a different model class.
  8. Refit and recheck. Diagnostics is iterative — keep going until residuals look healthy.

When & how often

How Often Should You Run Diagnostics?

  • Every time you fit a regression you intend to share, publish, or deploy. No exceptions.
  • After any data update — new observations can introduce new outliers or change leverage patterns.
  • After adding or removing predictors — model structure changes the residual landscape.
  • Before drawing strong conclusions — especially around p-values, effect sizes, and policy implications.
⟁ Worked Example

Example: Diagnosing a Marketing Regression

A marketing team fits a model of weekly sales against TV spend, digital spend, and promotional discount intensity:

Sales = 120 + 0.85·TV + 1.4·Digital + 22·Discount

The fit looks reasonable. But before deploying, the team runs diagnostics on 100 weeks of data:

  • Residual vs Fitted: shapeless cloud around zero — ✅ linearity holds.
  • Q-Q plot: points lie on the diagonal except for two clearly above the line on the right tail — ⚠️ two high-residual weeks.
  • Scale-Location: flat — ✅ equal variance holds.
  • Residuals vs Leverage: one point sits outside Cook's distance threshold (Dᵢ = 1.4) — ⚠️ influential point.

Investigation: the influential week was Diwali, when discount went to 60% (vs typical 10-15%) and sales spiked. The week is genuine, not a data error — but it's an extreme observation pulling the discount coefficient upward.

Action taken: the team refits the model without that week, gets a discount coefficient of 18 (vs 22). They report both — "discount effect is ₹18 on typical weeks, ₹22 if extreme promotional weeks are included." Honest, more useful, and decision-makers now know about the dependence on extreme weeks.

The lesson: Diagnostics turn a single number into a nuanced understanding. Influential points are not automatic problems — they are signals to investigate. The right response is rarely "delete" — it's usually "report transparently and refit to understand."
Real-life examples

Where Residual Analysis Saves Real Models

🏦 Credit Scoring

Banks run residual diagnostics on credit risk models monthly. Drifting residual patterns flag changes in customer behaviour (e.g. recession indicators).

🌡️ Climate Modelling

Climate scientists check residuals from temperature models for autocorrelation and trend leftovers. Diagnostic failures indicate missing physical mechanisms.

🚚 Logistics Forecasting

Delivery time models are diagnosed weekly. Fan-shaped residuals reveal that long-distance deliveries have more variable times — handled by log-transform.

💊 Clinical Trials

Drug efficacy models are required to include diagnostics in regulatory submissions. Outlier patients are flagged, investigated, and reported — never silently dropped.

🎓 Education Research

Models of student outcomes against school inputs are checked for influential schools. Often a single outlier school changes the whole policy conclusion.

⚙️ Industrial Quality Control

Manufacturing process models run continuous diagnostics. Pattern changes in residuals signal equipment drift before defects appear in production.
