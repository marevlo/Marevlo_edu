◉ MODULE 4.7 · DISTRIBUTION-FREE METHODS

Non-Parametric Tests

Tests that work on the order (ranks) of values rather than the values themselves — so they remain valid when the data is not normal.

The t-tests and ANOVA studied earlier assume that the data follows a normal distribution. When this assumption is not satisfied — for example, with skewed data, ordinal data, or small samples — non-parametric tests provide reliable alternatives. These tests work on the ranks of the observations, which makes them robust to outliers and free from any distributional assumption. This module covers the three most widely used non-parametric tests: the Mann–Whitney U test for two independent samples, the Wilcoxon signed-rank test for paired samples, and the Kruskal–Wallis test for three or more groups. Every formula is explained symbol by symbol, with worked examples, two interactive simulations, multiple-choice questions, and an AI-evaluated descriptive section.

◆ 01
Ranks and Non-Parametric Methods
⟁ WHAT IS A RANK

From values to ranks

The rank of a value is its position when all values are sorted from smallest to largest. The smallest value gets rank 1, the next gets rank 2, and so on. Identical values share an averaged rank, called a tied rank.

Example

Suppose we have six observations: 12, 8, 15, 10, 8, 20. Sorted, they become 8, 8, 10, 12, 15, 20. The two values of 8 share ranks 1 and 2, so each receives the average rank (1 + 2) / 2 = 1.5. The remaining ranks follow naturally:

Value 8 8 10 12 15 20
Rank 1.5 1.5 3 4 5 6
⟁ PARAMETRIC VS NON-PARAMETRIC

Two families of tests

A parametric test assumes that the data follows a specific distribution — most often the normal distribution — and uses parameters like the mean and standard deviation. A non-parametric test makes no such assumption. It works on the order of values rather than the values themselves.

AspectParametric (t-tests, ANOVA)Non-parametric (rank tests)
AssumptionNormal distribution requiredNo distribution assumed
Works onRaw valuesRanks of values
ComparesMeansMedians or distributions
OutliersStrongly affectedRobust — outliers become extreme ranks only
PowerHigher when assumptions holdSlightly lower when normal data
Use whenData is approximately normalSkewed, ordinal, or small samples

Why ranks help

  • Robustness. Replacing a value of 1000 with 5000 affects the mean dramatically, but its rank remains the largest — unchanged.
  • Distribution-free. The rank of a value does not depend on the underlying distribution; only on its position.
  • Applies to ordinal data. Likert-scale survey responses and other ordinal scales can be ranked but not always meaningfully averaged.
Trade-off. Rank-based tests slightly lose statistical power compared to their parametric counterparts when the normality assumption holds. They are preferred whenever the assumption is in doubt.
◆ 02
Mann–Whitney U Test
⟁ TWO INDEPENDENT SAMPLES

What it does

The Mann–Whitney U test compares two independent groups to test whether the values in one tend to be larger or smaller than the values in the other. It is the non-parametric counterpart of the two-sample t-test. Instead of comparing the two means, it compares the two rank sums.

Hypotheses

H₀: The two populations have the same distribution.
H₁: One population tends to produce larger values than the other.

Procedure

Pool the two samples into one combined list, sort it from smallest to largest, and assign ranks. Then compute the rank sum for each group. The U statistic is built from these rank sums.

⟁ MANN–WHITNEY U STATISTIC
U₁ = n₁·n₂ + n₁(n₁+1)/2 − R₁
Compute U for one group from its rank sum. The U for the other group satisfies U₁ + U₂ = n₁·n₂. The test uses the smaller of the two values.
⟁ SYMBOL EXPLANATION
U₁
The U statistic for group 1. The smaller of U₁ and U₂ is used as the final test statistic.
n₁
Sample size of group 1.
n₂
Sample size of group 2.
n₁·n₂
The product of the two sample sizes — the maximum possible value of U.
R₁
The rank sum of group 1 — the sum of the ranks assigned to all observations in group 1.
n₁(n₁+1)/2
A correction term that depends on the size of group 1.
⟁ DECISION RULE

How to interpret U

If the two groups have similar distributions, the rank sums R₁ and R₂ should be roughly proportional to their sample sizes, making U₁ and U₂ close in value. If one group consistently has larger values, its rank sum will be much larger, and one of the U values will be small — signalling evidence against H₀.

For small samples, U is compared to critical values in a Mann–Whitney table. For larger samples (each group with at least 10 observations), U is converted to a z-score using its known mean and standard deviation under H₀.

⟁ WORKED EXAMPLE — COMPARING TWO TRAINING PROGRAMS

Problem. Two training programs are evaluated on the productivity scores of their participants. Program A (n₁ = 5): 22, 28, 25, 30, 27. Program B (n₂ = 5): 18, 24, 20, 23, 19. Test at α = 0.05 whether the two programs differ.

Step 1 — Pool and sort

Combined sorted values: 18, 19, 20, 22, 23, 24, 25, 27, 28, 30

Step 2 — Assign ranks

18(B)=1, 19(B)=2, 20(B)=3, 22(A)=4, 23(B)=5, 24(B)=6, 25(A)=7, 27(A)=8, 28(A)=9, 30(A)=10

Step 3 — Rank sums
R₁ (A) = 4 + 7 + 8 + 9 + 10 = 38
R₂ (B) = 1 + 2 + 3 + 5 + 6 = 17
Step 4 — Compute U
U₁ = 5·5 + 5·6/2 − 38 = 25 + 15 − 38 = 2
U₂ = 5·5 + 5·6/2 − 17 = 25 + 15 − 17 = 23
U = min(U₁, U₂) = 2
Step 5 — Decision

For n₁ = n₂ = 5 and α = 0.05 (two-tailed), the critical value of U is 2. Since the observed U is 2, we reject H₀. There is evidence that the two programs produce different productivity outcomes (with Program A scoring consistently higher).

◆ 03
Wilcoxon Signed-Rank Test
⟁ PAIRED OBSERVATIONS

What it does

The Wilcoxon signed-rank test is the non-parametric counterpart of the paired t-test. It is used when the same subjects are measured twice (for example, before and after a treatment) and the differences are not normally distributed.

Hypotheses

H₀: The median of the differences is zero (no change).
H₁: The median of the differences is not zero (there is a change).

Procedure

  1. Compute the differences d_i = (after) − (before) for each pair.
  2. Discard any difference equal to zero.
  3. Take the absolute value |d_i| of each remaining difference and assign ranks (smallest |d_i| gets rank 1).
  4. Attach the original sign of each difference back to its rank — these are the signed ranks.
  5. Compute W⁺ (sum of positive signed ranks) and W⁻ (sum of absolute negative signed ranks). The test statistic is the smaller of these two.
⟁ WILCOXON SIGNED-RANK STATISTIC
W = min( W⁺, W⁻ )
The Wilcoxon test statistic is the smaller of the positive and negative rank sums. A small W indicates that one sign dominates, providing evidence against H₀.
⟁ SYMBOL EXPLANATION
W
The Wilcoxon test statistic.
W⁺
The sum of ranks of differences that are positive (cases where the "after" value is larger).
W⁻
The sum of ranks of differences that are negative (cases where the "after" value is smaller). Ranks are taken from |d_i|.
d_i
The difference for pair i, computed as (second measurement) − (first measurement).
|d_i|
The absolute value of the difference — used for ranking.
min(·,·)
The minimum function — returns the smaller of the two values.
⟁ DECISION RULE

How to interpret W

If H₀ is true (the median of the differences is zero), positive and negative differences should be roughly balanced, making W⁺ and W⁻ close in value. If the treatment has a real effect, one of the sums becomes much smaller than the other, leading to a small W. For small samples, W is compared to critical values in a Wilcoxon table; for larger samples (typically n ≥ 20 non-zero pairs), W is converted to a z-score.

◆ 04
Kruskal–Wallis Test
⟁ THREE OR MORE GROUPS

What it does

The Kruskal–Wallis test generalizes the Mann–Whitney test to three or more independent groups. It is the non-parametric counterpart of one-way ANOVA. Instead of comparing group means, it compares the rank sums of the groups.

Hypotheses

H₀: All k groups come from the same distribution (equal medians).
H₁: At least one group has a different distribution.

Procedure

  1. Combine all observations from all groups into one pool.
  2. Sort the pooled values and assign ranks (with averaged ranks for ties).
  3. Compute the rank sum Rᵢ for each group i.
  4. Compute the H statistic.
  5. Compare H to a chi-square distribution with k − 1 degrees of freedom.
⟁ KRUSKAL–WALLIS H STATISTIC
H = [ 12 / ( N(N+1) ) ] · Σᵢ ( Rᵢ² / nᵢ ) − 3(N+1)
The H statistic measures how unequal the rank sums are across the groups. Larger values of H indicate stronger evidence that the groups differ. Under H₀, H approximately follows a chi-square distribution with k − 1 degrees of freedom.
⟁ SYMBOL EXPLANATION
H
The Kruskal–Wallis test statistic.
N
The total number of observations across all groups.
k
The number of groups being compared.
nᵢ
The sample size of group i.
Rᵢ
The rank sum of group i — the sum of the ranks assigned to observations in that group.
Rᵢ²
The square of the rank sum of group i.
Σᵢ
Sum over all groups from i = 1 to i = k.
df
Degrees of freedom = k − 1, used for the chi-square reference distribution.
⟁ WORKED EXAMPLE — COMPARING THREE BRANCHES

Problem. A company measures customer-satisfaction scores at three branches. Branch A (n=4): 70, 75, 78, 72. Branch B (n=4): 65, 60, 68, 62. Branch C (n=4): 80, 82, 85, 78. Test at α = 0.05 whether the three branches differ.

Step 1 — Pool and rank

Sorted: 60(B), 62(B), 65(B), 68(B), 70(A), 72(A), 75(A), 78(A), 78(C), 80(C), 82(C), 85(C)

Ranks: 60→1, 62→2, 65→3, 68→4, 70→5, 72→6, 75→7, 78→8.5 (tie), 78→8.5 (tie), 80→10, 82→11, 85→12

Step 2 — Rank sums
R_A = 5 + 6 + 7 + 8.5 = 26.5
R_B = 1 + 2 + 3 + 4 = 10
R_C = 8.5 + 10 + 11 + 12 = 41.5
Step 3 — Compute H
N = 12, k = 3, each nᵢ = 4
Σ Rᵢ²/nᵢ = 26.5²/4 + 10²/4 + 41.5²/4 = 175.5625 + 25 + 430.5625 = 631.125
H = [12/(12·13)] · 631.125 − 3·13 = (12/156)·631.125 − 39
H = 0.07692 · 631.125 − 39 = 48.548 − 39 = 9.548
Step 4 — Decision

df = k − 1 = 2. For χ² ≈ 9.548 with df = 2, the p-value ≈ 0.0085. Since 0.0085 ≤ 0.05, reject H₀. There is evidence that the three branches have different satisfaction scores.

◆ 05
Choosing Between Parametric and Non-Parametric Tests
⟁ DECISION GUIDE

Each non-parametric test studied in this module has a parametric counterpart. The following table summarizes the correspondence and helps you choose the right method.

SituationParametric TestNon-Parametric Test
One sample, compare against a fixed valueOne-sample t-testWilcoxon signed-rank (on d_i = x_i − μ₀)
Two independent samplesTwo-sample t-testMann–Whitney U test
Paired (matched) samplesPaired t-testWilcoxon signed-rank test
Three or more independent groupsOne-way ANOVAKruskal–Wallis H test

Practical guidelines

  • If the data is approximately normal and sample sizes are reasonable, the parametric test is preferred — it has slightly higher statistical power.
  • If the data is clearly skewed, has heavy outliers, or is on an ordinal scale, use the non-parametric alternative.
  • For very small samples (n < 15), non-parametric tests are typically safer because normality cannot be assessed reliably.
  • When in doubt, run both tests. If they agree, the conclusion is robust. If they disagree, examine the data for distributional issues.
◆ 06
Real-World Applications

Clinical Research

Comparing recovery scores or pain ratings on ordinal scales, where the underlying distribution is often skewed and rank tests give valid results.

Survey Analysis

Comparing Likert-scale responses across groups. Likert data is ordinal, so rank-based methods are the appropriate inferential tools.

Quality Control

Detecting differences between production lines using small samples or visually-graded scores where outliers are common.

Machine Learning Model Comparison

Comparing model performance across many datasets, where the accuracy distribution is highly skewed and a few outliers dominate.

Behavioural and Social Sciences

Comparing response-time or ranked-preference data, where assumptions of normality are rarely satisfied.

Economics and Finance

Testing whether returns from different strategies differ when the return distributions have heavy tails and outliers.
