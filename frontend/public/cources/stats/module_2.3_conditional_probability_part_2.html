Independence & Total Probability

We'll dissect independent vs. dependent events, untangle the classic confusion with mutual exclusivity, and end with the Law of Total Probability — the bridge that sets up Bayes' theorem in Part 3.

2.3.5
Independent Events
What independence means

Information That Doesn't Transfer

Two events are independent if knowing whether one occurred provides zero information about whether the other occurred. The events live in completely separate causal worlds — they don't influence each other, don't share underlying mechanisms, and don't update each other's probability.

The formal characterization is beautifully simple. If A and B are independent, then learning B happened doesn't change our estimate of A:

P(A | B) = P(A)

The conditional probability is just the unconditional probability. The information transfer is zero. Compare this to P(A | B) > P(A) (B makes A more likely — positive dependence) or P(A | B) < P(A) (B makes A less likely — negative dependence).

Memory hook: Independence means "B doesn't talk to A." If you know B, your belief about A stays exactly where it was.
⟁ Mathematical Definition of Independence
P(A ∩ B) = P(A) × P(B)

Derivation: Start with the multiplication rule from Part 1: P(A ∩ B) = P(A | B) × P(B). If A and B are independent, then P(A | B) = P(A). Substituting gives P(A ∩ B) = P(A) × P(B). This is the cleanest test for independence — just check whether the joint probability factors into the product of marginals.

Three equivalent ways to state independence:

P(A | B) = P(A) — B tells us nothing about A
P(B | A) = P(B) — A tells us nothing about B
P(A ∩ B) = P(A) × P(B) — joint factors into marginals
Coin examples

The Classic Coin Flip Pair

Flip a fair coin twice. Let A = "first flip is Heads" and B = "second flip is Heads."

  • P(A) = 0.5 — the first coin doesn't care about anything
  • P(B) = 0.5 — the second coin doesn't care about the first
  • P(A ∩ B) = P(both Heads) = 1/4 = 0.25
  • P(A) × P(B) = 0.5 × 0.5 = 0.25 ✓

The joint factors exactly into the product, so A and B are independent. This is exactly what physical intuition tells us: a coin has no memory. The second flip is a fresh experiment regardless of what happened on the first.

The gambler's fallacy is the false belief that a long streak of Heads makes Tails "due." For independent events, this is mathematically impossible. The coin doesn't remember. After 10 Heads in a row, P(next Heads) is still exactly 0.5.
Dice examples

Two Dice — When Independence Holds and When It Doesn't

Roll two distinct dice. Let A = "first die shows 6" and B = "second die shows 6."

P(A) = 1/6, P(B) = 1/6. P(A ∩ B) = 1/36 = (1/6)(1/6). ✓ The two dice are physically independent.

But now consider a more subtle case. Same two dice, but redefine:

  • A = "first die shows 6"
  • C = "sum of both dice is greater than 8"

P(A) = 1/6. P(C) = 10/36 (count: 9,9,9,9 for sum=9; etc.) ≈ 0.278. P(A ∩ C) = 4/36 (rolls (6,3), (6,4), (6,5), (6,6)) ≈ 0.111.

P(A) × P(C) = (1/6)(10/36) ≈ 0.046. But P(A ∩ C) ≈ 0.111. These are different. So A and C are not independent — knowing the first die is 6 makes a high sum much more likely.

Lesson: Independence is a property of events, not of the underlying experiments. Two dice are physically independent — but events you construct from them may or may not be statistically independent. Always check with the formula.

Real-world examples

Where Independence Lives in the Wild

🎰 Casino Games

Successive roulette spins are independent. Past spins do not influence future ones. Casinos rely on this — no streak strategy can beat the house, mathematically.

📡 Network Packets

In well-designed packet networks, the probability of any one packet being dropped is approximately independent of other packets, allowing engineers to model end-to-end reliability cleanly.

🧬 Genetic Loci

Distant genes on different chromosomes assort independently during meiosis (Mendel's second law). This is the foundation of classical genetics.

🔬 Random Sampling

When samples are drawn with replacement (or from an infinite population), each draw is independent of every other. This is the bedrock assumption of the Central Limit Theorem.

🤖 Naive Bayes Classifier

The "naive" assumption is that features are conditionally independent given the class. It's often technically wrong, but powerful enough to drive spam filters, sentiment analysis, and document categorization.

⏱️ Poisson Processes

The number of events in disjoint time intervals (calls to a call center, photons hitting a detector) is independent under Poisson assumptions — the basis of queueing theory.

2.3.6
Dependent Events
Information transfer intuition

When One Event Tells You Something About Another

Two events are dependent if knowing one changes the probability of the other. Formally, A and B are dependent if and only if P(A | B) ≠ P(A). The information transfer is nonzero.

Dependence comes in two flavors:

Positive Dependence

  • P(A | B) > P(A) — B makes A more likely
  • Knowing it's cloudy increases the probability of rain
  • Knowing the email contains "free" increases P(spam)
  • Knowing a customer bought bread increases P(bought butter)

Negative Dependence

  • P(A | B) < P(A) — B makes A less likely
  • Knowing a card is a King reduces P(it's a Queen)
  • Knowing a student is a STEM major reduces P(humanities major)
  • Knowing it's morning reduces P(restaurant is open for dinner)
Sampling without replacement — the canonical dependent case

The "Without Replacement" Trap

The most common source of dependence in introductory problems is sampling without replacement. Every time you draw without putting the object back, you change the composition of what remains — and therefore change every future probability.

Classic setup: a deck of 52 cards. Let A = "1st card is an Ace" and B = "2nd card is an Ace."

  • P(A) = 4/52 = 1/13 ≈ 0.077
  • P(B | A) = 3/51 ≈ 0.059 — one Ace already gone
  • P(B | not A) = 4/51 ≈ 0.078 — all four Aces still in the remaining 51 cards

The conditional probabilities are different, so A and B are dependent. Drawing an Ace first transfers information — it tells the second draw "one fewer Ace is available."

With Replacement

  • Card is returned and deck reshuffled before next draw
  • Each draw is identical and independent
  • P(B) = P(B | A) = 4/52 always
  • Mathematical simplicity

Without Replacement

  • Card stays out after each draw
  • Each draw depends on all previous draws
  • Probabilities change at every step
  • Use the multiplication rule with conditional probabilities
Updating probabilities

Dependence as the Source of Learning

Dependence is not a "problem" — it's the fundamental mechanism of learning from data. Every machine learning algorithm exploits dependence between features and labels to make predictions. If features were truly independent of the label, no learning would be possible.

Examples of productive dependence:

  • Spam filter: The word "viagra" is heavily dependent on the label "spam." Useful.
  • Medical diagnosis: Fever is dependent on the presence of infection. Useful.
  • Credit scoring: Payment history is dependent on default risk. Useful.
  • Recommendation: Past purchases are dependent on future preferences. Useful.
Insight for AI/ML: Independence is mathematically clean but informationally empty. Dependence is messy but rich. All predictive systems harvest useful dependencies to make accurate forecasts.

2.3.7
Mutual Exclusivity vs Independence
⚠ Critical Confusion Alert: Mutual exclusivity and independence are completely different concepts, but their names sound similar enough that almost every beginner conflates them. This single confusion has caused more wrong probability answers than any other mistake. We will untangle it now — permanently.
The two definitions side by side

What Each Term Actually Means

Mutually Exclusive (Disjoint)

  • The two events cannot both happen at the same time
  • Their intersection is empty: A ∩ B = ∅
  • Mathematically: P(A ∩ B) = 0
  • It's about physical/logical impossibility of co-occurrence
  • Example: "Roll a 3" and "Roll a 5" on the same die — only one can happen

Independent

  • The two events don't influence each other
  • Knowing one tells you nothing about the other
  • Mathematically: P(A ∩ B) = P(A) × P(B)
  • It's about informational/statistical isolation
  • Example: "Coin 1 = H" and "Coin 2 = T" — both can happen, neither affects the other
Why they are NOT the same — the crucial theorem

The Surprising Theorem That Resolves Everything

Here's a fact that initially feels paradoxical but becomes obvious once you see it:

If A and B are mutually exclusive AND both have nonzero probability, then they are NECESSARILY DEPENDENT.

Proof: Suppose A and B are mutually exclusive with P(A) > 0 and P(B) > 0. Then P(A ∩ B) = 0. If they were also independent, we'd need P(A ∩ B) = P(A) × P(B) > 0. Contradiction.

Intuition: Think about it this way. If A and B can't both happen, then learning that B happened tells you something huge about A — namely, that A definitely did not happen. P(A | B) = 0. That's a massive information transfer. Massive dependence. The opposite of independence.

Common mistakes

The Specific Errors People Make

Mistake 1: "These two events can't happen together, so they're independent."   Wrong. They can't happen together means they're mutually exclusive, which (assuming nonzero probabilities) makes them dependent, not independent.
Mistake 2: "P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B) for independent events."   Wrong. That formula is for mutually exclusive events. For independent events: P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B) − P(A) × P(B).
Mistake 3: "If A and B are independent, then they're unrelated, so they can't both happen."   Wrong. Independent events can absolutely both happen — they just happen with the product of their individual probabilities. Two coins can both land Heads.
Mistake 4: "Disjoint and independent mean the same thing in probability."   Wrong. They are almost opposites. Disjoint events convey maximal information (one happening rules out the other); independent events convey zero information.
2.3.8
Law of Total Probability
Partition intuition

Slicing the Universe Into Disjoint Cases

Sometimes we don't know P(A) directly, but we know how A behaves under different conditions. The Law of Total Probability lets us assemble the unconditional probability from a collection of conditional probabilities by carefully accounting for every possible "case."

The key concept is a partition. A partition of the sample space is a collection of events B₁, B₂, …, Bₙ that:

  • are mutually exclusive (no two overlap) — Bᵢ ∩ Bⱼ = ∅ for i ≠ j
  • are exhaustive (together they cover everything) — B₁ ∪ B₂ ∪ … ∪ Bₙ = Ω

Picture this: the partition slices Ω into clean, non-overlapping pieces — like cutting a pizza. Every outcome lies in exactly one slice.

⟁ Partition Visualization
Watch how event A (the cyan curve) is decomposed into pieces, one inside each partition slice.
Event A (the cyan ellipse) slices across all four partition pieces. The total probability of A is the sum of A's portions inside each Bᵢ. This is the geometric heart of the Law of Total Probability.
⟁ Law of Total Probability
P(A) = Σᵢ P(A | Bᵢ) × P(Bᵢ)

Where B₁, B₂, …, Bₙ form a partition of Ω. Expanded out for a 3-part partition:

P(A) = P(A | B₁)P(B₁) + P(A | B₂)P(B₂) + P(A | B₃)P(B₃)

Why it works: Since the Bᵢ are mutually exclusive and exhaustive, every outcome in A belongs to exactly one Bᵢ. So A decomposes as A = (A ∩ B₁) ∪ (A ∩ B₂) ∪ … ∪ (A ∩ Bₙ), and these pieces don't overlap. The third Kolmogorov axiom (additivity for disjoint events) gives P(A) = Σ P(A ∩ Bᵢ). Then the multiplication rule rewrites each P(A ∩ Bᵢ) as P(A | Bᵢ) × P(Bᵢ).

Hidden scenarios — a classic worked example

Worked Example — Defective Items From Two Factories

A company sources widgets from two factories. Factory 1 supplies 70% of widgets and produces 2% defects. Factory 2 supplies 30% and produces 5% defects. What is the overall probability that a randomly chosen widget is defective?

Step 1 — Identify the partition. B₁ = "from Factory 1", B₂ = "from Factory 2". These partition all widgets (mutually exclusive, exhaustive). P(B₁) = 0.70, P(B₂) = 0.30.

Step 2 — Identify the conditional probabilities. Let D = "widget is defective". P(D | B₁) = 0.02, P(D | B₂) = 0.05.

Step 3 — Apply the law. P(D) = P(D | B₁)P(B₁) + P(D | B₂)P(B₂) = (0.02)(0.70) + (0.05)(0.30) = 0.014 + 0.015 = 0.029.

Conclusion: 2.9% of all widgets are defective. We computed an unconditional probability from a set of conditional probabilities — that's the magic of the partition.

Why it matters for Bayes

The Bridge to Bayes' Theorem (Part 3 Preview)

The Law of Total Probability is the workhorse denominator of Bayes' theorem. Whenever you see Bayes' formula written as:

P(A | B) = P(B | A) × P(A) / P(B)

The P(B) in the denominator is almost always computed using the Law of Total Probability:

P(B) = P(B | A) × P(A) + P(B | ¬A) × P(¬A)

This is why mastering total probability now is essential — it's the gateway to every Bayesian computation we'll do in Part 3.
