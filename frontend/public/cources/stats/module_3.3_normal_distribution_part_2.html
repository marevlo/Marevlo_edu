Standardization — Turning Any Normal into Standard Normal
The universal translator

What is Standardization?

Remember from Part 1: a Z-score tells you how many standard deviations a value is from the mean. Standardization is just the process of taking a whole bunch of values from any normal distribution and converting all of them into Z-scores at once.

It uses the exact same formula we learned:

Z = (X − μ) / σ

The result: every original value becomes a Z-score in N(0, 1). Once you've done this translation, you can compare anything to anything, look up probabilities in a single universal Z-table, or feed the data into a machine learning model.

Mental picture: Imagine every Normal distribution is a different language. Standardization translates each one into a single universal language — N(0, 1). Once translated, everyone reads from the same dictionary.
A simple analogy

Standardization is Like Currency Conversion

Imagine you have:

  • $100 in your pocket (USD)
  • €85 in another wallet (EUR)
  • ¥12,000 in a third (JPY)

Which is most? You can't tell just by looking — they're in different currencies. But if you convert all three to one common currency (say USD), suddenly you can compare them directly.

Standardization is the same idea, but for normal distributions. Every distribution has its own "currency" (its own μ and σ). Z-scores are the universal currency — they let you compare apples to oranges to bananas. Fair, clean, comparable.

Why bother standardizing?

Three Big Reasons to Standardize

1. Compute Probabilities

  • Z-tables give probabilities for N(0, 1)
  • Standardize, then look up
  • Works for any μ, σ
  • One table → infinite distributions

2. Compare Across Scales

  • Test scores in different subjects
  • Heights (cm) vs weights (kg)
  • Salaries in different currencies
  • Anything measured differently

3. ML Preprocessing

  • Neural networks train faster
  • Gradient descent converges better
  • Features on similar scales
  • Standard step in every ML pipeline
A worked walkthrough

Example: Adult Heights

Adult male heights are roughly Normal with μ = 175 cm and σ = 7 cm. What is the probability that a random man is taller than 189 cm?

Step 1 — Standardize: Convert the height 189 cm into a Z-score.

Z = (189 − 175) / 7 = 14 / 7 = +2.00

Step 2 — Translate the question: "P(X > 189)" becomes "P(Z > 2.00)" in the standard normal.

Step 3 — Look up: From the Z-table (or software), P(Z > 2.00) ≈ 0.0228 ≈ 2.3%.

Interpretation: Only about 2.3% of adult men are taller than 189 cm. Notice we didn't need to plug into the complicated Normal formula at all — we just standardized and looked up a value.

The shortcut works for any normal distribution. Want to know the probability that a test score above 90 in a different class? Standardize first, then look up. Want the probability a stock return exceeds 5%? Standardize, then look up. The same single Z-table answers them all.

The reverse transformation

Going from Z back to X

Sometimes you start with a Z-score and want to find the corresponding original value. For example: "What value lies exactly 1.5 standard deviations above the mean?"

Just rearrange the Z-score formula:

⟁ Reverse Z-Score Formula
X = μ + Z · σ

In words: "Start at the mean, then walk Z standard deviations in the appropriate direction (positive = right, negative = left)."

Example: If adult heights are N(175, 49) (so σ = 7), what height corresponds to Z = 1.5?

X = 175 + 1.5 × 7 = 175 + 10.5 = 185.5 cm

So a man who is 185.5 cm tall sits exactly 1.5 σ above the average height.

The Empirical Rule — The 68-95-99.7 Magic
A rule you'll use forever

The Famous 68-95-99.7 Rule

For ANY Normal distribution — no matter the values of μ and σ — these three percentages always hold:

68%
μ ± 1σ
About 68% of values fall within 1 standard deviation of the mean
95%
μ ± 2σ
About 95% of values fall within 2 standard deviations of the mean
99.7%
μ ± 3σ
About 99.7% of values fall within 3 standard deviations of the mean
Why memorize this? These three numbers let you do quick mental probability estimates for any normal-shaped data — without tables, calculators, or software. Just remember the pattern: 68, 95, 99.7.
Where do these numbers come from?

Why "Empirical"?

The word "empirical" means "based on observation." These percentages come straight from the math of the bell curve — when you compute the area under the curve between μ−σ and μ+σ, you always get about 68%. Same for the other two zones.

They are universal — they apply to every normal distribution, from heights to test scores to particle physics measurements. The values of μ and σ don't matter. The percentages stay the same.

Practical use

Quick Mental Estimates

The empirical rule lets you do quick probability estimates without any tables or software:

  • "Roughly 2/3 of people" → within 1σ of the mean (68%)
  • "Roughly 95% of people" → within 2σ of the mean
  • "Almost everyone (99.7%)" → within 3σ of the mean
  • "Beyond 3σ" → rare event, only 0.3% chance
Concrete Example — IQ scores: IQ scores are roughly Normal with μ = 100, σ = 15.
  • 68% of people have IQ between 85 and 115 (the "average" range)
  • 95% of people have IQ between 70 and 130
  • 99.7% of people have IQ between 55 and 145
  • An IQ above 145 is extremely rare (about 0.15% of people)
More everyday examples

Empirical Rule in Action

Example 1 — Adult heights (men): N(175 cm, 49) → σ = 7 cm.

  • 68% of men: between 168 and 182 cm
  • 95% of men: between 161 and 189 cm
  • 99.7% of men: between 154 and 196 cm

Example 2 — Newborn weights: N(3.4 kg, 0.25) → σ = 0.5 kg.

  • 68% of newborns: between 2.9 and 3.9 kg
  • 95% of newborns: between 2.4 and 4.4 kg
  • 99.7% of newborns: between 1.9 and 4.9 kg

Example 3 — SAT scores (single section): N(500, 10000) → σ = 100.

  • 68% of test-takers: between 400 and 600
  • 95% of test-takers: between 300 and 700
  • 99.7% of test-takers: between 200 and 800 (the full score range!)
