Exploratory Data Analysis

Before models, before predictions, before any sophisticated machine learning — there is EDA. The disciplined art of looking at your data, finding what it tells you, and discovering what it hides.

This module covers the complete EDA workflow: walking through a real-world dataset, finding hidden patterns, detecting suspicious anomalies, generating hypotheses worth testing, and finally — telling a story with data that humans actually believe and act on. EDA is what separates a data scientist who actually understands their data from one who just runs algorithms on it.

What Is Exploratory Data Analysis?
Definition

The Idea

Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA) is the disciplined practice of examining a dataset to understand its structure, summarise its main characteristics, spot patterns, detect anomalies, and generate hypotheses — all before building any formal model or running any statistical test.

The term was coined by statistician John Tukey in 1977. His core message: "Look at the data first." Don't jump to models. Don't trust assumptions. Get your hands dirty. Use plots, summaries, and curiosity.

Mental model: EDA is to data what a doctor's examination is to a patient. Before prescribing anything, you check vital signs, listen carefully, look for unusual symptoms, and only then form a diagnosis. Skipping EDA is like prescribing medicine without ever examining the patient.
Why it matters

Why EDA Is Non-Negotiable

  • Models can lie convincingly. A regression with R² = 0.95 can be powered by one extreme outlier. Without EDA, you trust the number.
  • Bad inputs ruin good algorithms. Garbage in, garbage out. EDA reveals missing values, encoding errors, and impossible ranges before they corrupt downstream work.
  • Assumptions hide. Linear regression assumes linearity, normal residuals, constant variance. EDA tests all three before fitting.
  • Real insight comes from looking. The most impactful business discoveries — fraud rings, viral content patterns, hidden customer segments — almost always surface during EDA, not from the final model.
  • It's the cheapest insurance you can buy. Two hours of EDA prevents weeks of debugging downstream.
The Anscombe lesson: Four famous datasets (Anscombe's quartet) have identical means, identical variances, identical correlations, and identical regression lines — yet are visually completely different. Numbers alone can fool you. Plots cannot. EDA insists on plots.
When to use it

The "When" of EDA

Always Use EDA

  • Whenever you receive a new dataset
  • Before building any model
  • Before reporting any number to a stakeholder
  • After data ingestion changes
  • When a model behaves unexpectedly

Tempting to Skip — But Don't

  • "I already know this data" — schemas change silently
  • "It's just a quick prototype" — prototypes ship
  • "The team validated it" — validation can miss edge cases
  • "Time pressure" — bugs cost more than EDA does
The five pillars

What This Module Will Build

EDA has five core activities. We'll walk through each in turn:

  • ① Real-world dataset walkthrough — how to first examine a fresh dataset systematically.
  • ② Finding patterns — using plots and summaries to surface relationships.
  • ③ Detecting anomalies — spotting outliers, errors, and unusual records.
  • ④ Generating hypotheses — turning observations into testable questions.
  • ⑤ Data storytelling — translating findings into narratives that drive decisions.

Real-World Dataset Walkthrough
What — A systematic first look

The "First 10 Minutes" Protocol

When you receive a new dataset, the first thing you do is not jump to plots or models. You run a checklist — the same checklist every time. This protocol prevents you from missing structural problems that would silently corrupt all later work.

The checklist has five steps:

  • Shape — how many rows? How many columns? Is the dataset what you expected?
  • Types — what's the data type of each column? Numeric, categorical, dates, text?
  • Missing values — which columns have nulls, and how many?
  • Summaries — mean, median, min, max, unique counts. Anything that violates basic logic?
  • A few rows — actually look at 5–20 sample rows. What does a single record represent?
Why this protocol works: it forces you to confront the data before you confront your assumptions about the data. Almost every "we found a model bug after deployment" story traces back to a step that was skipped here.

⟁ Five-Number Summary — The First Plot Beyond the Mean
{ Min, Q1, Median, Q3, Max }

For every numeric column, compute these five numbers. Together they describe the distribution far better than the mean alone — and they're the foundation of the boxplot, the single most useful EDA visual.

Min — smallest value (check for impossible values like negative amounts)
Q1 — 25th percentile (bottom of the typical range)
Median — 50th percentile (the middle value — more robust than mean)
Q3 — 75th percentile (top of the typical range)
Max — largest value (check for impossible highs and outliers)
IQR = Q3 − Q1 — the "interquartile range" — how spread out the middle 50% is

Why these five: they're robust. A single huge outlier won't move the median or Q3 much, but it will balloon the mean. EDA prefers robust summaries because real data is messy. The five-number summary is your honest snapshot.

How to use it

The Walkthrough Workflow in Practice

In Python/pandas, the walkthrough is essentially these commands:

  • df.shape → check dimensions
  • df.dtypes → check column types
  • df.isnull().sum() → missing values per column
  • df.describe() → numeric summaries (count, mean, std, min, 25%, 50%, 75%, max)
  • df.head(20) and df.sample(20) → actually look at rows
  • df.nunique() → unique counts (catches encoding issues — e.g., expected 10 cities, found 200)

These six lines of code are the absolute baseline. Run them on every new dataset. Always. Without exception.

Finding Patterns
What — Hidden structure

What "Finding Patterns" Means

Once you've walked through the dataset, the next step is to look for patterns — relationships between variables, trends over time, groupings, and any structure that wasn't obvious from raw rows.

Patterns come in four broad kinds:

Trends

  • Value rises or falls over time
  • Example: monthly sales going up
  • Visualised with: line plots

Relationships

  • Two variables move together
  • Example: price vs demand
  • Visualised with: scatter plots

Distributions

  • How values are spread
  • Example: skewed income data
  • Visualised with: histograms, boxplots

Groupings & Clusters

  • Records that behave similarly
  • Example: customer segments
  • Visualised with: grouped bar plots, heatmaps
Why patterns matter

Patterns Are the Raw Material of Insight

A model can only predict what the data shows. If you don't find the pattern manually, no model will reliably extract it either. Patterns are how data scientists earn their salary — anyone can compute a mean, but recognising that "weekend orders have completely different sizes than weekday orders" requires careful exploration.

The data science loop: Look at data → see pattern → form question → check pattern more carefully → refine question → ultimately model it. The first three steps are EDA. Without them, the modeling stage has no clear target.
⟁ Correlation Coefficient — The Pattern-Strength Number
ρ(X, Y) = Cov(X, Y) / (σ_X · σ_Y)

The Pearson correlation coefficient ρ measures how strongly two numeric variables move together linearly. Values range from −1 (perfect negative) to +1 (perfect positive). Zero means no linear relationship.

Cov(X, Y) — covariance: average product of deviations
σ_X, σ_Y — standard deviations of X and Y
ρ ∈ [−1, +1] — scale-free linear association strength

EDA rule of thumb: |ρ| > 0.7 is a strong relationship worth investigating; 0.3 to 0.7 is moderate; below 0.3 is weak. But always plot — correlation only captures linear patterns. Curved or threshold relationships can have ρ ≈ 0 yet be highly informative.

How to find patterns systematically

The Pattern-Finding Workflow

  1. 01 Univariate first. For each numeric column, plot a histogram. For each categorical column, plot a bar chart of counts.
  2. 02 Bivariate next. Scatter plots for numeric vs numeric. Boxplots for numeric vs categorical. Stacked bars for categorical vs categorical.
  3. 03 Correlation heatmap. Compute pairwise correlations for all numeric columns. The heatmap is a quick visual scan for relationships worth zooming into.
  4. 04 Group and aggregate. Try df.groupby('category').mean() for any candidate grouping. Different group means often reveal structure invisible in the overall distribution.
  5. 05 Time series check. If there's a date column, plot every numeric column against time. Seasonality, trends, and breakpoints often appear here.

Detecting Anomalies
What — Records that don't fit

What Counts as an Anomaly?

An anomaly (also called an outlier) is a data point that deviates strikingly from the rest of the data. Anomalies fall into three main categories:

Errors

  • Data entry mistakes — age = 250
  • Sensor malfunction — temperature = 999
  • Encoding issues — null displayed as -1
  • These should usually be fixed or removed

Real But Extreme

  • A genuine billionaire in income data
  • A genuine record-breaking event
  • Should be kept — they're real
  • But noted: they will distort means

Interesting Signal

  • Fraud transactions
  • Equipment about to fail
  • Viral content moments
  • These are the goldmines of EDA
Critical mindset: never automatically delete outliers. First understand which category they're in. An "outlier" might be the most important record in your entire dataset. Fraud detection is essentially anomaly detection — the anomalies are the answer.
⟁ IQR Method — The Classical Outlier Rule
Lower bound = Q1 − 1.5 × IQR
Upper bound = Q3 + 1.5 × IQR
Outlier ⟺ value < Lower bound OR value > Upper bound

This is the rule behind the whiskers of a boxplot. Any point outside [Lower, Upper] is flagged as an outlier candidate. The multiplier 1.5 is a Tukey convention — it catches roughly the most extreme 0.7% of a normal distribution.

Why IQR-based: IQR is robust. Unlike the mean and standard deviation (which themselves get pulled by outliers), Q1 and Q3 barely budge in the presence of a few extreme values. So this rule won't fail catastrophically on heavy-tailed data.

⟁ Z-Score Method — When Data Is Roughly Normal
z = (x − μ) / σ
Outlier ⟺ |z| > 3

The z-score tells you how many standard deviations a value sits above or below the mean. For roughly normal data, only about 0.3% of values have |z| > 3, so anything beyond that threshold is suspect.

Caveat: z-scores assume roughly bell-shaped data. For skewed or heavy-tailed data (income, claim sizes, network traffic), the IQR method is safer. For normal-like data (heights, test scores), z-scores work well.

How to use anomaly detection

The Anomaly Workflow

  • ① Flag, don't delete. Mark anomalies in a separate column or filter. Never overwrite the source data.
  • ② Investigate each flagged record. Does it correspond to a known event (big sale, system outage)? Is it a data entry error? Or is it genuinely interesting?
  • ③ Decide per category. Errors → fix or remove. Real-but-extreme → keep but use robust statistics. Interesting signal → preserve and dig deeper.
  • ④ Use multiple methods. IQR and z-score sometimes disagree. Modern methods (Isolation Forest, DBSCAN, autoencoders) catch multi-dimensional anomalies. Use them when the data is complex.
  • ⑤ Document your decisions. Write down what you removed and why. Future you (or your teammate) will thank you.

Generating Hypotheses
What — Turning observations into questions

From Observation to Hypothesis

EDA produces observations. But observations alone don't move a project forward — they need to become hypotheses: testable statements that can be confirmed or refuted with data.

A good hypothesis has three properties:

  • Specific — about a clearly identified group or variable
  • Measurable — phrased so you could test it numerically
  • Falsifiable — the data could prove it wrong
Example transformation:
Observation: "I see lots of small orders on weekends."
Bad hypothesis: "Weekends matter for orders." (Vague.)
Good hypothesis: "Weekend orders have a median amount at least 20% lower than weekday orders." (Specific, measurable, falsifiable.)
Why hypotheses matter

The Bridge Between EDA and Statistical Testing

Without hypotheses, EDA is just sightseeing. With hypotheses, EDA becomes the engine of discovery. Every formal statistical test — A/B tests, t-tests, chi-squared tests, regression coefficients — operates on a hypothesis. EDA's job is to produce hypotheses worth testing.

The framework is two-sided:

⟁ The Hypothesis Pair
H₀ (null): no effect, no difference, no relationship
H₁ (alternative): there IS an effect, a difference, a relationship

Every hypothesis test pits H₀ against H₁. The null is the "boring" baseline — by default, we assume there's nothing special going on. The alternative is what EDA suggested might be true. A test produces evidence for or against H₀.

Example: H₀ = "weekend and weekday order amounts have the same median." H₁ = "they differ." A Mann-Whitney U test or t-test then quantifies the evidence. We'll learn the actual test procedure in later inference modules — EDA's job is just to suggest the H₁ worth testing.

How to generate hypotheses well

From Plot to Question

Every plot you make should end with the question: "What is this telling me, and what could I test?"

Common pattern-to-hypothesis translations:

What EDA ShowsPossible Hypothesis
Histogram has two peaksThe data contains two distinct subpopulations
Scatter shows clear upward trendX predicts Y with positive coefficient
Boxplots differ across categoriesGroup membership affects the outcome
Time series has annual cycleSeasonal pattern with period ≈ 365 days
Subset of points behaves differentlyThis subgroup has a distinct underlying process
Missing values cluster in one groupMissingness is not random; tied to a hidden variable
Working example

Hypothesis Generation on the E-Commerce Data

After EDA on our orders dataset, suppose we observed:

  • Customers from large cities tend to have higher amounts (mean: Mumbai ₹2,100 vs Indore ₹1,400)
  • Repeat customers (those with 2+ orders) average higher than first-timers
  • Order amounts spike sharply around festival weekends
  • One customer has 47 orders in 3 days — possible bot or reseller

These observations turn into four testable hypotheses:

  • H₁_A: Average order amount differs between top-tier and tier-2 cities (test: t-test on grouped means).
  • H₁_B: Repeat customers spend more per order than first-time customers (test: difference of medians).
  • H₁_C: Festival-weekend daily revenue is higher than non-festival weekend (test: paired comparison or regression with dummy variable).
  • H₁_D: Customer C-219 is a non-human account (test: pattern analysis, account verification).
This is real data science. One hour of EDA has produced four hypotheses, each of which could power a meaningful project, A/B test, or business decision. Without EDA, none of these would have been formulated.

Data Storytelling
What — Findings become narratives

Why Storytelling Is Half the Job

You can run perfect EDA, find a brilliant pattern, generate an exciting hypothesis — and if you can't communicate it to humans who make decisions, none of it matters. Data storytelling is the discipline of translating findings into narratives that move stakeholders to action.

A good data story has three ingredients:

  • A clear central question — what problem are we addressing?
  • Evidence with context — the data, but interpreted, not just shown
  • A recommendation — what should the audience do next?
The 3-30-3 rule: A great data story works in 3 seconds (the headline number), 30 seconds (the chart with caption), and 3 minutes (the full narrative with detail). If your insight only works in the 30-minute deep-dive, you've not yet told the story — you've still got raw analysis.
Why it matters

The Real Cost of Bad Storytelling

The most common cause of "data science work going nowhere" is not bad analysis — it's bad communication. Stakeholders who can't grasp your insight in 30 seconds will either ignore it, misunderstand it, or distort it. Months of careful work end up unused.

Conversely, a clearly told story can shift entire strategies. A single well-presented chart has shut down failing product lines, triggered major hiring rounds, and reshaped national policy.

How to tell a data story

The Storytelling Workflow

  1. 01 Start with the audience. Are they technical or executive? Do they want one number or a full report?
  2. 02 Lead with the headline. Open with the one-sentence insight — "Weekend orders are 23% smaller on average" — then back it up.
  3. 03 Choose one main chart. Resist the urge to dump every plot. One clean, well-annotated chart beats ten cluttered ones.
  4. 04 Add context. Compare to a baseline. "23% smaller" alone is forgettable; "23% smaller than weekday average, equivalent to ₹2.3M revenue gap per quarter" sticks.
  5. 05 Acknowledge uncertainty honestly. Confidence intervals, sample sizes, caveats. Stakeholders trust analysts who say "we can't be sure" more than those who claim absolute certainty.
  6. 06 End with a recommendation. "Therefore, we suggest piloting a weekend-bundle promotion in Q2 and measuring the lift." Action is the point of the whole exercise.
Working example — A full data story

From Raw EDA to a One-Page Story

📊 Title: "Weekend Order Drops Are Costing Us ₹9M / Year"

Question: Are we losing meaningful revenue on weekends compared to weekdays?

Finding: Across 52,000 orders over 12 months, median weekend order amount is ₹845, compared to ₹1,098 on weekdays — a 23% gap that holds across all six tier-1 cities.

Evidence: A side-by-side boxplot of order amounts by day-of-week. Saturday and Sunday boxes sit visibly lower than Monday–Friday boxes. Confidence interval on the difference: ₹185–₹321 (95%).

Context: Weekend traffic is 8% higher, but average ticket is 23% lower — net revenue per weekend day is roughly 17% below average. Annualised: ~₹9M opportunity gap.

Uncertainty: The gap may be partly explained by category mix (more impulse purchases on weekends). We have not yet controlled for product category.

Recommendation: Pilot a "weekend bundle" SKU mix in 2 tier-1 cities for one quarter. Measure lift via difference-in-differences against control cities. Decision point: April.

Notice how this story works at every length. The title alone tells the executive what's happening. The first paragraph tells the manager. The full text tells the analyst. The chart (a boxplot by day) is the one visual the team would actually screenshot.

Real-world data storytelling

Where Storytelling Matters

📈 Business Dashboards

Every effective KPI dashboard is a piece of data storytelling — it answers "is the business healthy?" in 30 seconds with the right metrics highlighted.

📰 Data Journalism

News organisations (FT, NYT, Bloomberg) employ data journalists whose only job is storytelling — finding patterns and presenting them with narrative arcs.

🏥 Public Health Reporting

COVID dashboards, vaccine rollout charts, and pandemic models that policy-makers act on are exemplars of high-stakes data storytelling.

🤖 ML Model Communication

Selling a model to stakeholders requires storytelling — explaining what the model does and why it matters in their language, not in ML jargon.

📊 Investor Pitches

The "traction slide" of every startup deck is a data story — converting metrics into a compelling growth narrative.

🧪 Research Papers

Even academic papers live or die by their storytelling. The same result can be a footnote or a headline depending on how it's framed and visualised.

Common Mistakes
Mistake 1 — Skipping EDA to "save time." The most expensive bug is the one that ships because no one checked. Two hours of EDA prevents two weeks of debugging.
Mistake 2 — Trusting summary statistics blindly. Mean = 50 can describe wildly different distributions. Always plot. Anscombe's quartet exists exactly to make this point.
Mistake 3 — Deleting outliers automatically. Some outliers are errors; some are the most important records you'll ever see. Investigate before removing.
Mistake 4 — Cherry-picking patterns. If you check 100 relationships, several will look "significant" by chance. EDA is for generating hypotheses, not confirming them. Confirmation needs fresh data and proper tests.
Mistake 5 — Confusing correlation with causation. EDA can show that X and Y move together. It cannot show that X causes Y. That requires experiments or causal inference methods.
Mistake 6 — Telling the story you wanted, not the one in the data. Confirmation bias is real. Run a hypothesis you would prefer to disprove. Honest storytelling beats persuasive storytelling every time.
Mistake 7 — Dumping all plots into the report. Choose. One thoughtful chart beats ten redundant ones. Your job is to make the story easier to read, not harder.
