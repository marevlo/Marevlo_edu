The Sampling-Without-Replacement Problem
When Binomial breaks

The Critical Independence Assumption

In Part 1, Binomial worked beautifully — but only under one strict condition: independence between trials. Each trial had to have the same success probability p, untouched by previous outcomes.

In many real-world scenarios, this is exactly false:

  • You draw 5 cards from a 52-card deck. After each draw, the deck has fewer cards.
  • You select 3 balls from an urn containing 5 red and 5 blue. After removing a red ball, the proportions change.
  • You sample 100 items from a batch of 500 to inspect for defects. Each pick depletes the batch.
  • You audit 20 transactions out of 1,000 — each one removed from the pool of "next picks."

These are all sampling without replacement from a finite population. The probability of success on the next pick depends on what's already been drawn. Independence is broken. Binomial does not apply.

The fix: The Hypergeometric distribution — purpose-built for sampling without replacement. It accounts for the depletion of the population as samples are taken.
A motivating example

The Balls-in-an-Urn Setup — Simple Example

A box contains 10 balls: 4 red and 6 blue. You draw 3 balls without replacement. What is the probability of getting exactly 2 red balls?

If we naively used Binomial(n = 3, p = 4/10 = 0.4):

P(X = 2) ≈ C(3,2)(0.4)²(0.6) = 0.288 (WRONG!)

Why is it wrong? Binomial assumes each draw has the same 40% chance of red. But that's not true! After drawing the first red ball, only 3 red balls remain out of 9 total — the probability drops to 3/9 ≈ 33%. Binomial ignores this change.

The correct answer using Hypergeometric: P(X = 2) = 0.30 (30%). Slightly lower than Binomial's 28.8% — the difference comes from accounting for the changing population.

Key insight: Hypergeometric is more conservative (narrower distribution) than Binomial because sampling without replacement gives you more information about what's left.

Hypergeometric Distribution
Definition

The Sampling-Without-Replacement Distribution

The Hypergeometric distribution models the number of "successes" in a fixed-size sample drawn without replacement from a finite population, where the population has been divided into two groups: successes and failures.

Concretely, set up:

  • Population of size N
  • K "success" items in the population (e.g., red balls, defective items, target cards)
  • N − K "failure" items (the rest)
  • Draw a sample of n items without replacement
  • Let X = number of successes in the sample

Then X follows a Hypergeometric(N, K, n) distribution. The possible values are constrained: X ranges from max(0, n − (N − K)) to min(n, K).

⟁ Hypergeometric PMF (Probability Formula)
P(X = x) = [ C(K, x) · C(N − K, n − x) ] / C(N, n)

What this means in plain English: This formula tells you the probability of getting exactly x successes when you draw n items from a population of N items, where K of those N items are "successes."

Breaking down each piece:

  • C(K, x) = "combinations" — the number of ways to pick x successes from the K successful items in the population
  • C(N − K, n − x) = the number of ways to pick the remaining (n − x) failures from the (N − K) failure items
  • C(N, n) = the total number of ways to pick any n items from all N items
  • Divide them: (favorable ways) / (total ways) = probability

Example: Box has 10 balls (4 red, 6 blue). Draw 3. Want P(exactly 2 red):

P(X = 2) = [C(4,2) · C(6,1)] / C(10,3) = (6 · 6) / 120 = 0.30 (30%)

⟁ Hypergeometric Mean & Variance (Average & Spread)
E[X] = n · (K / N)
Var(X) = n · (K/N) · (1 − K/N) · [(N − n) / (N − 1)]

E[X] = n · (K/N) — The Average:

  • Let p = K/N = the proportion of successes in the population
  • Then E[X] = n·p — exactly the same formula as Binomial!
  • Example: Population is 40% red (K=4, N=10). Draw n=3. Expected reds = 3 × 0.4 = 1.2

Var(X) — The Spread (The Key Difference from Binomial):

  • Binomial variance = n·p·(1−p)
  • Hypergeometric variance = n·p·(1−p) · [(N−n)/(N−1)]
  • The extra factor [(N−n)/(N−1)] is the finite-population correction — it's always ≤ 1
  • This means Hypergeometric variance is ALWAYS smaller than Binomial variance
  • Why? When you sample without replacement, each draw gives you information about what's left. You're more certain about the results.

Extreme case: If you sample the entire population (n = N), the correction factor becomes 0, so variance = 0. You know exactly what you have — no uncertainty!

The deep connection to Binomial

When Hypergeometric ≈ Binomial

If the population N is very large compared to the sample n, removing items barely changes the proportions. Sampling without replacement becomes essentially the same as sampling with replacement.

Rule of thumb: When n < 5% of N (i.e., n/N < 0.05), Binomial(n, K/N) is an excellent approximation to Hypergeometric(N, K, n). The finite-population correction factor (N−n)/(N−1) ≈ 1.

This is why polling 1,000 people out of 300 million Americans uses Binomial — at that sampling fraction (0.0003%), depletion is negligible.

It also explains a deep symmetry: Binomial is the limit of Hypergeometric as N → ∞ with K/N fixed. Just as Poisson is the limit of Binomial (large n, small p), Binomial is the limit of Hypergeometric (large population vs sample).

⟁ Worked Hypergeometric Examples
When to use Hypergeometric

Decision Guide

  • The population is finite and known (size N).
  • You can categorize items into two groups (successes vs failures).
  • You sample without replacement.
  • You want the probability of getting exactly x successes in a sample of size n.

Don't use Hypergeometric when: sampling with replacement (use Binomial), the population is so large that depletion is negligible (Binomial approximation works fine), or there are more than two groups (use Multivariate Hypergeometric).

Real-world Hypergeometric

Where Hypergeometric Lives

🏭 Quality Inspection

From a batch of 500 manufactured parts, sample 50 without replacement to inspect for defects. The number of defects found follows Hypergeometric.

🎴 Card Games

Probability of drawing exactly 2 Aces in a 5-card poker hand. Drawing without replacement = Hypergeometric(52, 4, 5).

🎰 Lottery Draws

Lotto with 49 balls, pick 6. The number of "winning" balls you match follows Hypergeometric — used for prize-tier calculations.

🧾 Tax Audits

From 10,000 tax returns, audit 200 without replacement. The number flagged as fraudulent ∼ Hypergeometric. Used for sampling-plan design.

🔬 Capture-Recapture

Wildlife biology: tag K animals, release them, then capture n. Tagged ones found follow Hypergeometric — basis of population estimation methods.

📚 Acceptance Sampling

A buyer accepts a shipment if no more than x defects appear in a sample of n. The decision rule is Hypergeometric-based.

🧬 Genome Sampling

From N genes, K are "differentially expressed." Sampling n at random — count of DE genes is Hypergeometric. Used in gene-set enrichment analyses.

📊 Survey Selection

Selecting respondents from a finite list (e.g., HR sampling employees) — small-population surveys use Hypergeometric for precise probabilities.

Distribution Selection — The Master Decision Tree

You've now seen all five distributions. The most important practical skill is knowing which one to use given a real-world problem. The framework below converts a verbal problem description into the correct distribution choice in five sequential questions.

Q1
Is there only one trial with two outcomes?   (e.g., "will this email be spam?" / "will this patient test positive?")
YES → Bernoulli(p)
Q2
Are there many independent identical trials with a fixed count, and you want successes?   (e.g., "in 100 coin flips, how many heads?")
YES → Binomial(n, p)
Q3
Are you waiting for the first success in independent trials?   (e.g., "how many sales calls until first 'yes'?")
YES → Geometric(p)
Q4
Are you counting events in a fixed window of time/space at a constant rate?   (e.g., "calls per hour" / "defects per square meter")
YES → Poisson(λ)
Q5
Are you sampling without replacement from a finite population?   (e.g., "audit 50 of 500 invoices" / "5-card poker hand")
YES → Hypergeometric(N, K, n)

The Concept Flow — How Distributions Connect

The five distributions are not independent species. They form a connected family — each emerging as you add or change a condition. Below is the master flow diagram for Module 3.1.

Bernoulli(p) single binary trial
↓   add: n independent trials, count successes
Binomial(n, p) count of successes
↓   flip the question: wait for first success
Geometric(p) trial of first success
↓   let n → ∞, p → 0, with np = λ fixed
Poisson(λ) events in continuous interval
↓   break independence: sample without replacement
Hypergeometric(N, K, n) dependent sampling
Final intuition

The Five Questions of Discrete Randomness

Each distribution is just a different mathematical answer to a slightly different question:

DistributionThe question it asks
Bernoulli"Did success happen?"
Binomial"How many successes happened?"
Geometric"When did success first arrive?"
Poisson"How many events occurred in this window?"
Hypergeometric"What happens to my count when the pool shrinks as I draw?"

Common Beginner Mistakes — Final Compilation
01
Using Binomial for sampling without replacement. If items are not replaced after each draw, independence breaks. Use Hypergeometric. The Binomial approximation works only when sample size is much smaller than the population (n/N < 0.05).
02
Confusing Bernoulli and Binomial. Bernoulli is a single trial; Binomial is the count over n trials. Bernoulli = Binomial(1, p). They sound similar; they answer different questions.
03
Using Poisson when the number of trials is fixed. Poisson is for events in continuous intervals with no fixed n. If you have a definite number of trials (10 coin flips, 5 attempts), it's Binomial, not Poisson.
04
Forgetting the memoryless property of Geometric. Past failures don't increase future success probability. The gambler's fallacy ("I'm due for a win") is a violation of this property — and a costly behavioral mistake.
05
Choosing distributions by formula memorization. Always choose by understanding the underlying random system: count, wait, sample, with/without replacement, fixed/variable trials. Match the story to the distribution.
06
Using Poisson when variance ≠ mean. If empirical variance is much larger than mean (overdispersion), Poisson is the wrong model — try Negative Binomial. If empirical variance is much smaller (underdispersion), use a different finite-count model.

QNA-Σ // Synthesis Core
Discrete Distributions Master Analyst — Complete
01
Distributions are stories of randomness. Each captures a recurring class of stochastic system. Matching the right distribution to a problem is the most valuable applied probability skill.
02
Bernoulli(p) — single yes/no trial. The atom of binary randomness. E[X] = p, Var = p(1−p). Variance peaks at p = 0.5.
03
Binomial(n, p) — count of successes in n independent Bernoulli trials. Four conditions: fixed n, independence, two outcomes, constant p. E[X] = np, Var = np(1−p).
04
Geometric(p) — trial of first success. PMF decays geometrically; mode is always at X = 1. E[X] = 1/p. Famously memoryless: past failures don't affect future probability.
05
Poisson(λ) — count of rare events in a fixed interval. Mean = Variance = λ — the unique signature. Emerges as the limit of Binomial when n → ∞, p → 0, np = λ.
06
Hypergeometric(N, K, n) — sampling without replacement from a finite population. Same mean as Binomial (np with p = K/N), but smaller variance due to finite-population correction (N−n)/(N−1).
07
The five distributions form a connected family. Bernoulli → Binomial (sum) → Geometric (wait) → Poisson (limit) → Hypergeometric (break independence). Knowing the relationships is as important as knowing the formulas.
08
The selection skill: always start from the question, not the formula. Is this a single trial or many? Counting or waiting? Discrete trials or continuous time? With or without replacement? Five questions, five distributions.
