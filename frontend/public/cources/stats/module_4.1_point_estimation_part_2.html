Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE) picks the value that makes the observed data most likely. Method of Moments (MoM) matches simple sample numbers (like averages) to their theoretical counterparts. TOPIC 3
Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE)
What it is

What is Maximum Likelihood Estimation?

Imagine you walk into a room and see a friend flipping a coin. You watch them flip it 10 times. The result: 8 heads, 2 tails.

Now I ask you: "What's your best guess for the probability that this coin lands on heads?"

You'd probably say something close to 0.8 (or 80%). Why? Because that's the value that best explains what you saw — 8 out of 10 heads.

That gut feeling — pick the value that makes the data you saw most likely — is exactly Maximum Likelihood Estimation.

In simple words: MLE says: "Out of all possible values for the unknown parameter, pick the one that makes the data you actually observed most likely to have happened." It's the "best explanation" approach to estimation.
The big idea

Likelihood — A New Word for an Old Idea

The word "likelihood" sounds fancy but it's just a fancy name for: "how probable is the data you saw, assuming a particular value of the unknown parameter?"

For our coin flip example:

  • If the true probability of heads were p = 0.1, then seeing 8 heads in 10 flips would be very unlikely. So this is a low likelihood.
  • If p = 0.5, seeing 8 heads is unlikely but possible. Medium likelihood.
  • If p = 0.8, seeing 8 heads is very probable! High likelihood.
  • If p = 0.99, we'd almost always see 10 heads, not 8. Slightly lower likelihood.

So somewhere around p = 0.8, the likelihood reaches a peak. That peak is the MLE estimate.

Visual way: Think of the likelihood as a hill. The MLE estimate is the value at the top of the hill — the value of the parameter for which the data is most likely.
Why we use it

Why MLE is So Popular

1. It Works for Almost Anything

  • Coin flips, heights, incomes, anything
  • Same recipe for any distribution
  • Universal estimation method

2. It Has Great Properties

  • Usually unbiased (for large samples)
  • Has smallest possible variance (often)
  • "Best possible" estimator in many cases

3. It's the Heart of ML

  • Training neural networks = MLE
  • Linear regression = MLE
  • Logistic regression = MLE
  • Used everywhere in AI

4. Easy to Implement

  • Computers find peaks easily
  • Optimization libraries do the work
  • One line of code in Python/R
How to do it

The MLE Recipe — Step by Step

Here's the 4-step recipe MLE follows every time:

  • Step 1 — Set up: Decide which distribution your data comes from (e.g., Normal, Binomial, Poisson).
  • Step 2 — Write the likelihood: Compute "how likely is the observed data" for each possible value of the parameter.
  • Step 3 — Find the peak: Find the parameter value where the likelihood is highest (use calculus, or computer).
  • Step 4 — Done: That value is your MLE estimate, written θ̂MLE.

You don't need to do all the math by hand — software does it for you. But understanding what's happening behind the scenes is the key skill.

⟁ The Likelihood Function — The Core of MLE
L(θ) = P(data | θ) = P(x₁|θ) × P(x₂|θ) × ... × P(xₙ|θ)

In plain English: The likelihood is the probability of seeing ALL your data points, assuming the true parameter equals θ. Since the data points are independent, we multiply the probabilities together.

L(θ) — likelihood function (treats data as fixed, θ as the variable)
P(xᵢ|θ) — probability of seeing one data point given θ
x₁, ..., xₙ — the observed data points
θ̂MLE — the θ value that maximizes L(θ)

What we want: Find the θ that makes L(θ) as big as possible. That θ is our MLE estimate.

⟁ Log-Likelihood — The Trick Statisticians Use
ℓ(θ) = log L(θ) = log P(x₁|θ) + log P(x₂|θ) + ... + log P(xₙ|θ)

Why take the log? Multiplying many tiny probabilities (like 0.001 × 0.002 × ...) gives super tiny numbers that computers struggle with. Taking the logarithm turns multiplication into addition, which is much easier and more stable.

Important: the value of θ that maximizes L(θ) is the same value that maximizes log L(θ). So we get the same answer — just easier math.

When to use it

When MLE is the Right Choice

  • You know (or can assume) the distribution shape of your data (e.g., "it's Normal" or "it's Binomial").
  • You have a reasonable sample size (MLE has best properties with lots of data).
  • You're building a machine learning model — almost every ML algorithm uses MLE in some form.
  • You want the "best" estimator in a mathematical sense (most informative use of data).
  • You can use a computer to find the peak (today, that's almost always the case).
⟁ Worked Example

Example: Estimating a Coin's Bias with MLE

You flip a coin 10 times and observe 7 heads, 3 tails. What's the MLE estimate for the probability of heads (p)?

Step 1 — Set up:

Each coin flip is a Bernoulli trial with unknown probability p. We want to estimate p.

Step 2 — Write the likelihood:

The probability of seeing exactly 7 heads in 10 flips is:

L(p) = p7 × (1−p)3

(The "10 choose 7" combination doesn't depend on p, so we can ignore it when finding the peak.)

Step 3 — Find the peak:

Take the derivative of L(p) (or the log of it), set it to zero, and solve. After the math:

p̂MLE = 7 / 10 = 0.7

Step 4 — Done:

Our MLE estimate: the coin lands heads about 70% of the time.

The beautiful result: For a Bernoulli/Binomial process, the MLE for p is just "number of successes ÷ total trials" — exactly what your intuition would say! MLE often gives us the same answer we'd guess intuitively, but with rigorous mathematical justification.

Real-life examples

Where MLE Powers the Real World

📧 Spam Filters

Naive Bayes spam filters use MLE to estimate how likely each word is in spam vs not-spam emails. Then they classify new emails.

🤖 Neural Networks

Training a neural network = maximizing the likelihood of the training data given the model's weights. Every loss function is a form of MLE.

💬 Language Models

ChatGPT and other language models are trained with MLE — picking weights that make real text most likely.

🧬 DNA Sequencing

Scientists use MLE to reconstruct DNA sequences from noisy reads — finding the sequence that best explains the lab readings.

🩺 Medical Diagnosis

Diagnostic models use MLE to estimate the probability a patient has a disease given their symptoms.

📊 Linear Regression

The "best fit line" through data points is the MLE — it makes the observed data most likely under a Normal noise model.

Real-life use

The Hidden Engine Behind Modern AI

Almost every machine learning model — from a simple linear regression to a giant language model — uses MLE under the hood. Here's how it sneaks in:

  • The "loss function" in any model is just −log likelihood.
  • Training the model = minimizing the loss = maximizing the likelihood.
  • So when an AI is "learning," it's literally doing MLE — finding parameters that best explain the training data.

If you understand MLE, you understand the deepest math behind most of AI today. It's that important.

TOPIC 4
Method of Moments (MoM)
What it is

What is the Method of Moments?

The Method of Moments (MoM) is the simplest way to build an estimator. It was invented by Karl Pearson in 1894 — long before MLE — and it's still useful today.

The idea is incredibly simple:

The MoM Principle: "Whatever the sample shows, the population probably shows the same thing." Take the average of your sample, and use it to estimate the population mean. Take the variance of your sample, and use it to estimate the population variance. That's it!

So if your sample average is 30, your MoM estimate of the true population mean is also 30. If your sample variance is 16, your MoM estimate of the population variance is 16. Match sample to truth — done.

What are "moments"?

"Moments" — A Fancy Word for Simple Things

The word "moment" sounds scary, but moments are just simple summary numbers. The most common ones:

MomentPopulation (truth)Sample (data)
1st momentPopulation mean μSample mean x̄
2nd momentE[X²] (avg of squared values)(1/n)·Σxᵢ²
2nd central momentVariance σ²Sample variance s²
3rd moment (related to)SkewnessSample skewness

The k-th moment is just the average of the k-th power of your values: E[Xk]. The 1st moment is the regular mean. The 2nd, 3rd, 4th moments capture more information about the distribution's shape.

How it works

The MoM Recipe — Step by Step

Here's the 3-step MoM process:

  • Step 1 — Pick how many parameters you need to estimate. If 1 parameter (like the mean), you need 1 equation. If 2 (like mean and variance), you need 2.
  • Step 2 — Write equations: set the sample moment equal to the theoretical (population) moment.
  • Step 3 — Solve the equations for the unknown parameters. The solutions are your MoM estimates.

That's it. No calculus, no optimization, no log likelihoods. Just matching averages to averages.

⟁ MoM — The Matching Equations
Sample moment = Population moment

For estimating one parameter (e.g., the mean μ):

x̄ = μ   ⟹   μ̂MoM = x̄

For estimating two parameters (e.g., mean μ and variance σ²):

x̄ = μ    AND    (1/n)Σxᵢ² = μ² + σ²

Solving these gives μ̂MoM = x̄ and σ̂²MoM = (1/n)Σ(xᵢ − x̄)². The MoM estimate of the mean is the sample mean, and the MoM estimate of variance is the sample variance.

The pattern: for each unknown parameter, write one equation matching a sample moment to its population counterpart. Then solve.

Why use MoM

Why MoM is Still Useful Today

1. It's Very Simple

  • No calculus needed
  • Often closed-form solution
  • Easy to explain to non-experts
  • Quick first-pass estimate

2. It Always Works

  • Even when MLE is hard
  • Doesn't require the full distribution
  • Works with limited info
  • Good starting point for harder methods

3. Often Matches Intuition

  • "Sample mean estimates population mean"
  • Same as your natural guess
  • Gives confidence the math is right

4. Computationally Cheap

  • No optimization needed
  • Direct formulas
  • Tiny computers can do it
When to use it

When MoM is the Right Tool

  • You want a quick estimate without fancy math.
  • You only know basic info about the distribution (means and variances), not the full shape.
  • You're introducing statistics to a non-expert — MoM is easy to teach.
  • You need a starting point for a more complex method like MLE.
  • Your sample is large — both MoM and MLE work well, and MoM is simpler.
  • You're working in economics, finance, or social sciences where MoM is traditional.
Watch out: MoM can be less efficient than MLE (slightly higher variance). For small samples or critical decisions, MLE is usually preferred. But MoM is great for quick analysis.
⟁ Worked Example

Example: Estimating Average Temperature with MoM

A meteorologist records the daily high temperature for one week:

24°C, 26°C, 22°C, 28°C, 25°C, 27°C, 23°C

The data is assumed to be Normally distributed with unknown mean μ and variance σ². Use MoM to estimate both.

Step 1 — Count parameters: We need to estimate 2 parameters (μ and σ²). So we need 2 equations.

Step 2 — Write the matching equations:

  • Equation 1: sample mean = population mean → x̄ = μ
  • Equation 2: sample variance = population variance → s² = σ²

Step 3 — Solve:

Sample mean: x̄ = (24+26+22+28+25+27+23) / 7 = 175 / 7 = 25°C

Sample variance: average of (xᵢ − 25)² = (1+1+9+9+0+4+4) / 7 = 28/7 = 4°C²

Step 4 — Done:

MoM estimates:

  • μ̂MoM = 25°C (estimated true mean temperature)
  • σ̂²MoM = 4°C² (estimated variance), so standard deviation σ̂ = 2°C
What we learned: the true average high temperature is around 25°C, and individual days vary by about ±2°C. We got these estimates without any complex math — just matching sample averages to population averages. That's the elegant simplicity of the Method of Moments.

Real-life examples

Where MoM Still Wins

💰 Economics & Finance

Estimating GDP statistics, inflation, and economic indicators often uses MoM for its simplicity and transparency to policymakers.

📈 Method-of-Moments in GMM

Generalized Method of Moments (GMM) — used in econometrics for Nobel-prize-winning research — is a sophisticated extension of MoM.

🌡️ Quick Field Estimates

Field biologists and ecologists use sample means and variances directly as estimates — quick MoM in action.

📚 Statistical Teaching

Every intro statistics course teaches MoM because it makes the connection between sample and population obvious.

Real-life use

Why You Should Know Both

  • Cross-checking: if MLE and MoM disagree wildly, something's wrong — maybe the distribution assumption.
  • Starting points: MLE is often hard to find. MoM gives a fast starting guess for the optimizer.
  • Communication: when explaining a model to clients or stakeholders, "the sample mean is our estimate" (MoM) is easier than explaining likelihood functions.
  • Sanity check: if your fancy MLE gives a wildly different answer from MoM, it might be wrong.
