Advanced Sampling Methods (MCMC)

When the posterior cannot be written down in closed form — and it almost never can — you don't compute it. You simulate it. You walk a Markov chain through parameter space, and the chain's trajectory becomes your samples.

This module covers four ideas in sequence: Monte Carlo intuition (using random samples to estimate quantities), Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) (the bridge from the random walks in Module 6.2 to Bayesian sampling), Gibbs sampling (one coordinate at a time), and Metropolis–Hastings (propose and accept). Together they are the computational engine behind modern Bayesian statistics, probabilistic programming, and large parts of AI.

Topic 1
Monte Carlo Intuition
What it is

Estimating Quantities by Sampling

Monte Carlo estimation is the idea of approximating a hard-to-compute quantity — often an integral, an expected value, or a probability — by drawing many random samples and averaging.

The name comes from the Monte Carlo casino. The method was developed in the 1940s by Stanislaw Ulam and John von Neumann while working on physics problems where the integrals were too complicated to solve by hand. They reframed the integral as an average over random samples, and the approach held.

One-line definition: To estimate the average value of f under a distribution p, draw many samples x₁, x₂, …, xₙ from p, compute f at each, and take the average. The estimate gets more accurate as n grows.
The Monte Carlo Estimator
μ̂ₙ = (1/n) · Σ f(xᵢ),    xᵢ ~ p(x)
Draw n independent samples from p(x), evaluate f at each, average the results. By the Law of Large Numbers this average converges to E[f(x)] as n grows. The error shrinks like 1/√n — to halve the error you need four times the samples.
μ̂ₙ — the Monte Carlo estimate (your final number)
n — number of samples drawn
f(xᵢ) — function evaluated at sample i
xᵢ ~ p — each sample drawn from distribution p
Why we need it

Why Bayesian Inference Forces Sampling

In Module 6.1 we learned that the posterior is P(θ | data) = P(data | θ) · P(θ) / P(data). The denominator P(data) is an integral over all possible θ:

P(data) = ∫ P(data | θ) · P(θ) dθ

In simple toy problems (one parameter, conjugate prior) this integral has a clean answer. In any real problem — neural networks, hierarchical models, epidemiology fits — it does not. The integral is across hundreds, thousands, or millions of dimensions. It is uncomputable directly.

The escape hatch: we don't actually need to compute P(data). If we can sample from the posterior, we can answer any question about it — means, intervals, predictions — by averaging across the samples. Monte Carlo is the workhorse.
How to use it

What You Can Estimate Once You Have Posterior Samples

  1. Posterior mean. Average the samples: (1/n) · Σ θᵢ. That's your point estimate.
  2. Credible interval. Sort the samples, take the 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles. That's a 95% credible interval.
  3. Posterior probability of a statement. Count the fraction of samples where the statement holds. e.g. P(θ > 0 | data) = fraction of samples above 0.
  4. Predictive distribution. For each sample θᵢ, simulate a new observation. The collection of simulated outcomes is the posterior predictive distribution.
⟁ Worked Example

Estimating π with Monte Carlo

A circle of radius 1 sits inside a 2×2 square. Circle area is π, square area is 4, so a uniform random point lands inside the circle with probability π/4.

  • ▸ Generate (x, y) uniformly in [−1, 1]²
  • ▸ Check if x² + y² ≤ 1 (inside the circle)
  • ▸ Repeat n times; let k = number inside
  • ▸ Estimate: π ≈ 4 · k / n

With n = 100 you might get π ≈ 3.2. With n = 10,000 you'd get something like 3.14. With n = 1,000,000 the estimate sits very close to the true value. This is the 1/√n convergence rate at work.

Topic 2
Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC)
What it is

A Chain That Walks the Posterior

Plain Monte Carlo needs you to draw independent samples from p(x). But for a posterior we often can't draw a single independent sample — we can only evaluate the unnormalised density (prior × likelihood) at a chosen point.

Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sidesteps this. Instead of drawing independent samples, you build a Markov chain (recall Module 6.2) whose long-run behaviour matches the posterior. You don't draw the posterior — you walk it.

The key idea in one line: design a Markov chain so that after enough steps, the visited states are distributed according to the target posterior. Then collect those states and treat them as samples.
The Proportionality That Makes MCMC Work
P(θ | data) ∝ P(data | θ) · P(θ)
The posterior is proportional to likelihood × prior. The unknown constant P(data) just rescales the whole distribution. MCMC algorithms only ever need ratios of the unnormalised density at different θ values — and ratios cancel the constant. This is why MCMC works without ever computing P(data).
P(θ | data) — target posterior (what we want to sample)
P(data | θ) · P(θ) — unnormalised density (what we evaluate)
∝ — "proportional to" — same shape, different scale
P(data) — the constant we never need to compute
Why it matters

Three Properties of a Useful MCMC Chain

For a Markov chain to give us valid posterior samples, three properties must hold. You don't need to prove them — just recognise what they mean.

1. Markov Property

  • Next state depends only on the current state
  • The chain forgets older history
  • Makes the chain easy to simulate

2. Stationary Distribution = Posterior

  • The chain settles into a target distribution
  • MCMC algorithms are built so the target = the posterior
  • Long-run visit frequencies match posterior density

3. Ergodicity

  • The chain explores all of the posterior
  • It does not get stuck in one region
  • Time-averages converge to true expected values
How to use it

Burn-In and the Practical Workflow

The chain starts somewhere — often far from the posterior's high-density region. The early states are not yet representative. They get thrown away. This warm-up phase is called burn-in.

  1. Pick a starting state. Any reasonable value of θ.
  2. Run the chain for burn-in. Commonly 1,000–10,000 steps. Discard these.
  3. Keep the next N states. These are your posterior samples.
  4. Run multiple chains from different starts to check they all reach the same region.
  5. Use the kept samples exactly like plain Monte Carlo — average, sort, threshold.
Common pitfall: a chain that only explores part of the posterior gives biased estimates. Always run multiple chains from different starts to spot this.
⟁ Real-Life Example

Cosmology Parameter Fitting

Cosmologists want to infer parameters like the universe's expansion rate and matter density from cosmic microwave background data. The posterior over these parameters is sharp, high-dimensional, and has no closed form. They run MCMC chains for millions of steps, throwing out the early burn-in, and use the surviving samples to compute credible intervals on each parameter. This is how modern cosmology actually gets its numbers.

Topic 3
Metropolis–Hastings
What it is

Propose, Compare, Accept or Reject

Metropolis–Hastings (MH) is the most widely used MCMC algorithm. Published by Metropolis and colleagues in 1953 and extended by Hastings in 1970, it is the engine behind almost every Bayesian inference library you will ever use.

The core idea: from the current state θ, propose a small random move to a candidate θ*. Compare how good θ* is compared to θ under the posterior. If θ* is better, always go there. If it's worse, go there sometimes — with probability that depends on how much worse it is.

Why accept worse moves at all? Always moving uphill would trap the chain at the peak. Occasional downhill moves let it explore the full shape of the posterior, not just its highest point.
Metropolis Acceptance Probability (Symmetric Proposal)
α = min( 1,   P(data | θ*) · P(θ*) / P(data | θ) · P(θ) )
After proposing θ*, compute the ratio of unnormalised posteriors. If the ratio is ≥ 1, accept the move (α = 1). If it's less than 1, draw u from Uniform(0, 1) and accept only if u < α. Otherwise stay at θ and record θ again.
α — acceptance probability between 0 and 1
θ — current state
θ* — proposed candidate
Ratio — unnormalised posterior at θ* over at θ
How to use it

One Iteration of Metropolis–Hastings

  1. Start at current state θ. The chain is here right now.
  2. Propose a candidate θ*. Typical: θ* = θ + a small random Gaussian step (a "random walk" proposal).
  3. Compute the unnormalised posterior at θ and at θ*. Just prior × likelihood, no normalisation.
  4. Form the ratio r = posterior(θ*) / posterior(θ).
  5. Compute α = min(1, r).
  6. Draw u from Uniform(0, 1). If u < α, the next state is θ*. Otherwise it stays at θ.
  7. Record the new state. Repeat from step 1.
The genius of the ratio: the unknown P(data) appears in both posteriors and cancels. That's why MH works without ever computing it. This is the proportionality technique from Topic 2 doing real work.
⟁ Worked Example

One MH Step on a Coin Posterior

Posterior shape: P(p) ∝ p¹⁴ · (1−p)⁶ (14 heads, 6 tails, uniform prior). Current state p = 0.6.

  • ▸ Current density: 0.6¹⁴ · 0.4⁶ ≈ 0.000018
  • ▸ Propose p* = 0.65. Density: 0.65¹⁴ · 0.35⁶ ≈ 0.000038
  • ▸ Ratio: 0.000038 / 0.000018 ≈ 2.11
  • ▸ α = min(1, 2.11) = 1 → always accept. Move to 0.65.

Now suppose next proposal is p* = 0.45.

  • ▸ Density at 0.45: 0.45¹⁴ · 0.55⁶ ≈ 0.0000003
  • ▸ Ratio: 0.0000003 / 0.000038 ≈ 0.008
  • ▸ α ≈ 0.008 → almost certainly reject. Stay at 0.65.
Tuning

Choosing the Proposal Width

The proposal has a width — for a Gaussian step, this is its standard deviation σ. Choosing σ well is the main practical decision in MH.

Too Small

  • Almost all proposals accepted (~100%)
  • Chain crawls in tiny steps
  • Takes forever to explore
  • Samples are heavily correlated

Too Large

  • Proposals land in low-density regions
  • Most rejected (~5% accepted)
  • Chain barely moves
  • Wasted compute

Just Right

  • Acceptance rate near 23% (high-dim) or 44% (1D)
  • Balanced exploration and acceptance
  • Modern libraries auto-tune σ during burn-in

Topic 4
Gibbs Sampling
What it is

One Coordinate at a Time

When the parameter θ has multiple components — say θ = (θ₁, θ₂, …, θ_d) — Metropolis–Hastings updates all of them at once. Gibbs sampling takes a different route: it updates one coordinate at a time, holding all the others fixed.

The catch: we need the conditional distribution of each coordinate given all the others. For many Bayesian models — especially with conjugate priors — these conditionals have clean closed forms even when the joint posterior does not.

The Gibbs idea: sample θ₁ given (θ₂, θ₃, …), then θ₂ given (θ₁, θ₃, …), then θ₃, and so on. Cycle through all coordinates. Repeat. The chain converges to the joint posterior.
The Gibbs Update Rule
θᵢ⁽ᵗ⁺¹⁾ ~ P( θᵢ | θ₋ᵢ⁽ᵗ⁾, data )
At iteration t+1, update coordinate i by sampling from its conditional distribution given the current values of all other coordinates (written θ₋ᵢ) and the data. Cycle through i = 1, 2, …, d, then repeat the cycle. No acceptance ratio — every Gibbs move is accepted.
θᵢ⁽ᵗ⁺¹⁾ — new value of coordinate i
θ₋ᵢ⁽ᵗ⁾ — all other coordinates, held fixed
P(θᵢ | …) — the full conditional distribution
i — coordinate index cycled 1 to d
How to use it

One Gibbs Cycle for Two Parameters (θ₁, θ₂)

  1. Derive the conditional distributions P(θ₁ | θ₂, data) and P(θ₂ | θ₁, data). For conjugate models, these are familiar distributions like Normal, Beta, or Gamma.
  2. Initialise with any reasonable (θ₁⁽⁰⁾, θ₂⁽⁰⁾).
  3. Sample θ₁⁽ᵗ⁺¹⁾ from P(θ₁ | θ₂⁽ᵗ⁾, data). Plug in the current θ₂.
  4. Sample θ₂⁽ᵗ⁺¹⁾ from P(θ₂ | θ₁⁽ᵗ⁺¹⁾, data). Use the just-updated θ₁.
  5. Record (θ₁⁽ᵗ⁺¹⁾, θ₂⁽ᵗ⁺¹⁾) as a joint sample. Repeat.
Why this works: repeatedly cycling through the conditional distributions causes the joint distribution of (θ₁, θ₂) to converge to the joint posterior. This is a deep result called the Hammersley–Clifford theorem.
⟁ Real-Life Example

Gibbs for a Normal with Unknown Mean and Variance

Observe data y₁, …, yₙ assumed Normal with unknown mean μ and unknown variance σ². With conjugate priors, both conditionals are easy:

  • ▸ μ | σ², data ~ Normal( weighted average, σ² / something )
  • ▸ σ² | μ, data ~ Inverse-Gamma( shape + n/2, scale from squared residuals )

The joint posterior P(μ, σ² | data) has no clean closed form. But each conditional is a standard distribution you can sample from in one line of code. Gibbs alternates the two steps and produces samples from the full joint.

Comparison

Metropolis–Hastings vs Gibbs — When to Use Which

Use Metropolis–Hastings When

  • You can evaluate the unnormalised posterior
  • But you cannot derive nice conditionals
  • Parameters interact in complex ways
  • You need a general-purpose, flexible sampler

Use Gibbs When

  • Each conditional is a known distribution
  • Conjugate priors make this clean
  • You want zero rejections (faster per step)
  • Common in hierarchical and mixture models
Trade-off: Gibbs is fast when conditionals are easy. If any conditional is hard, you fall back to a Metropolis step inside the Gibbs loop — called Metropolis-within-Gibbs. Modern probabilistic programming libraries (Stan, PyMC, NumPyro) pick automatically.

Where it is used

MCMC in the Real World

Probabilistic Programming

Stan, PyMC, NumPyro, Turing.jl — every modern Bayesian modelling library is built on MCMC. You write the model; they pick MH, Gibbs, or HMC.

Phylogenetics

Biologists use MCMC to infer evolutionary trees from DNA. Each chain step proposes a tweaked tree and accepts based on how well it explains the genetic data.

Image Reconstruction

MRI and CT scan reconstruction posteriors live in pixel space. Gibbs and MH samplers recover sharp images from noisy measurements.

Bayesian Neural Nets

Instead of one set of weights, Bayesian neural networks have a posterior over weights. MCMC (or its variants) is how you sample from it.
