Bias-Variance & Regularization

The single most important idea in machine learning. Why simple models underfit, complex models overfit — and how shrinkage saves you from both.

In Module 4.1 we met bias and variance in pure statistics. Now in Track 5 we apply them to predictive models: linear regression, polynomials, neural networks — anything that learns from data. The bias-variance tradeoff explains why the model that fits your training data best almost never predicts new data best. From there, we build the two most-used cures: Ridge and Lasso regression. By the end, you'll understand the math behind regularization in scikit-learn, XGBoost, deep learning weight decay, and almost every modern ML framework.

TOPIC 1
Underfitting vs Overfitting
The fundamental problem of learning

The Goldilocks Problem

Every predictive model has a dial labeled complexity. Crank it left and your model is too dumb to capture the real pattern — it underfits. Crank it right and the model memorizes every quirk of your training data, including the random noise — it overfits. Both extremes predict new data poorly. Your job as a modeler is to find the middle.

Underfitting — the model is too simple

The model can't capture the real shape of the data. Train error is high, test error is high, and the two are close together. Fitting a straight line to a clearly curved relationship is the classic example. Symptom: both errors are bad.

Overfitting — the model is too complex

The model fits the training data almost perfectly — every wiggle and bump, including the random noise. But on new data it falls apart. Train error is near zero, test error is huge. Symptom: a big gap between train and test performance.

The just-right zone

A model complex enough to capture the real signal, but disciplined enough to ignore the noise. Train error is small but not zero. Test error is close to train error. This is the goal.

The diagnostic in one number: generalization gap = test_error − train_error. A small gap with low errors = good fit. A small gap with high errors = underfit. A huge gap with near-zero train error = overfit.

⚠ UNDERFIT

  • Train error: HIGH
  • Test error: HIGH
  • Gap: small
  • Fix: more features, more complexity, less regularization

✓ GOOD FIT

  • Train error: LOW
  • Test error: LOW
  • Gap: small
  • You're done. Ship it.

⚠ OVERFIT

  • Train error: ~0
  • Test error: HIGH
  • Gap: HUGE
  • Fix: simpler model, more data, regularization, dropout
⟁ DEEP DIVE — POLYNOMIAL REGRESSION

The canonical example: fitting a polynomial of degree d

Suppose the true relationship is y = sin(x) + small noise, and we have 20 noisy points. Fit polynomials of degree 1, 3, and 15 to the same data:

  • Degree 1 (line): can't capture the sinusoidal shape — underfit. Train MSE ≈ 0.45, test MSE ≈ 0.48.
  • Degree 3: wiggles correctly through the data — just right. Train MSE ≈ 0.04, test MSE ≈ 0.05.
  • Degree 15: passes through nearly every point including the noisy ones — overfit. Train MSE ≈ 0.001, test MSE ≈ 1.2.

The degree-15 polynomial is the worst predictor of new data, even though it fits the training data best. This is overfitting in its cleanest form.

TOPIC 2
The Bias-Variance Decomposition
The most important identity in ML

Total Error = Bias² + Variance + Noise

In Module 4.1 we saw that for any estimator, MSE = Bias² + Variance. For predictive models, there's one more piece: irreducible noise — the part of the world that's genuinely random and no model can predict.

If we fix a test point x₀ and average prediction error over many possible training sets drawn from the same distribution, the expected squared error decomposes exactly as:

◆ THE BIAS-VARIANCE DECOMPOSITION
E[(y − f̂(x))²] = Bias[f̂(x)]² + Var[f̂(x)] + σ²
Three sources of error, added together. Bias² = how far the average prediction is from the truth. Variance = how much predictions wiggle across different training sets. σ² = irreducible noise (you can't beat it, no matter how good your model is).
f̂(x) = our model's prediction at x
y = the true value at x (with noise)
Bias[f̂(x)] = E[f̂(x)] − true f(x)
Var[f̂(x)] = spread of f̂(x) across training sets
σ² = noise variance (irreducible)

How complexity drives bias and variance in opposite directions

Here's the punch line. As you increase model complexity:

  • Bias goes DOWN — a more flexible model can capture more of the true pattern, so its average prediction lands closer to the truth.
  • Variance goes UP — a more flexible model is more sensitive to which specific training data it saw, so predictions wiggle more from one training set to another.
  • Noise σ² stays constant — it's a property of the world, not the model.

Total error is the sum, so it forms a U-shape against complexity: high on the left (bias-dominated), high on the right (variance-dominated), minimum in the middle. That minimum is what you're hunting for.

The key insight: the model that minimizes training error pushes bias all the way to zero — but at the cost of huge variance. The model that minimizes test error accepts a little bias in exchange for much lower variance. Regularization is the tool that lets you make that exchange.

⚠ HIGH BIAS

  • Underfit: model too simple
  • Predictions consistently off the truth
  • Same wrongness regardless of training set
  • Example: linear model on a curve

⚠ HIGH VARIANCE

  • Overfit: model too complex
  • Predictions jump around wildly from sample to sample
  • Memorizes noise instead of signal
  • Example: degree-15 polynomial on 20 points

TOPIC 3
Ridge Regression — Smooth Shrinkage (L2)
The first regularization tool

Ordinary linear regression and its problem

Ordinary least squares (OLS) finds the weights w that minimize squared error on the training set:

◆ ORDINARY LEAST SQUARES (OLS)
LOLS(w) = Σᵢ (yᵢ − xᵢᵀw)²
Pure training error. With many features, this can give huge weights that fit noise — high variance, overfitting.

The Ridge fix: add a size penalty

Ridge regression adds a term that punishes large weights. The model now has to balance fitting the data against keeping the weights small:

◆ RIDGE REGRESSION (L2)
LRidge(w) = Σᵢ (yᵢ − xᵢᵀw)² + λ · Σⱼ wⱼ²
The first term is OLS loss. The second term is the L2 penalty: the sum of squared weights, scaled by λ. The model accepts a little more training error in exchange for smaller weights — and smaller weights mean lower variance.
λ (lambda) = penalty strength · tunable
λ = 0 = pure OLS, no penalty
λ → ∞ = all weights → 0
Σⱼ wⱼ² = "size" of the weight vector, L2 norm squared

What Ridge does to the weights

  • Shrinks all weights smoothly toward zero as λ grows.
  • Never drives a weight exactly to zero — they get small but stay non-zero.
  • Spreads the load: if two features are correlated, Ridge gives them similar smallish weights instead of one huge and one tiny.
  • Has a closed-form solution: w = (XᵀX + λI)⁻¹ Xᵀy — fast to compute, works even when XᵀX is singular.
When to reach for Ridge: when you have many features that all contribute a little, and you don't expect any feature to be irrelevant. Default choice when in doubt.
TOPIC 4
Lasso Regression — Shrinkage + Selection (L1)
When you want sparsity

Same idea, different penalty shape

Lasso (Least Absolute Shrinkage and Selection Operator) does the same thing as Ridge — adds a penalty on weight size — but it uses the absolute value instead of the square:

◆ LASSO REGRESSION (L1)
LLasso(w) = Σᵢ (yᵢ − xᵢᵀw)² + λ · Σⱼ |wⱼ|
The second term is the L1 penalty: the sum of absolute weights. One small change (square → absolute value), one huge consequence: some weights become exactly zero. That's automatic feature selection, for free.
Σⱼ |wⱼ| = L1 norm of weight vector
Some wⱼ = 0 exactly · sparsity
λ ↑ → more weights become 0
No closed form · solved numerically

Why L1 produces sparsity (the geometric intuition)

Picture the constraint region — the set of weights with the penalty below some budget. For Ridge, the L2 ball is a smooth circle (or sphere in higher dimensions). For Lasso, the L1 ball is a diamond with sharp corners along the axes. The optimal solution is where the data's loss contour first touches this region.

  • The smooth Ridge circle is most likely to be touched on its side → all weights small but nonzero.
  • The Lasso diamond has its corners on the axes, so contact often happens at a corner → some weights become exactly zero.

◆ RIDGE — L2

  • Penalty: Σ wⱼ²
  • Constraint shape: smooth ball
  • All features kept, all shrunk
  • Handles correlated features gracefully
  • Has closed-form solution
  • Best when most features matter

◆ LASSO — L1

  • Penalty: Σ |wⱼ|
  • Constraint shape: diamond / cross-polytope
  • Some features dropped (weight = 0)
  • Picks one of a correlated pair, drops the other
  • Solved iteratively (coordinate descent)
  • Best when you want a sparse model
Elastic Net = combine both: λ₁·Σ|wⱼ| + λ₂·Σwⱼ². Gets sparsity from L1 and stability with correlated features from L2. Used heavily in genomics and text models.

TOPIC 5
Why Regularization Works — The MSE Trade
The deep reason

It's the bias-variance tradeoff in action

OLS is the unbiased estimator that minimizes squared training error. But unbiased ≠ best, because:

MSE = Bias² + Variance

Ridge and Lasso both introduce a small bias (because shrinking weights away from their OLS values means we're no longer hitting the unbiased optimum) — but they cut variance dramatically. Net effect: total MSE drops.

The shrinkage trade in one sentence

Regularization pays a small bias premium to buy a large variance discount.

The Bayesian interpretation (optional, but beautiful)

Regularization isn't just a hack — it has a clean probabilistic meaning. Adding a penalty to your loss is mathematically equivalent to maximizing a posterior instead of a likelihood:

  • Ridge ≡ linear regression with a Gaussian prior on the weights centered at 0. Bigger λ ⇔ tighter prior.
  • Lasso ≡ linear regression with a Laplace prior on the weights centered at 0. The Laplace's sharp peak at 0 is what produces sparsity.

You can think of regularization as expressing prior belief: "I think most weights should be small, and some might be exactly zero." Data updates that belief; the regularizer is the prior, the squared-error term is the (Gaussian) likelihood.

Choosing λ — cross-validation

The penalty strength λ is a knob you have to set. Too small and you barely regularize (overfit). Too big and you crush the signal (underfit). The standard procedure:

  • Split the training data into K folds (typically K = 5 or 10).
  • For each candidate λ in a log-spaced grid (e.g., 10⁻⁴ to 10²):
    • Train on K−1 folds, evaluate on the held-out fold.
    • Repeat for all K folds and average the test error.
  • Pick the λ with the lowest average validation error.
The "one-standard-error rule": instead of picking the absolute minimum, pick the largest λ within one standard error of the minimum. This favors simpler models with similar performance.
CONNECT
Where You'll See This in Practice

◉ scikit-learn

Ridge(alpha=λ), Lasso(alpha=λ), ElasticNet, RidgeCV, LassoCV. The alpha parameter is λ. Defaults are sane but always tune via CV.

◉ Deep Learning

Weight decay in optimizers (Adam, SGD) is L2 regularization — the same thing. PyTorch's weight_decay=1e-4 and TensorFlow's L2 kernel regularizer are doing Ridge on your network weights.

◉ XGBoost & GBMs

The reg_alpha (L1) and reg_lambda (L2) hyperparameters apply Lasso and Ridge penalties to the leaf weights of every tree. Plus tree depth limits act as a complexity constraint.

◉ Genomics & High-dim Data

When you have 20,000 genes and 200 patients, OLS is hopeless — XᵀX is singular. Ridge (and especially Elastic Net) is the workhorse for gene-expression studies and GWAS.

◉ Finance & Risk Models

Ridge stabilizes covariance estimates in portfolio optimization. Lasso is used for sparse factor models — pick a handful of significant factors out of hundreds.

◉ Text Classification

Logistic regression on bag-of-words has tens of thousands of features. Lasso drops the irrelevant words automatically; what's left is a tiny, interpretable model.
