01 — Why Meaning Is Hard

Words are slippery things

Languages took thousands of years to grow, and every one of them is riddled with ambiguity. We rarely have a single word for a thing — we mumble, mutter, and confer instead of merely "talk," each carrying its own shade of meaning. And one word can hold two unrelated lives at once.

Consider two sentences: "I want to go to the park" and "I will park my car." Same four letters; utterly different meanings. Add irony — where we say the exact opposite of what we mean — and you start to wonder how a machine could ever keep up.

And yet LLMs keep up beautifully. They manage this feat, in large part, through embeddings. So our whole task in this chapter is to build a rock-solid intuition for what an embedding actually is — because, as we'll see again and again, LLMs think in embeddings. Prompts, documents, the model's own knowledge — all of it is spoken in this one numerical language.

An embedding is how a machine stores the meaning of a word — not its spelling.

The same way you learned

Here's a comforting parallel. You learned language as a child by swimming in sentences — hearing the same words in countless contexts until their nuances soaked in. That learning never really stops. An LLM does the identical thing, only at inhuman scale: it reads millions or billions of sentences and gradually internalizes which words travel together. The mystery it solves along the way is twofold — how do you represent meaning as something a computer can compute, and how do you compress the lessons of billions of sentences into a few million numbers small enough to sit on a laptop? The answer to both is the embedding.

02 — What an Embedding Looks Like

A word becomes a list of numbers

An embedding is created from a word or sentence and stored as a very large vector — a long list of floating-point numbers. Each number, or component, measures the affinity between your target word and some other word in a vocabulary: how strongly the two belong together.

The book we're following uses a particular convention worth pausing on. Here, a number near zero means maximum affinity (the words are tightly bound) and a number near one means minimum affinity (barely related). So 0.0001 signals an extremely strong relationship, while 0.9999 means almost none. In the affinity bars below, a longer bar means a stronger bond — so we've drawn the bar length from the closeness, which is easier on the eye than the raw distance.

The "Menu" surprise — a teaching moment

Why does "Menu" sit at 0.9999, almost unrelated? Because in the billions of sentences the model read, "park" and "menu" rarely showed up near each other. Low co-occurrence → weak affinity. That single number is quietly telling you something true about the world: people don't talk about parks and menus in the same breath very often.

So what exactly is "affinity"?

Affinity is built by a patient counting process. The model iterates over hundreds of thousands — often millions or billions — of sentences containing the word, and notes which other words appear nearby. (It deliberately ignores filler words like the, to, and a, which sit beside everything and reveal nothing.)

Why should co-occurrence matter so much? Intuitively, words that keep company shape each other's meaning. See "park" beside "picnic" ten thousand times and a sense crystallizes: a park is a place where we picnic. But — and this is the crucial caveat — the effect depends on frequency. A pairing has to recur an enormous number of times to leave a measurable mark. A handful of stray co-occurrences are just noise; only sheer repetition forges a meaning the whole language agrees on.

1Everyday

"Bark" — tree or dog?

Strong affinity with "leash," "puppy," "loud" pulls one meaning out; affinity with "trunk," "oak," "peel" pulls the other. Same letters, two neighbourhoods.

2Business

"Apple" — fruit or phone?

A grocery catalogue's "apple" sits near "banana, orchard, crisp." A tech blog's sits near "iPhone, screen, battery." The embedding silently disambiguates which business you're in.

3Code

"Python" — snake or language?

Affinity with "import, function, loop" vs. "venom, reptile, jungle." A code-search tool leans on exactly this split to rank results.

03 — Visualising Meaning

Meaning lives on islands

Here's the property that makes everything work: when text is turned into its embedding, it lands near other text that means something similar. Similar meanings cluster into "semantic islands." Let's actually answer a question with this idea — "How can I go to the park?" — and watch the answer surface.

"go" → motion words "park" → place words intersection = the answer

The walk-through goes like this. The word "go" signals motion, so it sits among ride, drive, travel, move, run. A good answer — "drive a car or ride a bike" — contains those same motion words, so it lands on the Travelling Island too. Good: we know the answer is somewhere in that group. But the island also holds junk like "fly to Mars" and "paddle my boat."

To clear out the junk, we bring in the second word, "park." Park has weak affinity with boat, paddle, Mars but strong affinity with bike, car. So we look at the intersection of the two islands — sentences that are close to both "go" and "park" simultaneously. Filter out everything with low affinity to either, and a single hyper-relevant sentence is left standing: "you can drive your car or ride your bike to the park." That iterative narrowing — keep the strong, drop the weak — is the whole game.

Quick recap before we go faster

Words are symbols that can carry several meanings ("park" the place vs. "park" the verb). A word earns its shades of meaning by keeping frequent company with other words. LLMs, trained on billions of sentences, meet words in the same contexts we do — so they absorb the same nuances. Embedding vectors capture that, with each dimension reflecting how likely a vocabulary word is to appear near the target. And because closeness of vectors implies shared meaning, we can answer a question by iteratively discarding low-affinity sentences until only the relevant few remain.

That was a lot — it's meant to feel like drinking from a fire hose. The good news: from here on, machines do the inspecting for us.

04 — Measuring Closeness

The angle is the meaning

Inspecting affinity tables by hand is hopeless at scale — far too slow. We need a function that compares two whole vectors in one efficient stroke. The most popular choice by far is cosine similarity.

The idea is geometric and genuinely beautiful. A vector defines a single point in space; draw a line from the origin to that point and you get an arrow. Two words give two arrows, and between any two arrows there's an angle. Small angle → very similar meaning. Wide angle → unrelated. Cosine similarity simply reports the cosine of that angle: 1.0 when the arrows point the same way, near 0 when they're perpendicular (unrelated), negative when they oppose.

Interactive · drag to feel it

Swing the second arrow and watch how the angle maps to a similarity score. This is exactly what the cosine function computes — just in thousands of dimensions instead of two.

angle: 22°cosine similarity: 0.93≈ very similar

Our diagram is only two-dimensional, but a real embedding has hundreds or thousands of dimensions — and the principle holds in all of them. Take "car" and find every vector with a small angle to it, and you'll regrow the whole transport island: "bike," "drive," "truck." Do the same around "dog" and pets come tumbling out: "cat," "puppy," "leash." The cosine function is wonderfully cheap to compute even across thousands of dimensions, which is why it scales to real workloads. It's usually handed to you as an API once you install an embedding model — and an embedding model is simply an AI application whose one job is turning text into vectors.

4Everyday

Two compass needles

Point both north and they agree perfectly (cosine 1.0). Spin one east and they're unrelated (cosine 0). Meaning, like direction, is about where you point, not how long the arrow is.

5Business

Duplicate-ticket detection

Two support tickets worded differently — "can't log in" and "password won't work" — have a tiny angle between their embeddings, so a helpdesk auto-merges them.

6Code

Why we sometimes normalise

Some versions scale every vector to length 1 first, so only direction counts. But even un-normalised, the cosine of the angle works well as a similarity score.

05 — Embeddings in Code

Seeing it work for real

Theory's done; let's run it. This is the canonical "hello world" of embeddings: we take three sentences, turn each into a vector with a small, fast, well-regarded model (all-MiniLM-L6-v2), and compute how similar each pair is. Notice that this technique is gold for prompt engineers: before you ever call the LLM, you can measure whether a candidate piece of text is likely to actually help answer the prompt.

# A complete, runnable example — compare three sentences by meaning from sentence_transformers import SentenceTransformer from sklearn.metrics.pairwise import cosine_similarity import numpy as np # Step 1 — the sentences we want to compare sentences = [ "how do I go to the park", # 0 — our question "you can drive to the park", # 1 — a good answer "I love seafood" # 2 — totally unrelated ] # Step 2 — load a pre-trained model (small, fast, good quality) model = SentenceTransformer('all-MiniLM-L6-v2') # Step 3 — turn each sentence into its embedding vector embeddings = model.encode(sentences) # Step 4 — cosine similarity between every pair, all at once similarity_matrix = cosine_similarity(embeddings) # Step 5 — read the results in plain language pairs = [(0, 1), (0, 2), (1, 2)] for i, j in pairs: print(f"'{sentences[i]}' vs '{sentences[j]}': " f"{similarity_matrix[i][j]:.4f}")
▸ what prints to your console 'how do I go to the park' vs 'you can drive to the park': 0.7128 'how do I go to the park' vs 'I love seafood': 0.0461 'you can drive to the park' vs 'I love seafood': 0.0533

Read those three numbers and the whole chapter clicks into place. The question and its good answer score 0.71 — high, because they share the "going to the park" meaning even though they share almost no identical words. Both score near 0.05 against "I love seafood" — the seafood sentence lives on a completely different island, so the angle between them is wide and the cosine is tiny. The machine just judged meaning, not spelling. That's the payoff.

Of course, this little script won't survive production. There you might need to search thousands of documents in milliseconds — which calls for a purpose-built system. That system is the vector database.

A note on the numbers you'll see

Real models output similarity on a scale where 1.0 is identical and 0 is unrelated — the opposite direction from the affinity convention in the table earlier, where near-zero meant strongest. Both are valid; just always check which way your particular tool counts before you trust a threshold. Mixing the two conventions is one of the most common beginner bugs.

06 — Choosing Your Model

Not all embedding models are equal

Happily, you never build embeddings from scratch — dozens of pre-trained embedding models are a download away, each with its own personality from the data it was trained on. A great starting point for comparing them is the Hugging Face MTEB leaderboard, which ranks models across many tasks. Before committing to one for your project, weigh these factors:

▮Max tokens

The largest text the model will accept at once (a token ≈ 3–4 characters). If your chunk is too big, either pick a model with a higher limit or chunk smaller.

▤Memory

Models carry millions or billions of parameters that must load into RAM. Budget for this early — it bites in dev and test, not just production.

⊞Dimensions

The length of the output vector. More dimensions can capture more subtlety and nuance — at the cost of storage and compute.

$Cost

Run it locally, host it in the cloud, or call a provider's API. Infrastructure and per-call usage costs both matter.

⚕Training domain

Some models are steeped in a field like healthcare; others read the general internet. Match the model's world to your documents.

✦Zero-shot

Zero-shot models can place a brand-new word, sentence, or image sensibly without ever having trained on an example — ideal for niche, domain-specific data.

文Language

Some speak many languages; others are English-only. Obvious, but easy to forget until a multilingual query returns nonsense.

=Compatibility

Critical rule: the model that embeds your query must be the same one that embedded your documents. Check your vector DB's docs for supported models.

◎Model size

Billions of parameters means serious hardware. Bigger isn't automatically better for your budget or latency.

7Business

A hospital's notes engine

Pick a model trained on clinical text so "MI" reads as myocardial infarction, not "Michigan." Domain training quietly decides accuracy.

8Code

The mismatched-model bug

Documents embedded with Model A, queries with Model B — every search returns garbage because the two vector spaces don't line up. Compatibility isn't optional.

9Everyday

Translator vs. specialist

A multilingual model is the friend who speaks six languages passably; a domain model is the expert who knows one subject cold. Hire for the job you have.

07 — Vector Databases

A database that thinks in meaning

A vector database is to unstructured data what an RDBMS or NoSQL store is to structured data. Like them, it's tuned to query huge volumes fast — but unlike them, it returns results by semantic similarity rather than exact matches. You reach for one when you want to feed an LLM relevant chunks of your own trusted data — private documents the model never saw in training, or content you want it to rely on for precise control.

embedding step vector database user / prompt data flow

There are two processes to know. Ingestion (A) happens ahead of time: pull data from wherever it lives — a relational database, a file server, a web service — chunk it, embed each chunk, and store the vectors. Live query (B) happens per request: embed the user's prompt with the same model, search the database for the nearest chunks, staple those chunks onto the prompt, and hand the bundle to the LLM. This pattern of "fetch relevant context, then generate" is the backbone of RAG — retrieval-augmented generation — which the next chapter explores in depth.

08 — The Common Thread

Embeddings live in three places at once

Step back and a lovely symmetry appears. Embeddings aren't just a vector-database trick — they are the default language of GenAI, the single thread stitching the whole architecture together. The same kind of vector shows up in three places simultaneously:

①In the prompt

Your question

The user's prompt is converted to an embedding so we can ask the vector database for semantically similar data — not keyword matches, but meaning matches.

②In the database

Your documents

Every ingested chunk already sits there as a vector, waiting. The query vector simply finds its nearest neighbours.

③Inside the LLM

The model's knowledge

The LLM's own learned knowledge is stored as embeddings too. So when you pass it vectors, it can tap that knowledge and pull out more useful context to build its reply.

Prompt, retrieved data, and model knowledge all speak the same numerical dialect — which is exactly why they combine so smoothly. Once you can see embeddings as this connective tissue running through every box in the diagram, the rest of GenAI architecture stops looking like a pile of disconnected tools and starts looking like one coherent system.

09 — Chunking Documents

How you slice changes everything

When you ingest documents, you usually break them into small sections first — chunks that might map to chapters, sub-sections, or single sentences. Chunking buys efficiency: you rarely want to haul back a whole document. Even when a document fits inside the LLM's token limit, big retrievals cost more, clutter the conversation, and strain the memory used to cache chat history. Better still, chunking is where you get to apply real data-architecture thinking — using domain knowledge to sharpen the precision of every future answer.

The three dials you'll turn most are chunk size, chunking strategy, and chunk overlap. There are rules of thumb, but the honest truth is that the best settings come from tweaking and testing repeatedly (the subject of the next chapter). Two ideas anchor the decisions: aim for the minimum data that still lets the LLM answer well, and pick a splitting strategy suited to the document's shape.

A worked example: the Git book

Say you're loading a book about Git into a vector database so people can ask in plain English — like "record when the tips of branches and other references were updated" — instead of memorising obscure commands like reflog or rev-parse. Glancing at the chapter list, the topics look fairly independent of one another. The instinct, then, is to chunk by chapter with little or no overlap.

But watch what breaks. Someone asks, "how do I create a branch in Git?" The create-branch command lives in Chunk One. Yet genuinely useful neighbouring advice — when to create feature branches, i.e. branching strategy — sits in Chunk Two. With no overlap, the question's embedding matches Chunk One and never surfaces Chunk Two, because our slicing trapped all the closely-matching text in a chunk too small to hold the full context. The answer comes back thinner than it should.

The fix is to let the chunks overlap by 50%. Now the bottom half of Chunk One and the top half of Chunk Two contain the very same text — and therefore the same embeddings. Contextual continuity is preserved across the seam, so the branch question reliably pulls back both the command and the strategy. The cost of this generosity: more overlap raises the chance of returning redundant text for other queries, which inflates the LLM bill and clutters session memory, making conversations harder to manage. As with most of engineering, it's a trade-off you tune — never a free win.

10Business

Legal contracts

Clauses cross-reference each other constantly, so generous overlap keeps a definition attached to the clause that uses it. Slice too cleanly and meaning leaks out.

11Code

API docs

A function's parameters and its example often sit paragraphs apart. Overlap (or chunking by whole function) keeps them retrievable as one thought.

12Everyday

Tearing a recipe

Rip the ingredient list onto one scrap and the method onto another and neither scrap is much use alone. Overlap is leaving a few lines on both halves.

◆ Carry these forward
  1. An embedding is meaning as numbers. A word or sentence becomes a long vector; nearby vectors mean similar things. Spelling is irrelevant — direction is everything.
  2. Meaning is earned by company. Words gain their shades from frequently co-occurring with others — exactly how you learned language, just at billion-sentence scale.
  3. Cosine similarity reads the angle. Small angle, similar meaning. It's cheap even in thousands of dimensions, which is why it scales.
  4. Pick your model deliberately. Tokens, dimensions, domain, language, cost — and never mismatch the model between ingestion and query.
  5. Vector databases search by meaning, powering the "retrieve, then generate" RAG pattern that grounds LLMs in your own trusted data.
  6. Chunking is a design decision, not plumbing. Size and overlap directly shape answer quality — tune them, test them, respect the redundancy trade-off.
10 — Check Yourself

Four quick gut-checks

Answer each in your head, then reveal it. If all four land, embeddings are now part of how you think.

Why do "you can drive to the park" and "how do I go to the park" score high, despite sharing few words?
Because embeddings measure meaning, not vocabulary. Both sentences carry motion + park meaning, so their vectors point in nearly the same direction — a small angle, a high cosine score. Word overlap is irrelevant.
Your semantic search suddenly returns nonsense after a migration. First thing to check?
Whether the query and the documents were embedded with the same model. Mismatched models produce incompatible vector spaces, and similarity scores become meaningless. It's the classic compatibility bug.
A question keeps retrieving the command but never the surrounding advice. What knob do you turn?
Chunk overlap. With no overlap, related context gets stranded in an adjacent chunk the query never matches. Increasing overlap (e.g. 50%) preserves continuity across the seam — at the cost of some redundancy.
In how many places do embeddings exist in a RAG system, and where?
Three, simultaneously: in the prompt (so we can query by meaning), in the vector database (your stored documents), and inside the LLM (its own knowledge). They all speak the same numerical language — which is why they combine so cleanly.
