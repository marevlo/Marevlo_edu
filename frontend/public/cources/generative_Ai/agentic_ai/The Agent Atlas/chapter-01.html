01 — The Big Idea

You already know more than you think

Here is the thesis of this entire book, compressed into one sentence: building great GenAI applications is not about learning a hundred shiny new tools — it's about recognizing the decades-old engineering patterns hiding underneath them.

If you've ever sketched boxes-and-arrows on a whiteboard, you already understand abstraction and patterns intuitively. An abstraction lets you ignore noisy detail so the big shape becomes visible. A pattern is a reusable solution to a problem that keeps showing up. These two ideas are the lens we'll use to see through the fog of GenAI hype.

The wheel has already been invented. We just keep repainting it.

The GenAI world has a habit of reinventing everything as something new rather than extending what already works. That's great for marketing and exhausting for engineers. So before we touch a single agent framework, we're going to build a mental map — because a map drawn before you enter unfamiliar territory is worth ten guesses made while lost inside it.

03 — What Is an LLM, Really?

It depends who's asking

Ask "what is a zebra?" and every answer below is correct — each is simply useful in a different situation:

To a child

"A stripey animal that looks like a horse."

To a biologist

"A member of the genus Equus."

To a zookeeper

"A relative of the horse, 3.8–4.8 ft at the shoulder, 450–948 lbs, native to the African plains."

So when we ask "what is an LLM?", we should pick the answer most useful for building things. This book offers two, and you'll reach for whichever fits the problem in front of you.

① The optimistic view: the heir to Big Data

For decades, companies poured billions into data lakes, warehouses, and analytics — all to squeeze small insights out of mountains of data. LLMs are the next generation of that lineage, except they leap so far ahead they barely belong in the same category. They compress much of the internet into a few billion parameters, give a compelling impression of understanding, and replace dashboards with plain-language questions. In a sense, LLMs finally deliver the value Big Data always promised.

② The pragmatic view: a badly behaved web endpoint

For most of your real engineering challenges, the LLM is just a RESTful endpoint that happens to be non-deterministic, slow, occasionally unreliable, and not automatically secure. And that's good news — because you already own the toolbox for taming difficult endpoints: logging, retries, async callouts, rate limiting, failover, caching, and security layers all carry over unchanged.

1 Business

The "ask your data" dashboard

A retail ops team replaces a wall of BI charts with one text box: "Which stores missed their Q3 target and why?" The LLM is playing the Big-Data-heir role.

2 Code

Wrapping the flaky endpoint

You add a 3-retry exponential backoff and a 30s timeout around the model call — exactly the wrapper you'd put around any third-party API that sometimes hangs.

3 Everyday

The brilliant but unreliable intern

Astonishingly knowledgeable, occasionally makes things up, sometimes slow to answer. You'd never wire an intern's output straight into payroll without a check — same with an LLM.

04 — How LLMs Work

The one secret: guessing what comes next

Strip away the mathematics and one idea remains: an LLM predicts the most likely next word, over and over. No logic gates. No if-else statements. Just probability, learned from scanning a staggering amount of text.

You can feel exactly how this works with something you use every day — a search box. Start typing, and it offers the most probable continuations:

⌕ I want to buy
  • I want to buy a new jacketp · 0.31
  • I want to buy a carp · 0.24
  • I want to buy a couple of magazinesp · 0.11

Pick "a new jacket" and a fresh set of probable next words appears. Keep choosing, and a whole sentence builds itself — one likely word at a time.

A search box learns its probabilities from a database of past queries. Now imagine the same trick, but the "database" is nearly the entire internet. That, at a ten-thousand-foot level, is what an LLM does — and it's why the very same mechanism can finish a line of poetry, draft a report, or solve a math problem.

A delightful confession

If this feels incomplete, relax — nobody fully understands why it works so well either. AI is the rare field with two research branches: one makes the discoveries, the other tries to explain them after the fact. The mystery is a feature of the field, not a gap in your knowledge.

4 Everyday

Phone keyboard swipe

"On my way" appears before you finish "On my" — your keyboard is running a tiny next-word predictor, the LLM's pocket-sized cousin.

5 Business

Email autocomplete

"Thanks for your patience and…" → "understanding." A customer-service tool predicting the polite, probable ending of a sentence.

6 Code

The "temperature" dial

Set temperature near 0 and the model almost always picks the single most likely next token; raise it and it samples bolder, less probable words. You're literally tuning the gamble.

06 — What Is Agentic AI?

A team of specialists, not one genius

Agentic AI is simply component-based architecture applied to systems that use LLMs. The idea that many small, specialized parts beat one giant monolith has been engineering best practice for decades — agentic AI just brings it to language models.

One agent might be brilliant at reasoning; another at summarizing huge documents; a third at fact-checking. The interesting questions are about how they collaborate — who passes what to whom, and in which order. Some collaboration shapes work beautifully; others cause chaos. Those shapes are the patterns the rest of this book is built around.

7 Business

The insurance claim crew

A reader agent extracts facts from a claim PDF, a policy agent checks coverage rules, and a writer agent drafts the decision letter. Three specialists, one pipeline.

8 Everyday

A restaurant kitchen

The prep cook, the grill, the saucier, the plater — no single chef does everything. Specialization plus handoffs is exactly how multi-agent systems flow.

9 Code

Reason → summarize handoff

A reasoning agent solves a thorny problem and emits a verbose chain of thought; a summarizer compresses it into three crisp bullets for the user. Output of one becomes input of the next.

Orchestration vs. Choreography

Two ways to coordinate the crew — and both are ancient enterprise ideas:

Orchestration is a conductor model: one central component decides who acts and when. Choreography has no conductor — each component knows its own cues and reacts to events, coordinating in a decentralized dance. You'll meet both repeatedly.

The liberating catch

You do not need a fancy "agentic framework" to build an agentic app. Mature, trusted middleware — message brokers, service buses — already orchestrate and choreograph components in nearly every language. Building on tools that earned engineers' trust is often the wiser path, especially at scale.

What a "prompt-as-blueprint" looks like

Because patterns are now the shared language of "vibe coding," you can describe an architecture to an LLM the same way you'd brief an engineer:

# Describe the microarchitecture, not the framework ROLE: Orchestrator coordinating three specialist agents. AGENTS: - reader # extracts structured facts from a document - policy # checks facts against coverage rules (a TOOL/adapter) - writer # composes the final letter FLOW: orchestration # central control, sequential handoffs MEMORY: keep claim context in short-term "session" memory; log every decision to long-term "system of record". RETURN: a grounded, checked decision letter.

07 — Check Yourself

Three quick gut-checks

Tap each question, answer it in your head, then reveal the response. If all three click, you've got Chapter 1.

An "agent's short-term memory" — what have you actually built before?
Session state. It's the per-conversation scratchpad that lives only as long as the interaction. You already know how to persist it, expire it, and reason about race conditions when two requests touch it at once.
In one phrase, how does an LLM produce text?
It repeatedly predicts the most probable next word — like a search box autocompleting, but trained on a near-internet-sized body of text. No logic, no rules: pure learned probability.
If agentic AI is "nothing new," what exactly is new?
The microarchitectures — fresh combinations of agents — are new, and so is the LLM at the center. But the underlying software patterns (components, adapters, orchestration, choreography) are decades old. New arrangement, old building blocks.
◆ Carry these forward
  1. Old ideas, new costumes. Nearly every GenAI term maps to a concept you already use. Translate first, panic never.
  2. An LLM is two things at once — the heir to Big Data, and a badly behaved web endpoint. Pick the lens that fits your problem.
  3. The whole magic is next-word prediction. Probability, scaled to the internet. Even experts can't fully explain why it works.
  4. Agentic AI = component architecture for LLMs. Specialists collaborating via orchestration or choreography — patterns as old as enterprise software.
  5. Learn patterns, not products. Tools change weekly; principles don't. Bet on the principles.
