01 — What to Feed It

Two kinds of document, not one

The very first step in building an agentic application is finding the data worth feeding it — and then migrating that data into a vector database through a workflow that chunks, embeds, and stores. But here's a subtlety most people miss when they start: you should hunt for two distinct kinds of document, not one.

When we chat with ChatGPT or Gemini, we're usually content typing plain text. The real enterprise power, though, comes from supplying the LLM with extra material — and that material splits into two families. There are documents that carry raw data (the financials, the records, the facts to act on), and there are documents that carry process and "how-to" knowledge (the formulas, definitions, and procedures describing how the data should be used). The magic happens when you combine them in a single prompt.

Data tells the LLM what. Process documents tell it how.

A familiar analogy: it's just a class

If you've written object-oriented code, you already know this pattern intimately. A class bundles two things: functions (the behaviour, like foo()) and data (the fields, like ints and strings). RAG does the very same separation — only at the document level. The "how-to" document plays the role of the functions; the data document plays the role of the fields. Once you see that parallel, the whole approach stops feeling exotic and starts feeling like home.

The ZZZ Auto Insurance example

Make it concrete. ZZZ Auto Insurance wants to analyze its financials under the non-standard IFRS 17 accounting rules and compare them to GAAP. So the accountants upload two things: the IFRS 17 manual (the "how-to" — full of procedures and worked examples) and the company's financial documents (the raw data to operate on). A carefully written prompt then ties them together — something like "use the IFRS 17 manuals to analyze the financial document provided…" — alongside the relevant chunks pulled from the vector database by a semantic query. The LLM applies the how to the what, and produces an IFRS-compatible report. That separation of process from data, at the document level, is an extraordinarily powerful paradigm.

But none of it works until those documents are found and migrated into the vector database. The job might be small — a few dozen files — or enormous, spanning thousands. Either way, the tool that makes the migration bearable is a good ETL pipeline.

02 — Choosing the Mover

The challenge of moving enterprise data

Enterprises wanting to power GenAI with their own documents hit a wall fast. You can't simply attach thousands of files to an LLM call — both because the model has resource limits and because flooding it with irrelevant data produces unpredictable answers. So you must find a way to supply the prompt with only the most relevant pages, paragraphs, or sections. That means gathering everything and chunking it into a vector database first — and in a real enterprise, "gathering everything" is genuinely hard:

◇Scattered

Many homes

Documents live everywhere: file servers, drives, relational databases, SharePoint, Workday, AWS S3, GCS, HTTPS, SFTP — rarely in one place.

◇Many formats

Mixed types

PDF, spreadsheet, plain text, DOCX — each needing different extraction logic before it can be embedded.

◇Spread thin

Answers are diffuse

The content you need may be smeared across hundreds or thousands of documents, none of which holds the whole answer.

◇Uncategorized

No topics yet

Collections often aren't sorted by topic, so a preprocessing step to discover topics may be required.

◇Irregular

Different structures

Some PDFs have neat chapters and subchapters; others are a formless wall of text. One strategy won't fit all.

On top of these structural headaches, you must decide how to handle errors, enforce policy, and manage document versions. Walk this checklist before you write a line of pipeline code:

?

Error handling. When the pipeline hits an error, do you roll back the whole batch, log the offending file and continue, or something else?

?

Security & regulation. How do you enforce rules around PII, SOX, and GDPR as data flows through?

?

Freshness. When documents change, how do you update the embeddings — and how often?

?

Versioning. When a new version of a document is released, how do you handle the old one?

?

Topic collection. How do you gather data from a collection by topic?

?

Parallelism. How many ingestion pipelines should run at once?

Three ways to build the pipeline

The best move is to start with a mature ETL tool that fits your organization's needs and skills. If your company already licenses one, that's almost certainly your top candidate. Otherwise, you have three broad options:

1

Code your own

Only sensible when you have relatively few data repositories. Libraries like Spring Boot's ETL pipeline give you the plumbing for a DIY project — but you own every edge case.

2

GenAI tools like LangChain

Worth exploring, but given how recently these appeared and their limited enterprise exposure, don't be surprised if they underperform on throughput, security, and overall maturity.

3

A mature ETL platform ✓ our choice

Tested across many enterprise systems, with robust support and documentation. These tools took years to reach their maturity and have earned the trust of leaders and developers alike. As Chapter 1 warned: in frothy times, many new tools try to replace proven ones and flame out. Evaluate carefully — what's genuinely new, and is it as mature?

The book's pick — Airbyte

This book uses Airbyte: large community, open-source, multi-language. True to the book's theme, it doesn't force you to learn a new language like Python just to build GenAI apps — though if you already know Python, its SDK is excellent. The lesson isn't "always pick Airbyte"; it's "prefer a proven mover over an unproven one, and one your team can actually operate."

Tool choice is only half the battle. You also have to plan: deciding inputs and outputs, acceptable latency, security, and constraints — with business and IT working together on near- and long-term targets. Five planning activities matter most, and we'll walk each one: capacity planning & throughput, incremental synchronization, robust management/testing/CI-CD, security & compliance, and a testing & validation plan for continuous ingestion.

03 — Capacity Planning

Forget gigabytes — think embeddings per second

If you've sized ETL jobs before, you probably counted gigabytes. For vector ingestion, that's the wrong unit. The metric that actually governs your pipeline is embeddings per second (EPS) — how many embeddings you can produce and write without blowing past rate limits or budget.

Just as web engineers reason in requests per second (RPS), data engineers feeding a vector database reason in EPS. Every embedding call counts against an API quota, consumes bandwidth, and costs money. A realistic enterprise target lands somewhere between 50 and 200 EPS, depending on the provider — beyond that, the bottleneck usually shifts from compute to I/O or to throttling in the embedding API, and you must reach for parallelization. (Hugging Face's MTEB leaderboard lists speed metrics if you want to compare models; the mteb utility lets you benchmark EPS yourself.)

The back-of-the-envelope math matters enormously, because once ingestion starts, stopping and restarting from scratch is painful and expensive. The formula is simple: tokens per chunk × average chunks per document × number of documents = total embeddings, then divide by your EPS for the time. Play with the numbers below.

Don't forget the updates

The same arithmetic governs maintenance. A nightly batch that touches just 1% of documents can still be tens of thousands of embeddings. Your pipeline has to absorb these incremental loads without stalling the other processes that depend on the vector store. Plan for the steady drip, not just the big initial flood.

04 — The Three Stages

Extract, Transform, Load

However you build it, a vector-ingestion pipeline has the same three stages every ETL pipeline has ever had — which means your existing instincts transfer directly. The only twist is what flows through each stage: clean text comes out of Extract, vectors come out of Transform, and the vector database receives the Load.

Extract / Transform embedding step Load → vector DB

The crucial design choice: each stage should scale independently, communicating through message queues or temporary storage, and each should acknowledge completed messages so no document is lost if a subprocess crashes mid-run. Extraction is often CPU-bound (parsing PDFs) and scales horizontally with ease; embedding is API-bound and punishes you with throttling if you crank concurrency too high; loading is I/O-bound. Treating them as three separate, independently-tunable stages is what keeps the whole thing reliable under load.

05 — Staying Current

Data is never finished

Real enterprises don't have static data. Contracts renew, manuals get revised, regulations update. A robust pipeline assumes any document can change at any time — so you need a strategy for keeping the vector store fresh. There are two basic approaches, plus a real-time option.

Full reloads

Dead simple: delete everything and re-embed the whole collection.

Expensive — you pay to re-embed unchanged documents over and over.

Incremental updates

Detect and re-embed only what changed — far cheaper, keeps the store current.

Requires change-detection machinery: a cursor field or content hash.

Airbyte and similar tools support both through an incremental sync feature. You define a cursor field — say updated_at — or hash the file's content; when the field or hash changes, only that record is reprocessed. For genuinely real-time needs, reach for Change Data Capture (CDC) connectors, which watch the source system's transaction log and stream inserts, updates, and deletes into your pipeline. True event-by-event streaming usually isn't necessary — CDC with a short polling interval (say, one minute) gets you near-real-time sync.

The conveyor-belt mental model

Picture a conveyor belt that never stops: new documents go on at one end, processed embeddings come off the other, and the belt keeps moving. Your pipeline should run continuously, even when individual connectors or external APIs fail. Each failure gets logged, retried, and — if it still won't go through — quarantined for a human to inspect. Continuous operation, not a one-time bulk job, is the goal.

7Business

Nightly contract sync

A legal team's CDC connector catches every contract edit within a minute and re-embeds just that clause — the rest of the vector store is untouched and the cost stays tiny.

8Code

Hash-based change detection

No reliable updated_at? Hash the file bytes. If the hash differs from last run, reprocess; if not, skip. Cheap, robust, and works on any source.

9Everyday

The newspaper, not the encyclopedia

An encyclopedia is reprinted whole (full reload); a newspaper publishes only what's new each day (incremental). Your living knowledge base wants to behave like the newspaper.

06 — Security & Compliance

The embeddings are as sensitive as the source

In a serious organization, your pipeline won't even be approved unless you can prove it meets security and privacy requirements. Security must be designed in before you assemble the pipeline, not bolted on after. Documents often hold PII, trade secrets, or confidential correspondence — and moving them through third-party services without controls is risky and can violate laws like GDPR or SOX. The critical insight people miss: an embedding carries the same sensitivity as the document it came from. Vectorizing a secret doesn't make it safe.

Before ingestion, define classification levels — public, internal, restricted, confidential — and let the classification decide which connector, storage system, or embedding service may touch the data. Highly sensitive documents might be embedded with an on-premises model rather than a cloud API, so they never leave your walls.

◆Field-level masking

Redact before you embed

Strip phone numbers, IDs, and account numbers before the text is vectorized — the embedding can't leak what was never in it.

◆Encryption

In transit & at rest

TLS for every transfer; AES-256 for stored chunks and embeddings. Non-negotiable in regulated environments.

◆Audit trails

Who, what, when

Store metadata recording who uploaded which document and when — essential when compliance comes asking.

◆Versioning

Keep the old copy

When a new version appears, retain the previous one for traceability. History is a compliance asset, not clutter.

Provable controls

Large organizations' compliance teams often demand demonstrable controls — not promises. Enterprise-grade ETL offerings (Airbyte Enterprise, for instance) support role-based access, secret-vault integration, and encrypted logs precisely so you can show your work to an auditor.

07 — Parallelism & Discipline

Scale each stage, then treat it like real software

Parallelism can slash ingestion time — but done carelessly it causes instability. The rule: each connector should declare its concurrency level, because different stages have different limits. CPU-bound work like PDF text extraction scales horizontally with ease. API-bound work like embedding does the opposite — push concurrency too hard and you trigger throttling and failed requests. That's exactly why the balanced design separates the pipeline into the three concurrent stages we saw — Extraction, Embedding, Loading — each scaling independently and acknowledging its own messages so nothing is lost when a subprocess crashes.

And here's the framing that ties this whole chapter together: a GenAI pipeline is software, and deserves the same DevOps discipline as any microservice. Store connector configs and transformation code in version control. Test in a staging environment before production. Treat schema changes — like altering a metadata field — as migrations with review and rollback plans.

CI/CD for embeddings

Continuous integration means every pipeline change — say, switching from text-embedding-ada-002 to text-embedding-3-large — automatically triggers tests verifying that vector dimensions still match and retrieval accuracy holds. Continuous deployment then promotes the change only when all tests pass. Coupled with CDC or incremental syncs, this lets you evolve chunking logic, embedding parameters, or security filters without downtime, confident that existing data stays consistent.

10Code

The model-swap test gate

Changing embedding models silently breaks search if dimensions mismatch. A CI test that asserts dimension equality and re-runs retrieval accuracy catches it before users ever notice.

11Business

Schema change = migration

Adding a "department" metadata field isn't a casual edit — it's a migration with a rollback plan, reviewed like any database change. Treat it that way and you avoid 2 a.m. surprises.

12Everyday

Three lanes, three speeds

Extraction, embedding, loading are like three lanes of traffic moving at different speeds. Force them all to one speed and everything jams. Let each flow at its own pace.

08 — Testing & Cost

Did the meaning survive the journey?

Testing an ingestion pipeline is more than checking that files arrived — you have to confirm the semantic meaning survived the trip. Build it up in layers, starting with unit tests for each stage: extraction tests verify that PDF-to-text preserves paragraphs and headings; embedding tests compare cosine similarity between known sentence pairs; load tests ensure vector dimensions match the model's output. Then run integration tests with sample prompts — query the database with a handful of known questions and manually inspect the top five chunks. If the matches are relevant, your embeddings and metadata schema are healthy; if not, revisit chunk size or preprocessing.

Finally, add continuous validation: as new documents arrive, sample 1% of embeddings and recompute similarities. A drift in average similarity or retrieval accuracy is an early warning of silent corruption or inconsistent preprocessing between runs — the kind of bug that's invisible until users complain.

Throttling is a financial control, not just a technical one

Because every token sent for embedding costs money, throttling guards your budget as much as your stability. Most providers publish rate limits (e.g. 3,500 requests/minute), and many ETL tools let you set per-connector rate limits to stay within quota. But the common mistake is throttling too aggressively — leaving expensive GPUs idle while documents pile up. The right approach is dynamic throttling: adjust concurrency based on real-time feedback from API response codes, using exponential back-off (1s, 2s, 4s, 8s) when you hit limits. For huge backlogs, schedule ingestion off-peak or use batch embeddings that pack several chunks into one request — optimizations that can cut costs in half in high-volume systems.

# Back off when the API pushes back, speed up when it doesn't delay = 1 # seconds while backlog: resp = embed(batch) if resp.status == 429: # rate limited sleep(delay) delay = min(delay * 2, 8) # 1→2→4→8s exponential back-off else: delay = max(delay / 2, 1) # GPUs idle? push harder commit(resp)

Measuring retrieval quality

Once data is in the vector store, you have to measure how well it actually performs — and there are standard metrics for it. Precision@k is the proportion of the top-k returned chunks that are genuinely relevant, and recall@k is the proportion of all relevant chunks that were retrieved. A reasonable baseline for enterprise documentation is precision@5 > 0.8 — meaning at least four of the top five chunks should be on-target.

The professional move is to automate this. Keep a test suite of (question → expected answer) pairs and re-run it periodically to catch regressions after any code or model change. This single practice is what transforms GenAI from hopeful experimentation into an engineering discipline grounded in measurable outcomes.

precision@k

How clean is the catch?

Of the top-k chunks you returned, what fraction are actually relevant? Punishes returning junk. Target: precision@5 > 0.8.

recall@k

Did you miss anything?

Of all the relevant chunks that exist, what fraction did you retrieve? Punishes leaving good material behind.

regression suite

Stay honest over time

A fixed set of question→answer pairs, re-run after every change, so a "small tweak" can't silently degrade quality.

13Business

The 80% gate

A support-doc bot ships only when precision@5 clears 0.8 on the test suite. Below that, too many irrelevant chunks confuse the LLM and frustrate users.

14Code

Drift detection

Sampling 1% of new embeddings nightly and watching average similarity caught a preprocessing bug that had quietly started stripping headings — before any user noticed.

09 — Beyond Simple Search

Four ways to make retrieval smarter

When data grows complex or voluminous, plain semantic search stops being enough. Early GenAI systems just ingested documents and generated embeddings; mature ones layer on structure. Here are four advanced techniques that, together, turn a humble vector database into a genuine knowledge platform.

🗂️ Taxonomy discovery

Retrieval can return relevant chunks while having no idea how they relate to the organization's broader concepts. Traditionally that structure is a taxonomy — a hierarchy of topics. In most enterprises taxonomies already exist but in fragments: product catalogs, compliance classifications, support topic trees — and ingestion into a vector database usually destroys whatever structure those systems had.

Taxonomy discovery rebuilds it from the embeddings themselves. Because embeddings encode meaning as position in space, clustering algorithms (k-means, hierarchical clustering) naturally group documents about similar concepts. An insurer's thousands of policies, claims procedures, and regulations cluster into recognizable groups — underwriting, claims handling, regulatory reporting — which engineers can then review and label into explicit categories.

The beautiful part: it evolves with the data. Re-cluster periodically and emerging topics surface automatically — invaluable in fast-moving fields like cybersecurity, where new threat categories appear constantly. Pair clustering with keyword extraction (TF-IDF or transformer-based) and you get candidate taxonomy trees that experts refine.

▸ Payoff: taxonomy labels become metadata filters. Instead of pure similarity, you can restrict retrieval to a specific conceptual branch — improving both precision and interpretability.

🔎 Hybrid search

Vector search is wonderful at meaning — "vehicle insurance" and "automobile coverage" land near each other despite sharing no words. But pure semantic search has a blind spot: queries that need an exact match. A product code, a legal clause number, a specific technical identifier — for these, old-fashioned keyword search often wins.

Hybrid search combines both, each covering the other's weakness:

Lexical (BM25)

Fast, deterministic, perfect for exact words and codes.

Misses synonyms — "vehicle" won't match "automobile."

Vector similarity

Captures meaning across different wording.

Can surface things semantically near but not precise enough.

A typical implementation computes both a lexical and a vector score, then merges them with a weighted formula; documents strong on both rank highest. Many vector databases now support hybrid retrieval directly, or integrate with Elasticsearch/OpenSearch in a two-stage design — lexical retrieves candidates, vectors rerank them.

▸ Bonus: hybrid search is more transparent. You can inspect the lexical component to see why a document matched — priceless in regulated settings where decisions must be explainable.

🧹 Data cleaning

No embedding model can rescue badly-prepared text — retrieval quality lives or dies on input quality. Before embedding, documents should be normalized into consistent text. The classic offenders and their fixes:

Repeated fragments — headers, footers, page numbers that recur on every page — must be stripped, because they introduce artificial similarity between unrelated documents. OCR errors from scanned PDFs (merged words, wrong characters) need spell correction and sentence segmentation, or the embeddings won't reflect the real meaning. Duplicates (the same file stored in three systems) should be caught with hashing before ingestion, so retrieval doesn't return three identical chunks and crowd out diversity. Metadata enrichment — tagging each chunk with department, project, or category — lets queries filter by attribute and sharpens precision. And terminology standardization ("customer" vs "client" vs "policyholder") improves interpretability and reduces ambiguity.

▸ Principle: clean data ensures embeddings represent meaningful content, not formatting artifacts. The bigger your collection grows, the more this pays off.

🕸️ Graph databases & knowledge graphs

Vector databases excel at similarity but are weak at explicit relationships — ownership, dependencies, causal links. Graph databases fill that gap by representing information as nodes (entities: a product, document, organization, concept) and edges (the relationships between them). Combine the two and you get something powerful: vector search finds the relevant pieces, and graph traversal reveals how those pieces connect.

Back to our insurer: a vector search retrieves passages about accounting rules; a graph database then links those rules to related entities — regulatory bodies, financial instruments, compliance procedures — letting the system reason in a structured way. Graphs also support explainability: when the system answers, the graph can show the chain of relationships that led there, which matters enormously in finance, healthcare, and law where decisions must be auditable.

Construction usually starts during ingestion: named-entity-recognition models pull out entities, relationship-extraction models infer how they connect, and the results are stored in the graph alongside the documents. At query time, retrieval can first fetch documents by embedding, then expand through graph relationships to gather more context — a technique known as GraphRAG.

▸ Result: explicit reasoning structures that take GenAI beyond similarity search into genuine analysis.

Toward integrated knowledge retrieval

These four aren't competing options — they're a natural evolution that stacks. Taxonomy discovery adds conceptual structure; hybrid search adds lexical precision; data cleaning ensures embeddings mean what they should; graph databases add a relational reasoning layer. Together they turn a simple vector store into a comprehensive knowledge platform that mirrors how humans actually organize knowledge — through both similarity and relationships. Increasingly, mature architectures combine vector databases, lexical engines, and graph databases as a matter of course.

10 — Check Yourself

Four quick gut-checks

Answer each in your head, then reveal it. If all four land, you can plan a real ingestion pipeline with confidence.

Why size an ingestion pipeline in embeddings per second instead of gigabytes?
Because the real constraints are the embedding API's rate limits and cost, both of which scale with the number of embedding calls — not raw file size. Each call counts against a quota, uses bandwidth, and costs money. EPS (typically 50–200) directly predicts both your runtime and your bill; gigabytes don't.
A document's text is confidential. Is it safe once it's been turned into an embedding?
No. An embedding carries the same sensitivity as the source document — it's a representation of the same meaning. Highly sensitive content should be masked before embedding and/or embedded with an on-premises model so it never leaves your environment, with encryption in transit and at rest.
A user searches for an exact legal clause number and gets fuzzy near-misses. What fixes it?
Hybrid search. Pure vector similarity is great for meaning but weak on exact identifiers. Adding a lexical (BM25) component — and merging the two scores — ensures exact matches like clause numbers or product codes aren't missed, while the vector half still handles synonyms.
Retrieval returns the right documents but can't show how concepts relate. What do you add?
A graph database / knowledge graph. Vector search finds relevant pieces; graph traversal reveals the relationships between them (ownership, dependencies, regulatory links) and supplies an explainable chain of reasoning. Combining the two at query time is GraphRAG.
