Meet Nexus.

A large financial services firm has hired you to build their next-generation fraud detection platform. Rules engines miss novel attack patterns. Classical ML requires constant feature engineering. The mandate: deploy an LLM that reads transaction context — merchant history, device telemetry, session patterns, free-text chargeback reasons — and outputs a fraud score in under 80 ms, twenty-four hours a day, across three continents.

Case Study · Nexus v0

The naive deployment failed on day one.

The platform team took a fine-tuned 70B-parameter model, wrapped it in a FastAPI server, and pointed production traffic at it. The first hour produced a predictable disaster: p99 latency at 4.2 seconds, GPU memory utilization at 34%, throughput capped at 1,100 tokens/sec, and a cloud bill projected at $2.8M per month for a single region. Fraud analysts rejected the deployment within 48 hours.

Over the next eight modules you will rebuild Nexus from first principles — applying every technique in the modern inference stack. By the final module, the same workload runs at p99 < 60 ms, GPU utilization above 88%, throughput above 40,000 tokens/sec per node, and cost reduced by 23×. Each module ends with a "Nexus progresses" checkpoint showing exactly which lever moved which metric.

The Three Pillars

What sets this course apart.

Every module is engineered around three non-negotiables that we believe separate world-class LLMOps material from generic tutorials.

Pillar 01

First-principles internals

We do not just tell you that PagedAttention is faster. We derive the memory math, show the block table layout, compare fragmentation under identical workloads, and walk through the actual vLLM source. You leave understanding why — not just what.

Pillar 02

A living case study

Nexus is not a toy example bolted on at the end. Every chapter adds one concrete architectural decision to the Nexus platform. By module eight, you have co-designed a production-grade fraud detection LLM, end to end, with measurable improvements at every step.

Pillar 03

Real numbers, real code

No pseudocode. Every technique ships with production-grade vLLM, PyTorch, TensorRT-LLM, or Triton code. Every claim comes with benchmarks — tokens per second, dollars per million tokens, p50/p99 latency across H100, H200, A100, and MI300X. The numbers match published results.

Learning Outcomes

What you will be able to do after.

A

Derive the roofline model from scratch

Given any GPU and model, compute arithmetic intensity, identify the bottleneck regime, and predict peak achievable throughput before writing a single line of code.

B

Choose the right quantization method

Select between GPTQ, AWQ, SmoothQuant, FP8, or QAT based on target hardware, accuracy budget, and calibration data availability — and defend the choice with numbers.

C

Configure vLLM and TensorRT-LLM for any workload

Tune PagedAttention block size, enable RadixAttention prefix caching, set up disaggregated prefill/decode, and benchmark speculative decoding acceptance rates for your domain.

D

Prune and distill models in production

Apply SparseGPT and Wanda for one-shot pruning, design a two-stage distillation pipeline, and configure 2:4 sparsity kernels with measurable quality retention.

E

Design MoE serving infrastructure

Lay out expert parallelism across GPUs, balance load with auxiliary losses, handle all-to-all communication overhead, and debug hot-expert skew in production.

F

Own an LLM system design interview

Walk any interviewer from a napkin sketch to a production-grade multi-region architecture with capacity math, failure modes, observability, and a defensible cost model.
