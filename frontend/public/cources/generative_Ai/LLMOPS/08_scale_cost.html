01 · The Inference Hardware Matrix

Five accelerators, six axes that matter.

Picking inference hardware is not about which chip has the highest FLOP/s on a spec sheet. It's about the interaction between the model, the workload, the serving stack, and the chip — and different chips win in different corners. This section lays out the five accelerators that matter for LLM inference in 2026, scored on the six axes that actually determine cost-per-token in production: HBM bandwidth, HBM capacity, FP8/INT4 support quality, interconnect (NVLink-class or not), software maturity, and total cost of ownership per throughput unit.

🔑

The two numbers that actually decide

For memory-bandwidth-bound decode workloads — which is the dominant mode for LLM serving after Module 1 — the two numbers that matter are HBM bandwidth (decode throughput ceiling) and HBM capacity (how many concurrent requests you can hold). Software maturity is the tie-breaker and FP8/FP4 is the multiplier. On bandwidth per dollar, MI300X and H200 both beat H100; on software maturity, H200 is the clear winner today; on capacity per dollar, MI300X wins but the software deficit bites on MoE and GQA-heavy models. For Nexus v7, H200 is the choice. We evaluate MI300X again in six months.

The hidden cost: software lift

The accelerator comparison is rarely decided on hardware alone. What decides it is the cost to port and maintain your stack on the new platform. A performance engineer's year costs more than a cluster of H200s — a supposed 30% hardware saving vanishes the moment a team needs to debug a missing kernel or rewrite a custom attention op.

What you get for free on NVIDIA today

vLLM, SGLang, TensorRT-LLM, all major PEFT libraries, FlashAttention-3, every quantization recipe (GPTQ, AWQ, SmoothQuant, FP8, QuIP#), speculative decoding with EAGLE-2, CUDA graph capture — shipped, tested, fast. Your team's time goes entirely into product, not kernels.

What costs engineering time elsewhere

MI300X: FlashAttention-2 via AITER is solid, FA-3 partial, some custom kernels missing. Trainium2: Neuron SDK ports most things but MoE and long-context optimization lag. TPU: excellent if you commit to JAX; painful if you maintain PyTorch. Expect 2-5 FTE-quarters to port a mature stack to a non-NVIDIA platform.

02 · The Cost-Per-Token Ladder

From $0.00094 to $0.00017.

Every optimization in this course produced a number. When you chain them together you get a cost ladder — a sequence of versions where each step subtracts a fraction of the per-token cost. This is the single artifact that defends the entire architecture to finance. It also shows which optimizations pay the most, which matters for prioritization when building the next system from scratch.

The lesson hidden in the percentages

The biggest single wins were quantization (M2, −61%) and batching/speculation (M5, −61%) and compression (M6, −68%). The second-biggest was KV engineering (M4, −41%). Attention kernels alone (M3) contributed less in isolation but were the enabler that made longer context and MoE possible. The system engineering of M7 contributed comparatively little in steady-state cost but was what let us actually deploy v6 at three 9's uptime — without which the steady-state cost would be irrelevant because the product would be unreliable.

The cost-per-token formula, in full

Production cost per output token — full decomposition cost_per_token =
  (gpu_$/hr × gpus_per_req × req_time_s / 3600) / output_tokens

where req_time_s ≈ TTFT + n_output_tokens × TPOT_effective
  TPOT_effective = TPOT / tokens_per_big_pass  (spec. decoding divides this)
  gpus_per_req ≈ 1 / effective_batch_size  (continuous batching divides this)

Every optimization in this course shows up in one of those terms. Quantization and MoE reduce the GPU-hours needed for the same throughput (via smaller compute and bandwidth per token). Continuous batching increases the effective batch size, which reduces gpus_per_req. Speculative decoding increases tokens_per_big_pass, which reduces TPOT_effective. Prefix caching reduces TTFT. Disaggregation reduces queue time embedded in TTFT. Understanding which term your optimization attacks is the entire skill of cost optimization.

03 · Capacity Planning for Spikes

Provision for the peak, pay for the average.

Nexus's traffic has a daily pattern (4× peak/trough), a weekly pattern (weekend dip), and an annual pattern (10× Black Friday spike). Provisioning for peak means paying 10× for 1% of the year; provisioning for average means dropping traffic 11 months out of 12. The right answer is neither. The right answer is a four-tier capacity stack with each tier committed at a different time horizon.

The four-tier capacity model

Capacity Tiers · Committed, Reserved, On-Demand, Spill

Sizing the tiers — the actual math

Capacity tier sizing formula peak_demand = 4× daily_peak × 2.5× Q4_multiplier = 10× trough
committed_cap = 0.40 × peak_demand ← serves P50 demand at 100% util, deepest discount
reserved_cap = 0.30 × peak_demand ← serves P80 demand, mid discount
ondemand_cap = 0.20 × peak_demand ← absorbs weekly peaks + 50% of Q4 spike
spill_cap = 0.10 × peak_demand ← multi-cloud, only Nov–Dec
blended_cost ≈ 0.52 × on_demand_cost (60% savings vs. pure on-demand)

The choice of 40/30/20/10 depends on the demand profile. A workload with flatter demand (business chatbot) shifts toward committed. A workload with spikier demand (consumer retail) shifts toward on-demand and spillover. The common mistake is either extreme: committing 100% (pay for peak capacity 24/7/365) or 100% on-demand (leave 60% of potential discount on the table).

Pre-provisioning for the known spike

For events you know about in advance — Black Friday, product launches, regulatory deadlines — you pre-warm capacity hours before the ramp. Cold-start of a new GPU pod takes 4–9 minutes (pull image, load weights, initialize KV pool, warm CUDA graphs). Autoscaling cannot respond to a 10-second spike; provisioning has to be ahead of the curve.

⚡

The pre-warm runbook

Nexus has a pre-warm runbook for Black Friday: T−6 hours scale committed + reserved + on-demand to 80% of expected peak (absorbs errors in forecast); T−2 hours activate spillover pool in secondary cloud (takes 30 min to warm); T+0 ramp begins, autoscaler handles residual spikes on already-warm pool; T+18 hours scale down to reserved baseline. Total pre-warm cost: about 0.3% of annual spend. Cost of getting caught flat-footed in 2023 (pre-Nexus-v7): 6 hours of elevated error rate, one apologetic board-level writeup, and an estimated $2.1M in lost merchant trust. The 0.3% is cheap insurance.

04 · War Stories

Six incidents that shaped the rules.

Every strict rule in this course corresponds to an incident where breaking it caused real pain. These are anonymized — the specifics are blurred, the company names removed — but the shape of each story is real, taken from Nexus's own on-call history and adjacent teams' postmortems. They're included because the rule, divorced from the story, is easy to explain away as paranoia. With the story, the rule sticks.

War Story · 01
The timestamp that destroyed the prefix cache
An intern on an upstream team shipped a helpful change: prepend the current date to the LLM system prompt so the model knows "today." Innocent. Reasonable. Shipped on a Wednesday afternoon. The RadixAttention hit rate fell from 78% to 4% within 20 minutes. TTFT p99 tripled from 72 ms to 240 ms. SLO compliance dropped to 96% in one region, triggering PagerDuty. Root cause took 90 minutes to find because the infra dashboards were all green — GPU util was fine, queue depth was normal, error rate was zero. The only signal that was off was the prefix-cache hit rate, which we'd added to the on-call dashboard the previous week for unrelated reasons. Without it, finding the cause would have taken a full day.
Rule born
System prompt must be byte-stable. All variable content goes AFTER the stable prefix.
Second rule
Prefix cache hit rate is a top-row alert, not a diagnostic metric.
Cost of the outage
~90 minutes of elevated latency; no revenue loss but one tenant filed a formal SLO credit request.
War Story · 02
The quantization that passed eval but failed in production
A candidate v6.1 model with a new FP8 KV cache scheme scored within 0.002 F1 of v6.0 on our entire offline eval set — essentially indistinguishable. Shipped via canary: 1% → 5% → 25% over three days, all green. At 25% the quality-drift sampler started flagging elevated scores on one specific tenant's traffic. Investigation revealed: that tenant's traffic contained a rare token pattern (merchant names with unusual Unicode characters) that was underrepresented in our eval set. FP8 KV quantization error compounded on those specific patterns in a way that produced subtly wrong fraud scores — enough to cause a false-positive burst on legitimate cross-border transactions. Rolled back in 90 seconds; tenant was made whole; we added a dedicated eval slice for every tenant's traffic distribution.
Rule born
Offline eval is necessary but not sufficient. Always run 7+ days in shadow on real traffic before canary. Always have an always-on quality sampler keyed by tenant.
Second rule
Eval slices must reflect the production traffic distribution, not the eval-dataset distribution. Underrepresented tenants are where regressions hide.
What saved us
Canary staging and the per-tenant quality sampler. Without both, this would have landed at 100% and taken days to diagnose.
War Story · 03
The autoscaler that scaled down during the spike
The original Nexus v3 autoscaler was configured on GPU utilization — a reasonable default at the time. During a traffic surge from an unannounced merchant migration, GPU utilization actually dropped from 78% to 52% — because the new traffic had longer prompts that caused prefill to queue behind decode, so throughput fell while utilization appeared lower. The autoscaler interpreted "lower utilization" as "over-provisioned" and scaled us down by 30% right as traffic was climbing. TTFT p99 went from 80 ms to 4.2 seconds in 8 minutes. We paged out, manually pinned capacity, and spent three days rewriting the scaling policy to use queue depth and request admission rate instead.
Rule born
Never autoscale LLM inference on GPU utilization alone. Queue depth + admission rate are the signals that reflect user experience.
Second rule
Scale-down decisions need a longer evaluation window than scale-up. Asymmetric thresholds: scale up on 30s sustained breach, scale down only after 10 minutes.
Cost
~8 minutes of hard user-facing outage. The most expensive mistake in Nexus's history to that point.
War Story · 04
The 2:4 sparsity that broke on INT4 weights
During v6 development, we naively combined 2:4 structured sparsity (for GEMM speedup on H100) with AWQ W4 quantization (for memory savings). The combination looked promising on paper: 4× memory reduction and ~1.9× compute speedup on dense layers. What we missed: the 2:4 sparsity pattern was applied to the INT4-quantized weights directly, which concentrated quantization error on the surviving weights and produced a model that scored 2.8 F1 points lower than either technique alone. Took two weeks and a frustrated message from a quantization researcher to figure out the right order: prune first in FP16, apply 2:4 structured mask, recover via short fine-tune, then quantize. Changed our M6 recipe permanently.
Rule born
Compression techniques compose non-commutatively. The order matters, and the default order is largest-preservation-cost last: prune → sparsify → quantize, with recovery fine-tunes between each step.
Second rule
"Both techniques work individually" does NOT imply "they work together." Always evaluate the combined model end-to-end.
Cost
Two weeks of engineering time; no production impact because we caught it before any rollout.
War Story · 05
The disaggregation that made everything slower
Early in v4 design, we tried to disaggregate prefill and decode across two separate data centers connected by regular internet routing — because there was spare H100 capacity in a DC across the city. It seemed like a clean separation. What we discovered the hard way: KV transfer over the internet added 180 ms of tail latency per request, which completely obliterated any scheduling win from separation. For a 200-token output, that's nearly as much as the entire budget. Rolled back to cohabitated-with-chunked-prefill in a day, and the next attempt at disaggregation used RDMA InfiniBand within a single rack group.
Rule born
Disaggregated prefill/decode requires NVLink or RDMA between pools. Anything slower makes it worse than cohabitation.
Second rule
Always measure the operational overhead of an optimization, not just the steady-state benefit. KV transfer is a tail-latency source that doesn't show up in throughput benchmarks.
Cost
One day of degraded latency; ~0.4% SLO miss that month.
War Story · 06
The draft model that became a liability
EAGLE-2 speculation gave Nexus v5 a 3.1× effective decode speedup — but the draft model was trained on a snapshot of traffic from eight weeks earlier. Over the following quarter, the traffic distribution shifted: more travel-category transactions, fewer retail. The draft model's acceptance rate drifted from 0.85 down to 0.62 without any alarm firing. Effective speedup fell to 1.9×. We didn't notice for six weeks — cost-per-token had drifted up 35% in that time, which showed up on the finance side as "compute spend growing faster than request volume" but hadn't been tagged as a technical regression. Finance caught it before engineering did. Now the draft model is re-trained weekly on a rolling window of recent traffic, and acceptance rate is a first-class on-call dashboard metric.
Rule born
Draft model freshness matters. Re-train weekly on recent traffic. Monitor acceptance rate as a top-row SLO-adjacent metric.
Second rule
Cost-per-token drift is a legitimate engineering signal, not just a finance concern. Engineering and finance dashboards should share the same source of truth.
Cost
~6 weeks of 35% cost overrun. A seven-figure lesson in "monitor what you optimize."
🔑

The common shape of every war story

Read the six stories above and a pattern emerges: the failure mode is never the thing being optimized. Quantization didn't break; it broke on a specific tenant's Unicode. Autoscaling didn't break; it broke because the signal was wrong. Disaggregation didn't break; the transport was wrong. EAGLE-2 didn't break; the draft model drifted. The lesson that unifies them: optimize the steady state, but instrument the failure modes. Every optimization ships with an alert on its own health. Prefix hit rate. Speculation acceptance rate. KV transfer latency. Quality drift. Because nothing in production stays steady-state forever.

06 · Debugging Drills

Four traps at the cost-and-scale level.

The hardest failures in LLM serving aren't in the model or the engine — they're at the economic and autoscaling layer, where signals are noisy, feedback loops are slow, and a single misread takes weeks to correct. These four drills are the M8-level issues: cost creep, hardware mismatch, autoscaler oscillation, and capacity-planning regressions. Each is a pattern that's specifically hard to see from inside a single module.

Drill · 01
Cost-per-million-tokens rising month-over-month at flat traffic
SYMPTOM
Finance flags it, not engineering. Monthly cost-per-million-tokens rose from $2.14 to $2.63 over 4 months (23% increase). Request count is flat. Latency metrics all green. No deploys that should have raised cost. Finance asks "what changed?"
WRONG HYPOTHESIS
"Cloud pricing went up" — check the invoice, pricing is stable. "GPU supply tightened" — but your provisioning is unchanged. Neither explains a 23% cost rise.
CORRECT DIAGNOSIS
The composite culprits, in descending order of probability: (a) speculation drift — α dropped from 0.85 to 0.68 silently; run M5's draft_model_acceptance_tracker.py. (b) Prefix cache hit rate drift — was 78%, is now 35%; run M4's radix_prefix_analyzer.py. (c) Quantization calibration staleness — F1 dropped, team auto-reverted some tenants to FP16 without telling cost accounting. (d) Prompt length bloat — avg prompt grew from 1,200 to 1,850 tokens because a product team added RAG context. Each of (a)(b)(c)(d) alone accounts for 5-10% cost; in combination, 20-30%.
FIX
Build a cost-attribution dashboard with four tiles: mean α, prefix hit rate, active quantization dtype, mean prompt length. If any moves >10% vs baseline, that's the suspect. For this specific case, the 23% cost rise usually decomposes as ~11% from α drift + ~8% from prefix drift + ~4% from prompt bloat. Fix speculation and prefix first (cheap — retrain/retune), then work with product teams on prompt length. Goal: get cost-per-million back to $2.20 within 30 days. Set an SLO on cost-per-token, not just on latency.
Drill · 02
H200 deployed; benchmark shows 15% WORSE throughput than H100
SYMPTOM
Provisioned H200s expecting 1.4× throughput over H100 (per HBM bandwidth spec). Measured on production workload: H200 gives 3,400 tok/s; H100 gives 4,000 tok/s. Rollback underway; team blames the hardware.
WRONG HYPOTHESIS
"H200 is defective / drivers are broken / NVIDIA overstated the spec." Not any of those. H200 is faster than H100 when the workload is actually bandwidth-bound.
CORRECT DIAGNOSIS
H200 improves over H100 in HBM bandwidth (4.8 TB/s vs 3.35 TB/s, +43%) but has the same SM compute throughput at FP8/INT4. If your workload is compute-bound, H200 gives zero gain. If your workload has hit a different bottleneck (CPU-side preprocessing, Python overhead, network), H200 can be neutral or even slightly slower due to different CUDA graph capture behavior. Check the actual bottleneck: DCGM DRAM_ACTIVE. On a memory-bound workload it's 85%+ on H100 and should rise to ~95% on H200; throughput gain of 40% is real. If it's 50% on both, the bottleneck is elsewhere (scheduler, Python, or gateway) and H200 can't fix it.
FIX
Before any hardware upgrade, profile the current system and identify the actual bottleneck. For memory-bound workloads at interactive batch (typical LLM decode at batch=1-64), H100 → H200 is a real 30-40% throughput win. For compute-bound workloads (large prefills, high batch, or MoE all-to-all heavy), H100 → H200 is marginal; consider B200 (which doubles compute) or more H100s. Rule: buy GPUs based on measured bottleneck, not on marketing comparisons. And always benchmark your real workload on the new hardware before committing provisioning.
Drill · 03
Autoscaler oscillating; 3× capacity cycled every 4 minutes
SYMPTOM
Pod count thrashing: 40 → 120 → 35 → 110 → 38 → 115 on a 4-minute cycle. GPU utilization looks schizophrenic — spiking to 95%, crashing to 15%, back to 95%. Request latency has bimodal distribution. On-call page fires every time pods drop too low. Cloud cost is 2.3× expected.
WRONG HYPOTHESIS
"Traffic must be spiky" — but upstream traffic histograms are smooth. The oscillation is internal, not external.
CORRECT DIAGNOSIS
Autoscaler is in a positive feedback loop. Likely: scale-up signal is "queue depth > threshold" (fast, firing at 30s window), scale-down signal is "GPU util < threshold" (fast, also 30s window), and scale operations take 60-90s. Cycle: queue grows → scaler adds pods → pods come online → queue drains → GPU util drops → scaler removes pods → queue grows again. The window is too short relative to pod startup time. Check HPA config: if scaleDown.stabilizationWindowSeconds is not set or is <300, this is the cause.
FIX
Three-step fix: (1) raise scaleDown.stabilizationWindowSeconds to 300-600 — the scaler now requires sustained low util before removing pods. (2) Switch scale-UP signal from "queue depth" to "predictive" (e.g., num_requests_waiting + num_requests_running × prefill_time_remaining / SLO) — this reacts before the queue builds. (3) Add hysteresis: different thresholds for scale-up (aggressive) vs scale-down (conservative). Rule of thumb: stabilization window should be at least 3× pod startup time. For LLM pods with warm weights on network storage, that's often 5-10 minutes. Always test the autoscaler under simulated spike-and-recover traffic before going live.
Drill · 04
Capacity plan was for 2× peak; real peak is 1.4× and system still melts
SYMPTOM
Team capacity-planned for Black Friday to absorb 2× normal peak. Real Black Friday traffic was 1.4× (lower than expected). System still degraded — p99 TTFT tripled, 3% error rate, pages firing. Post-mortem asks: "we had headroom, why did it fail?"
WRONG HYPOTHESIS
"Must have been an obscure traffic pattern." Partially true, but usually the answer is more concrete than that.
CORRECT DIAGNOSIS
Capacity planning counted "requests per second" at the baseline prompt length. Real Black Friday traffic had longer prompts (personalized product recommendations built longer RAG contexts), and longer outputs (checkout flows wanted detailed rationales). 1.4× request-rate × 1.3× prompt-tokens × 1.4× output-tokens = 2.55× actual token-load. Capacity headroom of 2× on RPS ≠ capacity headroom on tokens. Check: were prompt-length and output-length budgeted separately from RPS? If only RPS was budgeted, this is the failure mode.
FIX
Capacity-plan in token-seconds, not requests. The unit that matters is prefill_tokens + output_tokens × (prefill_throughput / decode_throughput) — equivalent compute-seconds per request. Forecast this for peak scenarios with realistic distributions of prompt and output length, not just RPS. Also: cap max_tokens at the gateway during peak (reduce output-length variance); pre-warm KV cache for common prompt templates; add an "expensive-request" admission policy that throttles only requests with projected tokens above p95. For next Black Friday, capacity budget is the p99 token-seconds, not the p50 RPS times 2.
💡

The common thread

M8 failures share a structural signature: a single-variable model of a multi-variable system. Cost looks flat when RPS is flat, but α × hit-rate × dtype × prompt-length all drift independently. H200 "speeds up" the GPU, but speedup only applies along one dimension (HBM BW). Autoscaler reacts to one signal in isolation, but its own actions change the signal. Capacity planning uses one unit (RPS) but the real constraint is multi-dimensional (token-seconds). The operator-level discipline is: when the system is behaving unexpectedly, list every variable that could have shifted, rank by likelihood, and check each in order. The one-variable hypothesis is almost never the complete story.

Q1 · Hardware decision
A team is evaluating H200 vs MI300X for serving a 70B MoE model. MI300X offers 36% more HBM (192GB vs 141GB) and 10% higher throughput-per-dollar. Why might H200 still win?
MI300X is slower in FP16.
Software maturity and all-to-all (MoE-critical) performance. NVLink and battle-tested vLLM/EAGLE-2/MoE kernels compensate for the hardware gap; an MI300X port costs 2-5 FTE-quarters and surfaces kernel gaps mid-deployment. The "hardware win" evaporates once you price engineering time.
MI300X has less HBM.
H200 uses less power.
Hardware decisions are 30% hardware, 70% software. The correct way to evaluate is weighted across all six axes (BW, capacity, FP8, interconnect, software, availability), with software maturity almost always above 20% weight.
Q2 · Cost decomposition
A team's LLM cost-per-token went up 35% over a quarter but GPU utilization is unchanged. What changed?
Nothing — costs naturally rise.
Effective throughput per GPU dropped. Most likely causes: speculation acceptance rate fell (draft model stale), or batch size collapsed (scheduler change), or prefix cache hit rate fell. All three change tokens_per_second without changing GPU utilization.
Hardware got more expensive.
Prompts got longer.
Cost-per-token = (GPU-hours / tokens-produced). GPU util tells you the numerator is steady; cost rising means the denominator dropped. The M8 war story #6 was this exact scenario — EAGLE-2 acceptance rate drift caught by finance before engineering.
Q3 · Capacity tiers
A team provisioning capacity for a workload with 3× daily peaks and 8× holiday peaks uses 100% committed-use GPUs. What's the economic problem?
Commit pricing is always more expensive.
Pay for peak capacity 24/7/365 when average is ~35% of peak. Committed-use wins for the always-on baseline portion; reserved + on-demand + spillover handle the variable portion. Mixed tiers give 40-60% blended savings.
Committed capacity can't handle spikes.
You can't get committed pricing for GPUs.
The 40/30/20/10 split (committed/reserved/on-demand/spillover) roughly matches the shape of most SaaS demand curves and leaves the hardware discount surface of committed contracts mostly utilized.
Q4 · War story pattern
What do the six war stories in this module have in common?
They all involve network failures.
The failure mode was never the thing being optimized — it was in the signal, the transport, the distribution, or the staleness of some component adjacent to the optimization. This is why you instrument not just the optimization but its own health metric (hit rate, acceptance rate, transfer latency, etc.).
They all happened on Black Friday.
They all involved quantization.
The unifying rule: "optimize the steady state, instrument the failure modes." Every production-grade optimization ships with an alert on its own health, not just the aggregate system's health.
Q5 · Composition order
Why does the order "prune → sparsify → quantize" produce better results than "quantize → sparsify → prune"?
Quantization is always the last step in ML pipelines.
Later steps compound error from earlier steps. Quantization error is smaller when applied to an already-sparse model because the surviving weights have more headroom in the representable range. The rule: apply the technique with the largest error budget last.
Sparsification doesn't work on quantized weights.
The order doesn't matter.
This is war story #4 in M8. Applying 2:4 sparsity to already-INT4-quantized weights concentrates quantization error on the surviving weights. Prune first in FP16, apply 2:4 mask, short recovery fine-tune, then quantize.
Q6 · Dominant wins
Across the full cost ladder (v1 $940 → v8 $17), which single module produced the biggest single cost drop for Nexus?
M2 (quantization) at −61%.
M6 (compression: MoE + distillation + 2:4) at −68%, though M2 and M5 are close behind. The top three wins (quantization, batching+spec, compression) account for ~85% of total cost reduction.
M3 (attention engineering).
M7 (production system).
The rule for prioritization: hit quantization, batching+speculation, and compression first. Attention engineering enables long context and new architectures but contributes less in isolation; production system engineering matters for uptime, not unit cost.
Q7 · Pre-warm rationale
Why does pre-warming capacity 6 hours before a known traffic spike cost ~0.3% of annual spend?
GPU rentals are always expensive.
6 hours × ~50% of peak capacity / (8,760 hours in year × blended capacity) ≈ 0.3%. The cost is small because pre-warm is a narrow time window against the whole year; the benefit is avoiding a 4–9 minute cold-start window where autoscaling can't catch up to a spike.
Pre-warming uses spot instances.
Peak hour pricing is lower.
The economic insight: a planned event costs a tiny predictable premium if you pre-provision; costs a large unpredictable reputation hit if you don't. The expected value calculation always favors pre-warming for known events.
Q8 · Roofline in cost terms
For a decode-heavy workload on H100, doubling HBM bandwidth (via H200) affects cost-per-token primarily by:
Cutting GPU prices.
Doubling decode throughput per GPU (since decode is memory-bandwidth-bound). Throughput doubling roughly halves GPU-hours per million tokens, which roughly halves cost-per-token — minus the price premium for H200 over H100.
Enabling larger batch sizes.
Reducing network traffic.
The roofline directly predicts this: decode-bound cost scales with memory bandwidth. Every hardware evaluation should start with "which side of the roofline is my workload on, and which axis of the new hardware helps me?"
Q9 · Three-tier logging
A team's LLM logs include every prompt and completion in clear text. A tenant invokes their GDPR right-to-deletion. Why is this nearly impossible to honor?
Logs can't be edited.
Full-content logs fan out across backup archives, analytics warehouses, cold storage, and derivative datasets. Without per-tenant keying and retention tiers built from day one, deletion becomes a multi-team archaeology project. The three-tier policy (metadata/hashes/full-with-retention) is not optional; it is retrofit at ~10× the cost of doing it right initially.
GDPR doesn't apply to LLMs.
Logs can't contain PII.
Tiered logging is a day-one architectural decision. The instinct to "log everything while you're getting started" is a GDPR violation factory. Build the pipeline once, correctly.
Q10 · The course, compressed
If you had to give a junior engineer one sentence that summarizes LLM inference optimization, which captures the course best?
"Use the biggest GPU you can afford."
"Always quantize to FP8."
"Every technique attacks one specific term in the cost-per-token equation; stacking them multiplicatively in the right order with the right instrumentation produces 50×+ improvements that feel impossible from outside and obvious from inside."
"vLLM solves everything."
The sentence captures the three ideas that pattern all of M1–M8: (1) optimizations are multiplicative, not additive; (2) ordering matters; (3) instrumentation is what makes the gain durable. Every module in this course was a variation on one of these three themes.
