The Framework

Every Evaluation Answers 4 Questions

Before any metric, any benchmark, any tool — ask these four. If you cannot answer them, you don't have an evaluation. You have noise.

01

What are we measuring?

Quality? Cost? Latency? Safety? Each lives in a different dimension. Pick one at a time. "How good is our RAG?" is too vague — break it into faithfulness, relevance, completeness.

02

Compared to what?

A score in isolation is meaningless. 0.74 — good or bad? You need a baseline (previous model, naive RAG, human), a reference (gold answers), or a relative ranking. No baseline, no signal.

03

On which data?

The eval set IS the metric. 20 cherry-picked examples lie. You need a stratified set covering easy / medium / hard, edge cases, adversarial inputs, and real production traffic samples.

04

What decision does it drive?

Ship/don't ship? A/B test winner? Block CI? Alert on regression? If a metric drives no decision, delete it. Every metric is a decision rule — or it's dashboard candy.

⚡ The trap: Most teams skip straight to "what metric should I use?" That's question 1. They forget 2, 3, 4 — and end up with a number that doesn't move when the system improves, or moves when nothing changes. Always answer all four before writing code.
The Three Families

Three Ways To Grade an Answer

Every LLM metric falls into one of three families. Knowing which family you're in tells you what you need — and what you don't.

FAMILY 01

Reference-Based

You have a gold answer. Compare the model's output to it.

• BLEU, ROUGE
• METEOR, BERTScore
• Exact match
Best for
Translation, summarization, QA with known answers
FAMILY 02

Reference-Free

No gold answer needed. Judge the output on intrinsic qualities.

• Faithfulness, relevance
• Coherence, completeness
• G-Eval, LLM-as-judge
Best for
Open-ended generation, RAG answers, creative tasks
FAMILY 03

Behavioral

Does the system do the right thing? Measure outcomes, not text.

• Task success rate
• Refusal correctness
• Tool-call accuracy
Best for
Agents, tool use, classification, safety

Which Family Do You Pick?

Decision Map
Situation You Have Use
Summarization vs. human-written summaries Gold summaries Reference-based (ROUGE / BERTScore)
Open-ended Q&A from your docs Source documents only Reference-free (faithfulness, relevance)
RAG hallucination check Context + answer Reference-free (NLI, FactScore)
Agent completes a checkout Pass/fail outcome Behavioral (success rate)
Tool-use correctness Expected tool call Behavioral (tool-call match)
Translation benchmark Reference translations Reference-based (BLEU)
Family 01

The Reference-Based Classics

Four metrics. Two are 20 years old. Two are modern. Each measures "similarity to gold" differently. Click each to explore.

Worked Example — Compute ROUGE-1 By Hand

REFERENCE (GOLD)
"The cat sat on the mat."
tokens: {the, cat, sat, on, mat}
CANDIDATE (MODEL)
"The cat sat on a mat."
tokens: {the, cat, sat, on, a, mat}
# ROUGE-1 = unigram overlap. Two flavors: precision & recall.
overlap = {"the", "cat", "sat", "on", "mat"}   # 5 shared tokens

precision = len(overlap) / len(candidate)   # 5 / 6 = 0.833
recall    = len(overlap) / len(reference)   # 5 / 5 = 1.000
f1        = 2 * p * r / (p + r)               # 0.909

# Notice: ROUGE rewards lexical overlap. "a" vs "the" is fine.
# Try "The feline rested on the rug" — ROUGE-1 = 0.4. BERTScore = ~0.92.
⚡ The hidden weakness: ROUGE/BLEU love copy-paste and hate paraphrase. For modern LLMs that paraphrase fluently, lexical-overlap metrics underestimate quality. That's why BERTScore and reference-free methods replaced them as defaults.
Family 02

The Four Reference-Free Dimensions

For RAG, you usually don't have a gold answer — but you have a question and retrieved context. These four dimensions are how you grade an answer anyway.

Faithfulness

Does every claim in the answer follow from the retrieved context? If yes → grounded. If no → hallucination.

aka groundedness

Relevance

Does the answer actually address the question? An answer can be true and grounded — but still off-topic.

aka answer relevance

Coherence

Does the answer flow logically? No contradictions, no jumps, no chunks bolted together awkwardly.

aka fluency

Completeness

Does the answer cover all parts of a multi-part question? Or did it answer half and stop?

aka coverage

All Four In One Example

QUESTION
"What's our refund policy and how do I file one?"
RETRIEVED CONTEXT
"Refunds are available within 30 days of purchase. To request one, email support@co.com with your order ID."
CANDIDATE ANSWER
"Refunds are available within 30 days. Email support@co.com with your order ID."
1.00
Faithfulness
Every claim is in the context
1.00
Relevance
Directly answers what was asked
0.95
Coherence
Two clean sentences, flows well
1.00
Completeness
Covers both "policy" and "how"
📍 You'll see these everywhere: Faithfulness + Relevance are the heart of RAGAS (Module 6.1). Coherence + Completeness show up in G-Eval and most LLM-as-judge rubrics. Lock these four names in — every RAG eval framework just renames them.
Going Deeper

Fact-Checking at Scale

Three techniques that let you grade thousands of answers automatically — the engines behind modern RAG evaluation.

TECHNIQUE 01

NLI

Natural Language Inference

Given a premise (context) and a hypothesis (claim from the answer), an NLI model outputs one of three labels: entailment, neutral, or contradiction.

ENTAILMENT
supports claim ✓
NEUTRAL
unrelated ?
CONTRADICTION
refutes claim ✗

Run a small NLI model (DeBERTa-v3, ~400M params) over every (context, claim) pair. Any claim that isn't entailment is a hallucination flag. Fast, cheap, deterministic.

TECHNIQUE 02

FactScore

Atomic Fact Verification

A single answer often contains many claims. FactScore breaks the answer into atomic facts, then verifies each one independently against the source. 

# Answer: "Marie Curie was a Polish physicist who won 2 Nobel Prizes."
atoms = [
    "Marie Curie was Polish.",           # ✓ verified
    "Marie Curie was a physicist.",      # ✓ verified
    "Marie Curie won 2 Nobel Prizes.",   # ✓ verified
]
FactScore = verified(atoms) / len(atoms)   # 3/3 = 1.00

Catches the subtle case where an answer is mostly right but slips one wrong claim in. ROUGE/BERTScore would call it "close enough" — FactScore exposes the lie.

TECHNIQUE 03

G-Eval

LLM-as-Judge with CoT

Give the judge LLM a rubric, ask it to reason through chain-of-thought, then emit a score 1–5. The CoT step boosts correlation with human judgment dramatically vs. naive "rate 1-5" prompts. 

prompt = """You are evaluating answer faithfulness.

Rubric (1-5):
  1 = mostly hallucinated
  3 = some unsupported claims
  5 = every claim grounded in context

Steps:
  1. List the claims in the answer.
  2. For each, mark supported / unsupported.
  3. Output final score.

Context: {ctx}
Answer: {ans}"""

Powerful and flexible — works for any dimension you can describe in a rubric. The catch: judge bias, cost, and non-determinism. Module 6.1 covers all the mitigations.

🎯 The progression: NLI is cheap and deterministic but coarse. FactScore is more granular but needs claim extraction. G-Eval is the most flexible but slowest and most expensive. Real RAG eval stacks use all three at different stages — fast NLI for CI, FactScore for offline analysis, G-Eval for the deepest dimensions.
The Ground You Stand On

Building a Golden Test Set

Every metric in this module is meaningless without a labeled set to compute it against. A 50-question golden set is the single highest-leverage artifact in a RAG project — and most teams skip it.

What a single golden record looks like 

{
  "id": "q-0042",
  "question": "What's the refund window for travel purchases?",
  "expected_answer": "30 days from purchase date.",
  "gold_chunks": ["doc_213#para3", "doc_213#para4"],
  "category": "policy_lookup",
  "difficulty": "easy",
  "answerable": true,
  "added_at": "2026-02-14",
  "added_by": "support-lead@..."
}

Five fields are non-negotiable: question, expected answer, gold chunks (for retrieval metrics), answerable (so refusal works), category (for slicing).

The right mix — get the distribution right

CategoryShareExample
Lookup (single doc)~40%"refund window?"
Multi-hop (2+ docs)~20%"how does X interact with Y?"
Aggregation / counting~10%"how many regions ship?"
Comparative~10%"X vs Y plan differences?"
Refusal / unanswerable~10%"share customer SSNs"
Edge cases / known bugs~10%regression triggers
STEP 01 · SOURCE QUERIES FROM REAL TRAFFIC

Pull anonymized logs of actual user questions — never invent the queries yourself. Sample by category, deduplicate near-duplicates with embeddings, and keep query phrasing intact (typos, lowercase, fragments included).

STEP 02 · DUAL LABELERS + ADJUDICATION

Have two domain experts independently produce the expected answer and gold chunks. Measure inter-annotator agreement (Cohen's κ). For disagreements, run a third adjudicator — and add the disagreement itself to the test set as "ambiguous" examples.

STEP 03 · SEMI-AUTOMATE WITH AN LLM, REVIEW BY HUMAN

Use an LLM to generate question candidates from each gold chunk (cheap, fast), then have humans accept / edit / reject. RAGAS's testset module is built around this — generate 200 candidates, keep the 50 best.

STEP 04 · VERSION IT LIKE CODE

Check the golden set into git (or DVC if too large). Every change is a PR with rationale. A failing PR run on the golden set blocks merge — same gate as unit tests. Tag releases (golden-v3.1) so you can re-run yesterday's eval on yesterday's data.

STEP 05 · GROW IT WITH FAILURES

Every customer-reported bug becomes a new test row. Every "weird" production query becomes a new row. Within 6 months the set will outgrow your initial seed by 3-5×.

⚡ The 50-question minimum: below ~50 examples, single-row noise dominates and you can't tell a 2-point improvement from random variance. Above ~500 you've over-invested for early-stage. Start at 50, double when you ship to production, double again at scale.
The Honest Hard Problem

The Oracle Problem in RAG Eval

Classical NLP eval assumes a single "ground truth" answer. RAG breaks that assumption — and pretending it doesn't is the cause of most "our eval doesn't predict prod" failures.

What "ground truth" actually means here

In sentiment analysis there's one label. In NER there's one span set. In RAG, "the correct answer" depends on:

  • When you ask. Policies change. Today's correct answer is tomorrow's wrong one.
  • Who's asking. Same question, different tenant, different correct answer.
  • What "answer" means. A 1-sentence summary, a 3-paragraph explanation, and a step-by-step walk-through can all be "correct."
  • Which chunks count as supporting. If 3 chunks all say the same thing, which is the "gold" one?

Practical mitigations

  • Multi-reference answers: store 2–5 acceptable phrasings per question. Score against the best match (use BLEU/ROUGE max-over-references).
  • Reference-free metrics: faithfulness and answer relevance (Module 6.1) measure the answer against the retrieved context, no gold answer required.
  • Date-stamp the test set: every record knows when it was written. Re-validate quarterly — drop rows whose source doc has changed.
  • Tenant-scoped sets: for multi-tenant SaaS, never share golden sets across tenants — same question can have different answers.

The trap to avoid

Building a "snapshot" golden set, then optimizing models against it for 6 months while production data drifts away from it. Numbers go up; users get more wrong answers.

The discipline that works

Treat your golden set as living code. Quarterly audit: do the questions still reflect real traffic? Do the answers still hold? Has any source doc changed? Re-label.

The unlock

Reference-free + reference-based metrics in parallel. Reference-free flags new failure modes; reference-based catches regressions. Each catches what the other misses.

⚡ Honest framing for stakeholders: "We don't have a single 'correct' answer. We have a measurable set of required properties the answer must satisfy — supported by retrieved evidence, addresses the question, and prefers the cited source over priors." That framing keeps the conversation grounded in what's actually measurable.
Production Reality

Query Distribution Shift — When Your Eval Stops Predicting Prod

A golden set is a sample. Production is the population. When the population drifts away from the sample, your eval still scores 0.9 while users see 0.6. Naming the phenomenon is the first defense.

Symptoms in the wild

"Eval looks great — users complain"

  • ✦ Faithfulness on golden set: 0.91 · weekly CSAT score: dropping for 3 sprints
  • ✦ A new product launch quietly added 1000 new entities; golden set has zero coverage
  • ✦ Holiday season changes the shape of questions (returns spike, gift inquiries appear)
  • ✦ A competitor's pricing change reshapes "vs X" comparison queries
What's actually shifting

Three flavors of drift

  • Topic drift: new categories of questions emerge (new product, new policy).
  • Phrasing drift: same topic, different surface form ("refund" → "money back" → "give me my $$").
  • Difficulty drift: users learn what your system can do, push harder, ask multi-hop / edge-case questions.

How to detect drift before users do

SIGNAL 01 · EMBEDDING DRIFT

Embed every production query. Once a day, compute the mean embedding of (last 7d) vs (golden set). Cosine drift > 0.05 → topic shift; investigate.

SIGNAL 02 · COVERAGE

For every production query, find its nearest neighbor in the golden set. % with neighbor cosine < 0.7 = uncovered traffic. Plot weekly.

SIGNAL 03 · RETRIEVAL CONFIDENCE

Track distribution of top-1 retrieval scores. A widening left tail = more "no good match" queries → new topics need new docs and new tests.

SIGNAL 04 · USER FEEDBACK SLOPE

Thumbs-down rate is noisy at the query level but stable in 7-day aggregate. A persistent upward slope despite stable eval scores → distribution shift.

⚡ The closed loop: production queries → coverage analysis → uncovered queries get human labels → new golden-set rows → re-run eval → re-train / re-prompt. Module 6.2's "living eval-set loop" is this exact pipeline, productionized.
