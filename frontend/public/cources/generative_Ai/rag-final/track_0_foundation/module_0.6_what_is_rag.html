The Pain
02 · Problem
Why Raw LLMs Catastrophically Fail

Every production AI engineer learns this the hard way. Below is what actually happens when you ship a raw LLM to real users — and the three architectural reasons it breaks.

Without RAG
Raw GPT-4o response · no retrieval
What's our company's refund policy for SaaS customers in the EU?
Based on standard SaaS practices, EU customers are typically entitled to a 14-day cooling-off period under EU consumer law, and refunds are usually processed within 5-7 business days. Most SaaS companies offer pro-rated refunds for annual subscriptions. I recommend checking your specific terms of service for details.
What just happened: The model invented a confident answer from training data averages. Your company doesn't offer pro-rated annual refunds. The 14-day clause doesn't apply to your B2B contracts. A customer service rep relying on this gets your company sued.
With RAG
Same model · retrieves from your docs first
What's our company's refund policy for SaaS customers in the EU?
Per the EU Service Agreement (v3.2)[1], EU SaaS customers may cancel within 30 days of initial purchase for a full refund. Annual subscriptions are non-refundable after day 30[2]; monthly subscriptions can be canceled anytime but no pro-rating applies. Refunds are issued to the original payment method within 10 business days.[1]
[1] eu_terms_v3.2.pdf, §4.1 — Refund Rights
[2] billing_policy_2026.pdf, §2.3 — Annual Plans
What just happened: Same LLM, but RAG retrieved your actual policy documents, fed them as evidence, and the model answered from facts — with citations a human can verify.

The Three Fatal Flaws of Raw LLMs

01

Frozen Knowledge

LLMs are snapshots in time. GPT-4o knows the world as of Oct 2023. Claude Opus 4.5 cuts off at Feb 2025. They can never know yesterday's earnings report, last week's product launch, or your CEO's latest memo.

Real impact: A customer support bot trained on docs from 18 months ago will confidently answer with prices that have since increased — guaranteeing every conversation ends in customer rage.
02

Hallucinations

When the model doesn't know, it doesn't say "I don't know." It interpolates plausible-sounding answers from patterns in its training distribution. The lie is delivered with the same confidence as the truth.

Real impact: In 2023, a NY lawyer used ChatGPT to draft a legal brief. It cited six cases — all fabricated. He was fined $5,000 by the judge. RAG, with mandatory citations, would have prevented this.
03

No Private Data

The LLM was trained on the public internet. Your wikis, codebase, customer tickets, contracts, medical records — none of it exists in the model's brain. Fine-tuning to inject knowledge is slow, expensive, and degrades general capability.

Real impact: A bank that wants to answer questions about its loan products cannot just ask GPT — the model has zero knowledge of their offerings. RAG lets them index 10,000 product docs in an afternoon.
The Mental Model
03 · Anatomy
Closed-Book vs Open-Book Cognition

The single most important frame in modern AI engineering. Once you see it this way, every RAG decision in the next 37 modules becomes intuitive.

Closed-Book

PARAMETRIC MEMORY ONLY

Picture a brilliant student locked in a room with no books, no internet, no notes. You ask a question — they answer entirely from what they remember from training. This is the raw LLM.

Fast — one forward pass, no I/O
Stuck in the past — knowledge is frozen at training cutoff
Confabulates when uncertain — fluency hides ignorance
No sources — cannot tell you where an answer came from
No private knowledge — only what was in the training corpus
→ When to use: creative writing, brainstorming, summarization of pasted text, code generation from general knowledge.

Open-Book (RAG)

RETRIEVAL + GENERATION

Same brilliant student — but now they have access to a giant searchable library. Before answering, they look up relevant pages, then synthesize an answer grounded in what they found. This is RAG.

Fresh knowledge — re-index whenever data changes
Citable — every claim traceable to a source document
Refuses gracefully when no evidence is found
Works on private data — index your own knowledge base
Slower & costlier — extra retrieval step, more tokens to process
→ When to use: Q&A over private/fresh data, customer support, enterprise search, legal/medical assistants, anything requiring citations.

Two Kinds of Memory in a RAG System

Understanding this is half the battle. A RAG system uses both — and knowing which is which tells you how to debug failures.

MEMORY TYPE A

Parametric (in the model's weights)

  • ▸ General language, grammar, world knowledge
  • ▸ Reasoning patterns, common-sense logic
  • ▸ How to structure an email, write code, etc.
  • ▸ Fixed at training time, costly to update
MEMORY TYPE B

Non-Parametric (in the vector DB)

  • ▸ Specific facts, documents, policies, products
  • ▸ Customer data, codebase, support history
  • ▸ Anything that changes daily/hourly
  • ▸ Updated by re-indexing — fast and cheap
🧠 Debugging insight: When a RAG system gives a wrong answer, ask: was it the retrieval that failed (wrong docs found) or the generation (right docs but bad synthesis)? Knowing which memory broke tells you whether to fix your chunking/embeddings (Tracks 2–3) or your prompt template (Track 4).
The Architecture
04 · Pipeline
The 7 Stages of Every Production RAG

Every system you'll study or build — Perplexity, Notion Q&A, GitHub Copilot Chat, Glean, Harvey — follows this same pipeline. Two phases, seven stages. Click any stage to deep-dive.

◀ INDEXING PHASE — done offline, once per data refresh ▶
◀ QUERY PHASE — done live, on every user request ▶

Follow One Question Through the Whole Pipeline

Concrete example: a support engineer asks "How do I rotate API keys without downtime?" Watch how the question is transformed at each stage.

① INGEST + CHUNK

Offline. Your engineering wiki has a 4,000-word doc titled "Production Key Rotation Runbook". We split it into 14 chunks of ~300 tokens each, preserving section headers as metadata.

② EMBED + INDEX

Offline. Each chunk → 1536-dim vector via text-embedding-3-small. Vectors stored in Qdrant with payload {title, section, url}. Indexed once, queried millions of times.

③ RETRIEVE + RERANK

Live. The question is embedded; Qdrant returns top-20 candidate chunks in ~8ms. A Cohere reranker scores them for true relevance → keeps top 5.

④ GENERATE

Live. Top 5 chunks stuffed into a system prompt with strict citation rules. GPT-4o produces a 4-step answer with [1][2] citations linking back to the runbook sections.

⏱ Latency budget: Stages ①② happen offline (zero user-facing latency). Stages ③④ together must run in under 2 seconds for a snappy chat UX — which is why we'll obsess over caching, async, and reranker speed in Track 7.
The Story
05 · Evolution
How RAG Was Born

A surprisingly short history. Knowing it helps you predict where the field is going.

2017

Attention Is All You Need

Google introduces the Transformer architecture. The era of large language models begins. No retrieval — just bigger and bigger frozen models.

2020 · APRIL

REALM (Google)

First serious attempt to combine a frozen LM with a learned retriever. Established that retrieval improves factual accuracy.

2020 · MAY

The RAG Paper (Meta AI)

Lewis et al. publish "Retrieval-Augmented Generation for Knowledge-Intensive NLP Tasks." The acronym RAG enters the vocabulary. The core architecture is exactly what we still use in 2026.

2022 · DECEMBER

ChatGPT goes viral

Suddenly the whole world wants LLMs to answer questions about their data. RAG becomes the obvious solution. Pinecone, Weaviate, Qdrant explode in usage.

2023 — 2024

The RAG Goldrush

LangChain, LlamaIndex emerge. Every enterprise builds a RAG. Most are bad. The community learns that chunking, retrieval quality, and evaluation matter more than the LLM choice.

2024 — 2025

Advanced RAG

Hybrid search, rerankers, HyDE, query rewriting, Self-RAG, CRAG. The field professionalizes. Naive RAG becomes a punchline; production RAG becomes a discipline.

2026 · NOW

RAG meets Agents

RAG becomes a tool used by agents that decide when to retrieve, from which source, and how to verify. The boundary blurs between RAG, agents, and MCP — exactly what your bundle is built around.

The Choice
06 · Tradeoffs
RAG vs Long Context vs Fine-Tuning

In 2026, GPT-4.1 has a 1M token context window. Claude Opus 4.5 has 500K. Gemini 2 has 2M. People ask: "If I can paste my whole corpus into the prompt, why do I need RAG?" It's the right question. Here's the engineering answer.

RAG

Retrieval-Augmented Generation
FRESHNESS
COST EFFICIENCY
CITATION SUPPORT
SCALES TO BIG CORPORA
SETUP COMPLEXITY
USE WHEN:
• Large/growing corpus (1k+ docs)
• Knowledge changes frequently
• Citations required
• Cost matters at scale

Stuff it all in the prompt
FRESHNESS
COST EFFICIENCY
CITATION SUPPORT
SCALES TO BIG CORPORA
SETUP COMPLEXITY
USE WHEN:
• Small corpus (<200K tokens)
• One-off analysis tasks
• Prototyping / experimentation
• Latency matters less than dev speed

Fine-Tuning

Bake it into the model
FRESHNESS
COST EFFICIENCY
CITATION SUPPORT
SCALES TO BIG CORPORA
SETUP COMPLEXITY
USE WHEN:
• Teaching a style/format/skill, not facts
• Domain language is alien to base model
• Need ultra-low inference latency
• Knowledge is stable for months+

The 30-Second Decision Tree

Q1 · Does your knowledge change more than once per month?
YES → RAG or Long Context. Never fine-tune live data.
NO → Continue to Q2.
Q2 · Is your corpus larger than ~200K tokens? (≈ 500 pages)
YES → RAG. Long-context is too expensive per query.
NO → Continue to Q3.
Q3 · Do you need citations? (legal/medical/financial use case)
YES → RAG. Always. Citations require retrieval traceability.
NO → Continue to Q4.
Q4 · Are you teaching format/style/behavior, not facts?
YES → Fine-tuning. RAG can't reliably change how a model writes.
NO → Use Long Context for prototypes, RAG when you ship.
💡 The 2026 reality: Most production systems combine all three. A customer support bot might be fine-tuned for tone (style), use RAG for product facts (freshness), and fall back to long-context for rare deep-dive questions (flexibility). The art is choosing the right tool per task — which is the whole point of this course.

The First Build
07 · Naive
Naive RAG in 30 Lines

The simplest possible RAG. You'll build this in Mini-Capstone 0, then spend the next 37 modules improving every single line of it. Every production system started here. 

# naive_rag.py — the seed of every production RAG
from dotenv import load_dotenv
from openai import OpenAI
from qdrant_client import QdrantClient
from qdrant_client.models import Distance, VectorParams, PointStruct

load_dotenv()
client = OpenAI()
qdrant = QdrantClient("localhost", port=6333)

def embed(text):
    return client.embeddings.create(
        model="text-embedding-3-small", input=text
    ).data[0].embedding

def index(docs: list[str]):
    qdrant.recreate_collection("docs",
        vectors_config=VectorParams(size=1536, distance=Distance.COSINE))
    qdrant.upsert("docs", [
        PointStruct(id=i, vector=embed(d), payload={"text": d})
        for i, d in enumerate(docs)
    ])

def retrieve(question, k=5):
    hits = qdrant.search("docs", embed(question), limit=k)
    return [h.payload["text"] for h in hits]

def ask(question):
    context = "\n\n".join(retrieve(question))
    prompt = f"""Answer using ONLY this context.
If the context doesn't contain the answer, say "I don't know."

Context:
{context}

Question: {question}"""
    return client.chat.completions.create(
        model="gpt-4o-mini",
        messages=[{"role": "user", "content": prompt}]
    ).choices[0].message.content

# ── usage ───────────────────────────────────────
index([
    "EU customers may cancel within 30 days for a full refund.",
    "Annual subscriptions are non-refundable after day 30.",
    "All refunds issued within 10 business days.",
])
print(ask("What is the EU refund policy?"))
LINE 14 · EMBED

One OpenAI call → text becomes a 1536-dim vector. We'll explore alternative embedding models in Track 1.

LINES 18-23 · INDEX

Store all vectors + their original text in Qdrant. Used once per data refresh. In Track 2 we'll learn proper chunking strategies.

LINES 25-27 · RETRIEVE

Pure dense cosine search. Track 3 will fix this — hybrid BM25 + dense + reranking beats this by 30-50% relevance.

LINES 29-39 · GENERATE

A bare-bones grounded prompt. Track 4 teaches citation injection, refusal patterns, and lost-in-the-middle mitigation.

WHAT'S MISSING?
  • ✗ No chunking — full docs treated as units
  • ✗ No hybrid search — semantic only
  • ✗ No reranking — top-k is final
  • ✗ No citations — answers can't be verified
  • ✗ No evaluation — quality is invisible
  • ✗ No caching — every query costs money
