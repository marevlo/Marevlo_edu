› Why the categorization matters

Not every checklist item carries equal weight. The critical items are the ten that, missing, will almost certainly cause an outage or security incident in the first weeks of production: sticky sessions, proxy buffering off, structured logging, liveness/readiness probes, OAuth, secret rotation, deprecation policy, on-call rotation, rollback plan, and the canary deploy. Ship without any one of them and you're betting you'll be lucky. Ship with all ten and you've cleared the bar that distinguishes a hobby deploy from a production one.

"We'll add it later" is an item you didn't tick

The honest discipline: the items you defer are the items that bite you. Every box you don't check should have a written-down reason and a date attached. If "we'll add OTel later" appears in three retrospectives, it should have been on the launch list. Use this checklist as your contract with the future, not a wish list.

› The Little's Law check

The math behind the calculator is just Little's Law: concurrent in-flight calls = arrival rate × average duration. At 50 RPS with 800ms avg duration, you have ~40 concurrent calls flowing through the cluster at any moment. Divide by your concurrency budget per replica to get your replica count, then add headroom for spikes and the N-minus-1 failure case. Everything else is engineering judgement around that equation.

Always plan for N-1

The right replica count isn't "how many do we need at peak" — it's "how many do we need at peak when one of them is being restarted for a deploy or has died". A three-replica deploy that's exactly at capacity becomes a two-replica deploy at 150% load the moment you push a new image. N-1 capacity is the right floor, and your rolling-update strategy should respect it.

› The five-minute rule

Every runbook entry should answer the question "what should the on-call engineer do in the first five minutes?" If your runbook starts with "investigate the dashboards and figure out what's wrong", you don't have a runbook — you have a wish. The good runbooks are scripts: open this dashboard, look at this metric, if X then run command Y, if Y fails then page Z. The investigation comes later, after mitigation, with a clearer head.

Runbooks rot — review them quarterly

A runbook written six months ago references a metric that's been renamed, an alert that's been retired, and a Slack channel that's been archived. Set a quarterly calendar reminder for every on-call rotation to walk through the top five most-fired alerts and verify their runbooks still work. The investment is small; the alternative is the on-call engineer at 3am hunting for a metric that no longer exists.

› The blameless postmortem template

Every SEV-1 and SEV-2 incident gets a written postmortem within 48 hours of resolution. The template is short on purpose — the goal is shared learning, not a literary exercise: 

# Incident YYYY-MM-DD: short title

Severity: SEV-1
Duration: 14:32 – 15:18 UTC (46 min)
Customer impact: ~3,400 users saw degraded latency or 5xx errors
Engineers paged: @alice, @bob
Trigger: deploy of v1.4.3 at 14:28

## Timeline (UTC, one line per event)
- 14:28  deploy of v1.4.3 to prod
- 14:32  pager fires: P95 latency > SLO for 5m
- 14:35  on-call confirms via dashboard, declares SEV-1
- 14:38  rollback to v1.4.2 initiated
- 14:46  rollback complete; metrics recovering
- 15:18  full recovery, alert clears

## What went well
- Multi-window burn rate alert fired correctly
- Rollback runbook worked first try

## What went wrong
- Canary deploy stage was skipped (rushed for deadline)
- The DB query regression wasn't caught by load tests because
  test data didn't include the cardinality that hits prod

## Action items (with owner and deadline)
- [@alice, 2026-06-15] make canary stage non-skippable in CI
- [@bob, 2026-06-08] load-test corpus updated to mirror prod cardinality
- [@team, 2026-06-30] runbook drill on quarterly rotation
No names in the "what went wrong" section

The non-negotiable principle of postmortems is blameless. Systems failed, not people. "Deploy was rushed for a deadline" is a finding; "Alice rushed the deploy" is a way to ensure your team stops being honest in postmortems. The action items can name owners; the diagnosis cannot name individuals. This is the discipline that lets your incident reviews actually improve the system instead of becoming performance reviews.

05  Decommissioning

Shutting a server down without leaving anyone in the dark.

Every server eventually retires. The discipline of a clean shutdown is the same as the discipline of a clean launch, in reverse. Press play to watch a graceful decommission: announce → drain → freeze → archive → remove. Each stage takes weeks in production — not because the technical work is slow, but because the dependents need real time to migrate.

A server retiring should follow the same discipline as a feature deprecating (Module 5.3): each stage long enough that every dependent has time to migrate. The mechanics are mostly the inverse of the launch checklist.

› What stays around after removal

Removed doesn't mean erased. Three artifacts outlive the server itself: the audit logs (for the retention window your compliance program requires — typically 1-7 years), the registry entry (marked archived so search results show the migration link), and the postmortem entries naming any incidents this server caused. A future engineer should be able to reconstruct, from those three, what the server did, when it ran, and why it stopped. That's the historical record you owe the future. 

# Registry update — mark server as archived
$ mcp registry update com.example.search \
    --status archived \
    --sunset-date 2026-08-28 \
    --replacement com.example.search-v2 \
    --migration-docs https://example.com/migrate/search

# Audit log retention — verify before tearing down storage
$ aws s3 ls s3://audit-logs/mcp/com.example.search/ --recursive --summarize
   Total Size: 412 GB
   Total Objects: 8,419
   Retention until: 2031-08-28  # 5y from sunset

# Final shutdown — only after sunset window expires
$ kubectl delete namespace mcp-search
$ aws route53 change-resource-record-sets ...  # remove DNS
$ aws iam delete-role mcp-search-runtime         # clean up perms
The retro you owe at the very end

Final action of any decommission: a written retro. What did this server do? What did it cost? What did we learn? What would we build differently next time? Two pages, maybe three. Filed alongside the postmortems. The next time someone builds a similar service, this is the document they'll wish they had — make sure you write it.

Five checks before the Mini-Capstone.
Q1.You're auditing a new MCP server for launch. Sticky sessions are configured, OAuth is wired, observability is set up — but the team hasn't built a rollback plan. What's your call?
Right. Critical items aren't a wishlist; they're the things you can't ship without. The whole point of a categorized checklist is to make this judgment unambiguous: critical missing → no launch.
Q2.Your traffic is 80 RPS with 600ms average tool latency. Your concurrency budget per replica is 100. Ignoring spikes, what's the minimum honest replica count?
Yes. Little's Law gives the demand (48 concurrent). One replica fits the steady-state, but the rolling-deploy and replica-loss cases push you to N-1 = 2 surviving capacity, so N = 2 is the floor. Add 50% headroom for spikes and you land at N = 3 as the responsible answer.
Q3.The on-call engineer is paged at 3am: "P95 latency over SLO". The first action they should take is:
Correct. The five-minute rule: runbooks are scripts, not advisory. At 3am a tired engineer should not be improvising — they should be running the steps the team wrote down while well-rested. Diagnosis comes after mitigation, with a calmer head and a cup of coffee.
Q4.Your postmortem draft reads: "Alice rushed the deploy because the team had pressure from product." How should the final version handle this?
Exactly. Blameless doesn't mean responsibility-less — owners are still named on action items. It means the diagnosis describes systems, not people. This is how you get a culture where engineers report incidents honestly instead of hiding them. The cultural cost of "Alice rushed it" is enormous and pays no dividends.
Q5.You're decommissioning com.example.search after 2 years of production. After the final shutdown command, what should persist?
Right. Removed doesn't mean erased. The historical record (audit logs for compliance, registry archive for users still looking, postmortems for institutional memory) lives on. This is what you owe the future when you turn a system off.
