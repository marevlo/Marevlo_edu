GPT-4 &
the scaling
frontiers.

GPT-4 (OpenAI, March 2023) is a frontier model — its architecture is undisclosed, its training cost is estimated at $100M+, and its capabilities exceed GPT-3.5 on virtually every benchmark. What we can study: the scaling principles that produced it, the likely architectural choices (MoE, multimodal), and the emerging paradigm of inference-time compute scaling that may matter more than parameter count going forward. This module covers what we know, what we can infer, and what it means for CineMatch's model strategy.

What we know about GPT-4.

OpenAI's technical report for GPT-4 deliberately omits architecture details. What has been confirmed or reliably reported:

Why MoE makes sense at this scale

A dense 1.8T parameter model would be impractical — inference would require loading 1.8T × 2 bytes = 3.6 TB of weights per forward pass. With MoE, each token only activates ~220B parameters (2 of 8 experts per layer), making inference ~8× cheaper than a dense model of the same total size. MoE gets the knowledge capacity of 1.8T parameters at the inference cost of 220B. Module 7.1 covers MoE architecture in depth.

ArchitectureQuestion 01 / 07
If GPT-4 uses MoE with 8 experts per layer and routes each token to 2 experts, what fraction of the total parameters are active per token?
A100% — all experts process every token.
B50% — half the experts are used.
C12.5% — 1 of 8 experts.
D~25% of FFN parameters. In MoE, only the FFN is replicated into experts (MHA is shared). Each token routes to 2 of 8 FFN experts = 25% of FFN params. Since FFN is ~2/3 of block params, active params ≈ 100% of MHA + 25% of FFN ≈ 33% + 17% = ~50% of block params. But the FFN dominates at scale, so "2/8 of total" ≈ 25% is the rough active fraction. For 1.8T total → ~450B active.
02 / 08
Compute-optimal training — the Chinchilla lesson applied.

GPT-3 was under-trained (1.7 tokens/param vs Chinchilla's ~20 tokens/param). GPT-4 appears to have learned this lesson — with ~1.8T total parameters and ~13T training tokens, the ratio is ~7 tokens/param. Still below Chinchilla-optimal for the total parameter count, but reasonable for the active parameter count (~220B active × 60 tokens/param ≈ reasonable).

The compute budget evolution: GPT-3 → Chinchilla → GPT-4
GPT-3: 175B params, 300B tokens. Ratio: 1.7 tokens/param. Verdict: way under-trainedChinchilla showed: train a 70B model on 1.4T tokens → matches GPT-3 at 2.5× fewer params
Chinchilla: 70B params, 1.4T tokens. Ratio: 20 tokens/param. Verdict: compute-optimalThe golden ratio. Every dollar of compute is optimally split between model size and data.
LLaMA-1 (2023): 65B params, 1.4T tokens. Ratio: 21.5 tokens/param. Verdict: Chinchilla-optimalMeta applied the Chinchilla lesson directly. Module 3.1 covers LLaMA.
LLaMA-3 (2024): 405B params, 15T tokens. Ratio: 37 tokens/param. Verdict: over-trained on purposeTraining beyond Chinchilla-optimal improves inference quality at the cost of extra training compute — worth it if you serve the model millions of times.
The meta-lesson: Chinchilla-optimal minimizes training cost. But if you serve the model heavily, it's cheaper to over-train (spend more on training) than to over-size (spend more on inference per query).LLaMA-3 8B over-trained to 15T tokens: ~$3M training but then serves at $0.0001/query. A 70B model at 1.4T tokens: ~$1M training but $0.001/query.
Compute-optimalQuestion 02 / 07
LLaMA-3 8B was trained on 15T tokens — 37 tokens/param, nearly 2× the Chinchilla-optimal 20. Why intentionally over-train?
AMeta made a mistake — they should have trained a larger model.
BInference economics. Chinchilla-optimal minimizes the total of training + inference cost for a fixed number of queries. But LLaMA-3 8B will be served billions of times. Each query costs proportional to the active parameter count. Over-training an 8B model makes each query cheap ($0.0001), while the extra training cost (~$2M more) is amortized over billions of queries. A Chinchilla-optimal 16B model would give the same quality but cost 2× per query — unprofitable at scale.
CLLaMA-3 8B needed the extra data because it's small.
DOver-training improves safety, not quality.
03 / 08
Inference-time compute — the new scaling axis.

From GPT-2 to GPT-4, progress came from scaling training — more parameters, more data, more GPU-hours during training. In 2024, a new scaling axis emerged: inference-time compute. Instead of making the model bigger, let it think longer on hard problems.

OpenAI's o1 (September 2024) demonstrated this: the same base model, but with a "reasoning" chain before answering. On competition math, o1 jumps from GPT-4's ~40% to ~85% — not by being a bigger model, but by spending more compute at inference time through chain-of-thought reasoning.

Two scaling axes. 2019-2023: progress from bigger models and more data (horizontal, cyan). 2024+: additional progress from more inference-time compute (vertical, pink). o1 uses the same base model as GPT-4 but with extended reasoning chains, achieving dramatically higher performance on hard tasks.
Inference scalingQuestion 03 / 07
o1 spends ~30 seconds "thinking" before answering a math problem (vs GPT-4's ~2 seconds). What is it doing during those 30 seconds?
ALoading a larger model from disk.
BSearching the internet for the answer.
CGenerating a long internal chain-of-thought — exploring solution paths, checking intermediate steps, backtracking when a path fails, and refining the answer. This is autoregressive generation applied to reasoning: the model generates thousands of reasoning tokens (hidden from the user), where each token is informed by all previous reasoning. More tokens = more reasoning steps = better answers on hard problems. The model doesn't get smarter — it gets more careful.
DFine-tuning its weights on the specific problem.

Sweet spot for CineMatch: LLaMA-3 8B fine-tuned + RAG at $150/month. Domain-specific fine-tuning closes the gap with GPT-4 for recommendation queries. RAG provides factual grounding. 22× cheaper than GPT-4 API.
The "just use GPT-4" trap

Frontier models are often the worst value for domain-specific applications.

GPT-4 excels at general reasoning, complex multi-step problems, and tasks that require broad world knowledge. For CineMatch's specific needs (movie recommendations from a known catalog, review classification, spoiler detection), a fine-tuned 8B model with RAG matches GPT-4's quality at 1/22 the cost. The frontier model's advantage — broad generality — is wasted when the task is narrow and domain-specific. Use frontier models for prototyping and for genuinely complex reasoning. Use fine-tuned small models for production serving.

The volume is lower (users request explanations less often than search/classify), so the higher per-query cost is acceptable.
BLLaMA-3 8B — always cheapest.
CDeBERTa — it's the production encoder.
DNo model — use a template system.
05 / 08
Multimodal — vision as an input modality.

GPT-4V (Vision) accepts images alongside text. The architecture adds a vision encoder (likely a ViT variant — Module 6.1) that converts images into token-like embeddings, which are prepended to the text sequence. The decoder then attends to both image tokens and text tokens.

For CineMatch, multimodal capability enables: analyzing movie posters (visual style, mood, genre cues), processing user-uploaded screenshots ("what movie is this from?"), and understanding review images (memes, fan art, behind-the-scenes photos).

MultimodalQuestion 05 / 07
GPT-4V processes a movie poster by encoding it into ~576 visual tokens prepended to the text prompt. What's the computational impact?
ANo impact — images are processed separately from text.
BThe model needs a completely different architecture for images.
CImages can only be processed in special "vision mode."
D576 extra tokens means: 576 more positions in the attention computation (quadratic cost scales from T² to (T+576)²), 576 × d more KV cache per layer, and ~576 × (cost_per_text_token) more compute. For a 2K text prompt: (2576/2000)² ≈ 1.66× more attention compute. The text decoder doesn't change — it just receives more input tokens. This is why multimodal models are more expensive per query than text-only models.

The model selection framework for 2026.
CineMatch Integration

The complete CineMatch model stack — encoders + decoders, each matched to their task.

Tier 1 — High-volume, low-latency (encoders): DeBERTa-v3-base for classification and NER (~5ms, $0.0001/query). DistilBERT for CPU content moderation (~15ms). Sentence-BERT for embedding generation (batch). These handle 95% of CineMatch's ML queries.

Tier 2 — Mid-volume generation (small decoders): LLaMA-3 8B fine-tuned + DPO for the recommendation chatbot ($0.003/query, ~300ms). Handles conversational recommendations, spoiler-free summaries, and quick explanations. ~50K queries/day.

Tier 3 — Low-volume, high-quality (frontier): GPT-4 API for "explain why I'd like this movie" deep personalization ($0.02/query, ~3s). Complex reasoning over user history + cultural context. ~2K queries/day.

Cost structure: Tier 1: ~$200/month. Tier 2: ~$150/month. Tier 3: ~$1,200/month. Total: ~$1,550/month for the complete ML stack. Using GPT-4 for everything: ~$40,000/month.

StrategyQuestion 06 / 07
A startup competitor says: "We use GPT-4 for everything — classification, embeddings, chat, recommendations. One model, simple stack." What's the weakness?
AGPT-4 can't do classification.
BGPT-4 is less accurate than specialized models.
CThree weaknesses. Cost: using GPT-4 for classification ($0.01/query) vs DeBERTa ($0.0001/query) is 100× more expensive at high volume. Latency: GPT-4 takes ~1-3 seconds vs DeBERTa's ~5ms — unusable for real-time features. Dependency: the entire product depends on OpenAI's API availability, pricing, and rate limits. A mixed stack (specialized encoders + fine-tuned decoder + frontier for complex tasks) is cheaper, faster, and more resilient.
DThere is no weakness — simplicity is always best.

What we still don't know

GPT-4's architecture is unconfirmed. Treat all "reports" as informed speculation.

The MoE structure, the exact parameter count, and the training details are based on leaks and analysis, not official disclosure. OpenAI deliberately withholds this information for competitive reasons. As an engineer, treat these details as useful mental models, not facts. The principles — MoE for scaling, RLHF for alignment, inference-time compute for reasoning — are well-established regardless of GPT-4's specific implementation.
