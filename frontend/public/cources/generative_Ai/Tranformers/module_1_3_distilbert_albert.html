DistilBERT & ALBERT.
Making encoders small enough to ship.

BERT-base has 110M parameters and takes ~10ms per inference on a GPU. That's fine for prototyping — but for CineMatch serving 10,000 requests/second, it's expensive. This module covers two approaches to shrinking the encoder: DistilBERT (knowledge distillation — train a smaller student to mimic a larger teacher) and ALBERT (parameter sharing + factorized embeddings — same depth, fewer unique parameters). Both preserve 95%+ of BERT's quality at 40-60% of the cost. The choice between them depends on whether your bottleneck is latency, memory, or serving cost.

Notice the key difference: DistilBERT is faster at inference (6 layers → half the compute). ALBERT-base is smaller in memory (12M unique params → 9× smaller checkpoint) but not faster (still 12 layers of forward passes). These are different compression strategies for different deployment constraints.

The two axes of efficiency

Latency depends on the number of operations — layers × attention × FFN. Fewer layers = faster. Memory depends on the number of unique parameters — shared weights count once. DistilBERT cuts latency. ALBERT cuts memory. You need to know which is your bottleneck.

02 / 08
DistilBERT — knowledge distillation.

The idea: train a smaller student model to mimic the behavior of a larger teacher model. The student doesn't learn from the raw labels — it learns from the teacher's output probability distribution, which encodes richer information about the relationships between classes.

Knowledge distillation. The frozen teacher produces soft probability distributions over the vocabulary. The student learns to match these distributions (L_distill) while also learning from ground-truth MLM labels (L_mlm) and aligning hidden representations (L_cosine). Animated arrows show soft labels flowing from teacher to student.

The distillation loss — three terms.

Ltotal = α · Ldistill + β · Lmlm + γ · Lcosine (2.1)

Ldistill (soft cross-entropy): the student's output distribution should match the teacher's, computed with temperature T=4 to soften the probabilities. A teacher that gives P("great")=0.6, P("good")=0.2, P("excellent")=0.15 teaches more than the hard label "great" alone — it teaches the relationships between near-synonyms.

Ldistill = −Σv pteacher(v; T) · log pstudent(v; T)   where p(v; T) = softmax(zv / T) (2.2)

Lmlm: standard masked language model loss against ground truth (same as BERT pretraining). Lcosine: cosine similarity between teacher and student hidden representations at each layer — the student should produce similar intermediate representations, not just similar outputs.

Why temperature T=4 matters — softening the teacher's knowledge
Teacher logits for a masked position: z = [5.2, 2.8, 2.3, 0.1, −1.0, ...] (30K vocab)Top tokens: "great"=5.2, "good"=2.8, "excellent"=2.3, "nice"=0.1, "bad"=−1.0
With T=1 (normal softmax): P = [0.88, 0.08, 0.05, 0.005, 0.002, ...]"great" dominates at 88%. Soft label is nearly a hard label — student learns almost nothing about the other words.
With T=4 (softened): P = [0.31, 0.19, 0.17, 0.11, 0.08, ...]"great" is still top (31%) but the distribution is spread out. Now "good" (19%) and "excellent" (17%) have significant weight.
The student learns: "at this position, great/good/excellent are all plausible, nice is somewhat plausible, bad is unlikely." This teaches semantic relationships between words — richer than the hard label "great" alone.This is why distillation outperforms training from scratch on the same data — the teacher provides structured supervision.
DistillationQuestion 02 / 07
DistilBERT is trained with temperature T=4. What happens if you use T=1 (no temperature scaling)?
AThe model trains faster because the signal is clearer.
BThe soft labels become nearly one-hot — the student learns "the answer is 'great'" but not "good and excellent are close alternatives." The distillation degrades toward standard label training, losing the inter-class relationship information that makes distillation valuable. Empirically, T=4 outperforms T=1 by ~0.5 points on GLUE.
CThe model fails to converge.
DThe results are identical because softmax is invariant to temperature.
03 / 08
DistilBERT's architecture — where the layers go.

DistilBERT keeps BERT's hidden size (d=768) and attention heads (h=12) but cuts the layers from 12 to 6. The student is initialized from every other layer of the teacher — layers 1, 3, 5, 7, 9, 11 — giving it a strong starting point before distillation begins.

DistilBERT parameter breakdown
Embedding: same as BERT = 23.8M (same vocab, same d=768)The embedding table is not compressed — it's already efficient (O(V) parameters)
Encoder blocks: 6 × 7.1M = 42.5M (half of BERT's 85.1M)Each block is identical to BERT's — same d, same h, same d_ff. Just fewer of them.
Total: 23.8M + 42.5M = 66.3M ≈ 66M (60% of BERT's 110M)Latency: 6/12 = 50% of BERT's forward-pass compute. Quality: 97% of BERT's GLUE score.
The compression ratio: 40% fewer params, 50% faster, 3% quality loss. For production, this is almost always worth it.If BERT classifies at 92% accuracy, DistilBERT classifies at ~89.5%. At 10K requests/sec, the 50% latency savings = 50% fewer GPU-hours = 50% lower cost.
# Initializing DistilBERT from a BERT teacher
from transformers import BertModel, DistilBertConfig, DistilBertModel

teacher = BertModel.from_pretrained('bert-base-uncased')

# Student config: same d, h, but only 6 layers
config = DistilBertConfig(
    n_layers=6, dim=768, n_heads=12,
    hidden_dim=3072,  # d_ff = 4 × 768
)
student = DistilBertModel(config)

# Initialize student from every-other teacher layer
for i in range(6):
    teacher_layer = teacher.encoder.layer[i * 2]  # layers 0,2,4,6,8,10
    student.transformer.layer[i].load_state_dict(teacher_layer.state_dict())
print(f"Student initialized from teacher layers 0,2,4,6,8,10")
ArchitectureQuestion 03 / 07
Why does DistilBERT keep d=768 and cut layers, rather than keeping 12 layers and cutting d to 384?
ABERT's pretrained weights can't be split to d=384.
BSmaller d would reduce the vocabulary embedding size.
C12 layers are needed for any useful representation.
DTwo reasons. First, with d=768, you can initialize the student from teacher layers directly — each student layer copies the weights of a teacher layer exactly. With d=384, the weight matrices have different shapes and can't be copied. Second, cutting layers gives a clean 2× inference speedup (half the sequential computation), while cutting d gives a murkier 4× reduction in per-layer compute but the same number of sequential steps — the memory bandwidth and kernel launch overhead don't scale as cleanly.
04 / 08
ALBERT — same depth, shared weights.

ALBERT (Lan et al., 2020) takes a fundamentally different approach: instead of removing layers, it shares parameters across all layers. All 12 encoder blocks use the same set of weights. The forward pass runs through 12 blocks as usual — but all 12 reference the same MHA and FFN weights.

BERT: x₁ = Block₁(x₀), x₂ = Block₂(x₁), ..., x₁₂ = Block₁₂(x₁₁)
ALBERT: x₁ = Block(x₀), x₂ = Block(x₁), ..., x₁₂ = Block(x₁₁)    [same Block!] (4.1)

This is mathematically equivalent to applying the same function 12 times — a fixed-point iteration. The model learns one transformation that, when applied repeatedly, progressively refines the representations.

Factorized embedding projection.

ALBERT's second trick: decouple the vocabulary embedding dimension from the hidden dimension. Instead of a V × d embedding table (30K × 768 = 23.4M params), use V × E (30K × 128) + E × d (128 × 768), where E is a small intermediate dimension:

ALBERT's factorized embedding vs BERT's direct embedding
BERT: V × d = 30,522 × 768 = 23,440,896 params (21% of total)Each token gets a full 768-dim embedding directly
ALBERT: V × E + E × d = 30,522 × 128 + 128 × 768 = 3,906,816 + 98,304 = 4,005,120 params5.8× smaller! Token gets 128-dim, then linearly projected to 768-dim
ALBERT-base total: 1 shared block (7.1M) + factorized embeddings (4.0M) + LayerNorm/pooler (~0.9M) = 12Mvs BERT's 110M — 9.2× smaller checkpoint. But same inference compute (12 forward passes through the shared block).
Why does this work? Most vocabulary tokens are rare — they don't need a full 768-dim embedding. The 128-dim bottleneck forces the model to learn a compact token representation, then expand it. For common tokens, the expansion learns to recover the full representation; for rare tokens, the bottleneck is barely used anyway.This is the same principle as low-rank factorization in LoRA (Phase 2) — most information lives in a low-rank subspace.
ALBERT is NOT faster than BERT

Parameter sharing reduces memory, not computation.

This is the most common misconception about ALBERT. Sharing weights across 12 layers means the checkpoint is 9× smaller (12M vs 110M), and fine-tuning uses less GPU memory (the optimizer states are 12× smaller). But inference is the same speed — the forward pass still executes 12 sequential layers of full-sized MHA + FFN. The same number of multiplications happen; they just reuse the same weight matrices. ALBERT optimizes memory, not latency. If your constraint is serving latency, use DistilBERT. If your constraint is model download size or GPU memory, use ALBERT.

ALBERTQuestion 04 / 07
ALBERT-base has 12M unique parameters vs BERT-base's 110M. Yet ALBERT-base scores 80.1 on GLUE vs BERT's 79.6 — slightly better with 9× fewer parameters. How?
AParameter sharing acts as implicit regularization. With shared weights, the model can't overfit individual layers to specific training examples — the same weights must work at every depth level. This is similar to how weight tying in RNNs regularizes sequential models. Additionally, ALBERT uses the SOP (Sentence Order Prediction) objective instead of NSP — a harder, more useful pretraining task. The combination of better regularization and better pretraining produces higher-quality representations from fewer parameters.
BALBERT trains for more epochs to compensate.
CALBERT uses a larger vocabulary.
DThe comparison is unfair — different evaluation protocols.

CineMatch Integration

Three encoders in the production stack.

DistilBERT for real-time classification: CineMatch's content moderation pipeline classifies every new review for toxicity, spam, and sentiment. Runs on CPU at ~15ms per review. At 100K reviews/day, the 50% latency savings over BERT saves $1,200/month in compute.

ALBERT for mobile: CineMatch's iOS/Android app runs a local ALBERT model for offline review drafting suggestions. The 12M-param checkpoint downloads in 2 seconds on 4G. The 48MB inference footprint fits in mobile RAM alongside the app.

RoBERTa for batch embedding: CineMatch's nightly embedding pipeline re-encodes all new reviews with RoBERTa for the vector search index. Latency doesn't matter (batch job); quality does. RoBERTa's superior representations produce better search results.

Building a distillation pipeline — the production recipe.
import torch, torch.nn as nn, torch.nn.functional as F

def distillation_loss(student_logits, teacher_logits, labels, T=4.0, alpha=0.7):
    """Combined distillation + hard-label loss."""
    # Soft targets from teacher (temperature-scaled)
    soft_teacher = F.softmax(teacher_logits / T, dim=-1)
    soft_student = F.log_softmax(student_logits / T, dim=-1)
    L_distill = F.kl_div(soft_student, soft_teacher, reduction='batchmean') * (T ** 2)

    # Hard targets (standard cross-entropy)
    L_hard = F.cross_entropy(student_logits, labels)

    # Combined: α × soft + (1-α) × hard
    return alpha * L_distill + (1 - alpha) * L_hard

# Training loop sketch
teacher.eval()
for batch in train_loader:
    with torch.no_grad():
        teacher_logits = teacher(batch.input_ids).logits
    student_logits = student(batch.input_ids).logits
    loss = distillation_loss(student_logits, teacher_logits, batch.labels)
    loss.backward()
    optimizer.step()
The T² scaling trap

Don't forget to multiply KL divergence by T² when using temperature scaling.

When you compute softmax(z/T), the gradients are scaled by 1/T² relative to the unscaled case. If you don't multiply the loss by T², the distillation gradients are T²=16 times smaller than intended — the student barely learns from the teacher. This is a common bug that produces a student that's only marginally better than training from scratch. The T² factor compensates for the gradient scaling, ensuring the distillation signal has the right magnitude relative to the hard-label loss.

ImplementationQuestion 06 / 07
You distill a BERT teacher into a 6-layer student. After training, the student scores 74 on GLUE (vs expected 77). You check: T² scaling is correct, initialization is from teacher layers. What else could be wrong?
AThe student needs more layers — 6 is too few.
BCheck α (the soft/hard loss balance). If α is too low (e.g., 0.1), the student mostly learns from hard labels and barely uses the teacher's soft knowledge. If α is too high (e.g., 0.99), the student ignores the ground truth entirely. The sweet spot is typically α=0.5-0.7. Also check the learning rate — distillation benefits from lower LR than pretraining-from-scratch (the student is already well-initialized from teacher layers).
CThe teacher model is wrong — use a larger teacher.
DDistillation doesn't work — the 3-point gap is expected.

Beyond DistilBERT and ALBERT — the efficiency landscape in 2026.

DistilBERT and ALBERT pioneered encoder compression, but the field has moved further:

TechniqueMethodSize reductionQuality retention
Distillation (DistilBERT)Fewer layers, teacher supervision40%97%
Weight sharing (ALBERT)All layers share weights89%100%+
Quantization (INT8)Reduce weight precision from FP32 to INT875%99%
Pruning (structured)Remove entire attention heads or FFN neurons30-50%95-98%
Distill + QuantizeDistilBERT + INT885%96%

In production, these techniques stack: distill BERT to 6 layers, quantize to INT8, prune 20% of heads → a model that's 8× smaller and 4× faster than BERT-base, retaining 94% of quality. For CineMatch's CPU serving tier, this is the difference between 10 requests/sec and 80 requests/sec per core.
