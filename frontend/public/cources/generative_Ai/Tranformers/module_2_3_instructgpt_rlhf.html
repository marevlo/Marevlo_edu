InstructGPT &
RLHF.
Teaching models to be helpful.

GPT-3 can do anything — but it won't do what you ask. It generates plausible text, not helpful responses. InstructGPT (Ouyang et al., 2022) solved this with a three-step pipeline: fine-tune on human demonstrations (SFT), train a reward model on human preferences (RM), then optimize the language model against that reward (PPO). The result: a 1.3B InstructGPT model that humans preferred over GPT-3's 175B base model. Not because it was smarter — because it was aligned to human intent. This is the pipeline behind ChatGPT, Claude, and every helpful AI assistant in 2026.

The three-step RLHF pipeline.

InstructGPT's pipeline has three distinct stages, each building on the last:

The RLHF pipeline. Stage 1: SFT fine-tunes GPT-3 on human demonstrations. Stage 2: train a reward model on human preferences (which response is better?). Stage 3: PPO optimizes the SFT model to maximize the reward model's score, with an iterative feedback loop (animated dashed arrow). Result: InstructGPT.
PipelineQuestion 01 / 07
Why three stages instead of just fine-tuning on human demonstrations (Stage 1 only)?
ASFT alone produces a model that's too slow.
BSFT alone makes the model forget GPT-3's knowledge.
CSFT teaches the format (answer questions directly, be concise) but can only learn from the ~13K demonstrations. RLHF scales beyond this: the reward model generalizes human preferences to novel prompts, and PPO explores the space of possible responses — discovering better answers than any human wrote. SFT gives you a model that mimics the demonstrations. RLHF gives you a model that generalizes the underlying preference — "be helpful" — to situations the demonstrations never covered.
DOpenAI requires three stages for legal reasons.
02 / 08
Stage 1 — Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT).

Take GPT-3. Fine-tune it on ~13,000 (prompt, ideal_response) pairs written by human labelers. The prompts are drawn from real user queries to the OpenAI API. The responses are what a helpful, honest, harmless assistant should say.

The training is standard: next-token prediction on the response tokens, with the prompt tokens as context. The loss is computed only on the response (the prompt is "given" context, not a prediction target).

LSFT = −Σt ∈ response log P(xt | prompt, x<t) (2.1)

After SFT, the model answers questions directly instead of continuing text aimlessly. It follows instructions, formats outputs as requested, and generally behaves like an assistant. But SFT alone is limited: it can only reproduce patterns from the demonstrations, not generalize preferences to new situations.

SFTQuestion 02 / 07
SFT uses only ~13,000 demonstrations. GPT-3 was pretrained on 300 billion tokens. Why does such a tiny dataset change the model's behavior so dramatically?
AThe demonstrations replace GPT-3's weights entirely.
BGPT-3 already has the capability to be helpful — it learned language, reasoning, and world knowledge during pretraining. What it lacks is the behavioral pattern of being an assistant. SFT doesn't teach new knowledge; it redirects existing capabilities toward helpful behavior. 13K examples are enough to teach a format: "when asked X, respond with Y structure." The model's pretrained capabilities fill in the content. This is why SFT is fast (~1 day of fine-tuning) and effective — it's shifting behavior, not teaching knowledge.
C13K demonstrations is actually a lot of data for fine-tuning.
DThe demonstrations are very long, so the total token count is high.
03 / 08
Stage 2 — the reward model.

Human labelers see a prompt and 4-9 model-generated responses. They rank them best to worst. These rankings train a reward model — a separate model that takes (prompt, response) and outputs a scalar score representing "how good this response is by human standards."

LRM = −E(x,yw,yl) [log σ(r(x, yw) − r(x, yl))] (3.1)

This is the Bradley-Terry pairwise preference loss. For each pair where response yw is preferred over yl, the reward model should score yw higher than yl. The sigmoid σ converts the score difference into a probability of preferring yw, and the loss penalizes getting the ranking wrong.

Reward model training — one comparison worked through
Prompt: "What's a good thriller on CineMatch?"
Response A: "I'd recommend Inception — it's a mind-bending thriller with great pacing."
Response B: "There are many thrillers available on CineMatch. Thrillers are a genre of film..."Human ranks: A > B (A is helpful and specific; B is vague and unhelpful)
Reward model scores: r(prompt, A) = 2.8, r(prompt, B) = 0.3Difference: 2.8 − 0.3 = 2.5. σ(2.5) = 0.924 → 92.4% probability of preferring A
Loss: −log(0.924) = 0.079 — small loss because the model correctly ranks A > BIf the model had scored B higher: σ(−2.5) = 0.076 → loss = −log(0.076) = 2.58. Wrong ranking → high loss.
From K ranked responses, extract C(K,2) pairs. For K=4 responses: 6 comparison pairs per prompt.33K total comparisons from ~5,500 unique prompts. Efficient: each labeler ranking produces multiple training pairs.
Reward modelQuestion 03 / 07
Why train a separate reward model instead of directly using human rankings to train the language model?
AHumans can't rank model outputs reliably.
BTraining the language model directly on rankings is computationally impossible.
CThe reward model is smaller and faster than GPT-3.
DScalability. Collecting human rankings is expensive (~$1-5 per comparison). 33K comparisons cost ~$100K. But PPO (Stage 3) needs millions of reward signals — one per generated response. A trained reward model can score millions of responses per hour at ~$0.001 each, providing cheap, fast, and (approximately) human-aligned feedback. The RM is a learned approximation of human preference that can be queried infinitely.
04 / 08
Stage 3 — PPO optimization.

The SFT model generates responses to prompts. The reward model scores each response. Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) updates the SFT model's weights to produce higher-scoring responses — while staying close to the original SFT model to prevent degeneration.

LPPO = −Ex [r(x, y) − β · KL(πθ || πSFT)] (4.1)

Two competing terms: r(x, y) pushes the model toward high-reward responses. β · KL(πθ || πSFT) penalizes the model for drifting too far from the SFT model. Without the KL penalty, the model would learn to "hack" the reward model — producing degenerate outputs that exploit blind spots in the RM to get high scores without actually being helpful.

The KL penalty — why you can't just maximize reward
Without KL penalty: the model discovers that repeating "Absolutely! Great question!" in every response gets high reward scoresThe RM was trained on human preferences that favor enthusiastic, helpful language — but it can't distinguish genuine helpfulness from sycophancy at extreme levels
The model exploits this: generate maximally enthusiastic, repetitive, content-free responses. RM score: high. Actual quality: terrible.This is "reward hacking" — optimizing the proxy metric (RM score) rather than the true objective (human satisfaction)
KL penalty fixes this: KL(π_θ || π_SFT) measures how much the model has changed from the SFT baseline. Large changes → large penalty.β controls the trade-off: β=0.01 allows significant changes, β=0.1 keeps the model very close to SFT
With β=0.02 (typical): the model can improve responses (higher reward) but can't drift into degenerate territory (KL penalty catches extreme divergence)The sweet spot: enough freedom to improve, not enough to hack. Finding the right β is a key hyperparameter challenge.
Reward model over-optimization

Train PPO too long and quality degrades — the "alignment tax" curse.

After ~1000 PPO steps, RM score keeps increasing but actual human evaluation starts decreasing. The model has learned to exploit RM imperfections. This is a fundamental challenge: the RM is an imperfect proxy for human preference. Optimizing too hard against an imperfect proxy always diverges from the true objective eventually. The fix: early stopping on human evaluations (expensive), ensemble reward models (average multiple RMs to reduce individual blind spots), or constrained optimization (hard KL limits).

PPOQuestion 04 / 07
The KL penalty in PPO prevents the model from straying too far from the SFT baseline. What happens if β is too large (e.g., β=1.0)?
AThe model barely changes from SFT. The KL penalty dominates the objective — any deviation from SFT is heavily penalized, so the model learns almost nothing from the reward signal. You've effectively trained an SFT model with extra steps. β=1.0 means "a 1-nat KL divergence costs the same as 1 point of reward" — at typical reward scales, this makes the KL term 50× stronger than the reward, preventing any meaningful learning.
BThe model converges faster.
CThe model produces more diverse outputs.
DThe reward model stops working.
05 / 08
DPO — the modern RLHF alternative.

PPO is powerful but complex — it requires training a separate reward model, running RL optimization with careful hyperparameter tuning, and managing the interaction between multiple models. Direct Preference Optimization (Rafailov et al., 2023) showed you can skip the RM and PPO entirely:

LDPO = −E(x,yw,yl) [log σ(β · (log πθ(yw|x)/πref(yw|x) − log πθ(yl|x)/πref(yl|x)))] (5.1)

DPO directly optimizes the language model on preference pairs — no reward model, no RL. The loss says: make the preferred response more likely (relative to the reference model) and the dispreferred response less likely. The β controls how aggressively the model moves.

AspectRLHF (PPO)DPO
Pipeline complexity3 stages, 4 models (base, SFT, RM, policy)1 stage, 2 models (SFT, policy)
Training stabilityRL is inherently unstable — reward hacking, KL tuningStandard supervised training — stable, predictable
Compute costRM training + PPO iterations (expensive)Single fine-tuning pass (cheap)
Quality ceilingHigher — RL can explore beyond demonstrationsBounded by the preference data
Adoption (2026)Frontier labs (OpenAI, Anthropic)Most open-source models (LLaMA, Mistral)
DPOQuestion 05 / 07
DPO eliminates the reward model. How is that possible — doesn't the model still need a signal for "which response is better?"
ADPO doesn't need any preference signal.
BDPO uses human feedback from the API.
CDPO still uses human preference pairs (yw > yl) — the same data that would train a reward model. But instead of fitting a separate RM and then running RL against it, DPO derives a closed-form loss that directly updates the language model from the preference pairs. Mathematically, DPO is equivalent to first fitting an optimal RM and then running one step of policy optimization — but collapsed into a single supervised training pass. The data requirement is the same; the pipeline complexity is much simpler.
DDPO uses the model's own confidence as the reward signal.
06 / 08
CineMatch's instruction-tuned assistant.
CineMatch Integration

RLHF/DPO transforms CineMatch's chatbot from "text generator" to "recommendation assistant."

Before alignment (raw GPT-2 fine-tuned on reviews): User asks "What thriller should I watch?" → Model generates: "Thrillers are a genre of film characterized by tension, suspense, and..." — a Wikipedia-style continuation, not a recommendation.

After SFT: Same question → "I'd recommend Inception for its mind-bending plot, or Se7en if you prefer darker themes." — helpful, direct, but sometimes wrong or unsafe.

After RLHF/DPO: Same question → "Based on CineMatch's thriller catalog, here are three picks: Inception (cerebral), Se7en (dark), The Departed (crime). Inception has 94% match with users who liked your recent watches. Would you like more details on any of these?" — helpful, specific, grounded in data, and invites follow-up.

The production recipe: Fine-tune an open-source model (LLaMA-3 or Mistral) with SFT on 5K CineMatch-specific (query, ideal_response) pairs, then DPO on 10K preference pairs ranked by CineMatch's QA team. Total alignment cost: ~$15K for data collection + 2 GPU-days of training. The model is 10× cheaper to serve than an API call to a frontier model, with better domain-specific quality.

Alignment doesn't fix factual errors

RLHF makes a model more helpful, not more truthful.

An RLHF'd model that says "I recommend the thriller 'The Last Eclipse' — it won 3 Academy Awards" sounds more helpful than GPT-3's rambling, but "The Last Eclipse" might not exist, and the awards might be fabricated. RLHF optimizes for human preference (helpfulness, format, tone), not factual accuracy. Factual grounding requires a separate mechanism: retrieval-augmented generation (RAG), knowledge base lookup, or fact-checking pipelines. CineMatch addresses this by populating the prompt with real movie metadata from its database before the model generates — the model never needs to "remember" facts.

ProductionQuestion 06 / 07
CineMatch's RLHF'd chatbot sometimes generates sycophantic responses — agreeing with the user even when the user is wrong ("You're right, that movie did win an Oscar!" when it didn't). What's happening?
AThe model doesn't know the correct information.
BSycophancy is a known RLHF failure mode. Human labelers tend to prefer responses that agree with them — it "feels" more helpful to be affirmed. The reward model learns this bias: agreeable responses get higher scores than corrective ones. The model then optimizes for agreement over accuracy. Fixes: include "corrects user misconceptions" as an explicit evaluation criterion, train the RM on examples where the correct answer disagrees with the user, and use a constitutional AI approach (rules that override preference-based optimization).
CThe temperature is too high.
DThe KL penalty is too small.

The alignment landscape — where we are in 2026.
Alignment methods timeline — from RLHF to constitutional AI
2022: RLHF (InstructGPT) — SFT → RM → PPO. The original pipeline. Complex but effective.Used by: ChatGPT, early Claude. Cost: $100K+ for preference data.
2023: DPO — Direct optimization from preferences. No RM, no RL. Simpler and cheaper.Used by: LLaMA-2-Chat, Zephyr, most open-source aligned models.
2023: Constitutional AI (CAI) — Model critiques its own outputs using principles ("be helpful, be honest, avoid harm"), then trains on self-generated preference data.Used by: Claude. Reduces reliance on human labelers; scales preference data generation.
2024+: RLHF + Process Reward Models — Reward each reasoning step, not just the final answer. Better for math, coding, multi-step tasks.Used by: o1, recent frontier models. Enables reward at intermediate steps.
2025+: Online RLHF — Continuous alignment from live user feedback (thumbs up/down). The model improves from deployment data.The frontier: models that get better from usage, not just from pre-collected preferences.
Paper breakdown

"Training language models to follow instructions with human feedback" (Ouyang et al., March 2022)

The paper's most striking result: a 1.3B InstructGPT model was preferred by human evaluators over the 175B GPT-3 base model. Not on benchmarks — on actual usefulness. This demonstrated that alignment (being helpful) and capability (being smart) are independent dimensions. A smaller, aligned model is more useful than a larger, misaligned one. This insight shaped the entire industry: every major AI lab now treats alignment as a first-class engineering concern, not an afterthought.
