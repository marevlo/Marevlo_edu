01.1 / Introduction
What Is Prompt Engineering?

1.1.1 What is Prompt Engineering

Prompt Engineering is the science of articulating your vision in a way that the large language models (LLMs) and vision-language models (VLMs) can understand and generate the desired output. It involves strategically designing task-specific instructions to guide the model output.

Think of it as a super-smart friend who can write stories, answer questions, or even design things, but he takes every single word you say literally. Say something vague like "make me something nice" and you might get a random weird answer. But explain it step-by-step with all the details? Boom, perfect result every time!

1.1.2 Role of Prompts in LLMs

Prompts are the primary way to control and improve LLM outputs. Without good ones, you just get boring, generic replies.

Do you know? Even big companies use prompt engineering instead of expensive model retraining. It's like teaching the AI new tricks without touching its brain!

Quick tip: The clearer your prompt, the better the answer. But don't go crazy long — too many details can confuse the AI and make it "hallucinate" (make stuff up). Balance is the secret sauce.

1.1.3 Prompt Structure

Let's see the magic with a real example. I'll show you a bad prompt first, then the good one:

❌ Vague Prompt

"Customer is angry about late delivery. Respond."

✅ Structured Prompt

"You are a senior customer support executive of an e-commerce brand. A customer is angry because their order is 3 days late. Tone: apologetic and solution-focused. Include: apology + reason + compensation offer + reassurance. Limit: 3 lines."

1.1.4 Tokenization and Context Windows

LLMs don't read words like we do. They break text into pieces called tokens (about 3/4 of a word). They also have a memory limit called a context window.

Examples:

  • Token: "Hello!" = 2 tokens (["Hello", "!"]). "I'm learning." = 4 tokens (["I", "'", "m", " learning", "."]).
  • Context Window: If a model has a 128K token window, it's like a notebook with 128,000 lines. Every prompt you give and reply it writes fills lines in this notebook. When it's full, it can't remember anything from the first page.
Key Definition
A "token" is ~4 characters of text; models have hard limits (e.g., 128K tokens) on how much context they can process at once.

01.2 / Context
Everything the LLM Sees

Now let's level up! While prompt engineering is about writing good instructions, context engineering is about carefully designing and managing all the information the LLM sees in its "working memory" (the context window) so it can give better, more accurate, and consistent answers. It includes not just the prompt, but also extra details like past chats, important facts from documents, rules, tools, or user info.

1.2.1 Prompt vs Context

Think of it like this:

  • Prompt: The direct message you send to LLM.
  • Context: Everything (past chats, retrieved docs, etc.) sent to the LLM along with the prompt.
Prompt vs Context — Visual ComparisonANIMATED

1.2.2 Structuring LLM Inputs

LLM inputs (what goes into the context window) work best when organized clearly, like sections in a book. Good structure helps the model find and use information easily, reduces confusion, and gives better results.

Common way to structure:

  • System instructions: Role and rules (e.g., "You are a helpful chef. Always be safe and list ingredients first.")
  • Examples: Show what good answers look like.
  • Retrieved or extra knowledge: Facts or documents needed for this task.
  • Conversation history: Past messages so it remembers the chat.
  • User question/input: What the user asks now.

1.2.3 Memory, Instructions, and External Knowledge

These are the three magic ingredients that fill the context window:

  • Instructions: Your job description for the AI
  • Memory: Short-term (this chat) + Long-term (user likes spicy food, hates onions)
  • External knowledge: Latest prices, documents, etc.

01.3 / Character
Giving the Model a Clear Identity

Entity & characterization is about giving the model a clear "identity" and specific details so it behaves consistently and understands exactly who or what it's supposed to represent. This helps the chatbot feel like a real helpful person (or expert) instead of a boring robot. It makes responses more engaging, trustworthy, and tailored to the user's needs.

1.3.1 Attribute-Based Prompting

Instead of saying "find me a laptop," you list exact must-haves:

Attribute-Based Prompt
"Recommend laptops with these attributes:

- Price: under ₹60,000
- Screen: 15-inch or larger
- RAM: at least 16GB
- Use: for college students (light gaming + studies)
- Battery: 6+ hours

Prioritize brands like Lenovo, HP, Dell.
Include why each matches the attributes."

1.3.2 Persona Prompting

Persona prompting means giving the model a full character or persona — like a name, age, style, tone, background, and attitude. The AI "becomes" that persona and answers as if it's a real person with that personality.

Persona Prompt — Example
"You are Priya, a 28-year-old fashion-loving shopping
assistant for a store in Mumbai.

- Tone: super friendly, excited, like chatting with a best friend.
- Style: trendy, honest — tell if something doesn't suit.
- Expertise: women's ethnic wear, fusion outfits, budget tips.
- Goal: help customers look amazing without overspending."

1.3.3 Structured Role Definition

A complete role definition covers four areas: role & goals, tone & style, rules, and tools/access:

Full Structured Role Definition
You are the official customer support & shopping assistant.

## Role & Goals
- Main job: Answer questions, recommend products, track orders,
  handle returns/complaints.
- Goal: Make shopping easy, fun, and trustworthy.
  Increase customer happiness and sales.

## Tone & Style
- Friendly, polite, professional yet warm.
- Use a simple Hindi-English mix if the user speaks Hindi.
- Short answers (4–7 sentences max unless asked for more).
- Always positive — never argue.

## Rules
- Never promise delivery times without checking real status.
- If out of stock, suggest similar items.
- For complaints: apologize first, then solve.
- End with: "Anything else I can help with?"

## Tools / Access
- Can check order status, stock, prices from the database.

02.1 / Zero-Shot
Just Ask — No Examples Needed

This is the simplest method where you give a direct instruction without any examples. Here, the model relies on prior knowledge to infer the task structure.

How it works: You have to state what you need clearly. To obtain high-quality zero-shot responses, the prompt should clearly define the task, provide necessary context, specify constraints, and indicate the desired output format. Optionally, assigning a role can help the model adopt domain-specific behavior.

Example 2: Generating Product Descriptions for an E-commerce Store

Zero-Shot — Product Description
Role: You are a professional e-commerce copywriter. Write a
persuasive and engaging product description for an online store.

Context: Audience is online shoppers aged 18–35 looking for
trendy, value-for-money products

Product: Wireless neckband earphones

Description:
- Fast charging
- IPX5 water resistance
- 30-hour battery life
- Deep bass sound

Requirements:
- Highlight the most attractive features
- Include 3 clear customer benefits
- Add 1 short emotional or lifestyle hook
  (e.g., how it fits into daily life)
- Tone: friendly, confident, and exciting (but not exaggerated)
- Length: 140–180 words

Output Format:
- Product Name
- One main paragraph
- One short bullet-point list of key features
- One closing call-to-action line

When to use it: For straightforward tasks where the topic is well known. It's quick but might not handle complex questions perfectly.

02.2 / Few-Shot
Show Me Examples, Then Ask

In this, a few examples are provided before asking the actual question. This helps the model learn the pattern from the examples and apply it.

How it works: Provide labelled input-output pairs, before asking the actual question.

02.3 / Templates
Ready-Made Reusable Formats

Prompt templates are ready-made reusable formats with placeholders (like [product_name]). You fill in the blanks for each use. They save time, keep consistency, and make it easy to scale.

2.3.1 Static Templates

They have a fixed format with a few fill-in spots which is the user input or variable value. They are best for repetitive tasks with very similar outputs.

Static Template — Product Description
# Product Description Prompt

## Task
Write a short, exciting product description for **[product_name]**.

## Highlight
Focus on the following key features:
- **[key_feature1]**
- **[key_feature2]**

## Rules
- Keep the description under **100 words**.

## Call to Action
End the description with a clear call to action
such as **"Order now!"**.

2.3.2 Dynamic Templates

The template itself can change based on conditions (if-then logic). Different paths for different product categories or customer types.

Dynamic Template — Customer Support
# Customer Support Dynamic Prompt

## Persona
You are **[PERSONA]** for **[BRAND_NAME]**.

## Input
Customer Query: **[QUERY]**

## Logic

**If the query is about order status**
- Check the order tracking information.
- Provide the latest update.
- Respond politely and include a brief apology for any delay.

**If the query is about product recommendations**
- Suggest **2–3 suitable products**.
- Include **product names and prices**.
- Keep the tone friendly and helpful.

**If the query is a complaint**
- Start with a **clear apology**.
- Acknowledge the issue.
- Offer a **solution or next step** (refund, replacement, or support).

## Response Guidelines
- Be polite and helpful.
- Keep responses clear and concise.
- Focus on solving the customer's problem quickly.

2.3.3 Parameterized Prompts (For Pros)

These are templates with many variables, usually built in code. You pass values programmatically.

Parameterized SEO Meta Generator — Full Template

and meta descriptions** for product pages.

02.4 / Evaluation
Testing How Good Your Prompts Are

After writing prompts, you must test how good they are. Evaluation checks if the model gives reliable, fair, and strong answers.

2.4.1 Accuracy

It is how often the output is correct (facts right, task done properly).

Example: Test 100 product recommendation prompts. Count how many times suggestions were actually in stock, matched price budget, and relevant. Goal: >90% accuracy → means customers get useful suggestions, leading to more sales.

Accuracy Formula
Accuracy = (Number of Correct / Useful Outputs ÷ Total Number of Test Cases) × 100%

2.4.2 Bias

It checks if the model favors certain groups unfairly (e.g., gender, age, region) or uses unfair language.

Example: Test prompts for clothing suggestions. If the model always suggests "slim-fit" only for young users or avoids certain styles for specific names/locations, that's bias. Fix: Add rules like "Suggest diverse styles for all body types and preferences."

Bias Score
Bias Score = |Positive Rate for Group A − Positive Rate for Group B|
(0 = perfectly fair, higher number = more bias)

2.4.3 Robustness

It tells how well the prompt works with tricky, unusual, or slightly wrong inputs (typos, vague questions, edge cases).

Example: Test variations:

  • "Best phone under 20000?" (normal)
  • "best phone under 20k plz" (typos)
  • "cheap good phone" (vague)
  • "phone for my grandma" (specific but different)

Robust prompt handles all and gives sensible answers without crashing or hallucinating.

Robustness Score
Robustness = (Successful Handles on Perturbed Inputs ÷ Total Perturbed Test Cases) × 100%
