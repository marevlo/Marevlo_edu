MODULE 3.9 · BAYESIAN LINEAR REGRESSION Track 3 Module 3.9 10 / 12

Don't pick a point.
Keep the whole distribution.

Module 3.3 derived Ridge as the mode of the posterior under a Gaussian prior on coefficients. That mode is a single point — and a single point is exactly what classical regression has always been. Bayesian linear regression hangs on to the rest: with Gaussian-Gaussian conjugacy, the full posterior p(w | X, y) is itself a Gaussian, computable in closed form. From that single object you get the mean (= Ridge MAP), the covariance (= parameter uncertainty), the predictive distribution at any new x* with both noise and parameter uncertainty (uncertainty bands that widen far from training data), and — through the marginal likelihood — a principled way to pick hyperparameters without cross-validation. ARD takes the same machinery and turns it into Bayesian feature selection.

02 / The full posterior, in closed form
Gaussian-Gaussian conjugacy & sequential posterior contraction
Put a Gaussian prior on the coefficients, model the noise as Gaussian — and the posterior over coefficients is also Gaussian, whose mean and covariance are given by closed-form updates. As you observe data points one at a time, the posterior contracts around the truth: the contour ellipses shrink, the orientation rotates to align with the data, the mean slides from the prior's center toward the OLS estimate. Slide n below and watch the prior become a confident posterior.
model : y_i = wᵀ φ(xᵢ) + ε_i, ε_i ~ 𝒩(0, β⁻¹) φ(x) = [1, x] for our demo prior : w ~ 𝒩(0, α⁻¹ I) ← Gaussian prior on coefficients likelihood : p(y | X, w, β) = 𝒩(Φw, β⁻¹ I) ← Gaussian likelihood posterior : w | X, y ~ 𝒩(μ_N, Σ_N) ← conjugacy: also Gaussian Σ_N⁻¹ = α I + β Φᵀ Φ ← precision (1/variance) accumulates from data μ_N = β Σ_N Φᵀ y ← shrinks toward 0 from OLS by factor (β Φᵀ Φ) / (α I + β Φᵀ Φ) limits : α → 0 ⇒ μ_N = (ΦᵀΦ)⁻¹ Φᵀy = OLS α → ∞ ⇒ μ_N = 0 (prior dominates) Ridge MAP = μ_N with λ = α/β ← Module 3.3 recovered as the posterior mean

Deriving the posterior — Bayes' rule once, completing the square math · code

The posterior derivation is mostly algebra. Multiply the prior and likelihood, expand in the exponent, group quadratic + linear + constant terms in w, and the quadratic coefficient becomes the new precision while the linear/quadratic terms together give the new mean.

log p(w | X, y) ∝ log p(y | X, w) + log p(w) = −(β/2) ‖y − Φw‖² − (α/2) ‖w‖² + const = −(1/2) wᵀ [ α I + β ΦᵀΦ ] w + wᵀ [ β Φᵀy ] + const identify with 𝒩(μ_N, Σ_N) form: Σ_N⁻¹ = α I + β ΦᵀΦ μ_N = Σ_N · β Φᵀ y (posterior precision-weighted mean) this is the posterior — Bayes' rule, completed the square. Closed form, one matrix solve.
# Bayesian linear regression — the posterior in 7 lines import numpy as np def bayes_posterior(Phi, y, alpha, beta): """Returns posterior mean and covariance for w | y under Gaussian-Gaussian conjugacy.""" p = Phi.shape[1] Sigma_inv = alpha * np.eye(p) + beta * (Phi.T @ Phi) Sigma = np.linalg.inv(Sigma_inv) mu = beta * Sigma @ Phi.T @ y return mu, Sigma # sklearn equivalent — alpha_init, lambda_init for the priors; fits hyperparameters via empirical Bayes from sklearn.linear_model import BayesianRidge br = BayesianRidge(alpha_init=1.0, lambda_init=1.0, compute_score=True).fit(X, y) print(f"learned α = {br.alpha_:.3f}, λ = {br.lambda_:.3f}") print(f"posterior mean = {br.coef_}") print(f"posterior cov diag = {br.sigma_.diagonal()}") mean, std = br.predict(X_test, return_std=True) # predictive mean + std — the whole point

Two reminders. (1) Ridge connection. Setting λ = α/β in Ridge's normal equation gives exactly the posterior mean — Ridge IS Bayesian linear regression's mean. The difference is that Ridge throws away the covariance, while Bayesian regression hands it to you, ready to use for predictive intervals. (2) sklearn naming. Confusingly, BayesianRidge.alpha_ is the noise precision (our β) and BayesianRidge.lambda_ is the prior precision (our α). The same letters mean opposite things in different libraries; always check.

03 / The payoff: uncertainty in predictions, for free
The predictive distribution & widening bands
Once you have the posterior over w, the predictive distribution at any new x* follows by integrating out w. The result is also Gaussian, with a variance that decomposes cleanly: noise (irreducible, the same everywhere) plus parameter uncertainty (large where data is sparse, small where data is dense). That's why the uncertainty band below has the iconic "trumpet" shape — narrow in the heart of the data, wide at the edges. This is the entire unique payoff of the Bayesian view.
predictive at x*: p(y* | x*, X, y) = ∫ p(y* | x*, w) · p(w | X, y) dw = 𝒩(μᵀ φ(x*), 1/β + φ(x*)ᵀ Σ_N φ(x*)) ↑ ↑ ALEATORIC noise EPISTEMIC parameter uncertainty irreducible shrinks with more data constant in x* depends on x* → widens far from training contrast with OLS / Ridge: they give a single ŷ(x*) — no notion of uncertainty bootstrapping gives one — at huge computational cost Bayesian gives the WHOLE predictive Gaussian for free

04 / Empirical Bayes — picking hyperparameters without CV
The evidence & Type-II maximum likelihood
Where Ridge picked λ by cross-validation, Bayesian linear regression picks α and β by maximizing the log marginal likelihood — the probability of the observed data with w integrated out. That single objective penalizes overfitting (too-precise prior) and underfitting (too-loose prior) automatically, and is computable in closed form for conjugate models. The heatmap below shows log p(y | α, β) over a 2D grid; the optimum picks both hyperparameters simultaneously.
log marginal likelihood (the "evidence"): log p(y | α, β) = (p/2) log α + (n/2) log β − E(μ_N) − (1/2) log|A| − (n/2) log(2π) where A = αI + β ΦᵀΦ and E(μ_N) = (β/2)‖y − Φμ_N‖² + (α/2)‖μ_N‖² Type-II maximum likelihood (empirical Bayes): pick (α*, β*) = argmax log p(y | α, β) ⇒ implicit equations α = γ / ‖μ_N‖² and β = (n − γ) / ‖y − Φμ_N‖² γ = Σⱼ λⱼ / (α + λⱼ), λⱼ eigenvalues of β ΦᵀΦ ⇒ iterate to a fixed point — sklearn's BayesianRidge does this for you

Why evidence-based tuning works — & ARD as Bayesian feature selection theory · code

The log marginal likelihood is well-known to encode Occam's razor: too-simple models can't fit the data (penalty from E(μ_N) being large), and too-complex models spread their probability over too many configurations (penalty from log|A| being large). The evidence balances both, finding the simplest model that explains the data — without ever touching a held-out set.

log p(y | α, β) = log α^(p/2) ← favors LARGE α (simple) + log β^(n/2) ← favors LARGE β (good fit) − (β/2)‖y − Φμ_N‖² ← good fit term (large β + small residuals = good) − (α/2)‖μ_N‖² ← prior term (large α + small μ = good) − (1/2) log |α I + β ΦᵀΦ| ← Occam penalty: large effective d.o.f. = bad − (n/2) log(2π) Occam's razor : models with too many effective parameters get penalized via log|A|

Automatic Relevance Determination (ARD) generalizes the prior to a per-feature precision: w_j ~ 𝒩(0, α_j⁻¹) with its own α_j. Optimize each α_j via Type-II ML and irrelevant features see their α_j → ∞ (their coefficient gets pushed to zero), while relevant features see their α_j stay small. The result is Bayesian feature selection: a sparse model with calibrated uncertainty, without ever specifying a sparsity penalty.

from sklearn.linear_model import ARDRegression # ARD = per-feature priors, learned jointly via evidence maximization ard = ARDRegression( threshold_lambda=10000, # prune coefficients whose α exceeds this compute_score=True, ).fit(X, y) # inspect learned per-feature precisions — high α_j means "irrelevant" for j, alpha_j in enumerate(ard.lambda_): print(f"feature {j}: α_j = {alpha_j:.1e}, coef = {ard.coef_[j]:.3f}") # predict with uncertainty - same API as BayesianRidge mean, std = ard.predict(X_test, return_std=True)

ARD's coefficient sparsity is comparable to Lasso's, but with two key differences: (1) the sparsity emerges from the prior-precision optimization rather than from a non-smooth penalty (so the optimization is smoother), and (2) you get calibrated predictive uncertainty as a free byproduct. The cost is computational — ARD's evidence optimization is iterative and slower than Lasso's coordinate descent. Practical default: use BayesianRidge for uncertainty-aware regression, ARD when you want Bayesian-flavor feature selection, plain Ridge or Lasso when speed wins.

05 / When the extra machinery pays off
Bayesian vs frequentist — which when
Bayesian linear regression sits one layer up from Ridge: same point estimate, more downstream information. The question is whether you need that information enough to pay for the extra complexity.
RIDGE / OLS

You just need a prediction

  • Closed form, fast, well-understood
  • CV picks λ — usually sufficient
  • No uncertainty bands needed downstream
  • Default when prediction is the deliverable
BAYESIAN RIDGE

You need predictive uncertainty

  • Bands needed for decision-making (intervals, alarms, alerts)
  • Small data — uncertainty matters more than point precision
  • Hyperparameter tuning without held-out CV (small n, lots of features)
  • Downstream Bayesian pipeline expecting posteriors
ARD

Sparse + uncertainty-aware

  • Want feature selection + calibrated intervals
  • Smaller p (≤ 100); evidence optimization is iterative
  • Don't trust Lasso's arbitrary feature picks
GP REGRESSION (3.11)

Nonlinear Bayesian regression

  • Same math, infinite features via kernels
  • The natural sequel: GP posterior mean = Kernel Ridge with the kernel α/β
  • Use when you want uncertainty AND smooth nonlinearity
🧰

Reach for Bayesian linear regression when…

You need predictive uncertainty for downstream decisions (control loops, anomaly detection, A/B test analysis), you have small n and want to avoid the CV variance penalty, or your downstream pipeline is Bayesian end-to-end (probabilistic programming, hierarchical models). Skip when prediction is the sole deliverable and Ridge is sufficient. The natural next step is Gaussian Process Regression (Module 3.11) — same Bayesian machinery, infinite-dimensional feature space via kernels — and the broader GLM family (Module 3.10) which extends Bayesian regression beyond Gaussian likelihoods.
