K-Means is fast, but reality is rarely clean. Outliers exist. Categories exist. Large datasets exist. This part rebuilds partitioning from the ground up using K-Medoids, CLARA, and CLARANS.

01 — Why K-Means Breaks

The moment averages stop being trustworthy

K-Means uses a mean as the center of a cluster. That is mathematically elegant, but it creates a fragile system. The moment an extreme value appears, the center shifts toward it. The algorithm starts respecting noise as if it were structure.

That is why this part exists. We now move from the world of ideal numerical clouds into the world of outliers, categorical attributes, distance matrices, and representative real points.

Mental Model
K-Means finds an average. K-Medoids chooses a representative.
An average may not exist in reality. A medoid must be a real object already present in the dataset. That one design change makes the algorithm far more robust and far more interpretable.

Core weakness of K-Means: if you cluster spending, location, or customer behavior, one extreme outlier can drag the center far away from where most points actually live.

02 — K-Medoids

A cluster center that must be real

A medoid is the most central actual point in a cluster. It is not an average. It is not an invented location. It is one of the real observations that minimizes total distance to the other members of the cluster.

This single constraint changes everything. Because the center must remain one of the existing points, one extreme outlier cannot distort the center the way it distorts a mean.

Human intuition: if you want a representative customer to interview, you do not want an imaginary customer with 2.4 children and 0.5 dogs. You want a real customer from your database who best represents the group.

The objective

Optimization Objective
Minimize total dissimilarity to assigned medoids
\( E = \sum_i d(x_i, m_{c(i)}) \). Each point pays a distance cost to the medoid of its cluster. The algorithm tries to reduce the total dissatisfaction of the system.

03 — PAM Algorithm

Partitioning Around Medoids, step by step

PAM is the classic K-Medoids algorithm. Unlike Lloyd's algorithm in K-Means, PAM explicitly asks a hard question again and again: what if a different real point becomes the representative?

1

Initialize medoids

Pick k actual points to act as provisional representatives.

2

Assign points

Every point joins the nearest medoid based on your chosen distance or dissimilarity.

3

Try swaps

For each cluster, test whether a non-medoid point would be a better representative.

4

Compare total cost

If the swap lowers total dissimilarity, accept it. Otherwise reject it.

5

Repeat until no better swap exists

The algorithm converges when no local swap can improve the objective.

📐 Math Workout — PAM on a 1D dataset
Dataset: [2, 4, 10, 12, 50], with k=2. Suppose we begin with medoids 4 and 12. Compute the total cost, then test swapping 12 with 10.
Step 1 — Initial assignment
2 → 4 with distance 2
10 → 12 with distance 2
50 → 12 with distance 38
Step 2 — Initial total cost
E = 2 + 2 + 38 = 42
Step 3 — Try swap 12 → 10
2 → 4 with distance 2
12 → 10 with distance 2
50 → 10 with distance 40
Step 4 — New total cost
E' = 2 + 2 + 40 = 44
Because 44 is worse than 42, the swap is rejected. The old medoids remain. This is the core search logic of PAM: test, compare, accept only if better.

04 — Why It Works Better

Robustness, categorical data, and distance matrices

K-Medoids does not need a mean. It only needs a distance function or a dissimilarity matrix. That is what makes it much more general than K-Means.

✅ Numerical data
✅ Categorical records
✅ Text documents
✅ Images via embeddings
✅ Any pairwise distance matrix

If you can tell the algorithm how different two objects are, then K-Medoids can in principle cluster them. That is a huge conceptual jump. You are no longer limited to Euclidean geometry on raw numbers.

Real-world examples

Hospital placement: choose a real village, not an average coordinate in an empty field.

Customer archetypes: choose real customers to interview, not imaginary averages.

Document clustering: use cosine or learned embedding distance without ever needing a mean over raw text.

But be honest: K-Medoids is not magic. It is still a partitioning algorithm and still tends to prefer compact clusters. If your data forms rings, moons, or long chains, you need density-based or graph-based clustering instead.

05 — Scalability

Why PAM becomes painfully slow

PAM is robust, but it pays for that robustness. It has to evaluate many possible swaps. As the number of points grows, the search becomes expensive. On large datasets, this makes standard PAM impractical.

Complexity Intuition
Robustness is purchased with extra search
K-Means updates centers with a simple average. PAM must inspect alternative representatives and compare total costs repeatedly. That means more distance computations and more time.

Advanced note

Modern implementations such as FastPAM reduce redundant computations and make K-Medoids much more practical than naive textbook PAM.

Research Erich Schubert and Peter J. Rousseeuw proposed faster K-Medoids variants that significantly improve runtime for real applications.

06 — CLARA

Cluster large applications by sampling

CLARA solves the PAM speed problem with one simple idea: do not run the expensive search on the full dataset. Instead, take a small sample, find medoids on that sample, then evaluate those medoids on the full data.

1

Draw a small random sample

For example, 500 points from a much larger dataset.

2

Run PAM only on the sample

This gives candidate medoids quickly.

3

Score on the full dataset

Assign every point in the original data to the sampled medoids and compute total cost.

4

Repeat with new samples

Keep the best sample-generated solution.

Intuition: CLARA says, “I cannot search the whole mountain carefully, so I will inspect a few good slices of it and choose the best result.”

Weakness: if the sample misses important structure, the discovered medoids will also be poor. CLARA is fast because it accepts this approximation risk.

07 — CLARANS

Randomized search over the medoid landscape

CLARANS takes a different path. Instead of committing to one random sample, it treats clustering as a search problem over a huge graph of possible medoid sets.

Each node in this conceptual graph is one possible set of medoids. A neighbor is created by swapping one medoid with one non-medoid. CLARANS explores only a small random subset of these neighbors, which makes it faster than exhaustive PAM while often more flexible than CLARA.

Search View
CLARANS is local search with randomness
That makes it conceptually close to randomized hill-climbing and other metaheuristic optimization methods. It does not examine everything. It examines enough to move toward better regions of the solution space.

Why this matters for advanced learners

Once you see CLARANS as randomized search, clustering stops looking like a one-off algorithm and starts looking like a general optimization problem.

Research CLARANS is historically important because it connects clustering with search over combinatorial neighborhoods, which later influenced scalable data mining practice.
