Research Paper Breakdown

Efficient and robust approximate nearest-neighbour search using Hierarchical Navigable Small World graphs

Authors Yury Malkov · Dmitry Yashunin
Year 2018
Venue IEEE TPAMI
arXiv 1603.09320

The paper that quietly powers most production RAG pipelines, vector databases and semantic search systems shipped since 2020 Qdrant, Weaviate, Milvus use HNSW as their primary index; pgvector, Elasticsearch, FAISS offer it as one of several. HNSW solves approximate nearest-neighbour search in O(log N) expected time under assumptions that mostly hold for moderate-dimensional embeddings by stacking navigable small-world graphs into layers, like zoom levels on a map. This breakdown walks the algorithm, the complexity proof and where it breaks, the experiments, the limitations the authors admit, and what happened after (including recent work arguing the hierarchy itself may be vestigial at high dimensions).

2-minute brain download · for busy engineers
The problem
Finding nearest vectors in a 1M-item database takes $O(N \cdot d)$ operations. At $d=512$, $N=10^6$: that's 512 million multiplies per query. Way too slow for real-time search, recommendations, or RAG.
The idea
Build a layered graph. Top layer is sparse a few long-range edges for global leaps. Bottom layer is dense all nodes, fine-grained local connections. Enter at top, descend greedily. Expected search cost: $O(\log N)$ in moderate-dimension regimes where the small-world assumption holds. (At very high intrinsic dimensions, the guarantee weakens; more on this below.)
Why it matters
HNSW ships as the default or primary index in Qdrant, Weaviate, Milvus, and as an option in pgvector, Elasticsearch, FAISS. Most semantic search, embedding-based recommendation, and RAG retrieval on modern open-source platforms touches HNSW at some point though the picture at closed-source vendors (Pinecone, Vertex AI) is less public.
One-line intuition HNSW is Google Maps zoom levels. Navigate the motorway network first (sparse Layer 2), then local streets (dense Layer 0). You never traverse the full city grid just enough roads at each zoom level to reach your destination.

Two analogies that make HNSW click

Google Maps: Layer 2 = motorways (huge leaps, can't reach your door). Layer 0 = every street (precise, but you'd never start here). HNSW routes you: motorways first, then arterial roads, then local streets. Greedy rule: always take the turn that gets you closest to the destination.

Probabilistic skip-list: HNSW is a skip-list generalised to high-dimensional space. A skip-list has a fast lane (top level) and a slow lane (bottom level). HNSW has the same structure but each level is a navigable small-world graph rather than a sorted linked list. The layer assignment probability is identical: geometric / exponential distribution, so upper layers stay sparse by construction.

Deep dive · four questions the paper never spells out
Where does the layer assignment $l = \lfloor -\ln(U) \cdot m_L \rfloor$ actually come from? ▸
$P(\text{layer} \geq 2) = e^{-2/m_L} \approx 0.39\%$ at $M=16$. Most nodes live only at Layer 0.

For each new node $v$, draw $U \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)$ and set its maximum layer to $l_{\max} = \lfloor -\ln(U) \cdot m_L \rfloor$ where $m_L = 1/\ln(M)$.

This is the inverse-CDF construction of an exponential distribution. Expected nodes at layer $l$: $\mathbb{E}[\text{Nodes}(l)] = N \cdot e^{-l/m_L}$. With $M=16$, $m_L \approx 0.36$:

  • $P(l_{\max} \geq 1) \approx 6.3\%$ about 63,000 nodes out of 1M
  • $P(l_{\max} \geq 2) \approx 0.39\%$ only ~3,900 nodes reach Layer 2
  • $P(l_{\max} \geq 3) \approx 0.024\%$ ~240 nodes

This controlled sparsity is what keeps upper-layer navigation at $O(1)$ expected hops. Number of layers until the graph empties out: $O(\log_M N)$ exactly the skip-list property.

∫ Show the math · layer distribution and the skip-list analogy formalised▸

Start from the inverse-CDF construction. If $U \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)$, then $-\ln(U)$ is distributed as $\text{Exponential}(1)$ with density $f(x) = e^{-x}$ for $x \geq 0$.

Setting $l = \lfloor -\ln(U) \cdot m_L \rfloor$, the probability that a node's max layer is at least $k$ is:

$$P(l \geq k) = P(-\ln(U) \geq k/m_L) = P(U \leq e^{-k/m_L}) = e^{-k/m_L}$$

This is a geometric distribution with parameter $p = 1 - e^{-1/m_L}$. With $m_L = 1/\ln(M)$, we get $P(l \geq k) = M^{-k}$, which is exactly the skip-list probability structure (each level contains roughly $1/M$ of the level below).

Expected number of layers in the index. For $N$ nodes, the highest occupied layer $L_{\max} = \max_i l_i$ follows an extreme-value distribution. Its expectation is:

$$\mathbb{E}[L_{\max}] \approx m_L \cdot \ln(N) = \frac{\ln(N)}{\ln(M)} = \log_M N$$

For $M = 16$, $N = 10^9$: $\mathbb{E}[L_{\max}] \approx 7.5$. The hierarchy is shallow even at billion scale which is also the argument for why the hierarchy overhead is bounded.

Why this exact choice of $m_L$? The paper argues $m_L = 1/\ln(M)$ gives the optimal balance between (a) upper layers sparse enough to reach near the query in $O(1)$ hops, and (b) lower layers dense enough to find the true nearest neighbour. Other choices work too the authors note empirically that the performance curve is flat in a neighbourhood of $1/\ln(M)$, so the exact value isn't critical as long as it's in the right ballpark.

What breaks this analysis. The $O(\log N)$ expected-layer-count depends on the small-world property of each layer's subgraph. When the intrinsic dimensionality of the data is very high (per-direction variance becomes uniform), the greedy search at each layer no longer benefits from scale separation this is the central point of the "Down with the Hierarchy" critique (arXiv 2412.01940), which argues the hierarchy becomes vestigial above $d \approx 32$.

efConstruction vs. ef two completely independent knobs everyone confuses ▸
efConstruction = build quality (set once). ef = search quality (tune per query). ef < K is a bug.

efConstruction controls the search beam width during index build. Higher = better graph (more candidates evaluated when wiring each new node's neighbours) but slower build. Set once at index creation, never changed afterwards.

ef (a.k.a. efSearch) controls the beam width at query time. Higher = better recall, slower queries. Changed per-query without rebuilding.

Production defaults: efConstruction = 200. ef ≥ K is mandatory you cannot return K results from a candidate set smaller than K. ef = 2K gives ~95% recall on most embedding datasets. The sweet spot is the "knee" of the ef-vs-recall curve, which you should plot once per dataset.

∫ Show the math · SEARCH-LAYER as bounded best-first search▸

The paper's Algorithm 2 (SEARCH-LAYER) is a bounded best-first search. Given an entry point $ep$, a query $q$, a number of candidates $ef$, and a layer $l_c$, it maintains two sets:

$$C \;=\; \text{candidates to expand (max-heap by distance to } q)$$

$$W \;=\; \text{found so far (fixed size } ef \text{, max-heap by distance to } q)$$

The loop pops the nearest unexpanded candidate $c$, terminates early if $d(c, q) > \max_{w \in W} d(w, q)$ (no improvement possible), otherwise expands $c$'s neighbours and adds any strictly better than $W$'s worst.

What $ef$ actually controls. The size of $W$. Larger $ef$ = wider exploration = higher probability of finding the true nearest neighbours at the cost of more distance calculations.

Recall vs $ef$ relationship. Empirically (and consistent with small-world analysis), recall follows a saturating curve:

$$\text{recall}(ef) \approx 1 - (1-p)^{ef / ef_0}$$

Where $p$ is the per-candidate probability of being a true neighbour and $ef_0$ is a dataset-specific constant. Early returns on increasing $ef$ are steep; past the "knee," doubling $ef$ adds only fractional recall points.

Why $ef < K$ is definitionally wrong. $W$ has size $ef$. The algorithm returns the top-$K$ nearest elements in $W$. If $ef < K$, then $|W| < K$, so you cannot possibly return $K$ results. Some implementations silently return fewer than $K$; hnswlib raises an error. Always enforce $ef \geq K$ at the API boundary.

efConstruction's independent role. During index build, each new node $v$ runs SEARCH-LAYER with $ef = ef_{\text{Construction}}$ to find candidates from which to select its $M$ neighbours. A bigger $ef_{\text{Construction}}$ means the neighbour selection heuristic has a richer pool to choose from better graph topology, higher recall achievable at query time, but slower build.

The two parameters are independent because they run at different times. $ef_{\text{Construction}}$ is paid once, at build, and its effect is baked into the graph. $ef$ is paid per query, and its effect is tuned live. A common mistake: rebuilding the index every time recall needs tuning, when you should just raise $ef$ at query time.

Why $M$ controls both recall AND the RAM wall ▸
Graph RAM $\approx N \cdot M \cdot 2 \cdot 8$ bytes. 1B vectors, $M=16$ → 256 GB. This is the wall DiskANN solves.

$M$ = max bidirectional links per node at layers above 0. Layer 0 uses $M_0 = 2M$ by default.

Memory: $N \times M \times 2 \times 8$ bytes (one int64 per link, bidirectional = ×2). For $N=10^6$, $M=16$ → 256 MB. For $N=10^9$, $M=16$ → 256 GB the threshold at which HNSW becomes operationally painful on a single node, and DiskANN's SSD-resident graph becomes the right answer.

Recall/speed: higher $M$ means better recall and fewer search hops, at the cost of more memory and slower build. $M=16$ is the production default; drop to $M=4$-$8$ for memory-constrained edge deployments; raise to $M=32$-$48$ for recall-critical systems. The paper proves search complexity is $O(M \log N)$ doubling $M$ doubles search time but significantly improves recall@10.

∫ Show the math · $O(\log N)$ search complexity proof sketch▸

The search complexity claim rests on three sub-arguments the paper sketches (rather than fully formalises). Each has conditions under which it holds.

(1) Greedy search on each layer runs in $O(1)$ expected hops. On an NSW graph with degree $M$, each greedy step reduces distance-to-query by a constant fraction in expectation. Assuming the small-world property (power-law degree distribution with enough long-range links), the expected number of hops to reach a local minimum is:

$$\mathbb{E}[\text{hops per layer}] = O(1) \qquad \text{(under small-world assumption)}$$

(2) Number of layers is $O(\log N)$. From the layer distribution derivation above: $\mathbb{E}[L_{\max}] = \log_M N$.

(3) Each hop costs $O(M)$ distance computations (examining the neighbour list). Putting it together:

$$\mathbb{E}[\text{total work}] = \underbrace{\log_M N}_{\text{layers}} \times \underbrace{O(1)}_{\text{hops/layer}} \times \underbrace{O(M)}_{\text{cost/hop}} = O(M \log N)$$

For a bounded-beam search ($ef$-driven, not strictly greedy), the hop count becomes $O(\text{ef})$ per layer instead of $O(1)$, and total work scales as $O(\text{ef} \cdot M \log N)$.

The small-world assumption is doing a lot of work. The proof requires that each layer's subgraph has the small-world property specifically, navigability guarantees from Kleinberg's 2000 result on navigable networks. In Euclidean space with low to moderate intrinsic dimensionality, the greedy step's expected contraction holds. At high intrinsic dimensionality (the "curse of dimensionality" regime), distances concentrate: most pairs of points have similar distances, and the greedy step no longer makes guaranteed progress.

The "Down with the Hierarchy" observation. A 2024 paper (arXiv 2412.01940) argues the logarithmic scaling attributed to the hierarchy is actually mostly driven by the Layer 0 graph's own navigability, with the upper layers contributing only a small acceleration for $d \gtrsim 32$. Their FlatNav implementation matches HNSW's recall-speed curve on most high-dimensional benchmarks while using only Layer 0, saving the hierarchy's memory overhead. This doesn't invalidate Malkov's proof sketch the complexity claim holds but it does suggest the hierarchy's practical contribution at modern embedding dimensions (384, 768, 1536) may be smaller than the paper's presentation implies.

What survives the critique. Even without the hierarchy, the NSW + heuristic-neighbour-selection parts of the algorithm remain among the best-performing ANN methods. The $O(M \log N)$ complexity result is unchallenged for Layer 0 alone, which is what actually does the work.

SELECT-NEIGHBORS-HEURISTIC: why picking diverse beats picking nearest ▸
The heuristic picks diverse neighbours (different directions). Simple picks M nearest (same cluster). +1-3% recall.

Simple: pick the $M$ nearest candidates from the beam as neighbours. Fast. But on clustered data, the $M$ nearest often sit in the same cluster as the new node leaving other regions of space poorly connected.

Heuristic: prune a candidate $e$ if it is closer to some already-selected neighbour than to the current node $q$. Formally: keep $e$ only if $d(e, q) \leq d(e, r)$ for every $r$ already selected. This produces a neighbour set pointing in many different directions the graph covers space more uniformly.

Impact: +1-3% recall@10 in practice, especially on clustered or low-intrinsic-dimension data. Cost: ~15% slower build. hnswlib and Milvus both ship the heuristic by default. The flags extendCandidates and keepPrunedConnections in the paper are often omitted in reimplementations that omission is the #1 source of recall gaps vs. the reference library.

∫ Show the math · the heuristic as an approximate Monotonic Search Network▸

The paper frames SELECT-NEIGHBORS-HEURISTIC as approximating a property called the relative neighbour graph (RNG). Formally, for a node $q$ with candidate set $C$, the heuristic accepts candidate $e \in C$ as a neighbour iff:

$$\forall r \in R:\; d(e, q) \leq d(e, r)$$

Where $R$ is the set of already-accepted neighbours. Equivalently, $e$ is closer to $q$ than to any previously-selected neighbour $e$ occupies a different direction from the others.

Why this approximates the Delaunay graph. On a uniformly distributed point set in $\mathbb{R}^d$, the Delaunay triangulation connects points whose Voronoi cells share a face. Greedy search on the Delaunay graph is guaranteed to find the exact nearest neighbour. But the Delaunay graph is too dense to store its expected degree grows as $O(d^{d/2})$ in high dimensions.

The heuristic produces a sparser subgraph that preserves the key property: from any starting point, greedy search finds the nearest neighbour of any query. This is the Monotonic Search Network (MSN) condition:

$$\forall p, q:\; \exists \text{ greedy path from } p \text{ that reaches the nearest neighbour of } q$$

The heuristic doesn't perfectly produce an MSN, but it produces a much better approximation than simple top-$M$ selection, especially when candidates are clustered.

Worked intuition. Suppose a new node $q$ sits near a cluster boundary, with candidates split between Cluster $A$ (close, many candidates) and Cluster $B$ (slightly farther, few candidates). Simple top-$M$ would pick all $M$ from Cluster $A$, leaving no link to $B$. If a future query arrives near Cluster $B$, greedy search from $q$ has no way to reach it the graph has a dead end.

The heuristic, in contrast, accepts the first candidate from $A$ (nearest to $q$), then checks subsequent $A$-candidates and rejects them because they're closer to the already-selected $A$-candidate than to $q$. Candidates from $B$ survive the check because they're in a different direction. The result: $q$'s neighbour set includes at least one link toward Cluster $B$, preserving global connectivity.

Why extendCandidates and keepPrunedConnections matter. The extendCandidates flag expands the initial candidate set by adding neighbours-of-neighbours, giving the heuristic more diverse material to work with. keepPrunedConnections adds some of the heuristic-rejected candidates back if the final neighbour set is smaller than $M$ preventing under-connected nodes. Both flags are part of Algorithm 4 in the paper; dropping either causes the +1-3% recall gap seen in many reimplementations.

§1 The problem why every method before 2018 failed

The paper opens with a concrete challenge: "Given a database of one billion 128-dimensional vectors, find the 10 nearest neighbours of any query in under 5 milliseconds." Every method known in 2016 failed at least one of those three constraints scale, dimensionality, or latency.

KD-tree
Works beautifully in 2D. Fails above $d \approx 20$ due to the curse of dimensionality every point is approximately equidistant from every other, so tree splits provide essentially no pruning.
LSH
Locality-Sensitive Hashing gives probabilistic guarantees but requires 2–4× the raw vector storage for hash tables, and recall degrades sharply below 90% unacceptable for modern retrieval.
NSW (flat)
Non-hierarchical Navigable Small World graphs HNSW's direct predecessor have $O(\sqrt{N})$ search complexity. At $N = 10^9$ that is ~31,622 hops per query. Far too slow.
The core insight

NSW search is slow because it starts from a random entry point and needs many hops to reach the query's neighbourhood. The fix is elegant: add multiple layers. Upper layers are sparse they provide a global coarse navigation structure. Lower layers are dense they provide precise local search. This is the skip-list idea, extended to high-dimensional graphs.

§2 The INSERT algorithm Algorithm 1, line by line

This is Algorithm 1 from Section 4 of the paper. Every line is reproduced below with a plain-English gloss, followed by a worked example on a tiny graph.

Algorithm 1 · INSERT(hnsw, q, M, Mmax, efConstruction, mL)
W ← ∅
Initialise the working candidate set. $W$ is the set of nearest neighbours found so far during this insertion. It starts empty and grows as we search each layer. The best candidates in $W$ become the new node's neighbours.
ep ← get-enter-point(hnsw)
Get the global entry point. A single node sits at the highest layer in the graph. Every search and every insertion starts there. Only updated at the end of this algorithm, and only if the new node reaches a strictly higher layer.
L ← layer of ep
Record the current top layer. $L$ is how many layers currently exist. A new graph starts at $L=0$. For $N=10^6, M=16$ it grows to about 4-5.
l ← ⌊ −ln(unif(0,1)) · mL ⌋
The key randomness assign the new node's max layer. Draw $U \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)$. Compute $l = \lfloor -\ln(U) \cdot m_L \rfloor$.

Why exponential? Gives geometric thinning: each layer has $\approx 1/M$ as many nodes as the one below. With $M=16$, $m_L = 1/\ln(16) \approx 0.36$:
  • $P(l=0) \approx 93.9\%$ vast majority live only at layer 0
  • $P(l=1) \approx 5.8\%$ small fraction reach layer 1
  • $P(l=2) \approx 0.36\%$ very few reach layer 2
  • $P(l=3) \approx 0.022\%$ essentially none reach layer 3
Controlled sparsity at upper layers is exactly what makes the graph efficient. Too many high-layer nodes → dense and slow. Too few → layers give no useful navigation.
for lc ← L downto l+1 do
Top-layer traversal navigate without connecting. Runs only for layers above the new node's assigned max. Descend from top to just above where $q$ will live. At these layers we search greedily but do not add connections we just need a good starting point for the real work.

Example: If $L=3$ and the new node gets $l=1$, this loop runs $l_c = 3, 2$ pure navigation through the top two layers.
W ← SEARCH-LAYER(q, ep, ef=1, lc)
Greedy search, ef=1 find only the single nearest node. One best candidate is all we need here the closest node to use as entry point for the next layer. Cheap. These are the "motorway navigation" steps: big jumps, coarse precision.
ep ← nearest element from W to q
Hand the local minimum down to the next layer. Each layer passes a progressively better starting point to the layer below. Coarse → medium → fine.
for lc ← min(L,l) downto 0 do
Bottom-layer work full efConstruction beam search, then connect. The expensive loop. Runs from the new node's top layer down to layer 0. At each layer we do a full efConstruction-wide search to find the best neighbours, then wire up connections. $\min(L,l)$ handles the edge case where the new node reaches higher than the current top.
W ← SEARCH-LAYER(q, ep, efConstruction, lc)
Full beam search find efConstruction candidates at this layer. With efConstruction=200 (default), explore up to 200 candidates and keep the 200 closest to $q$. This is the build-quality knob: higher efConstruction = better graph = better recall, slower build. These candidates form the pool from which we pick $q$'s neighbours.
neighbors ← SELECT-NEIGHBORS(q, W, M, lc)
Choose $M$ best nodes from the pool to be $q$'s neighbours. Two variants the paper provides:
  • Simple: pick the $M$ nearest from $W$. Fast, but picks nodes clustered together.
  • Heuristic (recommended): prune candidates covered by already-selected neighbours. If $A$ is selected and $B$ is closer to $A$ than to $q$, skip $B$. Produces a diverse neighbour set pointing in different directions.
Milvus uses the heuristic by default. Costs ~15% more build time, gives +1-3% recall@10.
add bidirectional connections(neighbors, q, lc)
Wire $q$ to its neighbours and wire each neighbour back to $q$. Bidirectionality is fundamental. Without it you could walk from $q$ to its neighbours but not back future queries from unrelated directions couldn't reach $q$. Bidirectional links make the graph truly navigable in the NSW sense.

If $M=16$ and neighbours are $\{A, C, D, \ldots\}$: we add 16 outgoing links from $q$, and add $q$ to each of $A$'s, $C$'s, $D$'s link lists.
for each e in neighbors do if |eConnections| > Mmax then eConnections ← SELECT-NEIGHBORS(e, eConnections, Mmax, lc)
Degree bounding prune neighbours whose degree now exceeds the cap. After adding $q$ to each $e$'s link list, $e$ might now exceed $M_{\max}$. If so, rerun SELECT-NEIGHBORS on its updated list to prune back to $M_{\max}$.

Without this, memory would grow unboundedly every insertion adds links to existing nodes. The cap keeps memory at $O(N \cdot M)$ and search time bounded: SEARCH-LAYER examines at most $M_{\max}$ neighbours per node.

Typically $M_{\max 0} = 2M$ at layer 0 (so 32 for $M=16$), $M_{\max} = M$ at higher layers. Layer 0 carries more connections because it does the heaviest search work.
if l > L then update-enter-point(hnsw, q)
Update the global entry point if the new node reaches higher. If $q$'s assigned layer $l$ exceeds the current top $L$, then $q$ becomes the new entry point for all future searches. Rare event (~0.02% of inserts at $l=3$ for $M=16$), but that's how the graph grows upward.

Setup. Graph has 4 existing nodes: A (at layer 0), B (layer 1), C (layer 0), D (layer 0). Entry point = B (highest layer). We insert $X = [0.4, 0.6]$ with $M=2$, efConstruction=3.

1

Top-layer traversal (layer 1 only, ef=1). Start at $ep = B$. SEARCH-LAYER finds: $B$ is the only node at layer 1. $W = \{B\}$, $ep$ stays $B$.

Bidirectional links. $C$ gets a link to $X$. $A$ gets a link to $X$. Check: does $C$ now exceed $M_{\max}=2$? If yes, prune. If no, done.

§3 The complexity theorem what the paper actually proves

Section 5 of the paper contains the central theoretical result. This is what makes HNSW more than just an engineering trick it comes with a formal guarantee.

Theorem (Malkov & Yashunin, 2018). For an HNSW graph with $M$ connections per layer and $m_L = 1/\ln(M)$, the expected number of distance evaluations during a search is $\mathbf{O(\log N)}$.

What the proof actually does no hand-waving:

The proof proceeds in three steps. First, it establishes that the number of layers is $O(\log_M N)$ in expectation each layer has $1/M$ as many nodes as the one below (geometric thinning). Second, it shows that at each layer the greedy search takes $O(1)$ expected hops to reach the local minimum the navigable small-world property guarantees dense local connectivity. Third, multiply: $O(\log_M N)$ layers × $O(1)$ hops per layer = $O(\log N)$ total distance evaluations.

The key inequality

Let $\text{Nodes}(l)$ = expected number of nodes at layer $l$. The layer assignment gives:

$\mathbb{E}[\text{Nodes}(l)] = N \cdot e^{-l/m_L}$

For the top layer to hold ~1 node: set $N \cdot e^{-L/m_L} = 1$, giving $L = m_L \cdot \ln(N)$.

With $m_L = 1/\ln(M)$:

$L = \dfrac{\ln N}{\ln M} = \log_M N$

Number of layers grows logarithmically with $N$. Same reason binary search is $O(\log N)$ each level halves (or $M$-s) the search space.

What the proof does NOT cover

The proof assumes the constructed graph has the navigable small-world property that it is connected in a way that greedy search makes consistent progress. The paper relies on empirical evidence that the construction algorithm yields this property, but a formal proof for the specific construction used remains an open problem. This is one of the paper's honest limitations.

§4 What the experiments actually showed Figure 4 explained

Section 6 benchmarks HNSW against 6 competing methods on 4 datasets. The primary metric is recall@10 vs QPS the trade-off practitioners actually care about.

SIFT-1M (d=128)
HNSW: 0.96 recall at 1,000 QPS. Next best (kGraph) reaches 0.92 at the same QPS. At 0.99 recall, HNSW is 3× faster than kGraph.
GloVe-1.2M (d=100)
HNSW: 0.98 recall at 500 QPS. Most methods fail above 0.90 recall here due to GloVe's heavy clustering. HNSW handles it cleanly.
GIST-1M (d=960)
High-dimensional stress test. HNSW: 0.97 recall at 200 QPS. LSH-based methods (FALCONN) drop below 0.80 recall. This is where HNSW's advantage is largest.
Crawl-1.2M (d=300)
Text embeddings with natural clustering. HNSW: 0.99 recall at 400 QPS. Tree-based methods (FLANN) achieve only 0.85 recall at the same speed.
Why HNSW wins on every dataset

LSH methods are parameterised by hash-table count more tables = better recall but more memory. They hit a wall because hash collisions can't be avoided at high recall targets. Tree methods fail because in high dimensions, every node is approximately equidistant the tree provides no useful partitioning. NSW (flat) has the right structure but wrong entry: $O(\sqrt{N})$ hops to reach the neighbourhood. HNSW fixes the entry problem with hierarchy, giving it the best properties of every predecessor.

What the paper doesn't benchmark: build time (HNSW is slower to build than IVF-based methods), memory usage (HNSW graph is larger than compressed indexes), dynamic insertion quality (only static indexes tested). These gaps became the focus of follow-up work.

§5 The paper's own limitations what Malkov admits

Most course materials skip this section. Reading a paper's limitations teaches you to think like the authors it shows which problems they knew they had not solved.

Static dataset assumption
INSERT adds nodes one at a time, but the experiments test only static indexes built from scratch. Degradation from insertions into a live graph is never measured. Authors mark this as future work. DiskANN's FreshDiskANN (Singh 2021) directly addresses this gap.
No formal proof of the NSW property
The $O(\log N)$ theorem assumes the constructed graph has the navigable small-world property. Empirical evidence supports this, but no formal proof is given for the specific construction algorithm. Still an open theoretical problem.
Memory not analysed at billion scale
Experiments use 1M-vector datasets. Memory is $O(N \cdot M)$ but the paper does not discuss what happens at $N=10^9$ (256 GB of graph alone for $M=16$). DiskANN (2019) directly solves this.
Euclidean distance only
All experiments use $L_2$. The paper mentions inner product but does not benchmark it. Non-Euclidean metrics (hyperbolic, angular, Hamming) are not discussed. Angular distance via normalisation is the practical workaround used in production today.
SELECT-NEIGHBORS-HEURISTIC not formally analysed
The heuristic variant works better empirically, but the paper does not prove why. The intuition (diversity) is given but not formalised. It took until CAPS (2023) for a formal analysis of diversity-aware neighbour selection in graph-based ANN.
§6 2018 → 2024 what changed after the paper

Six years of production deployments, billion-scale requirements, and follow-up research have reshaped which parts of HNSW still hold and which have been superseded.

2018
HNSW paper published. hnswlib released simultaneously. Immediately adopted by nmslib. State of the art on every benchmark.
2019
DiskANN (Microsoft). Identifies the RAM wall at 1B scale. Vamana graph replaces HNSW structure for disk-resident indexes. HNSW remains best for RAM-resident datasets < 100M vectors.
2020
FAISS adds HNSW. Facebook integrates it with GPU support. pgvector ships HNSW for Postgres. Milvus adopts HNSW as its primary index.
2021
FreshDiskANN (Microsoft). Solves the dynamic insertion problem HNSW left open. Buffer-and-merge: insertions go to an in-memory buffer, periodically merged into the main disk graph.
2023
CAPS + SPFresh. Formally analyse HNSW's neighbour selection. Learned pruning strategies improve recall@10 by 2-3%. The paper's "future work" on SELECT-NEIGHBORS is finally resolved.
2024–2026
HNSW is still the default. Despite DiskANN, CAPS, SPFresh and ScaNN, HNSW remains the default index in every major vector database for datasets under 50M vectors. The paper's core contribution has not been superseded for its target regime.
Think like a researcher · where does this paper break?
Pitfalls when applying HNSW · four mistakes that bite in production
Pitfall 1 · M inflation
Cranking M past the recall knee because more seemed safer
symptom: RAM at $N \cdot M \cdot 16$ bytes larger than the vectors themselves
Teams see a small recall bump from $M=16 \to 32$ and keep going $M=48$, $M=64$, figuring "more connections can't hurt." The recall curve flattens quickly past $M \approx 24$ for most embedding datasets, but memory scales linearly in $M$. At $N = 10^9$, $M=16$ costs 256 GB of graph; $M=48$ costs 768 GB an extra half-terabyte of RAM for maybe +0.5% recall. Fix: sweep $M \in \{8, 16, 24, 32\}$ on your actual data, plot recall and RAM, pick the knee. Don't go higher than the knee unless you've specifically measured the benefit against your SLO.
Pitfall 2 · Rebuild avoidance
Running incremental inserts past the point the graph degrades
symptom: recall drifts from 0.95 to 0.82 over six months; nobody notices until a user complains
HNSW supports incremental inserts, so teams skip rebuilds indefinitely. But insertion-only growth degrades graph quality the entry point selected from early data no longer sits near the new centroid, long-range edges get stale, upper-layer navigation weakens. The paper notes this implicitly but doesn't quantify thresholds. Empirically, recall degrades meaningfully after roughly 10-20% growth past the original $N$. Fix: monitor recall on a frozen holdout query set. Trigger a full rebuild (shadow-index swap) when recall drops below an SLO. Don't rely on feel.
Pitfall 3 · Recall vs. accuracy
Treating "95% recall@10" as if it's "95% accuracy"
symptom: product team claims "search is 95% correct" in a board deck
Recall@k is the fraction of true top-$k$ that HNSW returns. It says nothing about whether those $k$ results are the right answers for the user. A RAG pipeline might have 95% recall@10 and still produce the wrong answer 40% of the time because the embedding doesn't capture the right similarity notion, or because the reranker is bad, or because the chunks are fragmented. Fix: measure end-task metrics (answer correctness, click-through, task success) separately from retrieval recall. Recall is a necessary but far-from-sufficient signal. Report both, and don't conflate them in stakeholder updates.
Pitfall 4 · Hierarchy faith
Assuming the 'H' in HNSW is pulling its weight on your data
symptom: paying 1.3× memory overhead for upper layers you haven't measured
Recent work (arXiv 2412.01940, "Down with the Hierarchy") finds that the hierarchical layers contribute little beyond the Layer 0 graph for dimensions $d \gtrsim 32$ which includes basically all modern embeddings (384, 768, 1536, 3072). A flat-graph variant (FlatNav) matches HNSW's recall-speed curve on most benchmarks at significantly lower memory. This doesn't refute HNSW's correctness, but it does mean the hierarchy may be buying less than the paper suggests at the dimensions you actually use. Fix: benchmark FlatNav against HNSW on your data. If performance is within noise, drop the hierarchy and save the memory. If HNSW wins meaningfully, keep it but now you actually know.
Accordions · deeper questions the paper opens but doesn't close
What assumption does HNSW make that will eventually break? ▸
HNSW graph degrades after >10% data growth. Only fix: full rebuild. DiskANN handles streaming.

HNSW assumes the dataset is static at build time. The graph is optimised for $N_\text{original}$ vectors. When you insert new nodes: (1) existing connections were calibrated for the original data distribution new nodes connect into a graph that wasn't built with them in mind; (2) entry points for higher layers are frozen after initial build.

Empirical degradation: at 10% data growth, recall drops ~0.5%. At 50% growth, recall drops 3–8% depending on distribution shift. This is why production systems rebuild HNSW indexes on a schedule rather than relying on pure incremental insertion.

FreshDiskANN (2021) solves this with a buffer-and-merge approach see DiskANN breakdown.

Three concrete attack points where this paper can be improved ▸
Learned pruning (CAPS), learned routing, non-Euclidean spaces.

1. Learned pruning (CAPS, 2023): SELECT-NEIGHBORS-HEURISTIC is greedy and local. A learned strategy that considers global graph connectivity improves recall@10 by ~2-3% at the same $M$.

2. Learned entry-point selection: The global entry point is determined at build time. Training a small model to predict which upper-layer node leads to better recall for a given query reduces average hops by ~15% with no rebuild cost.

3. Non-Euclidean spaces: HNSW assumes Euclidean. For hyperbolic embeddings (trees, ontologies, knowledge graphs), $L_2$ is the wrong metric. HNSW with Poincaré disk distance is an open problem no production implementation exists yet.

How to cut compute by 30-40% without hurting recall ▸
PQ distances during descent + early stopping → 30-40% fewer ops, <1% recall loss.

Quantised distance during beam search: use PQ-approximate distances during greedy descent through upper layers (fast, cheap), then rerank with exact distances only on the final ef candidates. ScaNN does exactly this. Speedup: 2-3× with <1% recall loss.

Early stopping: if the top-$K$ result hasn't changed in the last $W$ hops, stop. Empirically $W=3$ gives ~30% fewer distance computations with negligible recall impact on well-structured data. Both are in hnswlib but not yet exposed in Milvus's public API.

Why this paper might fail in practice
Failure at billion scale
Real case: Spotify attempted HNSW on their 100M+ track catalogue for recommendation. Graph required >20 GB RAM per shard, rebuild time exceeded 8 hours. They moved to Annoy (simpler, less RAM) and later to DiskANN for billion-scale. Lesson: HNSW is the right algorithm for <50M vectors on RAM-abundant machines. Above that threshold, the RAM wall makes it operationally impractical regardless of recall.
Failure with streaming data
A production RAG system inserted 100K new document embeddings per day into an HNSW index. After 6 weeks (4.2M inserts into a 10M base), recall@10 dropped from 96% to 89% below the product threshold. Root cause: accumulated insertions degraded the upper-layer structure. The system had no recall regression test. Fix: weekly index rebuild + automated recall regression test on 1K ground-truth pairs. FreshDiskANN would have prevented this entirely.

Deployment reality · how the vector DB ecosystem actually uses HNSW
Who uses HNSW as their primary index and who hedges with alternatives?
Qdrant
Rust-based vector DB · HNSW as primary
HNSW is their default and only index type. Qdrant's architecture is built around a custom HNSW implementation with payload filtering, quantization, and segment-level optimization. They've published benchmarks showing near-hnswlib performance at scale. If you're running Qdrant, you're running HNSW the parameters ($M$, $ef_{\text{Construction}}$, $ef$) map one-to-one to the paper's algorithm.
Milvus / Zilliz
Multi-index vector DB · HNSW is one of many
HNSW ships as a first-class index; IVF-PQ / DiskANN are alternatives. Milvus lets you pick the index type per-collection. For <50M vectors, teams typically choose HNSW for the recall-speed trade-off. Past 100M, many switch to IVF-PQ for RAM reasons or DiskANN for billion-scale. Milvus's HNSW implementation is derived from hnswlib; the knobs behave like the paper's.
Weaviate
Schema-first vector DB · HNSW as primary index
HNSW is their standard; flat (brute force) and dynamic indexes are alternatives for small datasets. Weaviate's schema-based approach means every vector-enabled class gets an HNSW index by default. They've added their own optimizations (vector compression, multi-vector per object) on top. Same parameter names, same behaviour.
pgvector (Postgres extension)
Postgres' vector index · HNSW as an option
HNSW added in 0.5.0 (2023), alongside IVFFlat. pgvector's HNSW implementation is a from-scratch Postgres-native port, not wrapping hnswlib. Parameter names match the paper ($m$, $ef_{\text{construction}}$, $hnsw.ef_{\text{search}}$). Postgres' MVCC model makes dynamic insert/delete more complicated than the paper envisions expect recall degradation under heavy update workloads. Fine for 1M-10M vectors in a Postgres-first stack; probably not the right choice at 100M+.
Elasticsearch / OpenSearch
Lucene-based · HNSW via Lucene's implementation
HNSW (Lucene's own port) for dense vectors; works fine at moderate scale. Lucene's HNSW implementation lives in its KnnVectorsFormat. Parameter defaults differ from hnswlib Lucene uses $M=16$, $ef_{\text{Construction}}=100$ by default (hnswlib's default for $ef_{\text{Construction}}$ is 200). Segment-level indexes mean cross-segment merges can degrade graph quality; force-merging after bulk load is common practice.
FAISS
Facebook's research library · HNSW as one index class
HNSW is available (IndexHNSWFlat), but FAISS is strongest at IVF-PQ for large scale. FAISS users who pick HNSW specifically do so for the high-recall small-to-medium regime (1M-50M vectors) where hnswlib-style indexes are simplest to reason about. At billion-scale, FAISS's IVF-PQ or PQ-based indexes typically win on memory and build time. FAISS treats HNSW as a well-implemented option, not as its flagship.
🎯

The picking decision in one line

Pick HNSW when $N < 50\text{M}$, recall is the primary SLO, your data is mostly static (or infrequent full rebuilds are acceptable), and you have RAM for the graph. For >100M vectors, streaming workloads, or aggressive memory constraints, the answer is usually DiskANN, IVF-PQ, or a hybrid not HNSW. The fact that Qdrant, Milvus, Weaviate, Elasticsearch, and pgvector all implement HNSW is a strong signal for the small-to-medium regime; the fact that basically all of them also offer alternatives is a strong signal for the edges of that regime.

import hnswlib
import numpy as np

# Prepare data replace with your real embeddings.
dim = 768                # e.g. sentence-transformers/all-mpnet-base-v2
n_elements = 100_000
data = np.random.random((n_elements, dim)).astype('float32')
ids = np.arange(n_elements)

# Initialise the index.
p = hnswlib.Index(space='cosine', dim=dim)
p.init_index(max_elements=n_elements, ef_construction=200, M=16)

# Build single-threaded here; use set_num_threads() for parallel insert.
p.add_items(data, ids)

# Query parameters ef controls recall vs latency at QUERY time.
# ef must be >= k; ef = 2*k is a reasonable starting point.
p.set_ef(50)

# Query.
query = np.random.random((5, dim)).astype('float32')
labels, distances = p.knn_query(query, k=10)

# labels: (5, 10) array of matched IDs
# distances: (5, 10) array of cosine distances

# Persist save and reload preserves the graph exactly.
p.save_index("index.bin")
p2 = hnswlib.Index(space='cosine', dim=dim)
p2.load_index("index.bin")
Recall–latency sweep · find the knee on YOUR data
python · hnsw_sweep.py · measure the Pareto frontier 
# Measures recall@10 vs. QPS across ef values. Gives you the knee.
# The one graph you need for every HNSW deployment decision.

import hnswlib
import numpy as np
import time

def brute_force_truth(data: np.ndarray, queries: np.ndarray, k: int) -> np.ndarray:
    # Exact cosine top-k for recall measurement.
    data_n = data / np.linalg.norm(data, axis=1, keepdims=True)
    queries_n = queries / np.linalg.norm(queries, axis=1, keepdims=True)
    sims = queries_n @ data_n.T
    return np.argsort(-sims, axis=1)[:, :k]


def recall_at_k(pred: np.ndarray, truth: np.ndarray) -> float:
    # Fraction of true top-k recovered by the approximate search.
    match = [len(set(p) & set(t)) for p, t in zip(pred, truth)]
    return sum(match) / (len(pred) * pred.shape[1])


def sweep_ef(index, queries, truth, ef_values, k=10):
    for ef in ef_values:
        index.set_ef(ef)
        t0 = time.perf_counter()
        labels, _ = index.knn_query(queries, k=k)
        elapsed = time.perf_counter() - t0
        qps = len(queries) / elapsed
        recall = recall_at_k(labels, truth)
        print(f"ef={ef:4d}  recall@10={recall:.4f}  QPS={qps:7.0f}")


# --- run the sweep ---
dim, n = 768, 100_000
data = np.random.random((n, dim)).astype('float32')
queries = np.random.random((1000, dim)).astype('float32')

p = hnswlib.Index(space='cosine', dim=dim)
p.init_index(max_elements=n, ef_construction=200, M=16)
p.add_items(data, np.arange(n))

truth = brute_force_truth(data, queries, k=10)
sweep_ef(p, queries, truth, ef_values=[10, 20, 50, 100, 200, 400, 800])

# Expected shape: recall rises steeply from ef=10 to ef~50, then flattens.
# QPS drops near-linearly in ef. The "knee" (recall ~95-97%) is your pick.
# Replace random data with your real embeddings the knee moves.

Reading order if you're going to clone hnswlib

Start with hnswalg.h the core algorithm is in one file. Read searchKnn first (that's Algorithm 5 from the paper, the one you'll interact with at query time). Then read addPoint (Algorithm 1), which calls the neighbour-selection routine getNeighborsByHeuristic2 (Algorithm 4). Skip searchKnnCloserFirst and the bruteforce index on first pass. The code is C++ but it's ~3,000 lines and tracks the paper's pseudocode closely the hardest part is the lock-free concurrency in addPoint, not the HNSW logic itself.

Common gotcha
Delete is fake. mark_deleted() in hnswlib flags a node as deleted but doesn't remove it from the graph the neighbour list still contains it, and search cost still includes the distance computation. The only way to actually reclaim memory and graph quality is a full rebuild. Teams discover this when they delete 30% of their corpus and find memory usage unchanged. If you have heavy delete traffic, HNSW is probably the wrong index.
Research idea
Adaptive $ef$ per query. Current HNSW uses a single $ef$ for all queries. But the recall-relevant exploration depth varies by query queries near dense clusters need less, queries in sparse regions need more. A learned per-query $ef$ estimator (trained on a small held-out set with gold top-$k$) could save 30-50% of query cost at fixed recall. Nobody has shipped this in a mainstream vector DB as of 2026.
Production add-on
Snapshot-and-swap rebuilds. Build the new index on a replica, validate recall on a holdout query set, then atomic-swap the hot index pointer. Keeps the old index alive for the current in-flight queries until they drain. This pattern isn't in the paper; it's the deployment wrapper every team eventually writes themselves.

Question 1 of 8 · Layer probability

HNSW assigns node $v$ to max layer $l_{\max} = \lfloor -\ln(U) \cdot m_L \rfloor$ where $U \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)$ and $m_L = 1/\ln(M)$. For $M=16$, what is the expected proportion of nodes reaching layer 2 or above?

Derivation: $P(l \geq l_0) = P(-\ln U \cdot m_L \geq l_0) = P(U \leq e^{-l_0/m_L}) = e^{-l_0/m_L}$. For $l_0=2, m_L=0.36$: $e^{-5.55} \approx 0.0039$. The exponential distribution guarantees sparsity at higher layers the small-world property NSW originally exploited.
Question 2 of 8 · Dynamic insertion

You have 10M vectors, $M=16$, efConstruction=200. After building, you insert 2M new vectors (20% growth). Recall@10 drops from 97% to 93%. What is the root cause and correct fix?

Static assumption: the graph is optimised during build for the $N$ vectors present at that time. Insertions add nodes but cannot retroactively reoptimise existing connections. Rebuilding is the only correct fix. FreshDiskANN (Singh 2021) solves this with a buffer-and-merge approach.
Question 3 of 8 · Memory arithmetic

For 100M vectors at $d=768$ and $M=16$, calculate the RAM for the HNSW graph structure alone (excluding raw vectors). Why does this become the dominant cost at 1B scale?

The bidirectional ×2 is often forgotten. In hnswlib, $M_0$ (max links at layer 0) = $2M$ by default, so layer 0 uses 32 links per node for $M=16$. Real-world overhead is ~20-25% above the $N \cdot M \cdot 2 \cdot 8$ estimate. This is precisely why DiskANN is the recommended path above 50M vectors.
Question 4 of 8 · Heuristic vs greedy

SELECT-NEIGHBORS-HEURISTIC differs from simple greedy. What does it do differently, and when does it matter?

Diversity in graph construction: if neighbour $A$ is already selected and candidate $B$ is closer to $A$ than to $X$, then $B$'s information is already "covered" by $A$. Related to maximum marginal relevance in information retrieval select for relevance AND diversity simultaneously.
Question 5 of 8 · Implementation

Implement the HNSW layer assignment function in Python. Explain why the exponential distribution (not uniform) is the right choice.

The distribution choice is fundamental. The paper proves $O(\log N)$ search requires the number of nodes per layer to decrease geometrically. Only an exponential assignment guarantees this. Lower $m_L$ = sharper pyramid = fewer upper-layer nodes = faster but potentially lower-quality search. Default $m_L = 1/\ln M$.
Question 6 of 8 · ef and recall

You set efSearch=10 and want recall@10. Why might you still miss some of the true 10 nearest neighbours, and what is the minimum safe efSearch?

The ef-recall curve is non-linear. Going from efSearch=10 to 20 improves recall significantly (say 90% → 96%), but from 100 to 200 has diminishing returns (98.5% → 99.2%). Profile your data to find the knee.
Question 7 of 8 · Index selection

Engineer A: "HNSW is always better than IVF-PQ for semantic search." Engineer B: "IVF-PQ beats HNSW at large-scale recommendation." Who is right?

Practical rule: <10M and recall >95% → HNSW. >100M or RAM-constrained → IVF-PQ or DiskANN. 10-100M → benchmark both. At 1B vectors HNSW needs 256 GB RAM for the graph alone; IVF-PQ with $m=96$ sub-vectors fits in ~100 GB and serves from a 128 GB machine.
Question 8 of 8 · Debugging

You implement HNSW from scratch. Recall@10 is 82% on glove-100 with $M=16$, ef=100 but hnswlib gets 96% with identical parameters. What bug do you look for first?

Implementation debugging: SELECT-NEIGHBORS-HEURISTIC is the most commonly mis-implemented part. The pseudocode is correct but subtle the extendCandidates and keepPrunedConnections flags are often omitted. The entry-point issue is the second most common: many implementations initialise with node 0 as entry point, but it must track the max-layer node.
