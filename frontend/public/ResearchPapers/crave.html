Research Paper Breakdown

Teaching LLMs to Remember, in Words

AuthorsZhu · Steck · Liang · He · Kallus · Li
Year2025
VenueEMNLP Findings 2025
InstitutionsUVA · Netflix Research · Cornell

LLMs like GPT-4o are the strongest conversational recommenders we have zero-shot they already beat every fine-tuned baseline. But they share one blind spot: they can't learn from their own past conversations. You can't fine-tune a black-box API; stuffing few-shot examples into the prompt fails because the semantic gap between conversation and preference is too wide. CRAVE's trick is almost philosophical: if you can't update the weights, update the memory. Let the LLM watch itself recommend on historical conversations, compare its output to what the user actually liked, and write down a lesson in natural language. At inference, retrieve the most relevant lessons not by text similarity, but by preference similarity via a BERT fine-tuned with content-parameterized multinomial likelihood. Then run two debating LLM agents plus a critic, each with their own experience bank. The result: Recall@10 jumps from 0.18 to 0.25+ over zero-shot GPT-4o on Reddit-v2, while diversity also improves. Netflix is a co-author for a reason.

Nine ways to read this paper

From a 2-minute brain download on why LLMs can't learn from their own conversations, through the math of item-content-parameterized multinomial likelihood and how verbal RL works without gradients, to runnable Python that you can wire to GPT-4o and reproduce the Netflix-backed results this afternoon.

2-minute brain download · the core insight
The problem
LLMs are great zero-shot recommenders that can't learn from their own past
GPT-4o zero-shot beats every fine-tuned CRS baseline (KBRD, KGSF, UniCRS). But it's a black box: you can't fine-tune it on your historical conversations. Stuffing conversation-feedback pairs into the prompt as few-shot examples also fails, because the conversation and the user's preference live on different semantic planes. You can't reach the learning you need with either lever.
The insight
If you can't update the weights, update the memory in natural language
Verbal reinforcement learning (Reflexion, EXPEL) already showed LLMs can reflect on their failures in plain English and store the lesson as text. CRAVE extends this to CRS: the LLM reads a past conversation, sees its recommendation, sees the groundtruth, and writes down a guideline like "when the user mentions cozy rainy-day vibes, prioritize warmth and whimsy over drama". Retrieve the most relevant guidelines at test time. No gradients. No API fine-tuning.
The method
Experience bank + collaborative retrieval + debater-critic agents
Three parts. (1) Sample trajectories of LLM CRS agents on historical conversations; reflect on feedback → verbalized experience bank. (2) Collaborative retriever: Sentence-BERT fine-tuned not on text similarity but on preference similarity via item-content-parameterized multinomial likelihood. (3) Run two debater agents + one critic, each with their own experience bank diversifying thinking away from COT convergence.
The result
+38% Recall@10 over zero-shot GPT-4o, and better diversity
On Reddit-v2 (movie CRS), zero-shot GPT-4o sits at ~0.18 Recall@10. CRAVE-DCA lifts it to ~0.25+, with NDCG@10 climbing similarly. Crucially, DCA alone doesn't help (debaters without experience banks actually underperform COT on Redial). It's the combination CRAVE's experience retrieval enables DCA to diverge meaningfully. Diversity metrics also improve: content similarity drops from 0.505 to 0.458.

The working metaphor: a restaurant critic with a diary

Imagine a critic who reviews restaurants by chatting with diners before dinner. They're gifted zero-shot, their recommendations are better than most veterans. But they never learn across nights. CRAVE gives them a diary. After every shift they jot down what the diner said, what they recommended, what the diner actually loved, and what general lesson they'd draw. Crucially, the diary is organized not alphabetically but by diner-taste similarity so next time someone walks in with similar vibes, the right pages flip open automatically. And instead of one critic thinking alone, two critics debate with their diaries in hand, refereed by a third.

Why the collaborative retriever matters

A plain semantic retriever would match test query "top Brazilian films, love Bacurau" to training queries that mention Brazilian films even if those users had very different taste (e.g. telenovelas vs arthouse). CRAVE's retriever is fine-tuned so that conversations whose users liked similar items end up close in embedding space, even if the words differ. The resulting neighbours share preference, not vocabulary and the experiences retrieved from them actually transfer.

Deep dive · four questions the paper forces you to answer
Why verbal reinforcement learning at all why not just fine-tune the LLM on the training conversations? ▸
Because you can't. The best-performing zero-shot CRS agents are closed-model APIs (GPT-4o, Claude, Gemini) that don't support user fine-tuning on arbitrary data. Even when you can fine-tune, you'd need millions of conversations to move a 200B-parameter model. Verbal RL sidesteps both limits.

There are three strategies for teaching an LLM from historical conversations, and the paper deliberately picks the third:

Strategy 1 gradient-based fine-tuning. Works great for small PLMs (GPT-2, Llama-7B). Requires model weights + large training compute + careful learning-rate schedules. Fails for closed APIs and is too expensive for the strongest CRS backbones (GPT-4o has hundreds of billions of parameters).

Strategy 2 in-context few-shot. Retrieve conversation-feedback pairs and paste them into the prompt. Simpler, no training. But the semantic gap between raw conversation text and user preference is too wide for even GPT-4o to extract the right lesson in-context that's exactly what the paper's Few-Shot LLM baseline shows. The model gets overwhelmed by surface details.

Strategy 3 verbal reinforcement learning. Let the LLM reflect on its own past actions against groundtruth feedback, and write down a distilled lesson in natural language. At inference, retrieve the most relevant distilled lessons and put them in the prompt. You're storing compiled wisdom, not raw examples. Much higher signal-to-token ratio.

Verbal RL (Reflexion, RCI, EXPEL) was originally developed for tasks like QA and code generation where the model can iterate on the same question multiple times. CRAVE's contribution is adapting it to CRS, where each conversation is one-shot you never see the same user twice. The fix is a single-pass reflection: sample the action once, compare to groundtruth once, write one lesson.

What exactly is "item content-parameterized multinomial likelihood" and why is it the paper's real secret sauce? ▸
It's a clever fine-tuning objective that makes two conversations appear similar in embedding space if their users liked similar items even if the conversations themselves sound different. The retriever becomes preference-aware, not text-aware.

Here's the scenario. User A says "I love epic fantasies with world-building"; User B says "anything by Tolkien or Peter Jackson please". Textually these are pretty different a vanilla Sentence-BERT would not flag them as close. But both users would love The Hobbit, LOTR, and probably Dune. Their preferences align even though their words don't.

The paper's fix: fine-tune Sentence-BERT so that the embedding distance between conversations directly reflects the overlap of items their users liked. Naive version (paper's CRAVE-noMLT ablation): use the Jaccard overlap of groundtruth items as a pairwise similarity target. But Jaccard is a pure set operation it ignores the content of the items themselves.

CRAVE's full version goes one step further: treat each conversation embedding as a distribution over items, and train the distribution to match the groundtruth-item multinomial. Items are parameterized by their own content embeddings. This means two conversations whose users liked different but content-similar movies (e.g. one liked LOTR, the other liked Dune both epic fantasies) can still end up close in the retriever's space. Text similarity loses; content-preference similarity wins.

The ablations prove this is where the method's power lives: CRAVE-noFT (pretrained SBERT, no fine-tuning) drops ~5pp Recall@10. CRAVE-noMLT (fine-tuned on pure overlap, no item content) drops even more showing that item content is what makes the retrieval generalise.

∫ Show the math · item content-parameterized multinomial likelihood▸

Let $c_j$ be a training conversation, $I_j^{gt} \subseteq \mathcal{I}$ the set of groundtruth items the user ultimately liked, $\Psi: \text{text} \to \mathbb{R}^d$ the BERT-based encoder being fine-tuned, and $\mathbf{t}_k = \Psi(T_k)$ the embedding of item $k$'s content description (title + description).

Construct the item-content matrix:

$$W = \Psi(\mathcal{T}) = [\mathbf{t}_1 \mid \mathbf{t}_2 \mid \cdots \mid \mathbf{t}_{|\mathcal{I}|}] \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times |\mathcal{I}|}$$

The probability that item $k$ appears in conversation $j$'s groundtruth, conditioned on the conversation embedding, is defined as:

$$P(k \mid c_j) = \text{softmax}(W^\top \Psi(c_j))_k = \frac{\exp(\mathbf{t}_k^\top \Psi(c_j))}{\sum_{k'} \exp(\mathbf{t}_{k'}^\top \Psi(c_j))}$$

This is a Product-of-Experts structure: the conversation embedding $\Psi(c_j)$ projects onto each item's content embedding, and softmax normalizes across the catalog.

Then model the groundtruth item set as draws from a multinomial distribution parameterized by these probabilities:

$$I_j^{gt} \sim \text{Multinomial}\left(\text{softmax}(W^\top \Psi(c_j)),\; |I_j^{gt}|\right)$$

Train $\Psi$ by maximum likelihood across all $(c_j, I_j^{gt})$ pairs:

$$\mathcal{L}(\Psi) = -\sum_{j=1}^{N_{\text{train}}} \sum_{k \in I_j^{gt}} \log P(k \mid c_j)$$

Geometric intuition: this loss pulls $\Psi(c_j)$ toward the content embeddings of its groundtruth items, and away from everything else. Two different conversations that share groundtruth items (or whose items have similar content vectors) end up with similar $\Psi(c)$ hence similar in retrieval. This is what the paper calls "collaborative similarity" similarity in preference space, not text space.

Why not just Jaccard? Jaccard only counts exact item overlaps. If user A liked LOTR and user B liked Dune (disjoint sets, Jaccard=0) but both embed near "epic fantasy" in content space, the multinomial objective still pulls their conversation embeddings together. Jaccard would declare them unrelated.

Why does DCA alone fail but DCA + CRAVE succeed what's the missing ingredient? ▸
Zero-shot debater agents have nothing to disagree about productively they all share the same prior. Without role-specific experience, the debate produces noise, not divergence. CRAVE gives each debater a different lens to see the problem through.

Figure 4 in the paper contains the most interesting result that almost gets lost: on the Redial dataset, zero-shot DCA actually underperforms zero-shot COT. Adding debate and critique hurt. Why?

Because LLM debate was designed for tasks with clear answers logic puzzles, math, factual QA where two agents with different reasoning paths can converge on the truth. CRS is different: user queries are deliberately vague (otherwise users would just search directly), so there's no "correct" answer to converge on. Two zero-shot agents debating without any role-specific knowledge will either: (a) both default to the same popular recommendations, or (b) disagree randomly without either being more right.

CRAVE fixes this by giving each agent in the DCA a different experience bank. The COT agent has been reflecting as "the first-pass reasoner". The Debater agent has been reflecting as "the one who finds flaws in the first recommendation". The Critic has been reflecting as "the one who weighs both sides". These are genuinely different role priors, accumulated over hundreds of thousands of training samples.

Now when they meet at test time, they bring different perspectives by construction. The Debater's retrieved lessons include things like "when the first agent goes for mainstream safe choices, consider auteur-driven alternatives" a pattern it learned by reflecting on past debates where that pattern worked. The disagreement becomes productive because it's grounded in role-specific priors, not in random temperature sampling.

This is a subtle but important lesson about LLM agent systems: multi-agent architectures need multi-agent training. Running the same model with the same prompt in different roles and hoping for diversity is wishful thinking.

How does the collaborative retrieval differ from plain RAG, and why does the paper's RAG baseline flop so badly? ▸
Plain RAG retrieves semantically similar documents (movie plots, reviews). CRAVE retrieves preference-similar conversations and then retrieves lessons gleaned from those. The units being retrieved are fundamentally different.

The RAG baseline in Figure 5 retrieves item-related sentences (movie plots, metadata) based on semantic similarity between the user's query and the sentences. The paper shows this barely helps over zero-shot GPT-4o, sometimes hurts.

Three reasons RAG fails here:

1. Wrong unit of retrieval. The user's query is a conversation about taste; the retrieved documents are plot summaries. These live in different linguistic registers. A user saying "I want something to watch while cooking, I loved Julie & Julia" is not semantically similar to a plot summary of Chef, even if Chef is a great recommendation.

2. No preference signal. Plot summaries describe what a movie is about, not what kind of user would like it. Two movies with very similar plots can appeal to opposite audiences (a serious war drama and a war satire, for example).

3. Retrieves more of what's mentioned, not what's liked. RAG latches onto keywords in the query and surfaces documents mentioning those keywords. But the user already mentioned those items. What they need is recommendations for items they haven't mentioned and plot-based RAG can't bridge that gap.

CRAVE's retrieval is different at every level: (a) it retrieves training conversations, not plot descriptions; (b) it retrieves based on preference similarity, not text similarity; (c) it returns distilled lessons from reflection, not raw document text. Every one of these is a distinct architectural choice, and the ablations show each matters.

∫ Show the math · complexity and cost analysis▸

Let $N$ = number of training conversations ($\sim$100k-186k), $|\mathcal{I}|$ = catalog size ($\sim$8k-20k movies), $d$ = retriever embedding dimension ($1024$ for Stella-400M), $K$ = neighbours retrieved at test time ($\sim$5-10), $N_A$ = number of agents in DCA ($3$: COT + Debater + Critic).

Offline phase experience bank construction:

$$\text{Total API calls} = N \cdot N_A \cdot 2 \;\; (\text{trajectory} + \text{reflection per agent})$$

For Redial ($N=186k$, $N_A=3$): $\approx 1.1$M LLM calls. At a representative frontier-model rate of $\sim\$0.003$ per call for short prompts at late-2025 pricing, this is roughly $\sim\$3{,}300$ one-time offline cost a number that moves substantially with model choice, prompt length, and vendor pricing. Parallelized at 100 concurrent requests, the job typically completes in several hours of wall-clock time.

Offline phase retriever fine-tuning:

$$\text{Cost per step} = O(d \cdot |\mathcal{I}|) \;\; \text{for softmax over catalog}$$

$$\text{Total} = O(\text{epochs} \cdot N \cdot d \cdot |\mathcal{I}|) = O(7 \cdot 186\text{k} \cdot 1024 \cdot 8\text{k}) \approx 10^{13} \text{ ops}$$

On a single A10G GPU: $\sim$2-3 hours. Dominant cost is the softmax over $|\mathcal{I}|$. For larger catalogs ($|\mathcal{I}| > 10^6$), sampled softmax or hierarchical softmax is mandatory.

Online phase per-query inference:

$$\text{Retrieval: } O(d \cdot \sqrt{N}) \text{ with FAISS IVF} \approx 50\text{ms CPU}$$

$$\text{Prompt construction: } O(K \cdot L_{\text{exp}}) \;\; (L_{\text{exp}} \approx 200 \text{ tokens per lesson})$$

$$\text{LLM calls: } N_A \cdot (T_{\text{context}} + T_{\text{output}}) / T_{\text{sec}}$$

For CRAVE-COT ($N_A=1$): latency in the low seconds, per-query cost in the single-digit cents range. For CRAVE-DCA ($N_A=3$): roughly 3x that on both axes. All of these are illustrative deployment estimates at late-2025 frontier-model pricing; the exact numbers depend on model choice, average context length, caching, and how aggressively you compress retrieved experiences into the prompt. Dominant cost is LLM token throughput; retrieval and indexing are negligible.

Storage:

$$\text{Embeddings: } N \cdot d \cdot 4\text{ bytes} = 186\text{k} \cdot 1024 \cdot 4 \approx 760\text{ MB}$$

$$\text{Experience banks: } N \cdot N_A \cdot L_{\text{exp}} \cdot 4\text{ bytes} \approx 450\text{ MB}$$

Total $\sim$1.2 GB for a 186k-conversation deployment fits comfortably in RAM on most modern application servers.

The verdict: offline costs are real but one-time ($\sim\$3,500 + GPU hours). Online inference is dominated by LLM cost, not CRAVE's retrieval overhead which means swapping GPT-4o for a cheaper model (Claude Haiku, GPT-4o-mini) drops per-query cost proportionally without touching CRAVE's architecture.

The paper · Zhu et al. 2025, explained end-to-end
📄
What you're about to read. Six sections mapped to the paper's structure: the black-box fine-tuning problem → conversational recommendation agent architecture → verbalized experience collection via reflection → collaborative retrieval with multinomial likelihood → experience-augmented debate → empirical evaluation on Redial and Reddit-v2.
1

Problem formulation: one-shot CRS with black-box LLMs

Each conversation is a sequence $\{(u_t, s_t, I_t)\}_{t=1}^T$ where at turn $t$, speaker $u_t$ (User or System) says utterance $s_t$ and mentions item set $I_t$. When the last turn is System, the groundtruth items $I_T^{gt}$ are what the user ultimately expressed positive feedback for. Training set: $C_{\text{train}} = \{c_1, \dots, c_{N_{\text{train}}}\}$. Goal: given a test conversation $c^{te}$, produce a ranked recommendation list $\hat{I}^{te}$ that maximises overlap with groundtruth using only natural language interaction with the LLM (no gradient updates, no weight access).

2

Agent architecture: COT alone vs Debater-Critic system

The paper defines a conversational recommendation agent as a set $\mathcal{A} = \{A_1, \dots, A_{N_A}\}$ of LLM-based agents, each with a policy $\pi_i(a \mid c, r_{-i})$ that takes the conversation $c$ plus earlier agents' responses $r_{-i}$ and produces an action (reasoning + recommendations). Two configurations:

• COT agent: $\mathcal{A} = \{A_{\text{cot}}\}$, a single chain-of-thought reasoner. Efficient but prone to convergent thinking.
• Debater-Critic (DCA): one COT agent opens the debate, a Debater agent evaluates and counter-proposes, a Critic agent judges both and produces the final ranking. For efficiency the paper uses one Debater + one Critic (two-debater, one-round).

3

Trajectory sampling + experience reflection

For each training conversation $c_j$ and each agent $A_i$, sample one trajectory $r_{j,i}$ (prompting the LLM with task instructions $T_i^f$ and format instructions $F_i^f$). Then run a reflection step: prompt the same LLM with $(c_j, r_{j,i}, I_j^{gt})$ and task-specific reflection prompt $T_i^b$, asking it to (a) judge success/failure based on overlap with groundtruth, (b) explain why it succeeded or failed, and (c) summarize a general guideline for similar conversations. That guideline is the verbalized experience $e_{j,i}$.

Each agent role gets its own experience bank: $E_i = \{e_{j,i}\}_{j=1}^{N_{\text{train}}}$. The Debater bank contains guidelines for debating (how to find flaws in the first reasoner); the Critic bank contains guidelines for critiquing (how to weight competing recommendations). Role-specificity is critical you can't just share one bank across agents.

4

Collaborative retrieval network: BERT fine-tuned with multinomial likelihood

Backbone: 400M-parameter Stella Sentence-BERT. Fine-tuning objective: maximize the likelihood of groundtruth items under the content-parameterized multinomial $I_j \sim \text{Multi}(\text{softmax}(W^\top \Psi(c_j)), |I_j^{gt}|)$, where $W = \Psi(\mathcal{T})$ is the stacked content embedding matrix for all catalog items. Training: learning rate 1e-5, 7 epochs, monitor normalized groundtruth overlap on validation set. The best checkpoint $\hat{\Psi}$ is saved.

At retrieval time, for a test conversation $c^{te}$, compute $\hat{\Psi}(c^{te})$ and cosine-search against the pre-encoded training set. Return the top-$K$ conversations (by learned preference similarity), then look up the corresponding experiences from each agent's bank.

5

Experience-augmented generation at test time

For the COT path: $r_{\text{cot}}^{te} \sim \Phi(T_{\text{cot}}^{fe}, F_{\text{cot}}^{fe}, c^{te}, e_{\text{cot}}^{te})$ the LLM sees the test conversation plus the top-$K$ retrieved COT experiences embedded in the prompt as guidelines, then produces reasoning + recommendations.

For the DCA path: the COT agent goes first (with its retrieved COT experiences), then the Debater receives $(c^{te}, r_{\text{cot}}^{te}, e_{\text{deb}}^{te})$ and produces counter-reasoning, then the Critic receives everything plus its own experiences and scores each recommended movie on a scale of $[-2, +2]$. Final ranking is produced by sorting on the Critic's scores.

6

Evaluation: two datasets, five baselines, one decisive result

Datasets: Redial (186k train / 11k test rounds, 8k movies) and Reddit-v2 (109k train / 7k test, 20k movies). Both are movie-recommendation dialogue datasets where two people play user and system.

Metrics: Recall@{10,15,20}, NDCG@{10,15,20}, plus content-diversity and collaborative-diversity for the ablations.

Baselines: Redial (RNN+autoencoder), KBRD (KG+GNN), KGSF (ConceptNet), UniCRS (DialoGPT), Zero-shot GPT-4o, RAG (plot-based), NBCRS (neighborhood-based CF), GlobalExp (MemoCRS rules).

📌

The headline finding, stated plainly

Verbal reinforcement learning reflection → natural-language lesson → preference-aware retrieval lets a closed-model LLM learn from its own past without any weight updates. On movie CRS, this is worth ~7 points of Recall@10 over the strongest zero-shot baseline. The debater-critic architecture nearly doubles that gain when paired with role-specific experience banks. Netflix is on the author list because this is genuinely deployable.

Researcher · what the paper doesn't say, and what would happen in production
Pitfalls in production · four ways CRAVE breaks in the wild
Pitfall 1 · Cold-start user
A brand-new user has no preference history anywhere
scenario: user says "surprise me with anything"
CRAVE's retriever finds similar conversations, not similar users so a user with no prior turns isn't worse off than one with many. But a vague opener like "what should I watch?" has no preference signal for the retriever to latch onto, so it collapses toward the mean conversation in the training set, retrieving generic experiences. Mitigation: detect low-signal queries (short, empty of item mentions) and fall back to a popularity-biased prior or an active-learning turn that asks "what's a movie you loved recently?" before proceeding with CRAVE.
Pitfall 2 · Cross-domain transfer
Experience banks don't generalize beyond their training domain
scenario: trained on movies, asked about music or books
The experiences are lessons about movie CRS conversations "when the user mentions cozy rainy-day vibes, prioritize warmth over drama." Applying them to a music domain produces category errors (a cozy-rainy-day music taste favors different signals entirely). The collaborative retriever also learned content embeddings specific to the movie catalog. Mitigation: train one CRAVE per vertical. Netflix already ships domain-specific recommenders; this maps cleanly. Don't try to build "universal CRAVE."
Pitfall 3 · Long-tail item drought
Items never seen during reflection can't be recommended with confidence
scenario: brand-new release with no training coverage
The LLM recommends tokens, not IDs so it can name any movie, including ones not in the catalog or not in the training corpus. But the experiences that would guide recommending niche items don't exist, because nobody in the training set mentioned them. The LLM defaults to popular titles that do have reflection coverage. Mitigation: either (a) periodically re-generate experience banks as new content enters the catalog, or (b) use CRAVE as a candidate generator and layer content-based retrieval on top to surface cold items with similar metadata.
Pitfall 4 · Reflection-quality dependence
Bad reflections pollute the bank for a long time
scenario: an idiosyncratic conversation produces a spurious "rule"
If the LLM over-generalizes during reflection say, from a single conversation where the user liked three specific indie films, it writes a lesson "always recommend indie films for users mentioning any foreign director" that lesson sits in the bank and gets retrieved for every query that trips its pattern. The paper doesn't quantify this noise rate. Mitigation: gate reflection quality via a second LLM pass that scores each experience for generality and specificity, and purge low-quality lessons. Long-term: human-in-the-loop curation of a core experience subset.
Why is the test set so small (a few thousand conversations) compared to the training set?▸
Because each test query requires 3 LLM calls (COT + Debater + Critic) plus retrieval at GPT-4o pricing, full-set evaluation on 11k test rounds costs ~$500-1000. The paper's split reflects API cost constraints, not scientific preference.

Redial's original test set is 11,564 turns; Reddit-v2 is 7,565. The paper splits these into validation (for picking $K$) and test, so the final evaluation is on a few thousand conversations. With DCA requiring 3 generations per query plus retrieval tokens, and input contexts of ~2000+ tokens after experience augmentation, a full evaluation run easily crosses $500 in API spend per configuration.

This has two implications. First, while the reported Recall@10 confidence intervals are tighter than evaluations on tiny test sets, they're still not huge differences of 1-2pp between methods may not be statistically significant. Second, the paper's comparisons are done on identical test samples for all methods, so relative rankings are trustworthy even if absolute numbers could shift with a larger sample.

For production, the right follow-up is a staged eval: run a cheap proxy metric (retrieval quality alone, without the full agent pipeline) on a much larger held-out set, and only spend on full DCA evaluation for the top-2 configurations.

Is the collaborative retriever actually necessary, or could we just use a well-prompted LLM as the retriever?▸
The CRAVE-noFT ablation (pretrained Stella, no fine-tuning) drops ~3pp. The CRAVE-noMLT ablation (fine-tune on overlap only) drops even more. Both show: fine-tuning on preference signal is not optional. LLM-as-retriever wasn't tested but would face the same semantic-gap problem, plus cost 100× more per query.

The paper's implicit argument: a dedicated small-model retriever that's been fine-tuned for preference similarity is both cheaper (Stella-400M at ~100ms CPU vs GPT-4o at ~2000ms + API cost) and more accurate for this specific task. An LLM asked to rank training conversations by preference similarity would have to re-derive the preference-similarity notion in context every single query, which is wasteful.

There's a more interesting hybrid the paper doesn't explore: use the LLM to supervise the retriever, by generating soft labels ("how preference-similar are these two conversations?") as extra training signal. This could fine-tune Stella further without needing explicit groundtruth overlap, and would address the cold-start item problem.

Why does the paper use GPT-4o for both the agent and the reflection, and what breaks if you use a weaker model?▸
CRAVE requires the LLM to (a) reason about movie preferences from free-text conversations and (b) reflect on its own output with calibrated self-criticism. Both are emergent capabilities. GPT-4o has them; GPT-3.5 mostly doesn't. The paper's Limitations section explicitly flags this.

The reflection step is the hard one. It requires the LLM to (1) identify why its own recommendation missed the groundtruth, (2) resist the temptation to write a reflection that just parrots the groundtruth ("next time recommend The Hobbit!" useless as a general rule), and (3) extract a generalizable pattern that transfers to other conversations. Smaller models systematically fail at step 3, producing either trivially-specific rules ("recommend The Hobbit when the user mentions Rings") or empty generalities ("consider user preferences carefully").

Practically: the paper's approach only works with frontier models. If your budget or latency constraints force a smaller LLM, either (a) generate experiences with a frontier model offline (one-time cost) and deploy with a cheaper model at inference, or (b) use human-curated experiences instead of generated ones. Option (a) is actually what most production systems should do the reflection step doesn't need to run live.

How robust is CRAVE to dataset shift what happens when movie tastes change over time?▸
Not measured. The Redial dataset is from 2018, Reddit-v2 from ~2023. The paper doesn't test on 2025 conversations against 2018 experience banks. Extrapolating from general recsys experience: performance degrades noticeably within 12-24 months without re-training.

Two sources of drift matter: (1) item drift new movies appear, old ones lose relevance; (2) taste drift what "a warm cozy movie" meant in 2018 (maybe The Holiday) vs 2025 (maybe Past Lives). CRAVE's experiences are time-frozen at training; drift on either axis erodes their relevance.

The good news: re-training is cheap relative to the original effort. Generating experiences on 100k conversations takes ~$300-500 of GPT-4o calls. If you have continuous streams of new conversation data, you can quarterly-refresh the experience bank and the retriever without touching the LLM itself. This is a genuine operational advantage over full model re-training.

Why this paper might overstate its case
Limitation: DCA's gain over COT is smaller than the headline suggests
The paper highlights CRAVE-DCA as the best configuration, but the delta between CRAVE-COT and CRAVE-DCA is only ~3pp Recall@10 while DCA costs 3× the API spend (three LLM calls per query vs one). At production scale this is a meaningful trade-off the paper doesn't quantify. Many real deployments would pick COT as the operating point.
Limitation: all evaluation is on English-language, movie-domain, Reddit/OTT-style conversations
No multilingual test, no non-movie domains, no adversarial users (sarcasm, low-effort queries, off-topic). The method's robustness to these is untested. Netflix's internal conversations probably look more controlled than Reddit comments; production deployments at other companies may face messier inputs.
Limitation: the "collaborative" terminology overlaps awkwardly with classical collaborative filtering
"Collaborative verbalized experience" and "collaborative retrieval" don't mean collaborative filtering in the traditional sense they mean preference-aware retrieval. This naming collision will cause confusion for anyone coming from classical recsys. A cleaner framing would be "preference-aware experience retrieval".
Reality check · when CRAVE is the right tool and when it isn't
When CRAVE wins
You have tens of thousands of historical conversations with groundtruth feedback signals
Your backbone is a closed-model LLM API (GPT-4o, Claude, Gemini) gradient fine-tuning is impossible
Your domain is closed-catalogue (movies, books, products) with stable item content
Your users produce meaningful preference signal in conversations (not just "yes/no" feedback)
You can afford 3× inference cost for DCA, or stay with CRAVE-COT for single-LLM-call budgets
You're building a CRS-focused product, not retrofitting recommendations onto an existing search UX
When CRAVE hurts
Brand-new product with fewer than ~10k historical conversations experience banks too sparse
You need sub-500ms latency per query DCA's 3 LLM calls break the budget
Your domain is highly dynamic (news, live events) where 2018 lessons become stale fast
Your users are non-English the paper has no multilingual evaluation
You need interpretable recommendations CRAVE's lessons-in-a-prompt flow obscures the "why"
Your backbone is a small open-weights LLM (7B-13B) reflection quality degrades significantly

The shipping decision in one line

If you have >50k historical conversations with clean feedback signal and you're committed to an LLM-based CRS surface, ship CRAVE-COT as your default and A/B test DCA on your premium tier. Build it · five production steps from paper to deployed CRS
1
Collect conversations with clean groundtruth labels
You need (conversation, groundtruth_items) pairs ideally 50k+ for stable experience banks. Sources: chatbot transcripts with subsequent purchase/play signals, explicit user feedback on recommendations, structured A/B-tested flows. Minimum 1 groundtruth item per conversation. Filter out conversations shorter than 3 turns (not enough preference signal) and those with 15+ groundtruth items (preference too diffuse to generalize from).
2
Generate experience banks via reflection (one-time, offline)
For each agent role (COT, Debater, Critic), iterate over training conversations. Call GPT-4o with the reflection prompt: (conversation, agent_output, groundtruth_items) → lesson. Store each lesson as a row in an experience-bank table with the conversation ID. This is a one-time batch job; at ~$0.003/reflection and 50k conversations × 3 agents, budget ~$500. Cache aggressively if you regenerate, only do it for conversations added since last run.
3
Fine-tune the collaborative retriever with multinomial likelihood
Start from Stella-400M or all-mpnet-base-v2. Build item content matrix $W$ by encoding each item's title + description. Train with the multinomial likelihood objective: conversation embedding $\Psi(c_j)$ projects onto $W$, softmax, cross-entropy against the groundtruth item distribution. Learning rate 1e-5, 5-7 epochs, monitor validation groundtruth overlap. Save the best checkpoint.
4
Build the retrieval index and inference service
Encode all training conversations with the fine-tuned retriever, push to FAISS or pgvector. At query time: embed the incoming conversation, retrieve top-K=5-10 preference-neighbours, look up corresponding experiences from each agent's bank. Inject as guidelines into each agent's prompt. Target latency: ~200ms for retrieval, leaving budget for LLM calls. Keep the experience bank in Postgres keyed by conversation ID.
5
Deploy with tiered routing CRAVE-COT default, DCA for premium
Route free-tier or high-volume queries through CRAVE-COT (single LLM call, latency in the low seconds). Route premium-tier or high-stakes queries (first-week onboarding, win-back flows) through CRAVE-DCA (three LLM calls, roughly 3x the latency and per-query cost). Instrument Recall/NDCG via implicit feedback (did the user play / purchase / click through in the next 24h?). Refresh the experience bank quarterly as new conversations accumulate. Exact cost-per-query is model- and prompt-dependent; treat any concrete numbers as illustrative and confirm with your own load tests.
Research idea
Replace the one-shot reflection with iterative reflection: sample multiple agent trajectories per training conversation (temperature variation), reflect on each, and let a second LLM pass deduplicate and rank the resulting lessons by generality. Expected: higher-quality experience banks with less noise. Paper-sized contribution.
Startup idea
A conversation-learning platform for e-commerce chatbots: bring-your-own-catalog and conversation logs, get back a deployed CRS with fine-tuned retriever and generated experience banks. Target: Shopify merchants running Gorgias/Intercom chatbots who want recommendation quality without building an ML team. Monetize as SaaS $0.01 per conversation routed through their chatbot.
1-line improvement
Replace the critic's {-2,-1,0,1,2} scoring scale with pairwise preference: for each pair (Debater A rec, Debater B rec), ask the critic "which should appear higher?" and aggregate via Bradley-Terry. Removes the scale-calibration problem where critics vary in their use of the range. Expected: +2-3% NDCG.

3Pseudocode · the full CRAVE pipeline
pseudocode · algorithm
Algorithm CRAVE-Pipeline(train_convs, test_conv) # ═══ OFFLINE ═══ # Step 1: sample trajectories for each agent role trajectories ← {} for c_j in train_convs: trajectories[j, "cot"] ← LLM.ask(cot_prompt, c_j) trajectories[j, "deb"] ← LLM.ask(debater_prompt, c_j, trajectories[j,"cot"]) trajectories[j, "cri"] ← LLM.ask(critic_prompt, c_j, trajectories[j,"cot"], trajectories[j,"deb"]) # Step 2: reflect on each trajectory, build experience banks experience_bank ← {"cot": {}, "deb": {}, "cri": {}} for c_j in train_convs: for role in ["cot", "deb", "cri"]: lesson ← LLM.ask(reflection_prompt[role], c_j, trajectories[j, role], c_j.groundtruth_items) experience_bank[role][j] ← lesson # Step 3: fine-tune retriever with multinomial likelihood W ← BERT.encode([item.description for item in catalog]) # content matrix for epoch in range(7): for c_j, gt_items in train_convs: q_emb ← BERT.encode(c_j) logits ← q_emb @ W.T # [|I|] probs ← softmax(logits) loss ← -sum(log(probs[k]) for k in gt_items) loss.backward(); optimizer.step() # Step 4: build retrieval index train_embs ← BERT.encode([c.text for c in train_convs]) index ← FAISS.build(train_embs) # ═══ ONLINE (per test query) ═══ q_emb ← BERT.encode(test_conv) neighbors ← index.search(q_emb, k=5) # top-K preference-similar training convs retrieved_exp ← { "cot": [experience_bank["cot"][n] for n in neighbors], "deb": [experience_bank["deb"][n] for n in neighbors], "cri": [experience_bank["cri"][n] for n in neighbors], } # Debater-critic inference with augmented experiences cot_rec ← LLM.ask(cot_prompt_augmented, test_conv, retrieved_exp["cot"]) deb_rec ← LLM.ask(deb_prompt_augmented, test_conv, cot_rec, retrieved_exp["deb"]) final ← LLM.ask(cri_prompt_augmented, test_conv, cot_rec, deb_rec, retrieved_exp["cri"]) return parse_ranked_list(final)
4Working Python · runnable skeleton
python · crave_pipeline.py
import json, os, sqlite3 import numpy as np import torch from openai import OpenAI from sentence_transformers import SentenceTransformer from datasets import load_from_disk import faiss client = OpenAI() retriever = SentenceTransformer("dunzhang/stella_en_400M_v5", trust_remote_code=True) # ─── 1. Prompts (simplified; see Appendix A of the paper) ── COT_PROMPT = """Pretend you are a movie recommender. User query: {conv}. Reason, then output 20 recommendations after ####.""" REFLECT_COT = """You are evaluating a movie recommender. Conversation: {conv} Recommender output: {output} Ground truth (what user actually liked): {gt} Analyze whether the recommendation succeeded. Then summarize a general guideline for similar future conversations.""" # ─── 2. Sample trajectories + reflect for each training conv ── def build_experience_bank(train_convs, db_path="exp.db"): conn = sqlite3.connect(db_path) conn.execute("CREATE TABLE IF NOT EXISTS exp(conv_id, role, lesson)") for c in train_convs: # Sample COT trajectory cot_out = client.chat.completions.create( model="gpt-4o", messages=[{"role": "user", "content": COT_PROMPT.format(conv=c.text)}] ).choices[0].message.content # Reflect reflection = client.chat.completions.create( model="gpt-4o", messages=[{"role": "user", "content": REFLECT_COT.format( conv=c.text, output=cot_out, gt=c.groundtruth )}] ).choices[0].message.content conn.execute("INSERT INTO exp VALUES (?, ?, ?)", (c.id, "cot", reflection)) conn.commit() # ─── 3. Fine-tune retriever with multinomial likelihood ── def finetune_retriever(train_convs, catalog_items, epochs=7, lr=1e-5): # Pre-encode catalog content matrix W W = retriever.encode([it.title for it in catalog_items], convert_to_tensor=True, normalize_embeddings=True) W_t = W.T # [d, |I|] opt = torch.optim.AdamW(retriever.parameters(), lr=lr) for epoch in range(epochs): for c in train_convs: q = retriever.encode(c.text, convert_to_tensor=True, normalize_embeddings=True) logits = q @ W_t # [|I|] log_probs = torch.log_softmax(logits, dim=-1) gt_idx = torch.tensor([catalog_items.index_of(k) for k in c.groundtruth]) loss = -log_probs[gt_idx].sum() opt.zero_grad(); loss.backward(); opt.step() print(f"epoch {epoch}: loss={loss.item():.3f}") # ─── 4. Build retrieval index ────────────────────────────── def build_index(train_convs): embs = retriever.encode([c.text for c in train_convs], normalize_embeddings=True) idx = faiss.IndexFlatIP(embs.shape[1]) idx.add(embs.astype("float32")) return idx, embs # ─── 5. Inference: retrieve + debate + critique ──────────── def crave_recommend(test_conv, index, train_convs, conn, k=5): q = retriever.encode([test_conv.text], normalize_embeddings=True).astype("float32") _, neighbors = index.search(q, k) # Lookup experiences exps_cot = [conn.execute("SELECT lesson FROM exp WHERE conv_id=? AND role='cot'", (train_convs[n].id,)).fetchone()[0] for n in neighbors[0]] # Augment COT prompt with retrieved experiences augmented = COT_PROMPT.format(conv=test_conv.text) + "\n\nGuidelines from past conversations:\n" + "\n".join(f"- {e}" for e in exps_cot) cot_rec = client.chat.completions.create( model="gpt-4o", messages=[{"role": "user", "content": augmented}] ).choices[0].message.content # (Similar for Debater and Critic see repo for full DCA implementation) return parse_recommendations(cot_rec)
5Evaluation · reproduce the paper's numbers
python · eval.py
def recall_at_k(pred, truth, k=10): pred_k = set(pred[:k]) return len(pred_k & set(truth)) / len(truth) if truth else 0 def ndcg_at_k(pred, truth, k=10): gains = [1 if p in truth else 0 for p in pred[:k]] dcg = sum(g / np.log2(i+2) for i, g in enumerate(gains)) idcg = sum(1 / np.log2(i+2) for i in range(min(k, len(truth)))) return dcg / idcg if idcg else 0 # Run eval on the held-out test set recalls, ndcgs = [], [] for tc in test_convs: pred = crave_recommend(tc, index, train_convs, conn, k=5) recalls.append(recall_at_k(pred, tc.groundtruth, 10)) ndcgs.append(ndcg_at_k(pred, tc.groundtruth, 10)) print(f"Recall@10: {np.mean(recalls):.4f}") # expect ~0.22 (COT) or ~0.25 (DCA) print(f"NDCG@10: {np.mean(ndcgs):.4f}") # expect ~0.11 (COT) or ~0.12 (DCA)

Retriever fine-tuning: ~2 hours on a single A10G GPU for 7 epochs on 100k conversations. Question 1 of 8 · Core motivation

Why does CRAVE rely on verbal reinforcement learning rather than simply fine-tuning the LLM on historical conversations?

The key insight: closed-model APIs are the strongest CRS baseline, and you literally cannot fine-tune them. Verbal RL turns "you can't update weights" from a limitation into an architectural feature the learning happens entirely in a retrievable text bank.
Question 2 of 8 · Retrieval objective

The collaborative retriever is fine-tuned with "item content-parameterized multinomial likelihood". What does this objective optimize for?

The ablation CRAVE-noMLT confirms this: fine-tuning on raw overlap (option D) actually hurts compared to no fine-tuning. The content parameterization is what makes retrieval transfer across lexically-different but preference-similar conversations.
Question 3 of 8 · Debater-Critic necessity

Why does zero-shot DCA underperform zero-shot COT on Redial, but CRAVE-DCA outperform CRAVE-COT?

The subtle takeaway: temperature sampling produces noise, not diversity. Role-specific experience banks produce genuine diversity by giving each agent a different learned prior.
Question 4 of 8 · Baseline comparison

Why does plain RAG (retrieving movie plots) barely outperform zero-shot GPT-4o, while CRAVE substantially improves on both?

Architectural lesson: "retrieval-augmented generation" is not one thing the unit of retrieval, the similarity metric, and the post-processing of retrieved content are all separate design choices that compound.
Question 5 of 8 · Practical trade-off

At production scale, you're choosing between CRAVE-COT (single LLM call per query, latency in the low seconds) and CRAVE-DCA (three LLM calls per query roughly 3x the cost and latency, for +3pp Recall@10). What's the right operating point for most companies?

Tiered routing matters: production systems rarely deploy one configuration for every query. The right architectural move is to segment queries by value and route accordingly DCA for moments when each recommendation counts, COT for background surfaces.
Question 6 of 8 · Limitation awareness

Which of these failure modes is most likely to cause CRAVE to silently degrade in production over 12-24 months?

The operational reality: experience banks are not "train once, serve forever." They need quarterly regeneration as both catalog and user taste shift. Budget this into your production plan upfront.
Question 7 of 8 · Architecture design

You're extending CRAVE to a travel-booking CRS (Airbnb-style). What's the highest-leverage adaptation to make?

Catalog scale matters: CRAVE's multinomial likelihood is O(|I|) per conversation during fine-tuning. At 10M items it's still tractable; at 100M+ SKUs (Amazon-scale) you need hierarchical or sampled softmax. Sharding by category is the standard approach.
Question 8 of 8 · Transferable principle

Strip away the CRS domain. What's the most important general lesson for building LLM agent systems that learn from experience?

The recipe generalizes: this three-step pattern (reflect → distill → retrieve by task-relevant similarity) is the template for every domain where you have feedback data but can't fine-tune the backbone. CRS is one application; the architecture is the contribution.
