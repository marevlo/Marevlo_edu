Research Paper Breakdown

Multi-Agent Teams Hold Experts Back

AuthorsPappu · El · Cao · di Nolfo · Sun · Cao · Zou
Year2026
VenuearXiv preprint, Feb 2026
InstitutionStanford University

The assumption behind many multi-agent systems: more agents = better results. This paper tests that assumption for self-organising LLM teams where agents discuss freely rather than following fixed roles and finds it's wrong. Such teams consistently fail to match their best member's solo performance, losing up to 37.6% in the worst case (typical 10-25%) even when explicitly told who the expert is. The culprit: integrative compromise teams average expert and non-expert views rather than appropriately weighting expertise. Larger teams make it worse. The one silver lining: this consensus-seeking behaviour does improve robustness to isolated adversarial agents (coordinated attacks break the dilution argument). A trade-off between adversarial robustness and effective expertise utilisation that every multi-agent engineer needs to understand though structured architectures (ORCH, ROMA, LangGraph) sidestep the failure mode by design.

2-minute brain download · the core finding
The finding
Self-organising LLM teams where agents freely discuss rather than following fixed roles underperform their best individual member by 8-38% across every benchmark tested. Even when explicitly told "Agent X is the expert on this topic," teams still dilute that expertise through compromise. More undirected agents, worse results the opposite of the multi-agent promise, though structured architectures (ORCH, ROMA) sidestep this.
The mechanism
Integrative compromise. LLM agents exhibit a strong consensus-seeking tendency they average expert and non-expert views rather than appropriately weighting expertise. Conversational analysis shows agents move toward the group mean, not toward the expert's position. This gets worse with more agents: larger teams → more compromise → more expertise dilution.
The trade-off
The same consensus-seeking that destroys expertise utilisation also makes teams robust to adversarial agents. A bad-faith agent pushing wrong answers gets averaged out. So there's a genuine alignment-vs-expertise trade-off. Production systems must choose: guard against adversaries (team) or maximise accuracy (route to the expert).
One-line intuition When you ask 5 people a question and one of them is an expert, the expert's answer gets diluted by the other four even when everyone knows who the expert is. LLMs do this systematically because they're trained to be agreeable, producing compromise where they should defer.

Deep dive · four questions the paper forces you to reconstruct
Why can't LLMs simply defer to the expert even when told who the expert is? ▸
RLHF training rewards being agreeable and helpful, not deferring to authority. LLMs are "epistemic egalitarians" they weight all input equally.

The paper tests three information conditions: (1) No information agents don't know who's expert; (2) Revealed expertise agents are told "Agent X is the expert on this topic"; (3) Revealed expertise + instruction to defer. Even condition (3) fails. Why?

RLHF-trained models are optimised for agreement and helpfulness, not for hierarchical deference. When Agent X says "the answer is 42" and Agent Y says "I think it's 50," the model's instinct is to find middle ground ("perhaps 46?") rather than recognise that X's answer should dominate. This is the integrative compromise pattern. It's deeply embedded in the training signal.

The paper also finds this gets worse with conversational rounds. More discussion → more opportunities for compromise → greater drift from the expert's original answer. The expert's strong initial signal gets progressively diluted through each round of "collaborative" discussion.

∫ Show the math · consensus as weighted averaging and why the expert weight collapses▸

Idealise each agent $i$'s position after round $t$ as a point $x_i^{(t)} \in \mathbb{R}^d$ in an answer space (numeric answer, one-hot over options, or an embedding of a free-text response). Let there be $n$ agents, one of whom is the expert $E$.

In a truly deferential team, the group's round-$(t+1)$ position should weight the expert heavily:

$$x_{\text{team}}^{(t+1)} \;=\; \lambda_E \cdot x_E^{(t)} \;+\; \frac{1 - \lambda_E}{n-1} \sum_{j \neq E} x_j^{(t)} \qquad \text{with } \lambda_E \to 1 \text{ (ideal)}$$

What the paper observes empirically. LLM teams behave as if $\lambda_E \approx 1/n$ i.e. the expert's position gets the same weight as every other agent's. Equivalently, the team's position is roughly the simple average:

$$x_{\text{team}}^{(t+1)} \;\approx\; \frac{1}{n} \sum_i x_i^{(t)}$$

Why this loses the expert. If the expert's answer is correct with probability $p_E$, and non-experts are each correct with probability $p_N < p_E$, the expected accuracy of the simple average on a binary task is bounded above by:

$$\mathbb{E}[\text{accuracy}_{\text{team}}] \;\leq\; \frac{p_E + (n-1) p_N}{n}$$

For $n = 3$, $p_E = 0.9$, $p_N = 0.5$: team accuracy $\leq 0.63$ the expert alone would have been $0.9$. That is, the paper's central claim formalised.

Why discussion makes it worse. Across $T$ rounds, each round is another application of this averaging operator. Let $L$ be the averaging matrix where $L_{ij} = 1/n$ for all $i,j$. The expert's influence decays as:

$$x^{(T)} \;=\; L^T x^{(0)} \;=\; \bar{x}^{(0)} \mathbf{1} \quad \text{for any } T \geq 1$$

Because $L$ is rank-1 with the uniform-average as its sole non-zero eigenvector, a single averaging step already collapses all agents to the mean. More rounds don't help the paper's "discussion hurts more" finding reflects that each round reinforces the consensus-seeking behaviour rather than correcting it.

The RLHF connection. Instruction-tuned LLMs are trained on human preferences that reward agreeable, helpful, bridge-building responses. The expected response to disagreement in training data is a middle-ground compromise, not "the expert is right, I defer." This training signal gets reproduced inside multi-agent teams $\lambda_E \to 1/n$ is the expected outcome, not a bug.

How did they measure "integrative compromise" in the conversations? ▸
Epistemic deference analysis measuring whether the team's final answer moved toward or away from the expert's initial position across discussion rounds.

The paper develops an "epistemic deference score": for each discussion, track the distance between the team's aggregate position and the expert's initial position. If deference is working, this distance should decrease across rounds (the team converges on the expert). What actually happens: the distance increases the team drifts away from the expert toward the group median.

They also do conversational coding: categorise each agent's turn as "agree," "disagree," "compromise," or "defer." They find a strong negative correlation between "compromise" frequency and team accuracy. The best-performing teams are those where non-experts defer early, but this almost never happens spontaneously. LLMs prefer to "add value" by proposing alternatives, which poisons the consensus.

∫ Show the math · the epistemic deference score formalised▸

Let $x_E^{(0)}$ be the expert's initial position and $x_{\text{team}}^{(t)}$ be the team's aggregate position at round $t$. The paper's epistemic deference score at round $t$ is:

$$D(t) \;=\; d\big(x_{\text{team}}^{(t)},\; x_E^{(0)}\big)$$

Where $d(\cdot, \cdot)$ is an appropriate distance (Euclidean for numeric, edit distance for ranked lists, cross-encoder similarity for free-form answers).

Expected behaviour if deference works. For a team that genuinely defers to the expert, $D$ should decrease monotonically as non-experts align:

$$D(0) \geq D(1) \geq D(2) \geq \dots \geq 0$$

In the limit, $D(\infty) = 0$ (team reaches the expert's answer).

What the paper finds empirically. $D(t)$ increases for LLM teams across rounds. The team drifts away from the expert's initial position:

$$D(t+1) > D(t) \text{ with high probability under the paper's measurements}$$

This is the observable signature of integrative compromise. Each round, the non-experts contribute their positions with equal weight to the team's next position; the expert's position gets diluted; the team's aggregate moves toward the group centroid, which is farther from the expert than the expert's starting point.

Turn-level categorisation. The paper codes each agent's conversational turn as one of four types. Let $\alpha_i^{(t)}$ denote the category of agent $i$'s turn at round $t$:

$$\alpha \in \{\text{agree}, \text{disagree}, \text{compromise}, \text{defer}\}$$

The team's empirical accuracy correlates with the distribution over $\alpha$:

$$\text{corr}\big(\mathbb{P}[\alpha = \text{compromise}],\; \text{accuracy}_{\text{team}}\big) \;<\; 0$$

The "compromise" pattern non-experts proposing alternatives that are neither the expert's answer nor pure disagreement is the single largest predictor of poor team performance. The best-performing teams have high $\mathbb{P}[\alpha = \text{defer}]$ among non-experts, but this happens rarely without explicit intervention.

Why LLMs prefer compromise to deference. Instruction-following training rewards responses that "add value." A non-expert agent that simply says "I agree with Agent X" provides no new tokens and, by the training signal, looks low-effort. An agent that proposes a nuanced middle-ground looks thoughtful and is rewarded. This is the reward-hacking connection to multi-agent failure: each individual agent's RLHF-optimal behaviour is collectively pathological.

What's the adversarial robustness finding, and why does it complicate things? ▸
Consensus-seeking averages out adversarial agents too. The same mechanism that dilutes expertise also dilutes attacks. A genuine trade-off.

When the authors inject an adversarial agent (one programmed to push deliberately wrong answers), the team's consensus-seeking tendency helps: the adversary's extreme position gets averaged toward the group mean, reducing its impact. A solo agent that's been adversarially prompted can be 100% wrong; within a team, the damage is diluted to ~20-30% degradation.

This means the multi-agent "weakness" (consensus) is simultaneously a "strength" (robustness). You can't fix the expertise dilution without also losing the adversarial protection. Production systems face a genuine design trade-off: route directly to the expert (max accuracy, vulnerable to compromised agents) or use team deliberation (lower accuracy, robust to attacks).

∫ Show the math · the adversarial-robustness / expertise-leverage trade-off formalised▸

Frame the problem as a weighted estimator of a true answer $x^*$. Each agent contributes a position $x_i = x^* + \epsilon_i$, where $\epsilon_i$ is their per-agent error term.

Expert-only estimator. Take the expert's answer directly. The estimator's error is:

$$\text{err}_{\text{expert-only}} \;=\; \mathbb{E}[\|\epsilon_E\|^2] \;=\; \sigma_E^2$$

Where $\sigma_E^2$ is the expert's expected squared error on the task small by assumption (they're the expert).

Team-average estimator. The simple mean over $n$ agents. Under independence:

$$\text{err}_{\text{team-avg}} \;=\; \mathbb{E}\bigg\|\frac{1}{n}\sum_i \epsilon_i\bigg\|^2 \;=\; \frac{1}{n^2}\sum_i \sigma_i^2$$

For agents with non-expert error $\sigma_N^2 > \sigma_E^2$, the team-average error exceeds the expert-only error:

$$\text{err}_{\text{team-avg}} \;=\; \frac{\sigma_E^2 + (n-1)\sigma_N^2}{n^2} \;>\; \frac{\sigma_E^2}{n^2} \cdot n \;=\; \frac{\sigma_E^2}{n} \quad \text{usually} > \sigma_E^2 \text{ when } \sigma_N \gg \sigma_E$$

Adversarial case. Now suppose one agent $A$ is compromised: their $\epsilon_A$ is not drawn from a zero-mean distribution but is adversarially pushed to $\epsilon_A = \beta \cdot v$ for some large $\beta$ and unit vector $v$ chosen by an adversary.

Expert-only: if the adversary is not the expert, $\text{err}_{\text{expert-only}}$ is unchanged ($\sigma_E^2$). If the adversary is the expert (fully compromised pipeline), error spikes to $\beta^2$.

Team-average: the adversarial contribution gets diluted. The team's bias from the adversary is $\beta v / n$, so error becomes:

$$\text{err}_{\text{team-avg}}^{\text{adv}} \;=\; \frac{\sigma_E^2 + (n-2)\sigma_N^2 + \beta^2/n}{n^2} + \text{lower-order terms}$$

Dramatically smaller than $\beta^2$. This is the robustness the paper documents.

The trade-off as a Pareto front. Plotting (expected error under benign conditions) against (worst-case error under adversarial attack), the expert-only strategy has lower benign error but higher adversarial-worst-case error. The team-average has higher benign error but bounded adversarial damage.

$$\text{expert-only}: \big(\,\sigma_E^2,\; \beta^2\,\big) \qquad \text{team-avg}: \bigg(\,\tfrac{\sigma_E^2 + (n-1)\sigma_N^2}{n^2},\; \tfrac{\beta^2}{n^2}\,\bigg)$$

Neither dominates this is a genuine Pareto trade-off. The paper's key insight is that production systems can't have both. Choose based on whether the threat model is dominated by capability (pick expert-only) or by adversarial compromise (pick team-avg).

Caveat. The trade-off assumes the team-average dilutes adversaries effectively. If an adversary can compromise multiple agents or coordinate across the team, the dilution argument weakens quickly. The paper doesn't systematically test coordinated adversaries that's an open question for the follow-on work.

Why test on NASA Moon Survival and Lost at Sea what do these classic tasks add? ▸
These tasks have been used in human-team research for 50+ years. Known baseline that human teams DO achieve synergy. Shows LLMs fail where humans succeed.

NASA Moon Survival and Lost at Sea are staples of organisational psychology research. The key property: human teams on these tasks reliably outperform their average member and sometimes even their best member (strong synergy). This is the canonical evidence that "two heads are better than one" for humans.

By replicating these exact tasks with LLM agents, the paper gets a clean comparison: on the same tasks where human teams achieve synergy, LLM teams fail to. This rules out "the tasks are too hard" or "the benchmarks are unfair" these are known-quantity tasks where collaboration should help. The fact that it doesn't is a fundamental indictment of current multi-agent design.

The ML benchmarks (MMLU Pro, GPQA, HLE, MATH-500, SimpleQA) then confirm the finding generalises to frontier AI tasks, not just psychology experiments.

∫ Show the math · weak vs strong synergy and what the 37.6% gap actually measures▸

The organizational-psychology literature distinguishes two notions of synergy that the paper inherits.

Weak synergy. Team performance exceeds the average of its members:

$$A_{\text{team}} \;>\; \frac{1}{n}\sum_i A_i \qquad \text{(weak synergy)}$$

This is a low bar it just means collaboration does something better than random picking.

Strong synergy. Team performance matches or exceeds the best individual member:

$$A_{\text{team}} \;\geq\; \max_i A_i \qquad \text{(strong synergy)}$$

This is what the paper measures and it's what actually matters: a team that beats its best member genuinely produces something no individual could have produced alone.

The 37.6% gap. The paper reports LLM teams underperform their best member by up to 37.6%. In the strong-synergy notation, this is:

$$\frac{\max_i A_i - A_{\text{team}}}{\max_i A_i} \;=\; 0.376 \quad (\text{worst case across benchmarks})$$

Equivalently, on some benchmark settings, the team achieves only $62.4\%$ of what the expert alone would have achieved. Not just "failed to gain" actively lost performance by being put in a team.

Contrast with human-team baseline. On the same classic tasks (NASA Moon Survival, Lost at Sea), the organizational-psych literature reports humans achieving strong synergy: $A_{\text{team}} \geq \max_i A_i$ with meaningful probability. LLM teams on these tasks fail to achieve even weak synergy consistently. The gap is not "LLM teams are slightly weaker than human teams" it's "LLM teams fail at what human teams succeed at."

What the bound doesn't say. The 37.6% is a maximum observed gap, not a tight theoretical bound. Actual gaps vary by task, model mix, number of rounds, and prompting. On some tasks the gap is close to zero (teams match the expert); on others it reaches the 37.6% cited in the abstract. Quoting this number as "LLM teams are 37.6% worse than experts" overstates the finding the accurate framing is "LLM teams fail to match their expert across a range of tasks, with losses up to 37.6% in the worst case and typically between 10-25%."

The deeper message. For production systems that route to multi-agent deliberation specifically to harness distributed expertise, the expectation is strong synergy. The paper's finding is that this expectation is systematically violated for current instruction-tuned LLMs. Either the multi-agent architecture needs major redesign (e.g. ORCH's rule-level aggregation rather than free-form discussion), or the training signal needs to incentivize deference (a much harder ask), or the production system should route directly to the expert and skip the team entirely when expertise is identifiable.

The paper · Pappu et al. 2026, explained end-to-end
📄
What you're about to read. Six sections: the synergy hypothesis → experimental design (psych tasks + ML benchmarks) → concentrated vs distributed expertise results → expertise dilution with team size → adversarial robustness → the compromise mechanism.
§1Do LLM teams achieve synergy?

The paper frames its question precisely using org-psych terminology. Strong synergy = team performance ≥ best individual member. Weak synergy = team performance ≥ average individual member. The hypothesis: if multi-agent systems are worth the overhead, they should achieve at least weak synergy, ideally strong.

For human teams, decades of research show both forms of synergy on structured tasks. The paper asks: do LLM teams show the same patterns when coordination is unconstrained no fixed roles, no scripted workflows, just "discuss and decide"?

The key distinction

Most multi-agent papers test systems with designed coordination (fixed roles, scripted pipelines). This paper deliberately removes all coordination scaffolding to study what happens when LLMs must self-organise the condition closest to real deployment of "autonomous collaborators."

§2Experimental design

Two complementary task families, chosen for different properties:

Human psychology tasks
NASA Moon Survival, Lost at Sea, Student Body President. Classic tasks where expertise can be explicitly manipulated (one agent gets the "expert ranking" information). Controlled conditions. Known human baselines showing synergy.
Frontier ML benchmarks
MMLU Pro, GPQA Diamond, HLE, MATH-500, SimpleQA. Expertise is naturally distributed across heterogeneous models (GPT-4, Claude, Gemini, etc.) and varies problem-by-problem. Realistic conditions.

Three information conditions across both: (1) no expertise info, (2) expertise revealed ("Agent X is the expert"), (3) expertise revealed + instruction to defer to the expert. The protocol: Phase 1 (individual answers) → Phase 2 (share answers) → Phase 3 (multi-round discussion) → Phase 4 (final team answer).

§3Results concentrated expertise

In concentrated-expertise settings (one agent has the expert information, others don't), the headline results are stark:

NASA Moon
Team performance worse than expert solo by 12-25% across conditions. Even when told who the expert is. Even when told to defer.
Lost at Sea
Same pattern. Expert solo outperforms every team configuration tested. Performance degrades with more discussion rounds the opposite of what you'd want.
Student Body Pres
Expert agent's ranking almost perfectly matches ground truth. Team's ranking drifts toward compromise "everyone's a bit right" averaging.
Core finding: Across all concentrated-expertise tasks and all information conditions, self-organising LLM teams consistently fail to achieve strong synergy. Teams lose 8-38% relative to the best individual member. Revealing the expert's identity helps slightly but does not close the gap.
§4Expertise dilution scales with team size

Section 4.3 tests team sizes from 2 to 8 agents. The finding is clean and monotonic: more agents → more expertise dilution → worse performance.

The paper measures "relative synergy gap" how much the team underperforms vs the expert. At 2 agents, the gap is ~8%. At 5 agents, ~28%. At 8 agents, up to 38%. The relationship is approximately linear in the log of team size.

Why larger teams are worse

Each additional non-expert agent contributes one more voice pulling toward compromise. In a team of 8, the expert has 1/8 of the "conversational weight" and since LLMs weight contributions roughly equally rather than by expertise, the expert's position is diluted by 7 competing opinions. This is a structural problem, not fixable by prompting: the discussion dynamics inherently amplify the group mean.

§5The adversarial robustness trade-off

Section 4.4 introduces adversarial agents team members programmed to push incorrect answers. Here the consensus-seeking tendency helps:

A solo agent receiving adversarial input can be fully compromised. Within a team, the adversary's extreme position gets averaged toward the group center. The team absorbs the adversary's influence through the same compromise mechanism that dilutes expertise.

⚖

The fundamental trade-off

You can't fix expertise dilution without losing adversarial robustness. They share the same mechanism: consensus-seeking via integrative compromise. Making teams defer more to experts (fixing dilution) makes them defer more to adversaries too (breaking robustness). This is the paper's deepest practical insight.

§6Why it happens conversational analysis

Section 4.5 analyses the actual conversations to identify the failure mechanism. The authors code each agent's turn and find:

Integrative compromise dominates: agents rarely say "I defer to the expert." Instead, they say "Building on everyone's input, let's try this middle ground." Each compromise round pulls the team further from the expert's position. The key metric: compromise frequency correlates negatively with team accuracy (r = −0.67 across configurations).

This traces to RLHF training: models are rewarded for being helpful, balanced, and considerate of all input. Deference feels "unhelpful." Compromise feels "constructive." But in expertise-weighted tasks, compromise is exactly wrong.

Think like a researcher · implications and limits
Pitfalls when applying this finding · four mistakes that compound
Pitfall 1 · Over-generalising the 37.6%
Quoting the worst-case gap as if it's typical
symptom: "multi-agent teams are 37% worse than solo experts" in an exec deck
The 37.6% is a maximum observed loss across the paper's benchmarks not the mean. Typical gaps range 10-25%, and on some tasks the team matches the expert. Reading this as a universal law ("teams always lose 37%") overstates the finding and undermines the argument when colleagues find counter-examples. Fix: cite the paper's core finding (failure of strong synergy across diverse benchmarks) rather than the max number. If you need a number, use "typical 10-25% loss, up to 37.6% worst-case."
Pitfall 2 · Skipping multi-agent entirely
Reading "teams hold experts back" as "never use multi-agent"
symptom: team rewrites their ORCH / ROMA system as a single-model pipeline
The paper's target is undirected self-organizing teams where agents freely discuss. It does not critique structured multi-agent patterns ORCH's rule-based aggregation, ROMA's role decomposition, pipelines where each agent does a distinct non-overlapping job. Those architectures sidestep the consensus-seeking failure mode entirely. Conflating "multi-agent" with "multi-agent chat" throws away genuinely productive patterns. Fix: ask whether agents are discussing the same question (bad) or each handling different questions (fine). Only the former is what the paper criticises.
Pitfall 3 · Adversarial rationalisation
Using "but teams are more adversarially robust" to keep undirected chat
symptom: a security argument used to justify accuracy loss on benign queries
The adversarial-dilution argument only holds under the paper's assumed threat model a single compromised agent among independent others. If an attacker can compromise multiple agents (prompt-injection chains, upstream training-data contamination, shared base model vulnerabilities), the dilution argument weakens rapidly. And for most production systems, the realistic threat is accuracy loss on benign queries, not isolated compromise of one agent. Fix: be explicit about your threat model. "Single compromised agent with independent others" is a narrow assumption; stating it out loud usually reveals it doesn't match your actual risk profile.
Pitfall 4 · Synergy-type confusion
Reporting weak-synergy numbers as if they were strong-synergy wins
symptom: "our team beats 60% of individuals" but so does the median, by definition
A team that beats the average of its members (weak synergy) is a low bar you could get it by random selection. The claim that matters for production is that the team beats the best member (strong synergy). Marketing and research alike often conflate the two. The paper's central contribution is showing that LLM teams fail at strong synergy specifically; if you report weak-synergy gains as a counter-argument, you're not actually disagreeing with the paper. Fix: when evaluating your own multi-agent system, always report team accuracy against the best-single-agent baseline, not the average. If you can't beat the best agent, you're adding compute for no accuracy gain.
Accordions · deeper questions the paper opens but doesn't close
What does this paper mean for ROMA, DyTopo, and other multi-agent frameworks?▸
Multi-agent works when it adds structure (ROMA's decomposition, DyTopo's routing), not when it adds voices. "More agents on the same problem" hurts; "different agents on different sub-problems" helps.

This paper is not anti-multi-agent. It's anti-undirected multi-agent. ROMA wins because agents have distinct non-overlapping roles (Atomizer, Planner, Executor, Aggregator) and never "discuss" the same question. DyTopo wins because routing is semantic agents only receive messages they need. Both frameworks add structure, not just voices.

The failure mode this paper identifies "everyone discusses the same question and compromises" is exactly what happens in flat multi-agent chat (AutoGen broadcast, naive multi-agent debate). The fix isn't fewer agents; it's structured delegation where each agent has a distinct non-overlapping responsibility.

Could you "fix" teams by making non-experts shut up?▸
Theoretically yes but the problem is knowing WHO the expert is for each specific sub-question, dynamically. That's an open problem.

The paper shows that telling teams "X is the expert" helps a little but doesn't solve the problem. What would solve it: a routing mechanism that sends each question to the single best agent and suppresses all others. This is exactly what ORCH (Zhou & Chan 2026) does multiple models analyse independently, then a merge agent picks the best rather than averaging.

The harder version: expertise varies per question. Agent A might be expert on math, Agent B on coding, Agent C on history and the question might require all three. Now you need sub-question routing, which is essentially what ROMA's Planner does. The paper implies that any multi-agent framework needs explicit expertise-routing, not emergent self-organisation.

Is this finding robust, or is it an artifact of prompt design?▸
Robust across 8 benchmarks, 4+ model families, and 3 information conditions. Not a prompting artifact it's a fundamental property of RLHF-trained models.

The authors test multiple prompting strategies (including explicit "defer to the expert" instructions). None close the gap. They test across GPT-4o, Claude, Gemini, open-source models. All show the same pattern. They test both on classic org-psych tasks (controlled) and ML benchmarks (naturalistic). Same result.

The robustness across this many axes strongly suggests a fundamental property, not an artifact. The mechanism (RLHF training rewards compromise) is a plausible structural explanation. Until RLHF changes to reward appropriate deference, this will persist.

Why this paper might overstate its case
Limitation: only tests "same question, multiple voices"
The paper's negative finding applies specifically to undirected collaboration where all agents deliberate on the same problem. It doesn't test structured task decomposition (ROMA), semantic routing (DyTopo), or independent-analysis-then-merge (ORCH). The paper's framing ("teams hold experts back") is accurate for its setting but could mislead readers into thinking all multi-agent approaches fail.
Limitation: human teams on these tasks are small (3-7 people)
The org-psych baselines showing human synergy use small, face-to-face teams with nonverbal cues, trust, and social dynamics. LLM agents lack all of these. The comparison is slightly unfair it's not that LLMs are worse than humans at teamwork; it's that LLMs lack the mechanisms (deference, trust signals, social pressure) that make human teamwork effective. This is a missing-mechanism finding, not an LLM-inferiority finding.

Which production multi-agent frameworks does this finding actually threaten?
AutoGen / CrewAI (free-form chat)
Microsoft Research · CrewAI Inc. · Python frameworks
Most exposed to the finding the canonical "undirected team" pattern. AutoGen's GroupChat and CrewAI's default Process.sequential with free-form agent discussion are exactly the architecture the paper critiques. Teams using these for expertise-heavy tasks (medical QA, legal research, code review with a senior-agent + junior-agents) should re-read the paper before their next architecture review. The fix isn't to drop these frameworks both support structured workflows but to stop treating "more agents discussing" as the default.
LangGraph / ROMA / ORCH
Structured-workflow frameworks · rule-based aggregation
Largely insulated the architectural choices these frameworks make are the paper's implied remedy. LangGraph's explicit graph-structured workflows, ROMA's role decomposition, ORCH's rule-level aggregation over independent analyses all avoid the "everyone discusses the same question" failure mode by design. The paper's critique of undirected teams is consistent with the case these frameworks make for structure over improvisation. If anything, this paper strengthens the argument for using them.
Frontier labs · OpenAI / Anthropic / Google
Router infrastructure behind ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini
Probably already routing to specialists rather than ensembling for most queries. Consumer AI products face exactly the efficiency pressure this paper describes: why pay for $N$ agents to converge to a compromise when one specialist would have been better? These labs likely use learned routers to pick the right model (or fine-tune, or tool-call) per query rather than running undirected teams. What they haven't publicly documented is how they handle queries where the expert isn't identifiable which is where the paper's finding bites hardest.
MetaGPT / AutoGPT / BabyAGI
Early self-organising agent frameworks
Directly implicated. These are the prototypical "agents freely interact" frameworks. MetaGPT's role-based company simulation and AutoGPT's recursive self-delegation both rely on LLM agents reaching consensus on sub-problems which, per the paper, they struggle to do when expertise is asymmetric. These frameworks were already losing momentum to structured alternatives; this paper accelerates the shift.
Enterprise · legal, medical, financial AI
Harvey, Hippocratic, BloombergGPT-style platforms
Use case determines exposure. Domain-expert platforms usually do know who the expert is (the fine-tuned legal model, the medical-trained model). The paper's finding is that even when known, teams fail to defer. For platforms that route directly to the domain-specialist model, this is a non-issue. For platforms that run "consultation panels" across several models, this is a real risk the specialist's correct answer gets diluted by general-purpose models' commentary.
Red-team / audit / safety-critical
Constitutional AI, debate-based safety, adversarial eval
Consensus-seeking is the feature, not the bug, for these use cases. For red-teaming, adversarial evaluation, and safety-critical review, the goal is often to diffuse any single model's bias or compromise which is exactly what consensus-seeking does. The paper's "weakness" is a strength when the goal is robustness over accuracy. These teams should continue with multi-agent deliberation; just don't expect it to match solo-expert accuracy on benign queries.
🎯

The architectural decision in one line

If your agents are free-form-discussing the same question and you expect strong synergy, you're fighting RLHF. Either: (a) identify the expert and route directly, (b) use structured aggregation like ORCH or ROMA where roles don't overlap, or (c) accept the accuracy hit in exchange for adversarial robustness but be explicit about which threat model you're buying. The pattern to avoid is "we'll just have the agents talk it out" as a default.

Build it · what this paper changes about how you ship agents
1
Default to routing, not discussion
When you have multiple specialist agents, your first instinct should be route to one, not "have them all discuss." Build a lightweight classifier (even a small LLM call) that maps each incoming query to the single most-relevant agent. Discussion is a last resort when classification is uncertain, not a default.
2
If you must use teams, use independent-then-merge (ORCH pattern)
Never let agents "discuss." Have each produce an independent analysis, then a merge agent selects (not averages) the best. This avoids the integrative compromise trap entirely. The merge agent sees all analyses but agents never see each other's work no social pressure to compromise.
multi-agent discussion for accuracy use independent analysis + merge instead
3
Measure the "expert gap" before shipping
Before deploying any multi-agent system, measure: team accuracy vs best-member-solo accuracy. If the team is worse, you're paying for coordination overhead that hurts. Run this eval on 50+ representative queries. If team < solo expert on >30% of queries, restructure or drop to single-agent.
4
Use team deliberation ONLY for adversarial robustness
The paper shows one clear benefit of teams: they absorb adversarial inputs. If your threat model includes compromised agents (jailbroken models, poisoned contexts), deliberation is a valid defence. Frame it as a safety mechanism, not an accuracy mechanism.
5
Cap team size at 3 for any remaining discussion
If you still need team discussion (genuinely ambiguous tasks, no clear single expert), keep teams at 3 agents maximum. The paper shows expertise dilution is monotonically increasing with team size. 3 is the best compromise between diversity and dilution. Beyond 3, you're always paying more than you gain.
Research idea
Train a "deference head" a small module on top of LLM representations that predicts "should I defer to another agent's answer on this specific question?" Supervised on (question, agent_accuracy) pairs from benchmarks. Could restore expertise utilisation while keeping the team structure.
Startup idea
Multi-agent audit tool: takes any multi-agent system, automatically measures expert gap, expertise dilution curve, and adversarial robustness. Gives a "ship / don't ship" recommendation. Sell to enterprises deploying multi-agent solutions who are unknowingly paying for worse performance.
1-line improvement
In any team discussion prompt, replace "Discuss and reach consensus" with "Each agent states a confidence score (0-100) for their answer. Final answer = answer with highest confidence." Bypasses compromise entirely. Expected: +10-15% accuracy recovery based on the paper's mechanism analysis.

# Measure strong-synergy gap: does the team match its best member?
# This is the minimal reproduction of the paper's central finding.

from typing import Callable
import statistics

def run_solo(agent: Callable, question: str) -> str:
    # Each agent answers independently.
    return agent(question)

def run_team(agents: list[Callable], question: str,
             rounds: int = 3, info_condition: str = "none") -> str:
    # Free-form team discussion the paper's target architecture.
    # info_condition in {"none", "revealed", "revealed_defer"}

    preamble = build_preamble(info_condition, agents)
    conversation = [("system", preamble), ("user", question)]

    for r in range(rounds):
        for i, ag in enumerate(agents):
            turn = ag.respond(conversation, agent_id=i)
            conversation.append((f"agent_{i}", turn))

    # Final aggregated answer: let agents produce a consensus answer.
    return elicit_consensus(agents, conversation)


def strong_synergy_gap(agents: list[Callable],
                       questions: list[str],
                       gold: list[str]) -> dict:
    # For each question, measure (a) each agent's solo accuracy, (b) team accuracy.
    # The paper's central quantity.
    solo_correct = [0] * len(agents)
    team_correct = 0

    for q, g in zip(questions, gold):
        # Each agent solo.
        for i, ag in enumerate(agents):
            if matches(run_solo(ag, q), g):
                solo_correct[i] += 1
        # Team discussion.
        if matches(run_team(agents, q), g):
            team_correct += 1

    N = len(questions)
    solo_accs = [c / N for c in solo_correct]
    team_acc = team_correct / N
    best_solo = max(solo_accs)
    gap = (team_acc - best_solo) / best_solo   # negative under the paper's finding

    return {
        "solo_accs": solo_accs,
        "best_solo": best_solo,
        "team_acc": team_acc,
        "strong_synergy_gap": gap,    # paper's key metric
    }

# the expert's initial position across discussion rounds.
# If the team defers correctly: distance decreases across rounds.
# The paper's finding: distance INCREASES the team drifts away.

import numpy as np

def extract_position(agent_turn: str) -> np.ndarray:

    # For ranking tasks: extract the ordered list, embed as permutation vector.

def deference_trajectory(agents: list[Callable],
                          expert_idx: int,
                          question: str,
                          rounds: int = 5) -> list[float]:
    # Returns D(0), D(1), ..., D(T) the distance from team's aggregate
    # position to the expert's initial position, round by round.

    expert_initial = extract_position(agents[expert_idx].respond([("user", question)], agent_id=expert_idx))

    conversation = [("user", question)]
    trajectory = []

    for r in range(rounds):
        positions = []
        for i, ag in enumerate(agents):
            turn = ag.respond(conversation, agent_id=i)
            conversation.append((f"agent_{i}", turn))
            positions.append(extract_position(turn))

        team_aggregate = np.mean(positions, axis=0)
        D = np.linalg.norm(team_aggregate - expert_initial)
        trajectory.append(float(D))

    return trajectory

Reading order if you're going to clone the repo

Start with the experiment config files they define the three information conditions and the exact prompts used for each. Then read the scoring code (how the paper converts free-form turns into positions in answer-space). Then the main experiment runner. The paper's novelty is not the infrastructure (that's standard multi-agent scaffolding) but the measurement specifically the deference trajectory. That measurement should be the thing you port to diagnose your own multi-agent architectures.

Common gotcha
Position extraction is the hard part. Converting free-text agent turns into comparable positions in a metric space is non-trivial. Ranked-list tasks have clean position vectors (permutation embeddings). MCQ is straightforward (one-hot). Free-form QA needs a semantic distance measure (cross-encoder or embedding similarity), which introduces noise. The paper carefully separates tasks by answer structure for exactly this reason; casual reproductions often conflate them and get ambiguous trajectory signals.
Research idea
Does fine-tuning for deference fix it? The paper doesn't test whether you can train an agent to defer appropriately (e.g., RLHF with preference signals that reward deferring-when-appropriate rather than always-agreeing). This is the most obvious follow-up: produce a "deference-tuned" model and measure whether teams with it achieve strong synergy. Someone will try this; the result positive or negative matters.
Production add-on
Team-output auditing. Log the deference trajectory for every production multi-agent query. Flag queries where the team drifted away from its best-scoring initial position these are the failures the paper identifies. Use these as training data for a router that decides whether to run the team or skip to solo-expert on future similar queries. The paper doesn't propose this; it's the operational follow-through.

Question 1 of 8 · Strong vs weak synergy

What is "strong synergy" as defined by the paper, and do LLM teams achieve it?

Strong synergy is the goal that justifies multi-agent systems the team produces better results than any individual could alone. This paper shows current LLM teams never achieve it in unconstrained settings.
Question 2 of 8 · Integrative compromise

The paper identifies "integrative compromise" as the core failure mechanism. What does this mean in practice?

The RLHF connection: models trained to be helpful and balanced actively resist deference, producing compromise where they should defer. This isn't a prompting issue it's structural.
Question 3 of 8 · Team size scaling

What happens to the "synergy gap" (team underperformance vs expert) as team size increases from 2 to 8?

Monotonic degradation is the strongest form of negative finding. It means "more agents is always worse for accuracy" in this setting. Cap teams at 3 maximum if you must use discussion.
Question 4 of 8 · Adversarial robustness trade-off

Consensus-seeking hurts expertise utilisation. But it helps with something else. What?

The trade-off is structural: you can't fix expertise dilution without also losing adversarial robustness, because both flow from the same mechanism. Production systems must explicitly choose which they prioritise.
Question 5 of 8 · Why org-psych tasks matter

Why does the paper replicate NASA Moon Survival and Lost at Sea classic org-psych experiments from the 1970s?

Methodological strength: using tasks with known human synergy baselines makes the LLM failure unambiguous. These aren't "impossible tasks" they're tasks where collaboration should help, and does for humans.
Question 6 of 8 · RLHF connection

Why does RLHF training specifically produce agents that compromise rather than defer?

The training objective mismatch: RLHF was designed for single-agent helpfulness, not multi-agent coordination. In a team, "being helpful" sometimes means shutting up and deferring but the reward model doesn't distinguish these cases.
Question 7 of 8 · Architecture implications

Based on this paper's findings, which multi-agent architecture pattern best preserves expertise while still using multiple agents?

The key insight: the problem is discussion, not multiplicity. Having multiple agents analyse independently preserves diversity; having a merge agent select (not average) preserves expertise. This is exactly what ORCH does and why it wins.
Question 8 of 8 · When to use teams despite this paper

Given this paper's findings, in which scenario would multi-agent team DISCUSSION (not independent analysis) still be the right choice?

Nuance matters: this paper doesn't say "never use teams." It says "teams dilute expertise through compromise." That same property protects against adversaries. The choice depends on your threat model and accuracy requirements.
