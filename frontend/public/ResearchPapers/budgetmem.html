Research Paper Breakdown

BudgetMem agent memory that spends compute on demand

AuthorsZhang · Yue · Feng · Long · Bao · Jin · Zhang · Li · You · Qin · Wang
Year2026
VenuearXiv preprint, Feb 2026
Codegithub.com/ViktorAxelsen/BudgetMem

Most agent memory systems do their expensive work offline they pre-process the entire conversation history once and discard whatever doesn't seem important. But "important" is query-dependent: a fact useless for one question can be critical for another. BudgetMem flips this: it does memory extraction on-demand at runtime, conditioned on the actual query, and gives every module three budget tiers (Low/Mid/High). A small reinforcement-learning-trained router decides which tier to use per module per query. Easy queries route Low; hard queries route High; the cost-accuracy frontier becomes a runtime dial. Beats baselines under tight budgets across LoCoMo, LongMemEval, and HotpotQA. The router transfers from LLaMA to Qwen without retraining.

2-minute brain download · the core of BudgetMem
The problem
Existing agent memory systems do offline, query-agnostic memory construction pre-process the whole history once, throw away "unimportant" parts. But importance is query-dependent. A fact useless for question A might be critical for question B. Worse: prior runtime systems that fix this incur huge overhead and offer no explicit cost control.
The idea
Build memory on demand at runtime, conditioned on the query. Structure extraction as modules, each offered in three budget tiers: Low (cheap), Mid (balanced), High (expensive). A lightweight RL-trained router picks tiers per module per query, balancing task reward against cost.
Why it matters
Beats baselines under tight cost budgets across LoCoMo, LongMemEval, HotpotQA. Transfers from LLaMA → Qwen without retraining the router's policy generalises across base models. Three different "tiering strategies" (implementation, reasoning, capacity) tested each useful at different budget regimes.
One-line intuition Most memory systems are like a librarian who categorises every book the day they arrive, ignoring what readers will eventually ask. BudgetMem is a librarian who waits to hear the question, then decides how thoroughly to search based on the question's difficulty and how much budget you've given her today.

Why per-module tier selection beats per-query model selection

The naive alternative is "easy queries → cheap LLM, hard queries → expensive LLM" (model routing). BudgetMem's insight: different modules within the same query have different cost-quality sensitivities. For a typical question, the Retrieve module benefits from High tier (better recall pays for itself), while Filter is fine at Low (filtering has lower variance). Per-module tier picking exploits this one query might use [Retrieve:High, Filter:Low, Extract:Mid], another [Retrieve:Low, Filter:Mid, Extract:High]. Search space is 3M for M modules; the RL router learns the policy.

Deep dive · four questions the paper makes you reconstruct
Why do "offline, query-agnostic" memory systems leak information critical to specific queries? ▸
Importance is query-dependent. Pre-processing throws away facts that look unimportant in general but are essential for specific questions.

Consider an agent memory built from a long conversation. An offline system processes the whole history once, summarises it, and indexes the summary. Suppose the conversation included "I prefer my coffee black" the summariser may drop this as trivial.

Later, the user asks "Why didn't you remember my coffee preference?" the answer is gone. The summariser couldn't have known this would matter. The fundamental limitation: any summary that fits in less than the full history necessarily discards information, and you can't predict which information will be queried.

BudgetMem's runtime approach: don't pre-summarise. Keep raw chunks. When a query comes in, retrieve relevant chunks and extract just-in-time. The query conditions the extraction the same chunk gets extracted differently depending on what's being asked. Information that "wouldn't matter generally" can still be surfaced when its specific question arrives.

What are the three "tiering strategies" and when does each win? ▸
Implementation (method complexity), Reasoning (inference behaviour), Capacity (model size). Each captures a different cost axis. Optimal mix varies by budget regime.

The paper systematically studies three orthogonal ways a module can be made cheaper or more expensive:

  • Implementation tiering: swap a complex method for a simpler one. Low = simple keyword match; Mid = BM25 retrieval; High = dense embedding + reranker. Pure algorithmic cost.
  • Reasoning tiering: change the inference behaviour. Low = no chain-of-thought; Mid = brief CoT; High = full multi-step reasoning. Same model, different prompting.
  • Capacity tiering: use a smaller model for cheap tiers. Low = 7B; Mid = 13B; High = 70B. Same algorithm, different model sizes.

Findings: Implementation tiering is best at very tight budgets gains a lot of accuracy by switching from "no module" to "simple module." Reasoning tiering is best at mid budgets extracts more from existing models without paying for new ones. Capacity tiering dominates at high budgets when you can afford a 70B, it just works better. The router's job: pick across these axes per module per query.

How does the RL training balance task reward vs cost? ▸
Cost-aware reward: $r = r_{task} - \lambda \cdot c$. The hyperparameter $\lambda$ encodes user's cost-accuracy preference. Train at one $\lambda$, deploy with another by varying it.

The router is a small policy network (compact, the paper notes). Trained with PPO or similar policy-gradient method. Reward per query: task performance reward minus cost, where cost is measured in compute (often tokens × per-token price).

$r = r_{task}(answer, ground\_truth) - \lambda \cdot c(tier\_choices)$

The $\lambda$ coefficient is the cost-accuracy slider. Train at $\lambda = 1.0$ for balanced trade-off. At inference time, you can vary $\lambda$ to push the router toward cheaper or more accurate decisions per query, without retraining the LLM modules. This is how "explicit, controllable performance-cost trade-offs" work in practice.

The router is small enough to retrain quickly. The paper notes the policy is portable trained on LLaMA-based modules, it transfers to Qwen-based modules without retraining, suggesting the router learns query-difficulty patterns that generalise across base models.

Why does BudgetMem beat baselines at HIGH budget too, not just tight budgets? ▸
On-demand extraction avoids the offline-summarisation information loss. Even with unlimited budget, query-conditioned extraction beats query-agnostic preprocessing.

You'd expect a paper about budget-tier routing to win at tight budgets and lose (or tie) at high budgets once you have unlimited compute, there's no need to "route" anything. But BudgetMem also wins in performance-first settings.

Why? The on-demand design is independent of the routing mechanism. Even if the router always picks High tier (budget unconstrained), BudgetMem's query-conditioned runtime extraction still beats baselines that rely on offline preprocessing. The routing is one contribution; runtime extraction is another, larger one.

This is a nice "two-for-one" framing: the paper introduces both (a) runtime memory extraction as an architecture pattern, and (b) RL-trained routing on top. They evaluate jointly but the gains decompose. Practitioners can adopt the runtime approach without the routing if they don't have RL infrastructure.

What you're about to read. Six sections walking from the offline-vs-runtime trade-off → the modular memory pipeline → the three tiering strategies → the RL router design → experiments on three benchmarks → limitations and what's next.
§1Why offline memory falls short

The paper opens with a dichotomy in agent memory design:

Offline construction
Pre-process the whole history once. Store summaries / embeddings. Cheap at query time, but throws away query-critical information because importance is unknown at construction time.
Runtime extraction
Extract relevant memory at query time, conditioned on the question. Preserves query-critical info, but expensive and lacks explicit cost control.
Status quo runtime systems
Either resort to ad-hoc cost reduction or just throw more compute at the problem. No principled cost-quality slider.
The reframe

Treat runtime memory as a budget allocation problem. Each module has a cost-quality dial; the system picks each dial per query. The cost-accuracy frontier becomes a continuous, controllable surface rather than a fixed point.

§2The modular memory pipeline

BudgetMem structures runtime memory as a sequence of modules, each performing one well-defined operation:

Pipeline · query → answer (with intermediate states)
retrieve(q, history) → candidate_chunks # raw chunks possibly relevant to q
Retrieve. Pull a candidate set of raw memory chunks from the chunked history. No offline preprocessing required chunks are stored as-is.
filter(q, candidates) → filtered_chunks # discard clearly irrelevant ones
Filter. Drop chunks that clearly don't address the query. Reduces input size for downstream extraction.
extract(q, filtered) → memory_evidence # structured evidence supporting the answer
Extract. From the filtered chunks, distil structured evidence specifically supporting (or contradicting) the answer. This is where most "intelligence" lives.
answer = generate(q, memory_evidence)
Generate. Final LLM call that uses the extracted evidence to produce the answer. Outside the budget-tiering scope (downstream consumer of memory).

Each module exposes the same budget-tier interface: it accepts a tier choice (Low / Mid / High) and behaves accordingly. Module interfaces are uniform; only internal implementation varies per tier.

§3Three tiering strategies

The paper studies three orthogonal axes for realising tiers. Any module can be tiered along one (or, in principle, combinations) of these axes:

Implementation tiering
Vary method complexity. Low = lightweight rule-based; Mid = traditional ML (BM25, TF-IDF); High = dense embeddings + neural reranker. Same module interface, drastically different algorithmic cost.
Reasoning tiering
Vary inference behaviour. Low = direct answer, no thinking; Mid = brief CoT (few sentences); High = full multi-step reasoning with self-verification. Same model, different prompts.
Capacity tiering
Vary module model size. Low = small (7B); Mid = medium (13-30B); High = large (70B+). Same algorithm, different model classes.
When each wins
Tight budgets favour Implementation tiering (basic methods recover most performance cheaply). Mid budgets favour Reasoning. High budgets favour Capacity (big models pay off when you can afford them). The router learns this implicitly.
§4The RL-trained budget-tier router

The router is a compact neural policy network trained with reinforcement learning. It observes the query and intermediate states (e.g., output of upstream modules) and decides which tier to invoke next.

Algorithm · cost-aware policy training
for each training query q: trajectory = [] for module m in pipeline: state = (q, intermediate_outputs) tier = router(state) # Low/Mid/High output = m.run(tier, ...) trajectory.append((state, tier, cost(m, tier))) answer = generate(q, evidence) r_task = reward(answer, ground_truth) r_total = r_task − λ · sum(costs) policy_gradient_update(router, trajectory, r_total)
The training loop. Each query produces a trajectory of (state, tier-choice, cost) tuples. Final reward is task performance minus weighted total cost. PPO-style updates push the policy toward tier choices that yield high task reward without overspending. The $\lambda$ coefficient is the cost-accuracy slider adjusting it at training time changes the operating point.

Worked example: a hard multi-hop query like "What was the third paper my coauthor cited in chapter 4 of the doc we discussed last week?"

1
Router observes: long query with multiple resolution steps (third, chapter 4, last week). Encodes high difficulty.
2
Router picks: [Retrieve: High], [Filter: Mid], [Extract: High]. Heavy investment in retrieval (need precise multi-hop chunks) and extraction (need structured reasoning over them); medium filter (cheap quality is OK).
3
Cost: ~5K tokens. Accuracy: high. Router rewarded.
4
Compare: easy query "what's my coffee order?" gets [Low, Low, Low] → 0.4K tokens, equally accurate. Router learns to discriminate based on query embedding + difficulty signals.
§5Experiments three benchmarks, transferable router

BudgetMem is evaluated on three long-context QA benchmarks chosen to stress different memory properties.

LoCoMo (long conversation)
Multi-session dialogues. Tests retention across many turns. Shorter histories where cost gap to baselines is smaller (less to save), but BudgetMem still wins on quality.
LongMemEval
Long-term memory evaluation with diverse query types. The on-demand design's strongest setting query-critical info in long history that offline systems would lose.
HotpotQA (multi-hop)
Multi-document multi-hop QA. Tests whether the router correctly invests in heavy extraction for genuinely hard questions while staying cheap on easy lookups.
Cross-model transfer
Router trained on LLaMA-based modules, deployed with Qwen-based modules works without retraining. The policy generalises beyond a single base LLM, which is unusual and a strong practical signal.
The metric to watch

The paper reports F1, LLM-as-a-judge, and cost (tokens). The right way to read the results is the cost-accuracy frontier: at any budget level, is BudgetMem above the baselines? Yes, across all three benchmarks. At the tightest budgets the gap is largest that's where router benefit dominates.

§6Limitations and what's next
Tier set is fixed at 3
Three tiers (Low/Mid/High) is a design choice, not a fundamental constraint. More tiers might give finer cost-quality control but increase router action space and training difficulty. Future work could explore continuous tier interpolation.
RL training requires task-reward labels
The router needs ground-truth answers to compute task reward during training. For domains without labelled QA data, you can't train. LLM-as-a-judge can substitute imperfectly but adds noise to the gradient.
Modules must share interfaces
All Low/Mid/High implementations of a module must accept the same input and produce the same output type. Engineering discipline required. Mixing libraries with different interfaces requires adapter glue.
No support for combinatorial tiering across axes
The paper studies implementation, reasoning, capacity tiering as separate strategies. A genuine combinatorial search ("Low-implementation × Mid-reasoning × High-capacity") explodes the action space left for future work.
Router latency adds to wall-clock
Even though the router is "lightweight," it's a forward pass per module per query. For very latency-sensitive deployments, the routing overhead matters. The paper doesn't deeply analyse latency trade-offs vs token cost.
Think like a researcher · where this leads
What does "the router transfers from LLaMA to Qwen without retraining" tell you?▸
The router learns query-difficulty patterns that are mostly base-model-invariant. Strong evidence the routing problem is more about queries than about models.

If the router were learning model-specific quirks ("LLaMA is good at X, so route X to High when LLaMA module is involved"), transferring to Qwen would break it. The fact that it transfers means the router is mostly learning query-side features (difficulty, topic, length, hop count) rather than module-side features.

This is great for production: train the router once, swap underlying LLMs as needed (cost optimisations, model upgrades) without retraining. The router becomes a stable infrastructure layer above changing model providers.

It also suggests a bigger principle: query-difficulty estimation is a model-agnostic problem that LLMs (or small classifiers) can learn. This connects to broader work on adaptive inference (Budget-Aware Agentic Routing, etc.).

Three attack points where BudgetMem can be improved▸
Continuous tier interpolation, multi-axis tiering, online (no-label) training.

1. Continuous tier interpolation. Instead of discrete Low/Mid/High, let the router output a continuous "effort" scalar that smoothly interpolates between cheap and expensive behaviour. Easier exploration; finer control. Tricky to design module implementations need to support continuous knobs.

2. Multi-axis combinatorial tiering. Allow the router to pick (implementation, reasoning, capacity) jointly per module. Action space goes from 3 to 27 per module but unlocks new operating points. Need a curriculum or factored policy to make training tractable.

3. Online RL without ground-truth labels. For deployments without labelled QA data, train the router from downstream signals (user thumbs up/down, repeat queries, follow-up questions). Hard but practically critical for non-benchmark deployments. Connects to the broader RLHF / RLAIF literature.

Where does BudgetMem sit relative to model routing (cheap vs expensive LLM)?▸
Complementary and orthogonal. Model routing picks the LLM for the task; BudgetMem picks tier for the memory pipeline. Use both.

Concurrent work like "Budget-Aware Agentic Routing" (arXiv 2602.21227) addresses model routing at each step, pick a cheap or expensive LLM. BudgetMem addresses module routing within memory extraction, pick cheap or expensive implementation.

These are orthogonal axes. A production agent stack can have both: model router decides "this turn uses Claude vs DeepSeek;" BudgetMem decides "the memory module runs at Mid tier this query." Together they form a 2D cost-quality optimisation. Neither subsumes the other.

The combined system is the obvious next paper. Expect it within months.

Interactive decision
You're shipping a customer-support agent that answers questions about long support history per customer. Average history: 10K tokens. Token budget: $0.05 per query. Latency target: under 3 seconds. Which memory architecture?
Why this paper might mislead in production
Pitfall: λ tuning is the real product surface
Real case: a team adopts BudgetMem expecting auto-magical cost-quality balance. They train the router at $\lambda = 1.0$ (paper default) and ship. In production, hard queries get over-budget while easy queries are over-served neither customer happy. Root cause: the chosen $\lambda$ doesn't match their actual cost preference. Fix: treat $\lambda$ as a tunable per-product hyperparameter. Estimate the user's cost-accuracy curve from analytics; pick $\lambda$ on a Pareto frontier of (accuracy, cost) reasonable for your business.
Pitfall: routing overhead at scale
Real case: shopping-search agent does ~50 queries per user session. With 3 modules per query and a router forward pass per module, that's 150 router calls per session. Even at 50ms per call → 7.5s of router latency on top of the LLM cost. Root cause: the "lightweight" router isn't latency-free. Fix: batch router decisions when possible, use a smaller policy network, or skip the router on identified easy queries via cached previous decisions.

Build it · five steps to BudgetMem in production
1
Start by building the on-demand pipeline (no router yet)
Implement Retrieve / Filter / Extract as separate modules with clean interfaces. Always run at the same tier (e.g., Mid) initially. Verify quality matches or beats your offline-summary baseline. The runtime architecture alone is most of the win get this working before adding routing.
2
Implement Low/Mid/High tiers per module
For each module, decide which tiering axis fits: Implementation (good for Filter), Reasoning (good for Extract), Capacity (good for any LLM-bound module). Hardcode tier names; benchmark each tier's cost and quality independently. You'll need this data for router training rewards.
implementing all three tiering axes pick the one that fits each module best
3
Train the router with PPO and labelled queries
Need ~5K labelled QA pairs from your domain. Router input: query embedding + intermediate state features. Output: tier per module. Reward: F1 − λ × tokens. Train at multiple λ values; you'll deploy at the operating point matching your business.
4
Pick the deployment λ from a cost-quality Pareto sweep
Run the router at λ ∈ {0.1, 0.5, 1.0, 2.0, 5.0} on validation set. Plot cost vs F1. The "elbow" is your sweet spot. For a $0.05/query budget, find the λ that hits average cost just under $0.05 and pick that for production.
5
Monitor router decisions; A/B vs always-Mid
In production, log every router decision (tier per module per query). Build a dashboard showing tier distribution. Compare router-driven against fixed-Mid in an A/B. If gains are within 1-2%, you might not need the router fixed-Mid is simpler.
Research idea
Multi-axis tiering: train one router that picks (implementation, reasoning, capacity) jointly. Action space goes from 3 to 27 per module. Use a factored policy (one head per axis) to keep training tractable. Could unlock new cost-accuracy operating points.
Startup idea
"Cost-aware AI orchestration" platform: takes any agent pipeline, instruments it with module-level tiers, trains a router on the customer's QA data, ships a budget-controlled deployment. Sells to enterprises with strict per-query cost ceilings.
1-line improvement
Cache router decisions keyed by (query embedding hash, module). Many queries are similar; reuse decisions across them. Cuts router latency to ~0 for cache hits. Especially valuable for high-traffic deployments with repeated query patterns.
