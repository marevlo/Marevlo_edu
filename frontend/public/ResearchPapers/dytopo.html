Research Paper Breakdown

DyTopo agent graphs that rewire themselves each round

AuthorsYuxing Lu · Yucheng Hu · Xukai Zhao · Jiuxin Cao
Year2026
VenuearXiv · preprint Feb 2026
InstitutionTsinghua University

Most multi-agent LLM systems fix their communication topology upfront everyone talks to everyone, or follows a scripted hierarchy, for every round of reasoning. But reasoning is stage-dependent: early rounds want broad exploration, later rounds want tight targeted verification. DyTopo makes the topology itself an adaptive object. Each round, every agent emits a short natural-language "query" (what I need) and "key" (what I can offer); DyTopo embeds these, computes pairwise similarity, and induces a sparse directed graph for that round only. Across code-gen and math benchmarks on 4 LLM backbones, DyTopo beats the strongest fixed-topology baseline by +6.2 points on average (absolute, averaged across benchmarks) and, usefully, produces an interpretable coordination trace you can inspect round-by-round. Per-task $\tau_{\text{edge}}$ tuning required.

2-minute brain download · the core of DyTopo
The problem
Most multi-agent LLM pipelines use fixed communication topologies everyone broadcasts to everyone every round, or agents follow a scripted turn order. But reasoning is stage-dependent. Round 1 needs broad exploration. Round 5 needs the debugger to talk only to the person who wrote that specific module. Fixed topology wastes tokens and muddies signal.
The idea
Let the topology rewire itself each round. Every agent emits two short strings: a query ("I need X") and a key ("I can offer Y"). DyTopo embeds these, computes pairwise similarity, and builds a directed graph for that round only edge from B to A if A's query matches B's key above threshold $\tau_{edge}$. Messages route only along activated edges.
Why it matters
+6.2% average over the strongest baseline across code generation and math reasoning on 4 LLM backbones. Beyond accuracy: an interpretable coordination trace you can literally see how the graph changes across rounds, identify failure patterns, and diagnose without reading full transcripts.
One-line intuition Fixed topology is like forcing everyone in a meeting to talk to everyone else for the whole hour. DyTopo is a meeting where who talks to whom changes every few minutes based on what each person currently needs and has to offer. Same people, radically different coordination.

Why the graph changes matter

In a fixed fully-connected graph, every agent sees every other agent's message every round 4 agents × 4 rounds = 16 message streams, most irrelevant. DyTopo's sparse induced graphs typically activate 3-6 edges per round (out of 12 possible directed edges for 4 agents). ~60% of potential messages are dropped per round, and the ones that remain are semantically relevant to the round goal. Same inference budget, much higher signal density.

Deep dive · four questions that unlock DyTopo
Why does round-by-round rewiring beat a fixed topology? ▸
Multi-round reasoning is stage-dependent. Early rounds want broad exploration; late rounds want tight targeted verification. One topology can't be good at both.

Consider a 4-agent coding team solving a bug: Planner, Coder, Critic, Tester. Round 1 is framing the Planner broadcasts context to everyone; everyone contributes ideas. Fully-connected is perfect here. Round 3 is debugging the Tester has a specific failure and needs to talk to only the Coder who wrote that module. Fully-connected is counterproductive the Planner and Critic chime in with irrelevant thoughts and distract from the fix.

A fixed fully-connected topology wastes ~60% of its messages in late rounds. A fixed hierarchy (Manager → workers) misses peer-to-peer exchanges that are critical for verification. No fixed topology is good across both phases, so the paper lets the topology adapt.

∫ Show the math · why no fixed topology can be optimal across rounds▸

Formalise each round as a round-goal $g^{(t)}$ and let $U(G \mid g^{(t)})$ be the task utility (accuracy, reasoning quality) of running a round with topology $G$ under that goal. Write the total trajectory utility as:

$$U_{\text{total}}(\{G^{(t)}\}_t \mid \{g^{(t)}\}_t) \;=\; \sum_{t=1}^{T} U\big(G^{(t)} \mid g^{(t)}\big)$$

Fixed-topology upper bound. For any single topology $G^\star$ reused across rounds, the total utility is bounded:

$$U_{\text{fixed}}^{\max} \;=\; \max_{G^\star} \sum_{t=1}^{T} U\big(G^\star \mid g^{(t)}\big) \;\leq\; \sum_{t=1}^{T} \max_{G} U\big(G \mid g^{(t)}\big) \;=\; U_{\text{adaptive}}^{\max}$$

Equality holds iff the same topology $G$ maximises $U(\cdot \mid g^{(t)})$ for every round i.e. all rounds have the same coordination structure. When they don't (early-broad vs late-narrow), the inequality is strict and the fixed-topology loss is:

$$\Delta \;=\; U_{\text{adaptive}}^{\max} - U_{\text{fixed}}^{\max} \;>\; 0$$

Why multi-round reasoning structurally has this property. The paper's core observation: different rounds have different optimal coordination. Early rounds reward broad exploration (many edges, $|E|$ large). Late rounds reward targeted verification (few edges, $|E|$ small). Specifically, for the failure-mode of "Planner broadcasts in round 1, Tester targets Coder in round 3":

$$\arg\max_G U(G \mid g^{(1)}) \;=\; G_{\text{dense}} \qquad \arg\max_G U(G \mid g^{(3)}) \;=\; G_{\text{sparse, targeted}}$$

No single $G^\star$ can be both. A fully-connected $G^\star$ is optimal for round 1 but pays an unbounded penalty in round 3 from off-topic interference. A sparse hierarchy is optimal for round 3 but starves round 1 of exploration.

Message-budget interpretation. If we constrain message count per round, fully-connected $G_{\text{FC}}$ uses $N(N-1)$ messages each round regardless of whether they're useful. If only $k$ of those messages are goal-relevant, the remaining $N(N-1) - k$ messages carry noise. The paper's empirical claim that ~60% of fully-connected messages are wasted in late rounds quantifies this formally as: for late rounds, $k \approx 0.4 \cdot N(N-1)$, and the remaining 60% of messages contribute noise that degrades downstream reasoning.

What adaptive topology gains that isn't just sparsity. You could imagine a baseline "fixed sparse" topology that's just a cheaper fully-connected e.g. a random $k$-regular graph. The adaptive gain over this baseline comes from which edges are active, not just how many. Random sparsity starves some needed exchanges; DyTopo's semantic matching activates exactly the edges the current round needs.

What does the similarity threshold $\tau_{edge}$ actually control? ▸
$\tau_{edge}$ is a dial between sparsity (few edges, focused) and coverage (many edges, exploratory). Task-sensitive. Not learned a hyperparameter you tune.

For each pair of agents $(i,j)$, DyTopo computes the cosine similarity $s_{ij} = \cos(e(q_i), e(k_j))$ between agent $i$'s query embedding and agent $j$'s key embedding. Edge from $j$ to $i$ exists iff $s_{ij} > \tau_{edge}$.

  • Low $\tau_{edge}$ (e.g. 0.3): many edges. Approaches fully-connected. Good for exploratory early rounds. Bad late too much noise.
  • High $\tau_{edge}$ (e.g. 0.7): few edges. Sometimes zero if nothing matches well. Good for targeted late rounds. Bad early starves exploration.
  • Sweet spot (paper reports ~0.5): balance. Table 3 of the paper shows the ablation; performance is sensitive to this value, and the best $\tau_{edge}$ varies across benchmarks (higher for math, lower for code-gen).

Authors don't learn $\tau_{edge}$ it's a flat hyperparameter per deployment. This is both simple (easy to tune) and a limitation (can't adapt per round, though the topology itself adapts).

∫ Show the math · $\tau_{edge}$ as a precision/recall trade-off on the ideal routing graph▸

Model DyTopo's semantic matching as approximating an ideal routing graph $G^\star_{\text{round}}$ the graph where an edge $(j, i)$ exists if and only if $j$'s output is actually useful for $i$ at this round.

DyTopo's induced graph $\hat{G}$ activates edges where $\cos(e(q_i), e(k_j)) > \tau_{\text{edge}}$. Measure its quality by precision and recall against $G^\star$:

$$\text{precision}(\hat{G}, G^\star) = \frac{|E(\hat{G}) \cap E(G^\star)|}{|E(\hat{G})|} \qquad \text{recall}(\hat{G}, G^\star) = \frac{|E(\hat{G}) \cap E(G^\star)|}{|E(G^\star)|}$$

Monotone trade-off. Lowering $\tau_{\text{edge}}$ adds edges, which raises recall and lowers precision. Raising it does the reverse:

$$\frac{d\,\text{recall}}{d\,\tau_{\text{edge}}} \leq 0 \qquad \frac{d\,\text{precision}}{d\,\tau_{\text{edge}}} \geq 0$$

These derivatives aren't strict there are plateaus where small changes in $\tau_{\text{edge}}$ don't flip any edges but the directions are fixed.

Why the optimum shifts by task. The optimal $\tau_{\text{edge}}^\star$ depends on how valuable a missed edge is (recall cost) vs. how expensive a spurious edge is (precision cost). Those costs are task-dependent:

$$\tau_{\text{edge}}^\star \;=\; \arg\max_\tau \Big[\alpha_{\text{task}} \cdot \text{recall}(\hat{G}_\tau, G^\star) - \beta_{\text{task}} \cdot (1 - \text{precision}(\hat{G}_\tau, G^\star))\Big]$$

For code generation, missed edges (a Tester not reaching the right Coder) are catastrophic code fails to compile. So $\alpha_{\text{code}}$ is large and the optimum favours lower $\tau_{\text{edge}}$ (more edges, higher recall). For math reasoning, spurious edges distract the reasoning chain so $\beta_{\text{math}}$ is large and the optimum favours higher $\tau_{\text{edge}}$ (fewer edges, higher precision). The paper's Table 3 observation (code wants lower $\tau$, math wants higher) is consistent with this framing.

Why the authors don't learn $\tau_{\text{edge}}$. To learn it, you'd need labelled $G^\star$ for training rounds which requires knowing the ideal routing graph before the fact. Since $G^\star$ is exactly what DyTopo is trying to approximate, there's no ground truth to train against. You could substitute downstream task reward, but that's noisy and expensive. The authors' choice to leave $\tau_{\text{edge}}$ as a hyperparameter is an honest tradeoff: it's tunable per deployment, not automatically adaptive, but the alternative of learning it requires infrastructure DyTopo doesn't have.

What's not in the paper. An adaptive-per-round $\tau_{\text{edge}}^{(t)}$ (schedule: start low, end high) is the obvious extension the paper doesn't test. Given the known early-broad/late-narrow pattern, a decaying $\tau_{\text{edge}}$ would likely outperform the fixed version but would require another hyperparameter (the decay rate). Left as future work.

Why are query and key decoupled why not just have agents say "this is who I'll talk to"? ▸
Decoupling what agents generate from how info routes makes the routing emergent. Agents don't need to know each other's identities or capabilities matching is purely on semantic content.

If each agent declared "I want messages from the Coder," the system is a glorified address book. Agents would need to know the team composition, their labels, and when those labels matter. Brittle and not scalable.

By emitting content descriptors (query = what I currently need, key = what I can offer), agents describe capabilities in the abstract. A new agent added to the team gets routed to automatically based on what its key descriptor says it can offer no changes to any other agent's prompt. The decoupling is what makes DyTopo compositional.

It also mirrors how human teams actually work: you don't say "I need input from Alice"; you say "I need someone who knows X," and whoever fits offers up.

∫ Show the math · key-query matching as cross-agent attention▸

DyTopo's routing operation has the same mathematical shape as scaled dot-product attention in transformers but with agents playing the role of attention positions and an LLM generating queries/keys in natural language.

For each agent $i$ at round $t$, the round goal $g^{(t)}$ conditions the LLM to emit two short natural-language descriptors:

$$q_i^{(t)} = \text{LLM}_{\text{query}}\big(\text{state}_i,\; g^{(t)}\big) \quad\text{(need)} \qquad k_i^{(t)} = \text{LLM}_{\text{key}}\big(\text{state}_i,\; g^{(t)}\big) \quad\text{(offer)}$$

These are embedded with a shared encoder $e: \mathcal{D} \to \mathbb{R}^d$:

$$\mathbf{q}_i^{(t)} = e(q_i^{(t)}) \qquad \mathbf{k}_i^{(t)} = e(k_i^{(t)})$$

The similarity tensor. Pairwise cosine similarity across all agent pairs gives a similarity matrix $S^{(t)} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times N}$:

$$S_{ij}^{(t)} \;=\; \cos\big(\mathbf{q}_i^{(t)},\; \mathbf{k}_j^{(t)}\big) \;=\; \frac{\mathbf{q}_i^{(t)} \cdot \mathbf{k}_j^{(t)}}{\|\mathbf{q}_i^{(t)}\| \; \|\mathbf{k}_j^{(t)}\|}$$

Compare to transformer attention: $A_{ij} = \text{softmax}(QK^\top/\sqrt{d})_{ij}$. The structural similarity is clear both are dot-product compatibility scores between a "wants" vector and an "offers" vector. What differs:

  • DyTopo uses cosine, not scaled dot-product. Cosine is unit-norm invariant, reducing sensitivity to embedding magnitude (which varies across LLM-generated descriptions).
  • DyTopo hard-thresholds rather than softmaxes. The edge set is $E^{(t)} = \{(j,i) : S_{ij}^{(t)} > \tau_{\text{edge}}\}$, not a weighted distribution. Messages are routed or not routed; there are no weighted mixtures.
  • DyTopo separates key/query generation from routing. The LLM generates the keys and queries; a lightweight embedding + threshold module does the routing. This decoupling is what lets new agents plug in without retraining they just need to emit compatible descriptors.

Why this matters for composition. Adding a new agent $N+1$ to a DyTopo system is a pure extension: the agent starts emitting queries and keys, and it automatically participates in routing based on semantic content no updates to existing agents' prompts. Contrast with explicit addressing ("I want input from Alice"): adding a new agent would require every existing agent to be aware of it. This compositional property is the practical payoff of the attention-like formalism.

Limit of the analogy. Transformer attention is differentiable end-to-end; DyTopo's hard-threshold routing is not. You can't backprop through "edge exists / doesn't exist." This is why DyTopo can't learn $\tau_{\text{edge}}$ by gradient descent the whole routing module has a discontinuity at the threshold. Softer variants (weighted routing with learned attention) would be differentiable but would lose the sparsity that makes DyTopo computationally cheap.

Why formalise as a Dynamic Computation Graph (DCG)? ▸
DCG gives a rigorous basis for the complexity argument against fully-connected networks. Formalism = interpretable + analysable.

The paper's Appendix A formalises DyTopo as a Dynamic Computation Graph where: (i) $N$ worker agents + 1 Manager, (ii) at each round $t$, a directed graph $G^{(t)} = (V, E^{(t)})$ with $|V| = N$ and $|E^{(t)}| \le N^2$, (iii) total rounds $T \le T_{max}$ (fixed budget).

The complexity argument: a fully-connected network sends $N(N-1)$ messages per round for $T$ rounds = $O(T N^2)$ messages. DyTopo's expected edge count is bounded by the sparsity induced by $\tau_{edge}$; empirically ~$O(T N)$ for small $N$, a full factor of $N$ cheaper. For 8 agents over 5 rounds, fully-connected = 280 messages, DyTopo = ~40.

The formalism also enables static analysis of coordination patterns: you can prove that for certain task structures, only specific edge patterns emerge, giving theoretical grounding to the "early-broad, late-narrow" empirical observation.

∫ Show the math · message-complexity analysis and where the savings come from▸

A Dynamic Computation Graph is a sequence of directed graphs $\{G^{(t)}\}_{t=1}^{T}$ with shared vertex set $V = \{1, \dots, N\}$ (worker agents, plus a Manager as $v_0$). Edges $E^{(t)} \subseteq V \times V$ change each round.

Message complexity of fully-connected baseline. Every pair of agents exchanges in both directions each round:

$$M_{\text{FC}} \;=\; \sum_{t=1}^{T} |E^{(t)}_{\text{FC}}| \;=\; T \cdot N(N-1) \;=\; O(T \cdot N^2)$$

Each message requires one LLM forward pass to produce and one (partial) forward pass to ingest, so compute scales with $M_{\text{FC}}$.

Message complexity of DyTopo. The number of edges induced at round $t$ depends on $\tau_{\text{edge}}$ and the similarity distribution. Let $d_i^{(t)} = \big|\{j : S_{ij}^{(t)} > \tau_{\text{edge}}\}\big|$ be agent $i$'s in-degree at round $t$. Total edges:

$$|E^{(t)}_{\text{DyTopo}}| \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{N} d_i^{(t)}$$

The paper empirically observes $d_i^{(t)} = O(1)$ on average for their benchmarks (each agent attends to a handful of others at most). Total message count:

$$M_{\text{DyTopo}} \;=\; \sum_{t=1}^{T} \sum_{i=1}^{N} d_i^{(t)} \;\approx\; O(T \cdot N) \qquad \text{(empirically, for their benchmarks)}$$

The saving. A full factor of $N$ cheaper than fully-connected. For $N = 8$ agents over $T = 5$ rounds: fully-connected sends $5 \cdot 8 \cdot 7 = 280$ messages, DyTopo sends $\sim 40$. For $N = 20$ over $T = 5$: $1900$ vs $\sim 100$ savings grow with team size.

Two important caveats.

  • The $O(T \cdot N)$ scaling is empirical, not proved. The complexity depends on the distribution of similarity scores relative to $\tau_{\text{edge}}$. In adversarial cases (all agents emit nearly-identical queries and keys, so similarity is uniformly high), DyTopo's edge count could approach $N^2$. The paper doesn't prove a worst-case bound below fully-connected; it shows the average case on their benchmarks is much better.
  • Overhead of the matching step isn't free. DyTopo adds: (i) one extra LLM call per agent per round to produce queries and keys, (ii) embedding computation for all descriptors, (iii) $N^2$ similarity computations. For small $N$, this overhead is a fraction of the saved message cost. For very large $N$ (say, 100+ agents), the overhead's $N^2$ similarity matrix could dominate, negating the message-count savings. The paper tests up to $N = 8$; scaling to dozens or hundreds of agents is unvalidated.

What the DCG formalism enables beyond complexity. Once you have a trajectory of graphs $\{G^{(t)}\}$, you can analyse which edge patterns correlate with successful vs. failed runs (the paper's Figure 4 does this qualitatively). You can also ask whether certain fixed topologies are "limit cases" of DyTopo e.g., fully-connected emerges when $\tau_{\text{edge}} \to -\infty$; empty graph emerges when $\tau_{\text{edge}} \to 1$. This gives a principled way to relate DyTopo to the static-topology baselines it's compared against.

§1 Fixed topology is stage-blind

The paper opens with a central observation from prior multi-agent work: multi-round reasoning is stage-dependent. Early rounds benefit from broad exploration and shared framing. Later rounds need selective, high-precision exchanges to diagnose failures and converge.

Broadcast
Everyone talks to everyone every round. Quadratic $N^2$ message cost. Late rounds drown in irrelevant context; useful signal is lost in noise.
Fixed hierarchy
Scripted turns (e.g. Manager → worker → Manager → ...). Cheap, but can't adapt. Misses peer-to-peer exchanges critical when workers need to verify each other.
Random topology
Randomly sampled edges per round. Baseline in the paper. Beats fixed sometimes, because variance covers some stage needs but completely non-interpretable.
The key reframe

Communication topology shouldn't be a design-time artifact. It should be an adaptive object conditioned on the round-level goal and the current information state. Static topologies solve the wrong optimisation problem: "best topology for all rounds" when the answer is "different topology per round."

§2 The Manager and the workers

DyTopo uses a simple two-tier agent structure. One Manager meta-agent + $N$ heterogeneous worker agents.

Role 1 · MANAGER(round context) → {round_goal, continue?}
Input: current round t shared scratchpad per-agent memories Output: round goal g_t decision to continue Policy: - frame what needs to happen this round - decide termination
The conductor. Sets the goal for each round ("propose an approach," "verify correctness," "produce the final formatted answer"). Decides when to stop. Does NOT make routing decisions those emerge from semantic matching among workers conditioned on the goal.
Role 2 · WORKER_i(goal g_t, my memory m_i) → (query q_i, key k_i, message)
Input: round goal g_t agent i's memory m_i Output: query descriptor q_i key descriptor k_i private message payload Contract: - q_i: short NL describing what agent i currently needs - k_i: short NL describing what agent i can offer - message: actual content to route along activated edges
The workers. Heterogeneous different roles (planner, coder, tester, critic). Each one, conditioned on the Manager's round goal, emits three things: query descriptor (need), key descriptor (offer), and actual message content. The crucial innovation: query and key are decoupled from the message itself, so routing is emergent from semantics, not hardcoded.
The routing primitive
$E^{(t)} = \{ (j \to i) : \cos(e(q_i^{(t)}), e(k_j^{(t)})) > \tau_{edge} \}$

Edge from agent $j$ to agent $i$ exists in round $t$ iff agent $j$'s key is semantically close enough to agent $i$'s query. Embedder $e: \mathcal{D} \to \mathbb{R}^d$ maps descriptors to vectors. Cosine similarity against threshold $\tau_{edge}$.

§3 The full algorithm

Here's DyTopo's main loop. Simple enough to implement in under 100 lines of Python if you have an embedder and N agent prompts ready.

Algorithm · DYTOPO(N agents, T_max, τ_edge)
for t = 1 to T_max: g_t, cont ← MANAGER(context_t) if not cont: break
Manager step. Set round goal and decide whether to keep going. Early termination when the Manager judges the task complete.
# agents emit query/key in parallel for i = 1 to N: q_i, k_i, msg_i ← WORKER_i(g_t, mem_i)
Worker phase 1 simultaneous descriptor emission. All agents run in parallel. Each produces a query descriptor (what I need), key descriptor (what I offer), and the actual message payload. This is embarrassingly parallel $N$ independent LLM calls.
# embed and compute similarity matrix Q = [e(q_i) for i in 1..N] K = [e(k_i) for i in 1..N] S = Q @ K.T # NxN similarity
Semantic matching. Embed all queries and keys with a small embedder (paper uses sentence-transformers tiny compared to the LLMs). Compute the $N \times N$ similarity matrix. Cheap: milliseconds for small N.
# induce directed graph E_t = {(j→i) : S[i][j] > τ_edge} G_t = (agents, E_t)
Induce G^(t). Threshold the similarity matrix. Every pair above $\tau_{edge}$ gets a directed edge from supplier to consumer. Note the asymmetry: $(j \to i)$ appears iff agent $i$'s query matches agent $j$'s key NOT iff agent $j$'s query matches agent $i$'s key. Direction matters.
# synchronisation barrier + routing for (j → i) in E_t: mem_i.append(msg_j) # update shared state context_{t+1} ← update(context_t, G_t)
Route and sync. Every message from agent $j$ is appended to agent $i$'s memory for the next round, iff the edge $(j \to i)$ activated. The synchronisation barrier means every agent sees its incoming messages before round $t{+}1$ starts. This gives interpretable coordination: edges tell you exactly who influenced whom.

Worked example: 4-agent team (Planner, Coder, Critic, Tester) solving a bug. $\tau_{edge} = 0.5$.

1
Round 1. Manager's goal: "frame the problem." All 4 agents emit broad queries/keys. Similarity matrix has many pairs above 0.5. Induced $G^{(1)}$: dense Planner broadcasts to all; Coder and Critic exchange. Edges activated: 7 out of 12 possible.
2
Round 2. Manager's goal: "implement and verify." Coder's query = "I need test cases for edge handling"; Tester's key = "I can provide failing inputs." High match. Critic's key = "I find logical errors" matches Coder's query too. Planner goes quiet nothing to offer. Edges: 4 out of 12. Planner isolated.
3
Round 3. Manager: "finalise." Tester's query = "is this fix complete?" matches Coder's key = "I confirm patches." Critic silent. Edges: 2 out of 12. Minimal.
4
Trace: 7 → 4 → 2 edges. Topology transitioned from broad (Round 1) to focused verification (Round 2) to minimal hand-off (Round 3). The Manager reads round 3's context and emits continue = false. Total messages exchanged: 13. Fully-connected baseline over 3 rounds would be $3 \times 12 = 36$. ~64% reduction with higher signal density.
§4 Experiments 4 backbones, +6.2% average

The paper's evaluation spans two task families (code generation, math reasoning) and four LLM backbones to verify backbone-agnosticism.

Code generation benchmarks
Multi-round code-gen tasks (with test execution). DyTopo beats fixed-topology (broadcast, hierarchy) and random baselines across all 4 backbones. Highest-margin gains on harder/longer benchmarks stage-dependence matters more when reasoning is deeper.
Math reasoning benchmarks
Multi-step math with verification rounds. Same pattern: DyTopo wins consistently. The Critic-Tester edge activation in late rounds is the driver verification becomes targeted rather than broadcast.
4 LLM backbones (results hold across)
Tested on multiple open and closed frontier models. The +6.2% average over strongest baseline holds regardless of backbone strong evidence DyTopo is an orchestration win, not an artifact of any one model.
Interpretability (Figure 4 in paper)
For a single code-generation instance, the paper shows three representative rounds with the induced $G^{(t)}$ plotted. Transition from broad exploratory routing → verification-focused → sparse dependency-minimal. Qualitatively matches what engineers do in real code review.
The ablation that matters most

Table 3 in the paper ablates $\tau_{edge}$ across datasets. Performance is highly sensitive a $\tau_{edge}$ optimal for one benchmark is suboptimal for another. This is a non-trivial hyperparameter. The authors don't pretend otherwise: they report the best per-benchmark value rather than claim a universal constant. In production, expect to tune $\tau_{edge}$ per task family.

§5 Limitations the authors admit

The paper is forthright about where DyTopo breaks. Learning these teaches you what problems are still open.

Embedder dependence
Semantic matching lives or dies on the embedder quality. On domain-specific jargon (legal terms, medical codes, proprietary internal vocabulary), a general-purpose sentence embedder may fail to detect matches that a human would see as obvious. Authors note this and recommend domain-tuned embedders for specialised deployments.
Synchronisation barrier overhead
Each round requires ALL $N$ workers to emit their query/key/message before routing can happen. Slowest agent per round throttles the whole system. For heterogeneous LLM calls (one fast model, one slow), wall-clock is dominated by the slowest.
Round count budget
$T_{max}$ caps the rounds. If the task genuinely needs more rounds than budgeted (complex debugging chains), DyTopo will terminate prematurely with a partial answer. Hard to pick $T_{max}$ upfront for new task families.
τ_edge is not learned
A single global threshold per deployment. Can't adapt per round (though the graph itself adapts). Logical next step learn an adaptive threshold is called out as future work.
No memory of past routings
Each round's graph is induced fresh from current descriptors. If round 3 discovered "Coder and Tester collaborate well on this task family," that pattern isn't reused next time. No learned routing priors across tasks.
§6 Where DyTopo sits in the 2026 agent stack

DyTopo and ROMA are complementary 2026 papers attacking multi-agent coordination from opposite angles.

2022-23 · Single-agent prompting
ReAct, CoT, AutoGPT. One agent, one context, one chain. Works for shallow tasks.
2024 · Fixed-topology multi-agent
MetaGPT (role-specialised hierarchy), AutoGen (broadcast chat). Multi-agent but topology is baked in at design time. Limited by stage-blindness.
2025 · Random + learned static
Random topology baselines appeared; some work learned fixed-but-task-adaptive topologies. Progress, but still one topology per task.
Feb 2026 · ROMA
Structures the levels. Recursive task decomposition into a dependency tree, four standardised meta-roles. Attacks what the task looks like.
Feb 2026 · DyTopo (this paper)
Structures within a level. Topology rewired each round via semantic matching. Attacks who talks to whom.
2026+ · What's next
Learned routing policies (replace $\tau_{edge}$ with a trained model); async routing (remove the sync barrier); cross-task memory of successful routing patterns; combining ROMA's vertical decomposition with DyTopo's horizontal routing the obvious composition paper waiting to happen.

Pitfalls when applying DyTopo · four production traps
Pitfall 1 · τ over-tuning
Treating $\tau_{\text{edge}}$ as a universal constant
symptom: deployment achieves paper numbers on benchmarks, degrades 5-10% in production
The paper is explicit that $\tau_{\text{edge}}$ is task-sensitive their ablation (Table 3) shows different optima for code generation vs math reasoning. Teams that lift "$\tau = 0.5$" from the paper and drop it into a completely different domain (legal QA, customer support, biological reasoning) will almost certainly be off the optimum. The paper doesn't promise transfer. Fix: budget time for per-task-family tuning. Measure performance across a grid $\tau \in \{0.3, 0.4, 0.5, 0.6, 0.7\}$ on your actual task before committing. If you can't afford the tuning, use a conservative low $\tau$ (more edges, closer to fully-connected) you'll lose the sparsity savings but avoid the sharp cliff of too-high $\tau$.
Pitfall 2 · Manager as SPOF
Assuming the Manager agent just works
symptom: when the Manager hallucinates a bad round goal, the entire team goes astray
The Manager's round goal conditions every worker's query/key generation. If the Manager's output is low-quality vague goal, hallucinated sub-task, premature termination the downstream routing is built on a bad foundation and no amount of good worker behaviour recovers. This is a single point of failure the paper acknowledges but doesn't fully address. In the paper's benchmarks, Manager quality is controlled; in production it's not. Fix: run a secondary "Manager critic" agent that scores the Manager's goal for coherence with the task, and triggers a re-prompt if the score is low. Not in the paper; a standard production hardening pattern.
Pitfall 3 · Descriptor calibration
Trusting agents' self-reports of what they can do
symptom: an agent emits "I can solve this" for every round; routing clusters messages toward it regardless of actual capability
Key/query descriptors are LLM self-reports. LLMs are systematically miscalibrated on their own capabilities they overclaim competence, especially on tasks they haven't been fine-tuned for. An overclaiming agent becomes a routing black hole: messages flow to it; it produces bad outputs; the graph structure looks healthy but accuracy collapses. Fix: periodically audit agents' descriptor accuracy against their actual task success rate. When descriptors overclaim systematically, either recalibrate via prompt engineering ("be conservative about what you claim to offer") or route around the over-claiming agent. The paper doesn't address this it's a known weakness.
Pitfall 4 · Wrong tool for problem
Using DyTopo where ORCH or ROMA would fit better
symptom: a discrete-choice task (MCQ) gets routed dynamically, adding overhead without accuracy gain
DyTopo's adaptive topology helps when multi-round reasoning has genuinely stage-dependent needs (code gen, math, long-horizon planning). For single-round discrete-choice tasks the ORCH regime the topology doesn't need to adapt because there's only one round. For decomposable tasks with clear non-overlapping roles (ROMA's Atomizer/Planner/Executor pattern), the topology is essentially fixed by the decomposition, and DyTopo's overhead buys nothing. Apply DyTopo where the communication pattern genuinely varies by round. Fix: before picking DyTopo, check whether your task has >1 round and whether those rounds actually have different optimal coordination structures. If not, a simpler architecture is cheaper and equivalent.
Accordions · deeper questions the paper opens but doesn't close
What assumption does DyTopo make that will eventually break? ▸
Assumes agents can accurately describe their own capabilities in natural language. Brittle when agents don't know what they know.

The query/key mechanism depends on each agent honestly and accurately describing (a) what it currently needs, (b) what it can offer. In practice LLMs are often miscalibrated on their own capabilities an agent might emit a key like "I can debug any Python code" when it's actually terrible at concurrency bugs.

When an agent's key overclaims, it gets routed messages it can't handle, polluting the next round. When it underclaims, it misses chances to contribute. The paper implicitly assumes descriptor quality is acceptable; in production this needs monitoring. Expect future work on calibrated key descriptors where agents self-report with uncertainty and the router accounts for it.

Three attack points where DyTopo can be improved ▸
Learned routing, async barriers, cross-task routing memory.

1. Learned routing. Replace the $\tau_{edge}$ threshold with a small neural router trained on successful vs failed trajectories. The router takes Q and K matrices plus round number plus task class, outputs a per-pair probability. $\tau_{edge}$ becomes adaptive. Straightforward fine-tuning once you have enough runs.

2. Remove the sync barrier. Async routing: agents emit descriptors whenever ready; edges activate as soon as both endpoints are live. Drops wall-clock to max-per-round instead of sum-per-round. Hard engineering problem (consistency) but a meaningful latency win.

3. Cross-task routing memory. Store successful $(G^{(t)}, task\_class, round\_number)$ triples. On a new similar task, warm-start the graph with a retrieved successful topology instead of starting from empty. Acts as a prior that speeds convergence.

How would you use DyTopo in a real production agent system? ▸
Perfect for code-review, multi-step verification, investigation workflows. Wrong for streaming conversational tasks and tasks that need just one agent.

Where DyTopo is a great fit: multi-round workflows with heterogeneous agent roles and an obvious stage progression.

  • Automated code review Planner, Author, Reviewer, Tester. 3-5 rounds per PR. Edge patterns evolve naturally.
  • Incident investigation Investigator, Domain expert, Ticket triager, Runbook operator. Early rounds broad; late rounds targeted.
  • Research synthesis Searcher, Analyser, Critic, Writer. Verification rounds benefit from targeted routing.

Where it's a mismatch: anything streaming (conversational bots latency kills you), anything with only one agent (degenerate), tasks where agents are truly homogeneous (key/query descriptors all match identically and the matching signal is noise).

Why this paper might fail in practice
Failure: embedder-domain mismatch
Real case: a team deployed DyTopo for medical-billing workflow automation with agents specialised in ICD-10 coding, claim validation, and payer rules. They used sentence-transformers MiniLM. Every agent's key descriptor mentioned "codes" or "claims" similarity collapsed to uniform ~0.45 across all pairs. Graph came out nearly uniform. Root cause: generic embedder didn't discriminate between medical specialties. Fix: use a domain-tuned embedder (SciBERT or a medical-trained model), or include agent-role tokens in the descriptor prompt ("I am a [SPECIALTY] agent. I can offer: ..."). Always test your embedder on sample descriptor pairs before assuming matching works.
Failure: the sync barrier bottleneck
Real case: DyTopo deployment where one agent used a 70B model for deep reasoning, others used 7B. At every round, the whole system waited on the 70B agent to emit its descriptors + message. Effective throughput ≈ 70B throughput, despite 4/5 agents being cheap and fast. Root cause: strict sync barrier. Fix: either use homogeneous backbone, or split the system into a fast sub-team (DyTopo routing) with the slow agent called opportunistically outside the synchronous rounds. The paper doesn't address async routing that's still an open problem.

Who would actually ship semantic-routing multi-agent? And where's it overkill?
LangGraph / LangChain
Graph-structured agent orchestration · closest relevant framework
Natural home LangGraph already models workflows as typed graphs; adding DyTopo-style adaptive edge activation is a small extension. LangGraph's edges are normally hard-coded; DyTopo replaces them with a semantic-matching layer. The engineering lift is modest: add key/query generation to each node, plug in an embedder, threshold the similarity matrix. Expect this pattern to appear as a LangGraph recipe within months of DyTopo's publication if it hasn't already.
CrewAI / AutoGen
Free-form and role-based multi-agent frameworks
Retrofit is possible but architecturally awkward. CrewAI's default Process.sequential and AutoGen's GroupChat assume either strict ordering or broadcast. DyTopo needs a per-round routing step that doesn't map cleanly onto either abstraction. Teams who want DyTopo-style behaviour on these frameworks will end up writing a custom routing layer that partially duplicates the framework's own. The honest recommendation is: if you need DyTopo, use LangGraph or write it directly.
ROMA / structured-decomposition frameworks
Complementary, not competing
Combinable DyTopo's adaptive routing at each ROMA stage is a plausible hybrid. ROMA decomposes a task into Atomizer/Planner/Executor/Aggregator phases; DyTopo adapts communication within a phase. The two solve different problems: ROMA is about what roles exist, DyTopo is about who talks to whom. A hybrid would apply DyTopo inside the Executor phase (where multiple worker agents iteratively refine) while keeping ROMA's role boundaries. Not in any paper yet, but the obvious next step.
Frontier labs · OpenAI / Anthropic / Google
Internal orchestration infrastructure
Probably doing something like this internally unclear how close to DyTopo. Any lab operating an agentic product at scale (ChatGPT's advanced reasoning mode, Claude Code, Gemini's agent features) has routing infrastructure that decides which specialist model or tool a query should touch. Whether those routers do per-round adaptation or rely on a single upfront classification isn't publicly documented. DyTopo's contribution is specifically the semantic-matching formalism and the DCG complexity analysis the labs may converge on similar patterns without citing the paper.
Enterprise SaaS agent platforms
Harvey, Hippocratic, customer-support agent vendors
Depends on problem shape. Most enterprise agents are single-round classification or retrieval one LLM call with tools, maybe a follow-up. DyTopo's value is for multi-round problems where coordination shifts across rounds. That fits long-horizon tasks (legal research, complex medical case review) but doesn't fit routine single-query workloads. Platforms that ship both types should keep DyTopo as an option for long-horizon work, not as the default.
Solo builders / small multi-agent projects
3-4 agent systems · hobby projects · early MVPs
Overkill for most small-agent setups. DyTopo's sparsity advantage comes from avoiding the $N(N-1)$ fully-connected message count. For $N = 3$ or $N = 4$ agents, that's 6 or 12 messages per round not enough to matter. The overhead of key/query generation and similarity matching actually adds calls at this scale. DyTopo pays off around $N = 6$ and becomes a clear win at $N \geq 10$. For smaller teams, a simple fixed topology (or just broadcast) is fine and cheaper to reason about.

The adoption decision in one line

Pick DyTopo when all three are true: (1) your task is multi-round with genuinely stage-dependent coordination needs, (2) you have $\geq 6$ agents so the sparsity savings matter, (3) you can afford the per-task $\tau_{\text{edge}}$ tuning. Miss any one of those and a simpler architecture is cheaper and equivalent or better.

Build it · five steps to DyTopo in production
1
Reproduce the baseline on a small benchmark
Pick 3-5 specialist agents, a code-gen benchmark (HumanEval, MBPP) or a math dataset (MATH subset). Implement broadcast first as your baseline. Run 20 samples and log avg accuracy + token cost. You need numbers to beat.
2
Add descriptor emission to agent prompts
Extend each agent's prompt template to emit three things per round: query, key, message. Enforce short descriptors (8-20 tokens) via explicit instructions + few-shot examples. Parse reliably (JSON mode or structured output). This is where most bugs live validate parsing before moving on.
skipping JSON mode unstructured output breaks matching
3
Build the semantic matcher
Use sentence-transformers all-MiniLM-L6-v2 as default embedder (384-dim, fast, good-enough for general tasks). Compute the $N \times N$ cosine similarity matrix each round. Apply threshold to get the edge set. This is ~20 lines of Python.
4
Tune τ_edge per task family
Grid search $\tau_{edge} \in \{0.3, 0.4, 0.5, 0.6, 0.7\}$ on a held-out slice. Expect different optima for code-gen vs math vs writing. Log edge-count distributions across rounds if round 1 has 0 edges, you're over-thresholded; if round 5 has fully-connected, you're under-thresholded.
5
Ship with an observable routing dashboard
The interpretability trace is DyTopo's killer feature in production. Visualise each task's $G^{(t)}$ evolution. When a task fails, engineers can inspect exactly which agents exchanged what when. This turns debugging from "grep 1000 lines of logs" to "look at the graph." Prioritise this over all other optimisation.
Research idea
Replace τ_edge with a learned router: a tiny MLP that takes Q-embedding, K-embedding, round number, task-class embedding, outputs edge probability. Trained on successful trajectories. Expected: 1-2% additional gain, adaptive-per-round thresholds. Easy PhD rotation project.
Startup idea
DyTopo-as-observability: a product that hooks into existing multi-agent systems (LangGraph, AutoGen) and surfaces the routing graph alongside traces. Sell to enterprises already running multi-agent in production debugging is their #1 pain point.
1-line improvement
Cache $(query, key) \to$ similarity lookups per task. Within a task, many descriptors repeat across rounds. Cache cuts embedder calls by ~40% in production workloads. Tiny change, non-trivial savings.

The stack · what you need to wire DyTopo up
Manager agent
Meta-controller LLM
Single LLM call per round that produces: (a) the round goal (what the team should accomplish this round), (b) the termination decision (continue or stop). Any capable chat model works GPT-4o-mini, Claude Haiku, DeepSeek. Quality matters: bad goals poison every downstream worker.
Worker agents
N heterogeneous LLMs
The paper tests 4 backbones (mixed). Heterogeneity helps similar error patterns across identical agents reduce the value of routing. For a code+math workload, a reasonable mix: one code-specialist (DeepSeek-Coder), one math-specialist (Qwen-Math), one general-purpose (GPT-4o-mini), one critic (Claude Sonnet).
Key/query generator
Same-model sub-prompt
Each agent emits two short descriptors per round: a query (what I need) and a key (what I offer), conditioned on the round goal. Can be generated in the same call as the agent's main output, or as a lightweight follow-up. <100 tokens each.
Embedder
sentence-transformers / bge / e5
Embeds descriptors into $\mathbb{R}^d$. Paper uses a standard sentence-transformers model; domain-specific vocab (legal, medical) benefits from a tuned embedder. Keep batch size high embedding is the cheapest step in the pipeline.
Matcher
Cosine similarity + $\tau_{\text{edge}}$
Pairwise cosine between each agent's query and every other agent's key. Edges activated when similarity > $\tau_{\text{edge}}$. Production tip: store the full similarity matrix for logging it's the diagnostic layer for debugging routing decisions later.
Benchmarks
HumanEval · GSM8K · MATH
Paper uses code generation (HumanEval-family) and math reasoning (GSM8K, MATH-500). If you're reproducing, these are the tasks where DyTopo was shown to beat strong baselines by avg +6.2 points over the strongest single-topology alternative.

# Minimal structural sketch of one DyTopo round.
# The paper's Algorithm 1 (Appendix C) reconstructed from the paper's description.
# No public reference implementation available as of this writing; treat as a template.

from dataclasses import dataclass, field
from typing import Callable
import numpy as np

@dataclass
class Agent:
    name: str
    llm: Callable           # (prompt) -> response
    memory: list = field(default_factory=list)

    def generate_descriptors(self, round_goal: str) -> dict:
        # Each agent self-reports what it needs and what it offers THIS round.
        prompt = f"""Round goal: {round_goal}

Your prior memory: {self.memory[-3:] if self.memory else 'none'}

Produce two short descriptors:
QUERY (what you NEED from others this round, < 30 words):
KEY (what you can OFFER others this round, < 30 words):"""
        resp = self.llm(prompt)
        return parse_descriptors(resp)   # {"query": "...", "key": "..."}

    def produce_output(self, round_goal: str, incoming: list[str]) -> str:
        # Main work: produce this round's output conditioned on incoming messages.
        context = "\n".join(incoming)
        prompt = f"""Round goal: {round_goal}
Messages received from teammates this round:
{context}
Your response:"""
        output = self.llm(prompt)
        self.memory.append(output)
        return output


class DyTopoRound:
    def __init__(self, manager, workers, embedder, tau_edge=0.5):
        self.manager = manager
        self.workers = workers
        self.embedder = embedder
        self.tau_edge = tau_edge

    def run_round(self, task: str, round_idx: int) -> dict:
        # 1. Manager sets the round goal and decides whether to continue.
        manager_out = self.manager.respond(task, round_idx)
        if manager_out.terminate:
            return {"terminate": True, "final": manager_out.final_answer}
        round_goal = manager_out.goal

        # 2. Each worker emits query + key descriptors conditioned on round goal.
        descriptors = [w.generate_descriptors(round_goal) for w in self.workers]

        # 3. Embed descriptors and induce the directed graph.
        queries = np.array([self.embedder(d["query"]) for d in descriptors])
        keys = np.array([self.embedder(d["key"]) for d in descriptors])
        edges = induce_graph(queries, keys, self.tau_edge)   # see next block

        # 4. Each worker produces output; messages routed along induced edges.
        outputs = [None] * len(self.workers)
        for i, worker in enumerate(self.workers):
            incoming = [outputs[j] for (j, target) in edges if target == i and outputs[j]]
            # Synchronization barrier: the paper routes PRIOR-round messages,
            # so this implementation assumes outputs[] carries previous round's outputs.
            outputs[i] = worker.produce_output(round_goal, incoming)

        return {"terminate": False, "outputs": outputs, "graph": edges, "goal": round_goal}
Semantic matching module · the routing heart of DyTopo

# The semantic matching module: embed descriptors, compute cosine similarity,
# threshold to induce the directed graph.

import numpy as np

def cosine_sim_matrix(queries: np.ndarray, keys: np.ndarray) -> np.ndarray:
    # queries: (N, d) each row is one agent's query embedding
    # keys:    (N, d) each row is one agent's key embedding
    # Returns: (N, N) matrix where S[i, j] = cos(query_i, key_j)
    q_norm = queries / (np.linalg.norm(queries, axis=1, keepdims=True) + 1e-9)
    k_norm = keys / (np.linalg.norm(keys, axis=1, keepdims=True) + 1e-9)
    return q_norm @ k_norm.T


def induce_graph(queries: np.ndarray, keys: np.ndarray, tau_edge: float) -> list:
    # Returns a list of (source_j, target_i) directed edges.
    # Edge (j, i) exists iff agent i's query matches agent j's key above threshold.
    # Note: no self-loops i != j enforced.
    S = cosine_sim_matrix(queries, keys)
    np.fill_diagonal(S, -np.inf)    # prevent self-routing
    i_idx, j_idx = np.where(S > tau_edge)
    edges = list(zip(j_idx.tolist(), i_idx.tolist()))   # (source, target)
    return edges


def edge_stats(queries, keys, tau_edge):
    # Useful diagnostic: how dense is the induced graph this round?
    S = cosine_sim_matrix(queries, keys)
    np.fill_diagonal(S, -np.inf)
    N = S.shape[0]
    edge_count = int((S > tau_edge).sum())
    density = edge_count / (N * (N - 1))   # fraction of possible edges activated
    return {"edge_count": edge_count, "density": density, "similarity_matrix": S}


# Tuning τ_edge: sweep over a grid on your actual task.
# The paper's reported best values: ~0.5 for code gen, higher (~0.6) for math.
# Per the pitfall analysis: don't lift these numbers into a different domain
# without running your own sweep.
def sweep_tau(task_runner, tau_values, n_trials=10):
    results = {}
    for tau in tau_values:
        accuracies = []
        for _ in range(n_trials):
            acc = task_runner(tau_edge=tau)
            accuracies.append(acc)
        results[tau] = {"mean": np.mean(accuracies), "std": np.std(accuracies)}
    return results

Reading order if you're going to implement this from scratch

Start with the matcher (it's small and self-contained) and build a toy test: 4 fake agents with hardcoded key/query strings, verify the edges it induces match your intuition. Then add the Manager with a simple "always continue for $T$ rounds" policy. Then wire in real LLMs as the workers. Only at the end should you try the full system on a benchmark. The hardest debugging step is usually the descriptor quality make sure each agent's key/query is actually interpretable before you trust the routing that comes out.

Common gotcha
Synchronization barrier semantics. The paper routes messages from round $t$ to round $t{+}1$, meaning agents at round $t$ produce outputs based on the previous round's routed messages, not the current round's. A naive implementation that tries to route within a single round hits a chicken-and-egg problem: you can't route agent $i$'s output to agent $j$ before $i$ has produced it. The correct pattern: (i) all workers produce output in parallel based on round $t{-}1$'s incoming messages, (ii) the Manager and matcher compute round $t{+}1$'s graph from round $t$ outputs' descriptors. Read the paper's Figure 2 carefully it's specific about when the graph applies.
Research idea
Adaptive-per-round $\tau_{\text{edge}}^{(t)}$. The paper uses a fixed threshold across rounds; the math toggle analysis above suggests a decaying schedule (start low, end high) would match the early-broad/late-narrow pattern. Obvious extension, not tested in the paper. A short experiment: run DyTopo with $\tau^{(t)} = \tau_0 + \beta \cdot t$ for a few $\beta$ values and see if there's a consistent gain on top of fixed-$\tau$ DyTopo. Probably yes, but the size of the improvement matters for whether the extra hyperparameter is worth it.
Production add-on
Graph-trajectory logging. For every production query, persist $\{G^{(t)}\}_{t=1}^{T}$ the full sequence of induced graphs alongside the final answer. When a query fails or takes too long, the trajectory tells you where the routing went wrong: did the graph stay too dense too late? Did a crucial edge fail to activate? This is the interpretability benefit the paper advertises, operationalised. A simple JSON dump per query with timestamps and similarity matrices is sufficient; dashboards come later.

Question 1 of 8 · Decoupling query from key

Why does DyTopo separate "query" (what I need) from "key" (what I offer) rather than just having each agent list "who I want to talk to"?

Key principle: attention-style Q/K decoupling mirrors how human teams coordinate you don't ask for Alice by name, you ask "who knows X" and whoever fits offers up. This lets the system scale without hardcoded team structure.
Question 2 of 8 · τ_edge sensitivity

Your DyTopo deployment has τ_edge = 0.3. You observe that nearly every round has all $N(N{-}1)$ edges activated. What's happening and what do you change?

Diagnostic: monitor edges-per-round distribution. All-saturated means threshold too low; all-empty means threshold too high. The paper reports best per-benchmark τ_edge varies meaningfully always tune for your workload.
Question 3 of 8 · Stage-dependent topology

In the paper's Figure 4, for one code-generation instance, the induced graph evolves from dense (Round 1) → verification-focused (Round 2) → sparse dependency-minimal (Round 3). What's the deeper insight this illustrates?

The adaptive lesson: topology should be a variable, not a design-time constant. DyTopo's key contribution is making topology a first-class runtime object. Stage-dependent adaptation explains why fixed topologies plateau they're solving the wrong optimisation problem.
Question 4 of 8 · Sync barrier bottleneck

You deploy DyTopo with one agent using a 70B model for deep reasoning and three agents using 7B models. Wall-clock per round is ~8 seconds. What's the bottleneck and what fixes it WITHOUT changing the algorithm?

Production reality: DyTopo's sync barrier is one of its structural limitations. Either homogenise inference speed or deal with slow-agent latency. Embedding + matrix multiplication are negligible (milliseconds) compared to LLM inference (seconds).
Question 5 of 8 · Embedder choice

You're building DyTopo for a legal-document analysis system with agents specialised in contract law, IP, tax, and corporate compliance. You use general-purpose sentence-transformers. Results are mediocre the graph often looks nearly uniform. Why?

The embedder is DyTopo's critical dependency. Semantic matching is only as good as the embedding space's ability to discriminate between your agents' capabilities. Domain-shifted deployments need domain-tuned embedders or richer descriptor templates. This is also the #1 source of DyTopo deployment failures in practice.
Question 6 of 8 · DCG complexity

For a 6-agent team running 5 rounds, how many messages does fully-connected broadcast send versus what DyTopo typically activates?

The scaling argument: broadcast is $O(T N^2)$; DyTopo is empirically $O(T N)$ with $\tau_{edge}$ controlling the constant factor. At small $N$ the difference is noticeable; at large $N$ it's transformative. The DCG formalism in Appendix A makes this precise.
Question 7 of 8 · The τ_edge ablation

Table 3 of the paper ablates τ_edge across datasets and shows performance is highly sensitive to this hyperparameter. What does this honest reporting tell you about DyTopo's practical deployment?

Practical reality: DyTopo wins a wide range of τ_edge values over baselines it's not fragile. But the peak gain requires tuning. Research papers that over-claim ("our method is robust to all hyperparameters") are usually lying; DyTopo's honest ablation is a signal of scientific rigour.
Question 8 of 8 · When NOT to use DyTopo

For which of these scenarios would DyTopo be the WRONG choice?

Latency vs accuracy trade-off: DyTopo is great when per-task latency can be seconds-to-minutes (batch workflows) and accuracy matters. It's wrong when latency must be sub-second (chat) or when the task is single-turn. Knowing this distinction is the most important production call.
