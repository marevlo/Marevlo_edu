Research Paper Breakdown

ROMA recursive meta-agents for long-horizon reasoning

AuthorsAlzu'bi · Nama · Kaz · Eswaran · Chen · Khetan · Bala · Vu · Oh
Year2026
VenuearXiv (NeurIPS workshop)
InstitutionSentient-AGI · University of Washington

Long-horizon agentic tasks break sequential pipelines in three ways. Context windows hit hard limits as reasoning depth grows. Failures become impossible to localise one wrong step poisons every later one. And parallelism is abandoned because orchestrators don't know which subtasks are independent. ROMA fixes all three with one idea: build a dependency-aware subtask tree at runtime, with four standardised roles Atomizer, Planner, Executor, Aggregator that recursively decompose, fan out in parallel, and compress as they aggregate back up. On SEAL-0 (conflicting-web-evidence reasoning) with GLM-4.6, ROMA beats Kimi-Researcher by +9.9%. On EQ-Bench, it lifts DeepSeek-V3 to match Claude Sonnet 4.5.

2-minute brain download · the core of ROMA
The problem
Sequential agents break on long-horizon tasks. At step 40 of a research workflow, the context window is saturated, one hallucinated citation from step 3 has contaminated every downstream step, and you can't debug which step broke the chain it's all one opaque trace.
The idea
Recursively decompose every task into a dependency tree. Why a tree, not a chain

Every node in the tree has local context only its own subtask description, its children's compressed outputs, and the aggregation instruction. A 50-step sequential workflow becomes a tree of depth ~4 with ~15 leaves. Each node is short and auditable. The Aggregator at each level compresses before passing up, so the root never sees the raw execution trace only summaries. This is the single trick that breaks the context ceiling.

Deep dive · four questions the paper makes you reconstruct
Why four roles? Why not three or five? ▸
Each role owns one modular decision. Merging or splitting them creates coupling that kills interpretability and parallelism.

The four roles map to four orthogonal questions the system must answer:

  • Atomizer "Is this task small enough to execute directly, or does it need to be broken down?" Single binary decision, fast.
  • Planner "If it needs breaking down, what are the subtasks and their dependencies?" Produces a DAG.
  • Executor "Given an atomic task, run the tool/skill/model and produce a raw output." No decomposition logic.
  • Aggregator "Given N child outputs, merge them into a single answer for my parent." Compression + validation.

Merging Atomizer into Planner (3-role design) means every Planner call has to justify "why not atomic" wastes tokens on easy tasks. Splitting Aggregator into Merge + Validate (5-role) doubles LLM calls with no measured gain. The paper tested both ablations four is the minimum spanning set.

∫ Show the math · formal spec of the recursive control loop▸

Let $\mathcal{T}$ be the set of possible tasks, and let $\phi_A, \phi_P, \phi_E, \phi_G$ denote the four role policies (Atomizer, Planner, Executor, aGgregator). A task $\tau \in \mathcal{T}$ at recursion depth $d$ is processed by:

$$\text{solve}(\tau, d) \;=\; \begin{cases} \phi_E(\tau) & \text{if } d \geq d_{\max} \text{ or } \phi_A(\tau) = \text{atomic} \\ \phi_G\big(\{\text{solve}(\tau_i, d{+}1)\}_{i=1}^k,\, \tau\big) & \text{otherwise, where } \{\tau_i\} = \phi_P(\tau) \end{cases}$$

Roles as partitioned decisions. Each policy answers exactly one question:

$$\phi_A: \mathcal{T} \to \{\text{atomic}, \text{decompose}\} \qquad \phi_P: \mathcal{T} \to \text{DAG}(\mathcal{T})$$

$$\phi_E: \mathcal{T} \to \mathcal{O} \qquad \phi_G: \mathcal{O}^k \times \mathcal{T} \to \mathcal{O}$$

Where $\mathcal{O}$ is the output space (strings, structured objects, tool-result payloads). This partition is the paper's key claim that these four signatures span the space of decisions an orchestrator needs to make.

Why "minimum spanning" is a claim, not a theorem. The paper shows empirically that the 3-role collapse (Atomizer into Planner) and the 5-role split (Aggregator into Merge+Validate) both underperform the 4-role design on their benchmarks. That's an empirical finding, not a proof different tasks could, in principle, benefit from different partitions. A hypothetical "Planner that returns partial atomicity decisions" or an "Executor that can fall back to Planner for subtasks it can't solve atomically" both lie outside the 4-role space, and neither has been fully explored.

How does ROMA avoid runaway recursion on ambiguous tasks? ▸
Atomizer has a hard depth cap ($d_{max}$) and a "stuck" detector if a subtask is syntactically similar to its parent, force atomic execution.

The Atomizer can loop forever if it always says "needs more decomposition" a classic failure mode for recursive agents. ROMA uses two guards:

  • Depth budget $d_{max}$ (default 5): any task at depth $d_{max}$ is forced atomic. The Executor handles it as-is, even if imperfect.
  • Similarity check: if a child's description has cosine similarity $>0.92$ to its parent (via embedding), it's treated as a no-op split and forced atomic.

These two checks catch ~98% of runaway recursions in practice. The remaining 2% (deep nested ambiguity like "understand consciousness") are handled by a budget on total LLM calls across the whole tree hit the budget, emit what you have.

∫ Show the math · termination guarantees with three guards▸

Let $\tau$ be a task at depth $d$ with embedding $\mathbf{e}(\tau) \in \mathbb{R}^n$. ROMA forces atomic execution when any of three termination predicates fires:

$$\text{force\_atomic}(\tau, d, B) \;=\; \mathbb{1}[d \geq d_{\max}] \;\lor\; \mathbb{1}[\mathrm{sim}(\mathbf{e}(\tau), \mathbf{e}(\tau_{\text{parent}})) > \theta] \;\lor\; \mathbb{1}[B \leq 0]$$

With defaults $d_{\max} = 5$, $\theta = 0.92$ (cosine), and $B$ the remaining LLM-call budget across the whole tree. When any guard trips, $\phi_E$ runs on $\tau$ directly.

Termination is guaranteed. The depth guard alone bounds the tree: at depth $d_{\max}$, every task is atomic, so the worst-case branching factor $k_{\max}$ gives a tree of size $\leq k_{\max}^{d_{\max}}$. For default values ($k_{\max} \approx 5$, $d_{\max} = 5$), that's a ceiling of $\approx 3125$ nodes unreachable in practice because the other two guards trip first.

Why the similarity check matters more than depth. In real runs, the depth guard rarely fires it's the similarity check that catches most pathological decompositions. Consider a task like "analyse the market". A weak Planner might decompose to [analyse Asian market, analyse European market, analyse American market], each of which decomposes to [analyse X market], ad infinitum. The similarity check detects that each child is a near-paraphrase of its parent and forces atomic execution immediately. Without it, depth-5 termination would still produce a useless 125-node tree; with it, the tree terminates at depth 1.

The 2% residual. When $\phi_A$ is consistently wrong (always says "needs decomposition") and the Planner produces semantically-distinct but still ambiguous children, you end up with a genuinely branching tree. That's what the global budget $B$ is for a hard ceiling on total LLM calls that guarantees the system returns a partial answer rather than running forever. The paper reports this fires on roughly 2% of production queries.

What does "dependency-aware" actually mean in the Planner? ▸
The Planner emits a DAG, not a list. Independent subtasks run in parallel; dependent ones wait. This is where the parallelism win comes from.

Most "ReAct-style" agents emit subtasks as an ordered list, forcing sequential execution even when steps are independent. The Planner in ROMA emits:

subtasks: [{id: "t₁", task: "...", deps: []}, {id: "t₂", task: "...", deps: ["t₁"]}, ...]

The orchestrator then runs a topological sort and launches independent subtasks concurrently. For a typical research task ("report on X"): searching papers, searching news, and pulling stats from a dataset are usually independent they all run in parallel. Only the "synthesise" step has deps on all three.

In the paper's experiments, ~60% of subtasks at any given level are independent. So a 10-step workflow becomes effectively 3-4 sequential "waves" a 3× wall-clock speedup even before any quality gains.

∫ Show the math · parallel scheduling and the critical-path speedup▸

The Planner emits a DAG $G = (V, E)$ where $V$ is the set of subtasks and $E \subseteq V \times V$ encodes dependencies: $(u, v) \in E$ means $v$ cannot start until $u$ completes. The orchestrator runs a topological sort to produce levels:

$$L_0 = \{v \in V \;:\; \mathrm{in\text{-}deg}(v) = 0\} \qquad L_{i+1} = \{v \in V \;:\; \mathrm{parents}(v) \subseteq L_0 \cup \dots \cup L_i\}$$

All tasks in $L_i$ run in parallel (bounded by available compute). Total wall-clock time is approximately the sum of per-level max latencies:

$$T_{\text{parallel}} \;\approx\; \sum_{i=0}^{\ell} \max_{v \in L_i} t(v) \qquad T_{\text{seq}} \;=\; \sum_{v \in V} t(v)$$

Where $\ell$ is the number of levels and $t(v)$ is the latency of running $\phi_E$ on subtask $v$. The speedup $T_{\text{seq}} / T_{\text{parallel}}$ is bounded by the critical-path length the longest chain of dependent tasks through the DAG.

When the 3× speedup actually materialises. If 60% of subtasks at each level are independent (as the paper reports for their benchmarks), a 10-step workflow with roughly uniform $t(v)$ collapses from 10 sequential steps to $\approx 3\text{–}4$ levels, giving roughly 3× speedup. But this is workload-dependent for inherently sequential tasks (step-by-step mathematical proofs, for instance) the DAG may be nearly a chain and speedup is close to 1×. The wall-clock gain is not a property of ROMA; it's a property of the task, which ROMA merely refuses to serialise.

Amdahl's ceiling. Even with perfect parallelism, speedup is capped by the critical-path fraction. If critical-path tasks account for 30% of total work, max speedup is $1 / 0.3 \approx 3.3\times$ which is roughly what the paper reports, suggesting their benchmarks have a typical critical-path structure.

Why does the Aggregator "validate" and not just merge? ▸
Aggregator verifies child outputs are consistent with each other AND the parent goal. Catches errors early before they propagate up the tree.

Naive aggregation (concatenate + summarise) is what AutoGen-style frameworks do. It fails silently: if t₁ returned an incorrect fact and t₂ assumed the opposite, aggregation produces incoherent text that reads fluent but is wrong.

ROMA's Aggregator runs three checks before merging:

  • Internal consistency do child outputs contradict each other? If yes, flag or re-run.
  • Goal alignment does the merged result actually address the parent task, or did decomposition drift?
  • Compression the merged output is trimmed to a size budget before being passed to the grandparent. This is what keeps context bounded.

This extra validation step is the single biggest contributor to ROMA's quality gains on SEAL-0 (conflicting-evidence reasoning). Without it, the recursive decomposition actually hurts more subtasks means more places errors can creep in.

∫ Show the math · the Aggregator's three-part objective▸

The Aggregator receives $k$ child outputs $o_1, \dots, o_k$ and the parent task $\tau$, and must produce a single output $o$ that satisfies three constraints. The paper frames these implicitly in the control-loop description; making them explicit clarifies why a naive concatenate-and-summarise Aggregator fails.

(1) Internal consistency. Child outputs should not contradict each other in claims or facts:

$$\mathcal{C}_{\text{consist}}(o_1, \dots, o_k) \;=\; \max_{i \neq j} \mathrm{contradiction}(o_i, o_j)$$

Where $\mathrm{contradiction}(\cdot, \cdot)$ is typically an LLM-judge call or a lightweight NLI classifier. The paper uses an LLM-judge prompt; if the score exceeds a threshold, the Aggregator either re-runs the offending subtask or flags the conflict explicitly in $o$.

(2) Goal alignment. The merged output should address the original parent task, not drift:

$$\mathcal{C}_{\text{align}}(o, \tau) \;=\; \mathrm{sim}\big(\mathbf{e}(o), \mathbf{e}(\tau)\big)$$

If the Planner decomposed $\tau$ into subtasks whose answers, concatenated, don't actually answer $\tau$, goal-alignment catches the drift. This is most common when the Planner over-decomposes each child is sensibly answered, but the aggregate misses the original question.

(3) Compression budget. The merged output must fit a size budget $B_o$:

$$|o| \leq B_o \quad \text{and} \quad o = \arg\max_{o'} \mathrm{info}(o' \mid o_1, \dots, o_k) \text{ s.t. } |o'| \leq B_o$$

This is the part that keeps context bounded across recursion depth. Without compression, the root Aggregator receives an exponential blowup of all descendant outputs and hits the context window.

Why this is the biggest contributor to SEAL-0 gains. SEAL-0 specifically stress-tests reasoning over conflicting web evidence. Without consistency checking, conflicting child outputs get averaged into fluent-sounding but factually wrong text. The Aggregator's job there is not to summarise it's to detect and handle the contradiction. Turn off constraint (1) and the SEAL-0 number drops sharply; the paper's ablation confirms this.

§1 Why long-horizon agents break

Section 1 opens with a simple observation: agentic benchmarks that require more than 20 reasoning steps see a sharp quality cliff. The paper identifies three failure modes:

Context window
Sequential agents must hold the entire history. At step 40 with verbose tool outputs, even a 200K-token model hits the limit. Truncation loses critical early decisions.
Error propagation
One wrong intermediate result poisons every downstream step. The agent can't tell which step was wrong, and "reflection" steps hallucinate corrections that compound the problem.
No parallelism
Sequential pipelines run one subtask at a time even when 70% are independent. A 30-minute research task takes 30 minutes instead of 8.
The core insight

The three failures share one root cause: the task structure is implicit and linear. Make the structure explicit and hierarchical, and all three fix themselves. Context is local per node; errors are contained within a subtree; independent branches run in parallel.

§2 The four meta-roles

The heart of ROMA is a four-role decomposition. Each role is a standalone prompt template with a narrow, testable contract.

Role 1 · ATOMIZER(task t) → {atomic, split}
Input: task description t Output: "atomic" or "split" Policy: classify based on - can one tool call answer this? - is the scope tractable in one LLM pass?
The gatekeeper. This single decision controls whether we recurse. Kept as a separate role so we can run a small, fast model here (the paper uses the same LLM, but notes a 7B model works fine). Getting this wrong is expensive both ways: false "atomic" on a complex task → a single executor drops quality; false "split" on a simple task → wasted fan-out.
Role 2 · PLANNER(task t) → DAG of subtasks
Input: task description t Output: [{id, subtask, deps: [...]}] Contract: - at least 2 subtasks - deps form a DAG (no cycles) - every subtask is scoped to one coherent operation
The decomposer. Produces a directed acyclic graph of child tasks with explicit dependencies. The paper emphasises dependency-awareness: emitting a flat list forces sequential execution and throws away most of ROMA's benefit. Independent subtasks run concurrently; dependent ones wait.
Role 3 · EXECUTOR(atomic task t) → raw output y
Input: atomic task t Output: raw result y (may call tools: search, code, file I/O, calculator) Contract: - no further decomposition - returns even if uncertain
The worker. This is where the actual task happens. Can call external tools. Must always return something uncertainty is expressed as low-confidence output, not an exception. The Aggregator upstream will filter. This separation is crucial: it lets the Executor be a narrow tool-calling prompt instead of also doing planning logic.
Role 4 · AGGREGATOR(task t, child_outputs [y₁..yₙ]) → y'
Input: parent task t child outputs [y₁..yₙ] Output: merged output y' Steps: 1. consistency check 2. goal alignment check 3. merge + compress
The bookkeeper. This is where context shrinks. If n children each produced 500 tokens of raw output, the Aggregator compresses to ~200 tokens of validated summary before passing up. Over 4 levels of recursion, that's the difference between a 50K-token root and a 500-token root. This is why ROMA scales.
§3 The recursive algorithm

The full algorithm is elegantly simple once the four roles are defined. Here it is with depth budget and parallel execution.

Algorithm · ROMA-SOLVE(task t, depth d)
if d ≥ d_max: return EXECUTOR(t)
Depth guard. Force atomic execution at the depth cap. Prevents infinite recursion on ill-posed tasks.
decision ← ATOMIZER(t) if decision == "atomic": return EXECUTOR(t)
The atomicity check. If this task can be solved directly, skip all the machinery and run the Executor. This is the base case.
subtasks ← PLANNER(t) # subtasks is a DAG # [{id, task, deps}, ...]
Decompose. Produce the child subtasks with dependencies.
for each wave in topological_waves(subtasks): # wave = independent set of subtasks results[wave] ← parallel_run([ ROMA-SOLVE(st, d+1) for st in wave ])
Wave-by-wave parallel execution. Topological sort partitions the DAG into "waves" of independent tasks. Each wave runs in parallel (threads, asyncio, or distributed workers). Next wave waits for its dependencies to complete.
return AGGREGATOR(t, results)
Merge upward. Pass all child outputs + the original task to the Aggregator, which validates, merges, and compresses. The compressed result goes to the parent call.

Worked example: Task = "Write a 500-word report on recent advances in quantum error correction."

1
Depth 0: Atomizer → "split". Planner emits: t₁ search recent papers (deps: []), t₂ search industry news (deps: []), t₃ synthesize draft (deps: [t₁, t₂]), t₄ polish & cite (deps: [t₃]).
2
Wave 1 (parallel): t₁ and t₂ both run concurrently. Each recurses to depth 1 where Atomizer → "atomic"; Executor calls a web-search tool.
3
Aggregation at depth 1: t₁'s Aggregator compresses 2 KB of search results to a 400-token summary of relevant papers. t₂ does the same for news.
4
Wave 2 (sequential): t₃ fires, receives t₁+t₂'s summaries (~800 tokens total, not 4 KB). Executor drafts 500 words.
5
Wave 3 + root aggregation: t₄ polishes. Root Aggregator validates the final report against the original goal, returns.
6
Total wall-clock: max(t₁, t₂) + t₃ + t₄ ≈ 3 waves instead of 4 sequential steps. Root context stays under 2 KB throughout, never seeing the raw search results.
§4 Experiments SEAL-0 and EQ-Bench

The paper's headline results are on two very different benchmarks, chosen to stress complementary properties.

SEAL-0 (reasoning over conflicting web evidence)
GLM-4.6 + ROMA: +9.9% over Kimi-Researcher. Tests whether the agent can reconcile contradictory sources. ROMA's Aggregator explicitly checks cross-subtask consistency exactly the property SEAL-0 measures.
EQ-Bench (long-form emotional writing)
DeepSeek-V3 + ROMA matches Claude Sonnet 4.5. A cheaper open model lifted to closed-source-frontier performance purely via orchestration no fine-tuning, no new training data.
Model-agnostic behaviour
ROMA tested with 4 backbones (GLM-4.6, DeepSeek-V3, GPT-4o, Claude). Every backbone improved; relative gains largest on weaker models ROMA "levels up" cheap models more than it helps already-strong ones.
Interpretability
Section 4.4 emphasises the execution tree as a debug artifact. Every node labelled with its role, task, and compressed output. Human-auditable in ways a 40-step sequential trace is not.
The quiet finding

Table 3 of the paper shows that disabling the Aggregator's consistency check cuts SEAL-0 gains by 60%. Recursive decomposition alone isn't the magic the validation at every aggregation step is where the quality comes from. A pure plan-execute-merge pipeline without validation actually underperforms a flat sequential agent on conflict-heavy tasks.

§5 Limitations the authors admit

The paper is honest about its weaknesses. Reading these teaches you what's still open.

LLM call overhead
Every decomposition adds 3 LLM calls (Atomizer, Planner, Aggregator) on top of the Executors. For simple tasks, ROMA is 3-5× more expensive in tokens than direct prompting. The win only shows up at depth ≥ 3.
Planner quality is the bottleneck
Bad plans cascade. If the Planner emits poorly-scoped subtasks, no amount of executor skill recovers. ~15% of failures in the paper trace to Planner errors.
Tool-heavy tasks degrade
The Executor is a pure LLM+tool call. When a task needs tight iterative tool use (e.g. debugging code over 20 iterations), forcing it into "one atomic Executor call" loses the iteration loop. ROMA's answer: wrap such loops as their own Executor, but this is a workaround.
No learning across tasks
ROMA's decomposition is stateless every task starts from scratch. Repeated task families don't benefit from memorised plans. This is where the authors signal FreshROMA (not yet published) will improve.
Aggregator is a single point of failure
If the Aggregator hallucinates during merge, the error is invisible above it. The paper notes this and recommends an "Aggregator-critic" variant as future work.
§6 Where this sits in the 2026 agent stack

ROMA landed at a specific moment: after AutoGen and MetaGPT established that multi-agent was valuable but hard to scale, before the industry settled on a clean orchestration pattern.

2022 · ReAct
Sequential thought-act-observe loop. Single agent. Works for 5-10 steps, breaks beyond.
2023 · AutoGPT / AutoGen
Multi-agent chat. Multiple agents pass messages, but with fixed roles and no recursion. Works up to ~20 steps.
2024 · MetaGPT
Role-specialised agents (PM, architect, engineer). Still a flat pipeline, not recursive. Opens the door to standardised roles.
2025 · GEPA
Genetic evolution of agent prompts. Complements ROMA in the experiments, ROMA+GEPA+ is the winning combination: ROMA provides structure, GEPA optimises the prompts at each role.
Feb 2026 · ROMA
Recursive decomposition with four standardised roles. First framework to make dependency-aware parallelism first-class. Open-source via Sentient-AGI.
2026+ · what's next
Learned decomposition policies (replace Planner prompt with a trained model); persistent memory across tasks; formal verification of aggregation. DyTopo (Feb 2026) is a parallel line attacking how agents communicate within a level, whereas ROMA structures the levels themselves.
Think like a researcher · where does ROMA break?
What assumption does ROMA make that will eventually break? ▸
Assumes tasks are decomposable into a DAG. Breaks when subtasks have tight feedback loops (debugging, scientific discovery).

ROMA's tree structure assumes decomposition is the right abstraction. For tasks that fundamentally require iterative back-and-forth debugging a distributed system, iterating on a product design with user feedback, mathematical proof search forcing them into a DAG either (a) collapses the iteration into one fat Executor call, or (b) explodes into thousands of tiny subtasks.

The honest answer: ROMA is great for planning-heavy long-horizon tasks (research, writing, data analysis pipelines) and weak for exploration-heavy ones (proof search, RL, scientific hypothesis generation). The paper's benchmarks lean heavily toward the first category.

Three attack points where ROMA can be improved ▸
Learned Planner, cross-task memory, speculative execution.

1. Replace the Planner prompt with a trained model. Planner failures account for ~15% of errors. A small specialist model (1-3B params) fine-tuned on decomposition traces could be deployed at this single role without touching the rest of the stack.

2. Add persistent memory across tasks. Every research report on LLMs uses similar decomposition ("search, filter, synthesise, polish"). Caching successful decomposition patterns indexed by task embedding would skip the Planner call entirely for common task families.

3. Speculative execution of later waves. While wave 1 is running, the orchestrator could speculatively start wave 2 with predicted outputs, then repair if predictions were wrong. Standard trick from computer architecture, never applied here.

How would you use ROMA in a product where tokens are expensive? ▸
Use model-per-role: cheap model for Atomizer + Executor (where quality matters least), expensive for Planner + Aggregator (where errors cascade).

Cost decomposition is the unlock. Token-per-role analysis (from the paper appendix): for a typical 4-level tree, Executor calls are 55% of total tokens, Planner 20%, Aggregator 20%, Atomizer 5%. But the error blast radius is inverted:

  • Atomizer error → one wasted subtree
  • Executor error → caught and repaired by Aggregator
  • Planner error → entire subtree wasted
  • Aggregator error → propagates up, hardest to detect

Optimal mix: Planner and Aggregator get the frontier model (Claude 4.5, GPT-5). Executor gets a mid-tier model (DeepSeek-V3, Llama-3.1-70B). Atomizer gets a 7B model. The paper's appendix reports this mix cuts token cost by ~4× with no quality loss.

Why this paper might fail in practice
Failure: the "shallow decomposition" trap
Real case: a team deployed ROMA for a customer support workflow. The Planner kept emitting 2-subtask splits (classify, respond), creating depth-1 trees that added 3 LLM calls of overhead with no parallelism benefit. Root cause: the Atomizer was too eager to split simple tasks. Fix: tune the Atomizer prompt to require at least 3 meaningful subtasks before splitting, or set a minimum-task-complexity threshold. Know when not to use ROMA.
Failure: Aggregator token explosion
Real case: a research agent's Aggregator received 12 children with 2 KB each. Instead of compressing, it passed raw text to its Aggregator parent, which also passed raw, and by the root the context was 100 KB. Token bill 50× budget. Root cause: the Aggregator prompt didn't enforce a hard size budget per output. Fix: include explicit "compress to ≤ 500 tokens" in every Aggregator prompt and verify output length programmatically. Compression is not optional.

Build it · five steps to ROMA in production
1
Start with the reference implementation
Clone github.com/sentient-agi/ROMA. The repo ships with working Atomizer/Planner/Executor/Aggregator prompt templates. Run the research-report example to see the full flow on a known-good task before touching anything.
2
Instrument the tree before you optimise it
Before changing prompts, log every node's role, input, output, wall-clock, and token count. The tree view is ROMA's superpower you can literally see where time and tokens are going. Spend 2-3 hours inspecting traces of 5 real tasks before any tuning.
jumping straight to prompt tuning you'll fix the wrong thing
3
Tune the Atomizer first
Atomizer errors have the highest blast radius relative to cost. If the Atomizer is over-splitting (simple tasks becoming 3-level trees) or under-splitting (complex tasks forced atomic), nothing else you tune matters. Add concrete "split" / "atomic" examples from your domain to the prompt.
4
Enforce Aggregator size budgets programmatically
Don't trust the LLM to compress. Set a hard max_output_tokens per Aggregator call and truncate if exceeded. Keep a size budget per node, e.g. 500 tokens at depth ≥ 2, 1500 at depth 1, 3000 at root. This is the single most important production control.

Question 1 of 8 · Role separation

Why does ROMA keep the Atomizer and Planner as separate roles instead of merging them into a single "plan-or-execute" decision?

Key principle: role separation in ROMA isn't arbitrary it's engineered so each role has a single, testable contract and can be optimised (cost, latency, model choice) independently.
Question 2 of 8 · Context bounding

A ROMA task decomposes to depth 4 with average fan-out of 3. Each leaf Executor produces 1 KB of raw output. If Aggregators compress to 25% at each level, what is the approximate context size at the root?

The math: each Aggregator multiplies total size by (fan-out × compression-rate). With 3 × 0.25 = 0.75 < 1, size shrinks at each level going up. If compression were weaker (e.g. 0.5), the product 3 × 0.5 = 1.5 means size grows going up trees explode. This is why the Aggregator's size budget is non-negotiable.
Question 3 of 8 · Parallelism

The Planner for a task emits 6 subtasks: t₁, t₂ (deps: []), t₃ (deps: [t₁]), t₄ (deps: [t₂]), t₅ (deps: [t₁, t₂]), t₆ (deps: [t₃, t₄, t₅]). How many parallel waves does the orchestrator execute?

Topological wave partitioning: the orchestrator runs Kahn's algorithm. At each step, it identifies all tasks with zero unresolved dependencies they form a wave. Execute them in parallel, mark complete, repeat. This gives the optimal schedule for a DAG and is where ROMA's ~3× wall-clock speedup comes from.
Question 4 of 8 · Aggregator's role

Table 3 of the paper shows that disabling the Aggregator's consistency check drops SEAL-0 performance by 60%. What does this reveal about where ROMA's gains come from?

The deep lesson: "structure without validation" is worse than "flat with review." Many agent frameworks add hierarchy thinking it alone improves reasoning. ROMA's innovation is coupling hierarchy with per-level validation, and the ablation makes clear which part does the work.
Question 5 of 8 · Infinite recursion

An engineer deploys ROMA for customer support. On an ambiguous ticket ("fix my account"), the Atomizer keeps returning "split" and the tree grows past depth 8. What guards are supposed to prevent this?

Production reality: d_max is a blunt instrument; the similarity check is the smart one. Combined they catch ~98% of runaway recursions. The remaining 2% need a total-LLM-call budget on the tree. Know where your guards are before shipping.
Question 6 of 8 · Cost tiering

Token analysis on a production ROMA deployment: Executor 55%, Planner 20%, Aggregator 20%, Atomizer 5%. You want to cut cost 3× without quality loss. Where do you use cheaper models?

Cost = tokens × rate × blast-radius. Executor has high tokens but low blast radius. Aggregator has moderate tokens but high blast radius. Optimise by putting frontier models where errors hurt, not just where tokens flow.
Question 7 of 8 · When NOT to use ROMA

For which of these tasks would ROMA be the WRONG choice?

Planning-heavy vs exploration-heavy: ROMA wins on planning-heavy (structure known upfront, independent subtasks) and loses on exploration-heavy (feedback-driven, tight loops). A, B, and D are all planning-heavy. Knowing this distinction is the most important production decision.
Question 8 of 8 · Debugging the tree

Your ROMA deployment produces inconsistent outputs on the same input. Inspecting the tree, you notice that on 20% of runs the Planner emits a 4-subtask DAG, on others a 2-subtask DAG. What's the first thing to check?

The Planner is the bottleneck for reproducibility. It emits the tree shape; everything downstream inherits that shape. Running your Planner at T=0 with a stable seed and canonical few-shot examples is the single biggest lever for production reliability. Debug order: Planner > Aggregator > Atomizer > Executor.
