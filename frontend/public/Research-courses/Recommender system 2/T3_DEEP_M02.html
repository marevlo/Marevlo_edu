D1 / WORKED
RMSE — what a "10% improvement" actually means
↳ corresponds to M02 S1: The Million-Dollar Mistake
Real-life analogy

Imagine you're guessing people's heights. You guess 170cm for someone who is 175cm — you're off by 5cm. You guess 180cm for someone who is 165cm — you're off by 15cm. RMSE is the "average badness" of your guesses, but it punishes big errors more heavily than small ones because it squares each error before averaging. An RMSE of 10cm means "on average, my guesses are about 10cm off, with bigger misses weighted more heavily."

Root Mean Squared Error
$$\text{RMSE} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{|\Omega|}\sum_{(u,i) \in \Omega}\left(r_{ui} - \hat{r}_{ui}\right)^2}$$
$r_{ui}$The ACTUAL rating user $u$ gave item $i$ (e.g., 4 stars)
$\hat{r}_{ui}$Our model's PREDICTED rating for that same $(u,i)$ pair (e.g., 3.7 stars)
$r_{ui} - \hat{r}_{ui}$The error for this prediction. Here: $4 - 3.7 = 0.3$ stars
$(\cdot)^2$Square the error — makes big errors hurt MORE. A 2-star error costs $4\times$ as much as a 1-star error
$\Omega$The set of all (user, item) pairs with known ratings in the test set
$|\Omega|$Count of test ratings (Netflix Prize: ~2.8M test ratings)
$\sqrt{\cdot}$Square root brings the units back to "stars" instead of "stars-squared"
Worked: What did 10% RMSE improvement mean for Netflix?
Numerical
NETFLIX BASELINE

Netflix's own algorithm (Cinematch) had RMSE = 0.9525 on the test set. This means: on average, their predictions were off by ~0.95 stars on a 1–5 scale.

10% IMPROVEMENT TARGET
$$\text{Target RMSE} = 0.9525 \times (1 - 0.10) = 0.9525 \times 0.90 = 0.8573$$

The winners needed to get RMSE below 0.8573. The winning team achieved 0.8567 — barely clearing the threshold.

WHAT THIS MEANS IN PRACTICE

The improvement: average prediction error dropped from 0.95 stars to 0.86 stars. That's 0.09 stars better on average. On a 5-point scale, the difference between predicting 3.7 and predicting 3.8.

This is why Netflix never deployed it — a 0.09-star improvement in rating prediction doesn't meaningfully change which movies appear at the top of the ranked list. The metric was about prediction accuracy; the product needed ranking quality. They were measuring the wrong thing.

D2 / DERIVATION
Era 1: The user-kNN prediction formula
↳ corresponds to M02 S2: Era 1 — The Rating Era
Real-life analogy

You're at a dinner party trying to guess if you'll like a wine you haven't tasted. You look around the table. Three friends who usually agree with your wine taste have all tried it — one rated it "excellent," another "very good," another "good." But you know friend A's opinion matters most (you agree 90% of the time), friend B less (70%), and friend C least (50%). Your estimate is a weighted average of their opinions, weighted by how much you trust each one.

User-kNN prediction (mean-centered)
$$\hat{r}_{ui} = \mu_u + \frac{\sum_{v \in N_k(u)} \text{sim}(u, v) \cdot (r_{vi} - \mu_v)}{\sum_{v \in N_k(u)} |\text{sim}(u, v)|}$$
$\hat{r}_{ui}$Predicted rating: "what will user $u$ rate item $i$?"
$\mu_u$User $u$'s mean rating across all items they've rated. If Priya rates everything 3.8 on average, $\mu_u = 3.8$
$N_k(u)$The $k$ users most similar to $u$ who have also rated item $i$. These are the "dinner party friends"
$\text{sim}(u, v)$Similarity between users $u$ and $v$ — how much their past ratings agree. This is the "trust level" from the wine analogy. Typically Pearson correlation
$r_{vi}$Neighbor $v$'s actual rating of item $i$ — what your friend actually thought of the wine
$r_{vi} - \mu_v$Neighbor $v$'s DEVIATION from their own mean. If neighbor always rates 4+ but gave this movie a 2, the deviation is $2 - 4.2 = -2.2$ — a strong negative signal. This adjusts for personal baselines
Worked: Predict Priya's rating using 3 neighbors
Step by step
SETUP

Priya's mean rating: $\mu_\text{Priya} = 3.8$. She hasn't rated movie X.

Three neighbors who have rated movie X:

$$\text{Meera: sim} = 0.85, \quad r_{\text{Meera},X} = 5, \quad \mu_\text{Meera} = 4.2$$ $$\text{Arjun: sim} = 0.60, \quad r_{\text{Arjun},X} = 4, \quad \mu_\text{Arjun} = 3.5$$ $$\text{Vikram: sim} = 0.40, \quad r_{\text{Vikram},X} = 2, \quad \mu_\text{Vikram} = 3.0$$
COMPUTE DEVIATIONS
$$\text{Meera deviation} = 5 - 4.2 = +0.8 \quad \text{(she liked it MORE than usual)}$$ $$\text{Arjun deviation} = 4 - 3.5 = +0.5 \quad \text{(slightly above his average)}$$ $$\text{Vikram deviation} = 2 - 3.0 = -1.0 \quad \text{(he DISLIKED it vs his baseline)}$$
WEIGHTED SUM
$$\text{Numerator} = 0.85 \times 0.8 + 0.60 \times 0.5 + 0.40 \times (-1.0)$$ $$= 0.680 + 0.300 - 0.400 = 0.580$$ $$\text{Denominator} = |0.85| + |0.60| + |0.40| = 1.85$$
FINAL PREDICTION
$$\hat{r}_{\text{Priya},X} = 3.8 + \frac{0.580}{1.85} = 3.8 + 0.314 = \boxed{4.11}$$

Interpretation: Priya's predicted rating is 4.11 — slightly above her mean of 3.8. The strong positive signal from Meera (her most similar neighbor) outweighs Vikram's negative deviation, but Vikram pulls it down from what Meera alone would predict.

D3 / DERIVATION
Era 2: Matrix factorization loss + SGD update
↳ corresponds to M02 S3: Era 2 — The Netflix Prize
Real-life analogy

Imagine every person has hidden taste scores on 50 dimensions — how much they like "action vs drama," "fast-paced vs slow," "visually stunning vs dialogue-driven," etc. Every movie also has scores on the same 50 dimensions. A person's rating of a movie is just the alignment between their taste vector and the movie's attribute vector — a dot product. MF discovers these hidden dimensions from the rating data alone, without anyone labeling what the dimensions mean.

Matrix Factorization loss (FunkSVD / regularized MSE)
$$\mathcal{L} = \sum_{(u,i) \in \Omega}\left(r_{ui} - \mathbf{u}_u^\top \mathbf{v}_i\right)^2 + \lambda\left(\|\mathbf{u}_u\|^2 + \|\mathbf{v}_i\|^2\right)$$
$\mathbf{u}_u \in \mathbb{R}^k$User $u$'s embedding — a vector of $k$ numbers representing their hidden taste. Example: $\mathbf{u}_\text{Priya} = [0.8, -0.3, 0.5, \ldots]$ where each number is a "taste dimension"
$\mathbf{v}_i \in \mathbb{R}^k$Item $i$'s embedding — a vector of $k$ numbers representing its hidden attributes. Example: $\mathbf{v}_\text{saree} = [0.9, 0.1, 0.6, \ldots]$
$\mathbf{u}_u^\top \mathbf{v}_i$Dot product = predicted rating. $\hat{r}_{ui} = \sum_{d=1}^{k} u_{u,d} \cdot v_{i,d}$. If Priya's taste vector and the saree's attribute vector point in the same direction, the dot product is high → high predicted rating
$(r_{ui} - \mathbf{u}_u^\top \mathbf{v}_i)^2$Squared prediction error — same as RMSE but without the root and average. "How wrong were we about this rating?"
$\lambda(\|\mathbf{u}_u\|^2 + \|\mathbf{v}_i\|^2)$Regularization — prevents the embeddings from growing too large. Without this, the model memorizes the training data (overfits). $\lambda$ controls how much to penalize large embeddings. Typical: $\lambda = 0.01$

SGD update rules — how the model learns

The model learns by gradient descent — for each observed rating, compute the error, then nudge the user and item embeddings to reduce that error:

SGD updates (one training step)
$$e_{ui} = r_{ui} - \mathbf{u}_u^\top \mathbf{v}_i \quad \text{(prediction error)}$$ $$\mathbf{u}_u \leftarrow \mathbf{u}_u + \eta \left(e_{ui} \cdot \mathbf{v}_i - \lambda \cdot \mathbf{u}_u\right)$$ $$\mathbf{v}_i \leftarrow \mathbf{v}_i + \eta \left(e_{ui} \cdot \mathbf{u}_u - \lambda \cdot \mathbf{v}_i\right)$$
$e_{ui}$The error — how far off the prediction was. If $r_{ui} = 5$ and $\hat{r}_{ui} = 3.2$, then $e_{ui} = 1.8$
$\eta$Learning rate — how big a step to take. Too large: overshoots. Too small: learns slowly. Typical: $\eta = 0.005$
$e_{ui} \cdot \mathbf{v}_i$Gradient of the error w.r.t. $\mathbf{u}_u$ — "move the user embedding toward the item embedding if the prediction was too low, away if too high"
$-\lambda \cdot \mathbf{u}_u$Regularization pull — shrinks the embedding toward zero to prevent overfitting
Worked: One SGD step with k=2
Step by step
SETUP

$k = 2$ (two latent dimensions). $\eta = 0.01, \lambda = 0.1$.

$$\mathbf{u}_\text{Priya} = [0.5, 0.3], \quad \mathbf{v}_\text{saree} = [0.8, 0.2], \quad r_{\text{Priya,saree}} = 5$$
PREDICT
$$\hat{r} = \mathbf{u}^\top \mathbf{v} = (0.5)(0.8) + (0.3)(0.2) = 0.40 + 0.06 = 0.46$$ $$e = r - \hat{r} = 5 - 0.46 = 4.54 \quad \text{(big error — embeddings are undertrained)}$$
UPDATE USER
$$\mathbf{u}_\text{new} = [0.5, 0.3] + 0.01 \times \left(4.54 \times [0.8, 0.2] - 0.1 \times [0.5, 0.3]\right)$$ $$= [0.5, 0.3] + 0.01 \times ([3.632, 0.908] - [0.05, 0.03])$$ $$= [0.5, 0.3] + 0.01 \times [3.582, 0.878]$$ $$= [0.5, 0.3] + [0.0358, 0.0088] = [0.536, 0.309]$$

Priya's embedding moved toward the saree's embedding — she liked it, so her taste should align more with it.

UPDATE ITEM
$$\mathbf{v}_\text{new} = [0.8, 0.2] + 0.01 \times (4.54 \times [0.5, 0.3] - 0.1 \times [0.8, 0.2])$$ $$= [0.8, 0.2] + 0.01 \times ([2.27, 1.362] - [0.08, 0.02])$$ $$= [0.8, 0.2] + [0.0219, 0.0134] = [0.822, 0.213]$$

The saree's embedding also moved toward Priya's — symmetrically, both get pulled together when the rating is positive.

D4 / DERIVATION
Era 3: BPR loss + WRMF confidence
↳ corresponds to M02 S4: Era 3 — Implicit & Ranking
Real-life analogy — BPR

Forget predicting exact ratings. Instead, think of it like a tournament. Priya clicked on item A but not item B. We don't know HOW MUCH she likes A, but we know she prefers it over B. BPR trains the model on these pairwise preferences: "A should score higher than B for Priya." Millions of such pairs form the training data. The model learns to rank, not to predict scores.

BPR — Bayesian Personalized Ranking loss
$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{BPR}} = -\sum_{(u, i, j) \in D_S} \ln \sigma\left(\hat{r}_{ui} - \hat{r}_{uj}\right) + \lambda \|\Theta\|^2$$
$(u, i, j)$A training triple: user $u$ interacted with item $i$ (positive) but NOT with item $j$ (negative). Example: Priya clicked saree ($i$), did not click running shoes ($j$)
$D_S$The set of all such triples — sampled from the data. For each user, sample a positive item and a random unobserved item
$\hat{r}_{ui} - \hat{r}_{uj}$Score difference — "how much does the model prefer item $i$ over item $j$ for user $u$?" We want this to be POSITIVE (prefer the clicked item)
$\sigma(\cdot)$Sigmoid function: $\sigma(x) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-x}}$. Squashes any real number to (0, 1). When $\hat{r}_{ui} \gg \hat{r}_{uj}$, $\sigma \approx 1$ → $\ln(1) = 0$ → no loss. When $\hat{r}_{ui} \approx \hat{r}_{uj}$, $\sigma \approx 0.5$ → moderate loss. When $\hat{r}_{ui} \ll \hat{r}_{uj}$, $\sigma \approx 0$ → $\ln(0) \rightarrow -\infty$ → huge loss
$-\ln \sigma(\cdot)$Negative log — turns the sigmoid probability into a loss. The model is penalized when it ranks the negative item above the positive one
$\Theta$All model parameters (user embeddings $U$, item embeddings $V$)

WRMF — the confidence-weighted approach

Real-life analogy — WRMF

Think of every user-item pair as having a "certainty meter." Priya bought the saree 3 times — the meter reads HIGH (we're very sure she likes it). Priya never interacted with running shoes — the meter reads LOW (we're NOT sure if she dislikes them or never saw them). WRMF trains on ALL pairs — even the zeros — but weights them by this certainty meter.

WRMF — Weighted Regularized Matrix Factorization
$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{WRMF}} = \sum_{u,i} c_{ui}\left(p_{ui} - \mathbf{u}_u^\top \mathbf{v}_i\right)^2 + \lambda\left(\|\mathbf{U}\|_F^2 + \|\mathbf{V}\|_F^2\right)$$
$p_{ui}$Binary preference: $p_{ui} = 1$ if user $u$ interacted with item $i$, else $p_{ui} = 0$. This is the TARGET — we're predicting preference, not rating
$c_{ui}$Confidence: $c_{ui} = 1 + \alpha \cdot r_{ui}$. For observed interactions: $c_{ui} = 1 + 40 \times r_{ui}$ (where $r_{ui}$ might be the click count). For unobserved: $c_{ui} = 1$ (low confidence that the preference is truly zero)
$\alpha$Confidence scaling. Higher $\alpha$ = more trust in observed interactions vs unobserved. Typical: $\alpha = 40$. If Priya clicked the saree 3 times: $c = 1 + 40 \times 3 = 121$. If she never clicked running shoes: $c = 1$. The saree signal is 121× stronger
$\sum_{u,i}$Sum over ALL user-item pairs — not just observed ones. This is the key innovation: unobserved pairs are treated as "preference = 0 with low confidence," not as missing data
$\|\cdot\|_F^2$Frobenius norm squared — sum of all squared elements. Regularizes both user and item embedding matrices
D5 / DERIVATION
Era 4: Cross-entropy loss for CTR prediction
↳ corresponds to M02 S5: Era 4 — The Generative Era
Real-life analogy

You're a weather forecaster predicting rain tomorrow. You say "80% chance of rain." It rains. How "right" were you? Cross-entropy rewards you for giving high probability to things that happened and low probability to things that didn't. If you said 80% and it rained, you're rewarded. If you said 50% and it rained, you're penalized for being uncertain. If you said 10% and it rained, you're heavily penalized for being confidently wrong.

Binary cross-entropy (CTR prediction)
$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{BCE}} = -\sum_{(u,i)} \left[y_{ui} \log \hat{p}_{ui} + (1 - y_{ui})\log(1 - \hat{p}_{ui})\right]$$
$y_{ui} \in \{0, 1\}$Did user $u$ click item $i$? 1 = yes, 0 = no. The ground truth label
$\hat{p}_{ui} \in (0, 1)$Model's predicted probability that user $u$ will click item $i$. Output of a sigmoid layer
$y_{ui} \log \hat{p}_{ui}$When user DID click ($y=1$): loss = $-\log(\hat{p})$. If model predicted $\hat{p} = 0.9$: loss = $-\log(0.9) = 0.105$ (small — good prediction). If $\hat{p} = 0.1$: loss = $-\log(0.1) = 2.303$ (large — bad prediction)
$(1-y_{ui})\log(1-\hat{p}_{ui})$When user did NOT click ($y=0$): loss = $-\log(1 - \hat{p})$. If model predicted $\hat{p} = 0.1$: loss = $-\log(0.9) = 0.105$ (good). If $\hat{p} = 0.9$: loss = $-\log(0.1) = 2.303$ (bad — predicted a click that didn't happen)
D6 / REFERENCE
Loss function comparison — all four eras
↳ corresponds to M02 S6: The One Lesson
All four losses, side by side
$$\text{Era 1/2: } \mathcal{L}_{\text{MSE}} = \sum_{(u,i) \in \Omega}(r_{ui} - \hat{r}_{ui})^2 + \lambda\|\Theta\|^2$$ $$\text{Era 3a: } \mathcal{L}_{\text{BPR}} = -\sum_{(u,i,j)} \ln \sigma(\hat{r}_{ui} - \hat{r}_{uj}) + \lambda\|\Theta\|^2$$ $$\text{Era 3b: } \mathcal{L}_{\text{WRMF}} = \sum_{u,i} c_{ui}(p_{ui} - \hat{r}_{ui})^2 + \lambda\|\Theta\|^2$$ $$\text{Era 4: } \mathcal{L}_{\text{BCE}} = -\sum_{(u,i)} [y \log \hat{p} + (1-y)\log(1-\hat{p})]$$

MSE trains on observed ratings only → overfits to seen items, can't handle implicit data. BPR trains on pairwise comparisons → learns to rank, handles implicit data, but doesn't use unobserved pairs as signal. WRMF trains on ALL pairs with confidence weighting → handles implicit data, uses unobserved pairs, but treats all unobserved pairs the same (low confidence). BCE treats recommendation as classification → works with rich feature models (DeepFM, DCN), handles context, but requires explicit positive/negative labels.

For Kartify

Kartify has implicit data (clicks, purchases — no star ratings). MSE is out. BPR or WRMF at the retrieval stage, BCE at the ranking stage. This is why Module 02's narrative ends with "Kartify is an Era 3 product with Era 4 opportunities" — the loss functions for retrieval come from Era 3; the loss function for ranking comes from Era 4.

D7 / INTERVIEW
Interview problems — loss functions & eras
↳ tests M02 concepts
Problem 1: BPR training triple
Interview
PROBLEM

Given $\hat{r}_{u,\text{saree}} = 2.1$ and $\hat{r}_{u,\text{shoes}} = 2.5$, where the user clicked the saree but not the shoes. Compute the BPR loss for this triple (ignore regularization).

SOLUTION
$$x_{uij} = \hat{r}_{ui} - \hat{r}_{uj} = 2.1 - 2.5 = -0.4$$ $$\sigma(-0.4) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{0.4}} = \frac{1}{1 + 1.492} = \frac{1}{2.492} = 0.401$$ $$\mathcal{L} = -\ln(0.401) = 0.914$$

Loss = 0.914. This is high because the model ranks the unclicked shoes above the clicked saree — it got the pair backwards. After SGD updates, the model will nudge $\hat{r}_{u,\text{saree}}$ up and $\hat{r}_{u,\text{shoes}}$ down.

Problem 2: WRMF confidence
Interview
PROBLEM

A user clicked item A 5 times and never interacted with item B. With $\alpha = 40$, compute $c_{uA}$ and $c_{uB}$, and explain what this means for training.

SOLUTION
$$c_{uA} = 1 + \alpha \cdot r_{uA} = 1 + 40 \times 5 = 201$$ $$c_{uB} = 1 + \alpha \cdot r_{uB} = 1 + 40 \times 0 = 1$$

The loss for item A ($p_{uA} = 1$) is weighted 201× more heavily than the loss for item B ($p_{uB} = 0$). This means: the model is VERY confident the user likes item A (strong pull toward $\hat{r}_{uA} = 1$), but only mildly confident the user doesn't like item B (weak pull toward $\hat{r}_{uB} = 0$). If item B is actually great but unseen, the low weight lets the model eventually score it higher — unlike MSE which would hard-code it as negative.

Problem 3: Era mismatch
Interview
PROBLEM

A colleague trains an MF model with MSE loss on Kartify's implicit click data (binary 0/1). They report "RMSE = 0.12 — excellent!" Why is this misleading?

SOLUTION

With binary labels and 99.998% zeros, predicting $\hat{r} = 0$ for everything gives:

$$\text{RMSE} = \sqrt{\frac{0.00002 \times 1^2 + 0.99998 \times 0^2}{1}} = \sqrt{0.00002} = 0.0045$$

An RMSE of 0.12 is actually worse than predicting zero for everything (0.0045). The metric is meaningless on implicit data — it rewards a model that never recommends anything. This is exactly why Era 3 invented BPR and WRMF: you need a loss function designed for implicit data, not one borrowed from explicit ratings.
