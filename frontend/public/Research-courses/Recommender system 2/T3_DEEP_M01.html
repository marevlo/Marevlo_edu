D1 / DERIVATION
The mathematics of information overload
↳ corresponds to M01 S2: The Bookstore Problem

Module 01 asked: "At what catalog size does browsing fail?" Here's the formal answer.

Real-life analogy

Imagine you're at a buffet. With 10 dishes, you can taste each one and choose your favourite — you'll find the best dish with near certainty. With 100 dishes, you can taste maybe 20 — you'll probably find a good one, but you might miss the best. With 10,000 dishes, you'd taste 0.2% of the buffet. The chance you find the single best dish for your taste? Near zero. That's the discovery probability problem.

Discovery probability

Define the discovery probability $P_d$ as the probability that a user finds at least one relevant item through random browsing:

Discovery probability under random browsing
$$P_d = 1 - \left(1 - \frac{|\mathcal{I}_{\text{rel}}|}{|\mathcal{I}|}\right)^{b}$$
$P_d$Probability of discovering at least one relevant item
$|\mathcal{I}_{\text{rel}}|$Number of items relevant to this user (items they'd like if they saw them)
$|\mathcal{I}|$Total catalog size
$b$Number of items the user browses (their "attention budget")

This formula comes from the complement rule: the probability of finding at least one relevant item = 1 minus the probability of finding none. Each browse is an independent draw without replacement (approximated as with-replacement when $|\mathcal{I}|$ is large).

Worked: Discovery probability at different catalog sizes
Numerical
SETUP

Assume a user would like 50 items in the catalog (their relevant set $|\mathcal{I}_{\text{rel}}| = 50$). They browse $b = 30$ items per session.

CATALOG = 100 ITEMS
$$P_d = 1 - \left(1 - \frac{50}{100}\right)^{30} = 1 - (0.5)^{30} = 1 - 9.3 \times 10^{-10} \approx 1.000$$

With 50 relevant items out of 100, browsing 30 virtually guarantees finding something relevant. Browse alone is sufficient.

CATALOG = 10,000 ITEMS
$$P_d = 1 - \left(1 - \frac{50}{10{,}000}\right)^{30} = 1 - (0.995)^{30} = 1 - 0.860 = 0.140$$

Only a 14% chance of discovering something relevant through browsing! The user sees 30 items out of 10,000 — and the 50 relevant ones are needles in a haystack.

CATALOG = 480,000 ITEMS (KARTIFY)
$$P_d = 1 - \left(1 - \frac{50}{480{,}000}\right)^{30} = 1 - (0.9999)^{30} = 1 - 0.9969 = 0.0031$$

0.31% chance. At Kartify's scale, random browsing is almost useless for discovery. The user would need to browse ~9,200 items to have a 50% chance of finding one they'd like. That's 9,200 ÷ 12 items per session ≈ 767 sessions. This is why recommendation exists.

The crossover point

Setting $P_d = 0.5$ and solving for $|\mathcal{I}|$: the catalog size at which a user has a coin-flip chance of finding something relevant through browsing is approximately $|\mathcal{I}| \approx \frac{b}{\ln 2} \cdot \frac{|\mathcal{I}|}{|\mathcal{I}_{\text{rel}}|}$. For $b = 30$ and $|\mathcal{I}_{\text{rel}}| = 50$: $|\mathcal{I}| \approx 433$. Beyond ~400 items, browse-only discovery drops below 50%. This is the mathematical version of Module 01's "discovery threshold" widget.

D2 / FORMALIZATION
Multi-stakeholder objective
↳ corresponds to M01 S3: Three Forces in Tension

Module 01 described three forces: user value, platform value, and item/creator exposure. Here's how this tension is expressed mathematically.

Real-life analogy

Imagine you're a restaurant manager deciding the menu order. Your diners want the best food on top (user utility). Your business wants the most profitable dishes prominent (platform revenue). Your chefs want their specialties featured (creator exposure). You can't maximise all three — a menu ordered by profit puts the expensive wine first, but a menu ordered by diner preference puts the best-reviewed starter first. The multi-stakeholder objective is how you formalise the trade-off.

Multi-stakeholder recommendation objective
$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{total}} = \underbrace{w_1 \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{user}}}_{\text{user relevance}} + \underbrace{w_2 \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{platform}}}_{\text{business value}} + \underbrace{w_3 \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{fairness}}}_{\text{exposure fairness}}$$ $$\text{subject to: } \quad g_k(\pi) \leq \tau_k \quad \forall k \in \text{guardrails}$$
$\mathcal{L}_{\text{user}}$User relevance loss — e.g., negative nDCG (we want to maximize relevance, so we negate)
$\mathcal{L}_{\text{platform}}$Business loss — e.g., negative revenue per recommendation, negative CTR
$\mathcal{L}_{\text{fairness}}$Exposure fairness loss — e.g., variance in exposure across item groups
$w_1, w_2, w_3$Weights controlling the trade-off. These are DESIGN DECISIONS, not learned parameters.
$g_k(\pi)$Guardrail function — e.g., return rate, diversity index
$\tau_k$Guardrail threshold — "return rate must not exceed 8%"
$\pi$The recommendation policy (the ranked lists for all users)

In practice, $w_1 \gg w_2 > w_3$ for most consumer platforms — user relevance dominates, business value is a secondary objective, and fairness is enforced through guardrails rather than the primary objective. But the weights are never zero. A system with $w_2 = 0$ ignores business value entirely; a system with $w_3 = 0$ creates winner-take-all dynamics that kill marketplace health.

D3 / FORMAL DEFINITION
The recommendation problem — every symbol defined
↳ corresponds to M01 S4: What a Recsys Actually Is

Module 01 defined a recommender system in natural language. Here's the mathematical formulation that every algorithm in this track instantiates.

Formal problem statement
$$\text{Given: } \mathcal{U}, \, \mathcal{I}, \, \mathcal{C}, \, R \in \mathbb{R}^{|\mathcal{U}| \times |\mathcal{I}|}$$ $$\text{Find: } f: \mathcal{U} \times \mathcal{I} \times \mathcal{C} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$$ $$\text{Such that: } L_u = \underset{i \in \mathcal{I} \setminus \mathcal{H}_u}{\text{argsort-desc}} \; f(u, i, c) \text{ maximises a ranking quality metric}$$
$\mathcal{U}$The set of all users. Think of it as a list of every registered account on Kartify. $|\mathcal{U}| = M = 2{,}300{,}000$.
$\mathcal{I}$The set of all items. Every product in Kartify's catalog. $|\mathcal{I}| = N = 480{,}000$.
$\mathcal{C}$The context space. Everything about the MOMENT of recommendation: time of day, device, current session, weather, location.
$R$The interaction matrix. An $M \times N$ table where each cell records whether (and how) user $u$ interacted with item $i$. Most cells are empty.
$f(u,i,c)$The scoring function. Takes a user, an item, and the context, and outputs a real number — the predicted "utility" of showing item $i$ to user $u$ right now. EVERY algorithm in this track defines a different $f$.
$\mathcal{H}_u$User $u$'s history — items they've already interacted with. We exclude these from recommendations (no point recommending something they already bought).
$L_u$The recommendation list for user $u$ — items sorted by decreasing $f(u,i,c)$. The top-$K$ of this list is what the user actually sees.
Real-life analogy

Think of $f(u, i, c)$ as a personal shopper's internal scoring. When Priya walks into a store ($c$ = Saturday afternoon, browsing for wedding prep), the shopper mentally scores every item: "this Banarasi saree, for Priya, right now — score 0.92." "These running shoes, for Priya, right now — score 0.03." Then they present items in score order. Every algorithm in this track is a different strategy for how that shopper assigns scores.

Two task formulations

Rating prediction (Era 1–2)
$$\hat{r}_{ui} = f(u, i) \quad \text{where } f \text{ predicts the numeric rating}$$ $$\text{Evaluated by: } \text{RMSE} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{|\Omega|}\sum_{(u,i) \in \Omega}(r_{ui} - \hat{r}_{ui})^2}$$
$\hat{r}_{ui}$Predicted rating — e.g., "we think Priya would rate this saree 4.2 stars"
$\Omega$Set of observed (user, item) pairs with known ratings — the "non-empty cells"
RMSERoot Mean Squared Error — average prediction error. Lower is better. But as Module 02 explains, low RMSE ≠ good ranking.
Top-N ranking (Era 3+, Kartify)
$$L_u^K = \text{top-}K\left(\{f(u, i, c) : i \in \mathcal{I} \setminus \mathcal{H}_u\}\right)$$ $$\text{Evaluated by: nDCG@}K, \text{ Recall@}K, \text{ MAP@}K$$
$L_u^K$The top-$K$ items for user $u$ — the actual recommendation list shown to the user
$K$How many items to show. Kartify homepage: $K = 20$. Product page "similar": $K = 8$.
nDCG@KNormalized Discounted Cumulative Gain — measures ranking quality, penalising relevant items that appear low. Derived fully in DEEP 05.

D5 / WORKED EXAMPLE
Sparsity calculations — by hand
↳ corresponds to M01 S4 (Matrix) and S5 (Meet Kartify)
Real-life analogy

Imagine a massive spreadsheet with 2.3 million rows (one per Kartify user) and 480,000 columns (one per product). That's 1.1 trillion cells. Of those, only 18 million have any data — a checkmark meaning "this user interacted with this product." The rest are blank. If you printed this spreadsheet, each page showing 50×50 cells, you'd need 440 billion pages. Only 7,200 pages would have a single checkmark on them. That's the scale of the problem.

Sparsity across datasets — every step shown
Numerical
KARTIFY
$$M = 2{,}300{,}000 \quad N = 480{,}000 \quad |\Omega| = 18{,}000{,}000$$ $$\text{Total cells} = M \times N = 2{,}300{,}000 \times 480{,}000 = 1.104 \times 10^{12}$$ $$\text{Fill rate} = \frac{|\Omega|}{M \times N} = \frac{1.8 \times 10^7}{1.104 \times 10^{12}} = 1.63 \times 10^{-5} = 0.00163\%$$ $$\text{Sparsity} = 100\% - 0.00163\% = 99.998\%$$

Avg interactions per user: $18\text{M} / 2.3\text{M} = 7.8$. Out of 480K products, each user has seen ~8.

Avg interactions per item: $18\text{M} / 480\text{K} = 37.5$. Out of 2.3M users, each item was seen by ~38.

MOVIELENS 1M (BENCHMARK)
$$M = 6{,}040 \quad N = 3{,}952 \quad |\Omega| = 1{,}000{,}209$$ $$\text{Fill rate} = \frac{1{,}000{,}209}{6{,}040 \times 3{,}952} = \frac{1{,}000{,}209}{23{,}870{,}080} = 4.19\%$$

4.19% filled — 2,500× denser than Kartify. This is why algorithms that "work on MovieLens" may fail at production scale.

NETFLIX PRIZE
$$M = 480{,}189 \quad N = 17{,}770 \quad |\Omega| = 100{,}480{,}507$$ $$\text{Fill rate} = \frac{100{,}480{,}507}{480{,}189 \times 17{,}770} = \frac{100.5\text{M}}{8.53\text{B}} = 1.18\%$$

1.18% — moderately sparse. Dense enough for MF to work well. This is why the Netflix Prize was solvable.

WHY SPARSITY MATTERS

For item-kNN (Module 09), two items need shared users to compute similarity. The probability two random items share at least one user depends on their interaction counts:

$$P(\text{share} \geq 1 \text{ user}) \approx 1 - \left(1 - \frac{n_i}{M}\right)^{n_j}$$

For two average Kartify items ($n_i = n_j = 38$): $P \approx 1 - (1 - 38/2{,}300{,}000)^{38} = 1 - 0.9994 = 0.063\%$. Only 0.06% of item pairs have any co-interaction. 99.94% of item pairs have zero overlap — cosine similarity is exactly 0 for them. This is the ceiling that Module 13 identifies and that matrix factorization (Module 14+) breaks through.

D6 / FROM SCRATCH
Building $R$ from raw event logs
↳ corresponds to M01 S4: What a Recsys Actually Is (the matrix)

The interaction matrix $R$ doesn't exist in a database — it's constructed from raw event logs. Here's the complete pipeline:

# From raw logs to sparse interaction matrix
import pandas as pd
import numpy as np
from scipy.sparse import csr_matrix

# 1. Load raw event logs
# Columns: user_id, item_id, event_type, timestamp
logs = pd.read_parquet('kartify_events.parquet')

# 2. Filter to meaningful events (design decision!)
# Module 03 teaches why this choice matters
valid_events = ['click', 'add_to_cart', 'purchase']
events = logs[logs.event_type.isin(valid_events)]

# 3. Create contiguous integer IDs (sparse matrix requirement)
user_ids = events.user_id.unique()
item_ids = events.item_id.unique()
uid_map = {uid: idx for idx, uid in enumerate(user_ids)}
iid_map = {iid: idx for idx, iid in enumerate(item_ids)}

# 4. Map to indices
rows = events.user_id.map(uid_map).values
cols = events.item_id.map(iid_map).values
vals = np.ones(len(events))  # binary: 1 for any interaction

# 5. Build sparse matrix
R = csr_matrix((vals, (rows, cols)),
               shape=(len(user_ids), len(item_ids)))

# 6. Binarize (collapse multiple events for same pair)
R.data[:] = 1.0

# Verify
print(f"Shape:     {R.shape}")           # (2300000, 480000)
print(f"Nonzeros:  {R.nnz:,}")           # ~18,000,000
print(f"Fill rate: {R.nnz/(R.shape[0]*R.shape[1]):.6%}")
print(f"Memory:    {R.data.nbytes/1e6:.0f} MB")  # ~144 MB sparse vs ~8.8 TB dense
Why sparse

Dense matrix: $2.3\text{M} \times 480\text{K} \times 8$ bytes = 8.8 TB. Sparse CSR matrix: stores only 18M nonzero values + row/column pointers ≈ ~200 MB. That's a 44,000× reduction. Every algorithm in this track operates on sparse matrices. If you ever find yourself creating a dense $M \times N$ matrix for a recsys, stop — you're doing it wrong.

Problem 1: Sparsity at scale
Interview
PROBLEM

A music platform has 50M users, 80M tracks, and 10B streams. Compute the fill rate. If a user listens to 200 unique tracks on average, what fraction of the catalog has each user explored?

SOLUTION
$$\text{Fill rate} = \frac{10^{10}}{5 \times 10^7 \times 8 \times 10^7} = \frac{10^{10}}{4 \times 10^{15}} = 2.5 \times 10^{-6} = 0.00025\%$$

Each user explores: $200 / 80{,}000{,}000 = 0.00025\%$ of the catalog. Each user has heard 0.00025% of all available music. This is why Spotify's Discover Weekly — surfacing tracks from the unexplored 99.9997% — is so valuable.

Problem 2: Multi-stakeholder trade-off
Interview
PROBLEM

You're designing a recsys for a marketplace with sellers. Your model maximises user CTR perfectly. Why might this kill the marketplace long-term? Formalise the issue using the multi-stakeholder framework.

SOLUTION

Pure CTR maximisation sets $w_1 = 1, w_2 = w_3 = 0$ in the multi-stakeholder objective. The model discovers that a few popular sellers have high CTR and shows them to everyone. New and small sellers get zero exposure. Seller diversity drops. Popular sellers gain monopoly pricing power. Small sellers leave. Catalog diversity decreases. Eventually, user experience degrades because the catalog shrinks.

The fix: add $\mathcal{L}_{\text{fairness}}$ with $w_3 > 0$, or add a guardrail: $g_{\text{exposure}}(\pi) = \text{Gini}(\text{seller\_exposure}) \leq \tau$, ensuring exposure is distributed. This is Kartify's "seller exposure fairness" guardrail from Module 05.

Problem 3: Implicit vs explicit
Interview
PROBLEM

Why can't you simply train a regression model to predict $r_{ui}$ on implicit binary data $(0/1)$ using RMSE? Give a mathematical argument.

SOLUTION

If you include all $(u,i)$ pairs, the loss is:

$$\text{RMSE} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{MN}\sum_{u,i}(r_{ui} - \hat{r}_{ui})^2}$$

With $99.998\%$ of $r_{ui} = 0$, the loss is dominated by the zero-class. The model minimises RMSE by predicting $\hat{r}_{ui} \approx 0$ for all pairs — RMSE ≈ $\sqrt{0.00002} \approx 0.004$, which is excellent by RMSE standards but gives a useless recommender. If you restrict to observed pairs only ($\Omega$), you train only on positives and the model predicts $\hat{r}_{ui} \approx 1$ for everything — no discrimination between items. This fundamental problem is why Era 3 invented BPR (pairwise ranking) and WRMF (confidence-weighted regression with all pairs but low weight on zeros).
