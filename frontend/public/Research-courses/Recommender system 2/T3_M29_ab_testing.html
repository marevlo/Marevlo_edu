A/B testing —
the final judge.

You've built the models, the infrastructure, and the full stack. But none of it matters until you answer one question: does it make the business better? Not "does nDCG improve on the test set" — does revenue go up, do users come back, does the product grow? This module is about the bridge between "my model is better offline" and "we shipped it and the business improved." That bridge is the A/B test, and crossing it is harder than training any model.

01 / THE GAP
Why offline metrics lie — the gap that humbles engineers
Module 04 warned about this. Module 15's Netflix Prize proved it. Now you'll understand exactly why.

The team trains a new ranking model. Offline nDCG@10 improves from 0.42 to 0.48 — a strong 14% relative gain. They deploy it as an A/B test, expecting a significant CTR lift. After two weeks: CTR is flat. Revenue is flat. User retention is flat. The 14% offline improvement translated to zero online impact.

This happens at every company, on every team, repeatedly. It's the most common and most painful experience in applied recommendation. Understanding WHY it happens is more valuable than any model architecture.

The three causes of the offline-online gap

Cause 1: Selection bias in the test set. The offline test set only contains items that the old model chose to show. The new model might recommend different items — items for which we have no ground truth labels because no user ever saw them. The new model might be correctly identifying great items that the test set can't measure.

Cause 2: Different objectives. nDCG measures ranking quality — "are the relevant items at the top?" But the business cares about CTR, conversion, and revenue. A model that perfectly ranks items by relevance might rank a ₹99 kurta above a ₹2,999 lehenga — great nDCG, but the business loses revenue because the cheap item gets clicked instead of the expensive one.

Cause 3: Ecosystem effects. Offline evaluation treats each user independently. Online, users influence each other: if the new model shows everyone the same trending items, inventory runs out. If it shows more diverse items, users discover new categories but have lower immediate CTR. These ecosystem effects don't exist offline.

Pause — the Netflix lesson callback

In Module 15, the Netflix Prize-winning algorithm was never deployed. Why? Map the three causes above to what happened with the Netflix Prize.

The Prize optimized RMSE (pointwise prediction accuracy), not ranking or engagement. That's Cause 2 — different objectives. Netflix shifted from DVD-by-mail (where rating prediction mattered because users made deliberate choices from a small queue) to streaming (where ranking matters because users scroll through a large catalog). The model was perfect for the old product and useless for the new one. The offline metric (RMSE) was correct; the business metric (streaming engagement) was not measured.
02 / BUSINESS METRICS
The metrics that actually matter — beyond nDCG
nDCG is a model metric. The business runs on different numbers.

CTR (Click-Through Rate): clicks / impressions. Measures engagement. Easy to move, easy to game. A clickbait recommendation model has high CTR but low business value. Use as a guardrail (CTR shouldn't drop) but not as the primary objective.

CVR (Conversion Rate): purchases / clicks. Measures purchase intent. Harder to move than CTR because the user has to actually buy, not just click. Better signal of recommendation quality than CTR.

GMV (Gross Merchandise Value): total revenue through recommendations. The bottom line. GMV = impressions × CTR × CVR × average order value. This is what Kartify's CEO looks at.

Retention (D7, D30): fraction of users who return in 7/30 days. Measures long-term value. A recommender that optimizes for immediate clicks might show "junk food" recommendations — items that get clicked but don't build lasting satisfaction. Good recommendations bring users back.

LTV (Lifetime Value): expected total revenue from a user over their lifetime. The north star. Hard to measure (requires long observation windows), but the ultimate indicator of whether the recommender is building a healthy business.

Pause — metric conflicts

A new model increases CTR by 5% but decreases GMV by 2%. Should you ship it? What's happening?

The model is showing items that get clicked more (high CTR) but are cheaper or convert less (low GMV). Users click more but spend less. This could mean: the model is showing low-price items that are easy to click but low margin, or showing items that look attractive but don't match purchase intent. Do NOT ship. GMV is the business metric; CTR is a proxy. When they conflict, follow the money. Investigate what items are getting clicked more — are they genuinely useful (users find them and buy later) or are they engagement traps?
The metric hierarchy

Primary metric: GMV (or the business equivalent: watch time for video, sessions for social). This is what you optimize for and what determines the ship decision.
Secondary metrics: CTR, CVR, basket size, D7 retention. These help diagnose what's happening but don't determine the ship decision alone.
Guardrail metrics: Latency P99, error rate, recommendation diversity, category coverage. These must NOT degrade, regardless of primary metric improvement. A model that improves GMV by 3% but increases P99 latency from 200ms to 500ms should not ship.

03 / ANATOMY
Anatomy of a recsys A/B test — from design to decision
Every test follows the same structure: hypothesis, randomization, duration, analysis, decision.

Step 1: Hypothesis

"Adding SASRec as a retrieval source will improve GMV by 2% for users with 5+ prior sessions, because session-aware recommendations better match current browsing intent." Specific, measurable, and tied to a mechanism.

Step 2: Power analysis — how many users, how long?

# Power analysis: how many users do we need?
# Detect a 2% GMV lift with 80% power and α=0.05

baseline_gmv_per_user = 350  # ₹/user/week
std_dev = 500               # GMV is high-variance
mde = 0.02 * baseline_gmv_per_user  # minimum detectable effect = ₹7

# n per group = (Z_α/2 + Z_β)² × 2σ² / δ²
# = (1.96 + 0.84)² × 2 × 500² / 7²
# = 7.84 × 500000 / 49
# = 80,000 users per group

# With 10M DAU: assign 80K to control, 80K to treatment
# Duration: 1 week minimum (capture weekly patterns)
# Ramp: start at 1% traffic, ramp to 5% after 24hr guardrail check

Step 3: Randomization

Randomly assign users (not sessions) to control or treatment. User-level randomization ensures a user always sees the same model — session-level would cause inconsistent experience. Hash the user_id to determine group membership: hash(user_id) % 100 < 5 → treatment.

Step 4: Duration and guardrails

Run for at least 7 days (capture day-of-week effects). Check guardrails at 24 hours: latency, error rate, diversity. If guardrails are breached, kill the test immediately. Check primary metric at 7 days with pre-committed significance threshold (p < 0.05).

Pause — the peeking problem

On day 3, the test shows p=0.04 (significant!). The team wants to ship early. What's wrong with this?

Multiple testing / peeking inflation. If you check significance every day and stop when it crosses 0.05, your effective false positive rate is much higher than 5% — potentially 20–30%. The p-value at any single timepoint is unreliable because you're running multiple implicit tests. Fix: pre-commit to the analysis date. Use sequential testing methods (like always-valid p-values or Bayesian stopping rules) if early stopping is important. Never peek and ship.
04 / PITFALLS
Pitfalls that kill recsys A/B tests
Recsys tests have unique failure modes that don't appear in other A/B tests.

Pitfall 1: Novelty effect. The treatment group sees new, different recommendations. Users click more because they're novel, not because they're better. After 2 weeks, the effect fades. Solution: run tests for at least 2 weeks, and check if the effect is stable or decaying.

Pitfall 2: Position bias in evaluation. The new model might put different items in position 1. Position 1 always gets more clicks regardless of item quality. If the new model's position-1 item is worse but the position itself drives clicks, CTR might be flat even though the model is better. Solution: control for position in the analysis.

Pitfall 3: Interference between groups. If the treatment group buys out inventory of a popular item, the control group can't buy it — their experience is degraded by the treatment, violating the independence assumption. Solution: run tests on low-inventory items cautiously, or use synthetic hold-out approaches.

Pitfall 4: Simpson's paradox. The treatment wins overall GMV, but when you segment by user type: it loses for new users and wins for returning users. Since returning users have higher GMV, the overall number looks positive — but you're hurting new users, which will reduce long-term growth. Solution: always check subgroup effects, especially new vs returning.

The irreversible ship

Some model changes create feedback loops. If you ship a model that recommends more ethnic wear, users interact more with ethnic wear, which trains the next model to recommend even more ethnic wear. After 3 months, the catalog is effectively narrowed. Reverting the model doesn't revert the user behavior. Before shipping, ask: "If this change is wrong, can we undo it?" If not, run a longer test and require higher confidence.

05 / BEYOND A/B
Interleaving — faster, more sensitive recsys testing
A/B tests need 80K users per group. Interleaving needs 10× fewer. Here's how.

In standard A/B testing, group A sees Model A's recommendations, group B sees Model B's. You measure aggregate metrics per group. The signal is noisy because user-to-user variance is huge — some users are big spenders, some never buy. You need enormous sample sizes to detect small effects.

Interleaving shows BOTH models' recommendations to the SAME users, merged into a single list. The user interacts with items from both models without knowing which model produced which item. You count how often the user preferred Model A's items vs Model B's items.

Because each user serves as their own control, the within-user noise is eliminated. You only measure the treatment effect, not the between-user variance. This makes interleaving ~10× more sensitive than A/B testing — you can detect a 1% quality difference with 10K users instead of 100K.

Pause — when NOT to interleave

Interleaving is great for detecting which model produces better rankings. But when should you still run a full A/B test instead?

Interleaving measures ranking quality (which model's items get clicked). It does NOT measure business metrics like total GMV, retention, or basket size — because both models contribute to the same user's experience. For business decisions, you need full A/B tests with clean group separation. The workflow: use interleaving first to identify the better-ranking model (fast, small sample), then run a full A/B test to measure business impact (slower, larger sample). Interleaving is a screening tool, not a ship decision.
06 / KARTIFY
Kartify's experimentation protocol
The standard operating procedure for shipping any model change at Kartify.
Kartify experimentation protocol

Step 1: Offline evaluation. New model must beat BPR-MF + DCN-v2 baseline on held-out nDCG@10 by ≥ 3% relative. If not, don't proceed to online testing.

Step 2: Interleaving (3 days, 10K users). Merge new model's retrieval candidates with current model's. Measure Team Draft interleaving wins. New model must win ≥ 52% of comparisons (p < 0.05). If not, iterate offline.

Step 3: Shadow deployment (3 days). Run new model in parallel, log predictions, don't serve to users. Monitor: latency, memory, error rates. Ensure infrastructure handles the new model without degradation.

Step 4: A/B test (14 days, 80K users per group).
Primary metric: GMV per user (must increase ≥ 1.5%, p < 0.05).
Secondary: CTR, CVR, D7 retention, basket size (directional check).
Guardrails: P99 latency < 200ms, error rate < 0.1%, recommendation diversity ≥ 80% of baseline.
Subgroup checks: new vs returning users, mobile vs desktop, top categories.

Step 5: Ship decision. Requires: primary metric significant, ALL guardrails green, no adverse subgroup effects, eng team sign-off on infrastructure stability. Decision made in weekly recsys review meeting.

Step 6: Ramp and monitor. Ship to 10% → 50% → 100% over 5 days. Monitor all metrics at each ramp step. Kill switch ready.

After this module, you understand experimentation for recsys:

The offline-online gap: Three causes — selection bias, different objectives, ecosystem effects. Expect offline gains to not fully translate.
Business metrics: CTR (engagement), CVR (purchase intent), GMV (revenue), retention (loyalty), LTV (north star). GMV is the primary ship metric.
A/B test anatomy: Hypothesis → power analysis → randomization → duration → guardrails → analysis → decision.
Pitfalls: Novelty effect, position bias, interference, Simpson's paradox, irreversible feedback loops.
Interleaving: 10× more sensitive for ranking quality. Use for screening, not for ship decisions.
