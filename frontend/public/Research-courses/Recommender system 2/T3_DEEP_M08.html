formalized and computed.

Module 08 introduced the CF insight through story and intuition. This companion formalizes it: the co-interaction structure, the prediction mechanism, and a complete worked example on the 6×8 matrix from Module 01 — where you predicted Priya's rating by eye. Now you'll do it with math.

D1 / FORMALIZATION
The collaborative filtering prediction — formal
↳ corresponds to M08 S2: The CF Insight
Real-life analogy

Think of a friendship graph. You and your friend both loved restaurants A, B, and C. Your friend also tried restaurant D and loved it. You haven't been to D. The CF logic: since you agree on A, B, C, you'll probably agree on D. The math just counts HOW MUCH you agree (similarity) and weights the prediction accordingly.

Item-kNN collaborative filtering prediction
$$\hat{r}_{ui} = \frac{\sum_{j \in N_k(i) \cap \mathcal{H}_u} \text{sim}(i, j) \cdot r_{uj}}{\sum_{j \in N_k(i) \cap \mathcal{H}_u} |\text{sim}(i, j)|}$$
$\hat{r}_{ui}$Predicted score for user $u$ on item $i$ — "how much will Priya like the Silk Saree?"
$N_k(i)$The $k$ items most similar to item $i$ (pre-computed in Phase 1 from Module 09)
$\mathcal{H}_u$Items that user $u$ has interacted with — Priya's history
$N_k(i) \cap \mathcal{H}_u$Items that are BOTH similar to $i$ AND in Priya's history. These are the "evidence items" — items Priya has rated that are similar to the target
$\text{sim}(i,j)$Pre-computed similarity between items $i$ and $j$ (cosine, Jaccard, etc. from Module 10)
$r_{uj}$Priya's actual rating/interaction with item $j$. This is known data — she already interacted with $j$
NumeratorWeighted sum: each evidence item contributes its rating weighted by how similar it is to the target. Similar items get more vote
DenominatorSum of absolute similarities — normalizes so the prediction is on the same scale as the original ratings

For binary implicit data (Kartify — clicks are 1, non-clicks are 0), the formula simplifies because all $r_{uj} = 1$ for items in $\mathcal{H}_u$:

Item-kNN for binary implicit data
$$\hat{r}_{ui} = \sum_{j \in N_k(i) \cap \mathcal{H}_u} \text{sim}(i, j)$$

Just sum up the similarities — each item in the user's history that is similar to the target adds its similarity score. No need to normalize because we only care about ranking order, not absolute values.

D2 / WORKED
Predict Priya's rating — the 6×8 matrix from Module 01
↳ corresponds to M08 S3: You Already Did This
Item-kNN prediction: will Priya like the Silk Saree?
Complete workout
RECALL THE MATRIX
$$R = \begin{array}{c|cccccccc} & \text{Kurta} & \text{Shoes} & \text{Jacket} & \text{Saree} & \text{Cargo} & \text{Earrings} & \text{Belt} & \text{Tee} \\ \hline \text{Priya} & 4 & ? & ? & \mathbf{?} & ? & 5 & ? & ? \\ \text{Arjun} & ? & 5 & 4 & ? & 3 & ? & ? & 4 \\ \text{Meera} & 5 & ? & ? & 4 & ? & 4 & ? & ? \\ \text{Vikram} & ? & ? & 3 & ? & 5 & ? & 4 & 3 \\ \text{Ananya} & 3 & ? & ? & 5 & ? & ? & ? & 2 \\ \text{Rohan} & ? & 4 & 5 & ? & ? & ? & 3 & 5 \end{array}$$

We want to predict: will Priya like the Silk Saree? ($\hat{r}_{\text{Priya, Saree}} = ?$)

Priya's history: $\mathcal{H}_\text{Priya} = \{\text{Kurta}(4), \text{Earrings}(5)\}$

COMPUTE ITEM SIMILARITIES WITH SAREE

We need $\text{sim}(\text{Saree}, \text{Kurta})$ and $\text{sim}(\text{Saree}, \text{Earrings})$ — the two items in Priya's history.

Saree column: users who rated it = {Meera: 4, Ananya: 5}.

Kurta column: users who rated it = {Priya: 4, Meera: 5, Ananya: 3}.

Co-raters of Saree and Kurta: {Meera, Ananya}.

$$\text{Saree vec (co-raters)} = [4, 5] \quad \text{Kurta vec (co-raters)} = [5, 3]$$ $$\cos(\text{Saree}, \text{Kurta}) = \frac{(4)(5) + (5)(3)}{\sqrt{4^2+5^2} \cdot \sqrt{5^2+3^2}} = \frac{20+15}{\sqrt{41} \cdot \sqrt{34}} = \frac{35}{6.403 \times 5.831} = \frac{35}{37.33} = 0.938$$

Now Saree vs Earrings. Co-raters: {Meera}.

$$\text{Saree vec} = [4] \quad \text{Earrings vec} = [4] \quad \cos = \frac{16}{\sqrt{16}\sqrt{16}} = \frac{16}{16} = 1.000$$

Perfect cosine — but based on only 1 co-rater! This is exactly the bug from Module 11. With shrinkage ($\lambda = 5$ for this small matrix): $\text{shrunk} = 1.0 \times 1/(1+5) = 0.167$.

PREDICT (WITHOUT SHRINKAGE)
$$\hat{r}_{\text{Priya, Saree}} = \frac{\text{sim(Saree,Kurta)} \cdot r_{\text{Priya,Kurta}} + \text{sim(Saree,Earrings)} \cdot r_{\text{Priya,Earrings}}}{|\text{sim(Saree,Kurta)}| + |\text{sim(Saree,Earrings)}|}$$ $$= \frac{0.938 \times 4 + 1.000 \times 5}{0.938 + 1.000} = \frac{3.752 + 5.000}{1.938} = \frac{8.752}{1.938} = \boxed{4.52}$$

Predicted rating: 4.52 out of 5. The model thinks Priya will love the Silk Saree — consistent with what we intuitively predicted in Module 01!

PREDICT (WITH SHRINKAGE λ=5)

Shrunk similarities: Saree-Kurta (2 co-raters): $0.938 \times 2/7 = 0.268$. Saree-Earrings (1 co-rater): $1.0 \times 1/6 = 0.167$.

$$\hat{r}_{\text{shrunk}} = \frac{0.268 \times 4 + 0.167 \times 5}{0.268 + 0.167} = \frac{1.072 + 0.835}{0.435} = \frac{1.907}{0.435} = \boxed{4.38}$$

With shrinkage: 4.38 (slightly lower because the earrings signal, based on 1 co-rater, is discounted). The Kurta signal (2 co-raters, higher shrunk weight) now dominates. More robust prediction.

D3 / ANALYSIS
Computational complexity — CBF vs CF
↳ corresponds to M08 S4: CBF vs CF
Content-Based Filtering complexity
$$\text{Build item vectors: } O(N \cdot d) \quad \text{where } d = \text{vocabulary size}$$ $$\text{Build user profile: } O(|\mathcal{H}_u| \cdot d) \quad \text{(average history vectors)}$$ $$\text{Score all items: } O(N \cdot d) \quad \text{(cosine between profile and each item)}$$ $$\text{Total per user: } O(N \cdot d)$$
$N$Catalog size. Kartify: 480K
$d$Feature dimensionality (tag vocabulary). Kartify: ~2,000 unique tags
$|\mathcal{H}_u|$User's history size. Average: ~8 items
AdvantageNo dependence on $M$ (user count). New users can be scored immediately. Scales with catalog, not user base
Item-kNN CF complexity
$$\text{Build similarity matrix (offline): } O(N^2 \cdot \bar{m}) \quad \text{where } \bar{m} = \text{avg users per item}$$ $$\text{Score items for user (online): } O(|\mathcal{H}_u| \cdot K) \quad \text{where } K = \text{neighbors per item}$$
$N^2 \cdot \bar{m}$For each of $N^2/2$ item pairs, compute similarity over $\bar{m}$ shared users. With sparse data, most pairs have 0 overlap → actual cost is much lower than $N^2$
$|\mathcal{H}_u| \cdot K$For each of the user's ~8 history items, look up $K=50$ pre-computed neighbors and sum scores. Total: ~400 operations. Milliseconds
AdvantageOnline scoring is extremely fast ($O(|\mathcal{H}_u| \cdot K)$ ≈ 400 ops). The expensive $N^2$ computation is offline/nightly
DisadvantageOffline cost is $O(N^2)$ — for Kartify's 480K items, that's ~115 billion pair computations. With sparse optimization: ~45 min on 16 cores
Kartify numbers

CBF scoring: $480{,}000 \times 2{,}000 = 960\text{M}$ operations per user — ~100ms on CPU. Feasible for real-time but not fast. In practice, pre-compute nearest neighbors.

Item-kNN scoring: $8 \times 50 = 400$ operations per user — ~0.01ms. Trivial. The investment is in the nightly offline similarity computation, which runs once and serves all users all day.

Interview problems — CF foundations
↳ tests M08 concepts
Problem 1: Item-kNN prediction
Interview
PROBLEM

User Alice's history: {Movie A (rated 5), Movie B (rated 3)}. You want to predict Alice's rating for Movie C. sim(C,A) = 0.8, sim(C,B) = 0.3. Compute $\hat{r}_{\text{Alice,C}}$.

SOLUTION
$$\hat{r}_{\text{Alice,C}} = \frac{0.8 \times 5 + 0.3 \times 3}{|0.8| + |0.3|} = \frac{4.0 + 0.9}{1.1} = \frac{4.9}{1.1} = 4.45$$

Predicted rating: 4.45. Movie A (rated 5, high similarity 0.8) pulls the prediction up. Movie B (rated 3, lower similarity 0.3) pulls it slightly down. The weighted average reflects that C is more similar to A than B.

Problem 2: Cold-start asymmetry
Interview
PROBLEM

A new item arrives with zero interactions. Can item-kNN recommend it? Can user-kNN? Explain the asymmetry.

SOLUTION

Item-kNN: NO. The item has no interaction column → $\text{sim}(\text{new\_item}, j) = 0$ for all $j$ → never appears as a neighbor of any item → never gets recommended. Cold-start items are invisible to item-kNN.

User-kNN: YES (partially). If a user's neighbors have interacted with the new item, it can be recommended to that user. The new item benefits from having even a few early adopters — those users' neighbors can receive the recommendation. But for a truly zero-interaction item, even user-kNN can't help.

CBF: YES. The item's feature vector can be computed from metadata alone → it gets placed in feature space immediately → similar items' users can discover it. This is why CBF (Module 07) is the cold-start workhorse — it doesn't depend on interaction data at all.

Problem 3: Why CF finds cross-category patterns
Interview
PROBLEM

CBF says sim(saree, earrings) = 0.0 (no shared tags). But CF says sim(saree, earrings) = 0.61 (high co-purchase). Explain mathematically why CF can see this and CBF can't.

SOLUTION

CBF computes similarity in TAG SPACE: $\cos(\mathbf{t}_\text{saree}, \mathbf{t}_\text{earrings})$. Tags are {ethnic, silk, saree} vs {accessories, gold, earrings}. Dot product = 0. Zero overlap in feature space.

CF computes similarity in USER SPACE: $\cos(\mathbf{r}_\text{saree}, \mathbf{r}_\text{earrings})$ where each vector is the column of the interaction matrix (who interacted with each item). If 280 users bought both sarees and earrings, those 280 shared entries create a non-zero dot product in user space. The "similarity" lives in BEHAVIOR (who buys what), not in FEATURES (what items are). CBF is blind to behavioral similarity; CF is built on it.
