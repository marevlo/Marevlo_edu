The best recsys engineer I know spends 70% of their time on questions and 30% on code. The worst spends 100% on code and ships a system that optimises the wrong thing. This module gives you the questions. The next ninety-five give you the code.

01 / THE RULE
The 70/30 rule
Most recsys projects don't fail because the algorithm is bad. They fail because the problem was never properly defined.

A team at a mid-size e-commerce company spends four months building a deep learning recommender. It achieves state-of-the-art nDCG on their offline eval. They ship it. Click-through rate goes up 3%. The VP is happy.

Six months later, customer retention has dropped 2%. Returns have increased 8%. Customer satisfaction scores are down. What happened?

The model learned to optimise for clicks. Clicks are cheap — users click on curiosity, on outrage, on misleading thumbnails. The model surfaced products with clickbait-style images and aggressive discounts. Users clicked, bought impulsively, received products that didn't match expectations, and stopped coming back.

The algorithm worked perfectly. The objective was wrong.

This is why the questions come before the algorithms. Not as a formality — as the highest-leverage activity in the entire project. A well-framed problem with a mediocre algorithm outperforms a badly-framed problem with a perfect algorithm, every time.

Ten questions. If you can answer all ten before writing a line of code, the algorithm choice becomes obvious. If you can't answer even one, stop — go resolve it with stakeholders. That question is where the project will later fail.

02 / THE TEN
Ten questions to answer before picking an algorithm
Tap each question to see why it matters, and how Kartify answers it. Half will be obvious in 30 seconds. The other half will expose the decisions that determine success or failure.
01
What task am I solving?
▸
Rating prediction, top-N ranking, next-item, slate, session, or conversational? You learned these in Module 04. Write the task in one sentence. If you can't, the project is pre-algorithmic — go resolve scope first.
Kartify's answer

Homepage: slate recommendation (diverse rows). Product page: next-item / complementary. Cart: session continuation with complementary intent. Three surfaces, three tasks.

02
What feedback do I have?
▸
Explicit ratings, implicit clicks, explicit negatives, conversational? The feedback type rules in or out roughly half of the algorithms in this track (Module 03). Volume and bias profile of each signal matter as much as type.
Kartify's answer

Implicit positive: clicks, page views, add-to-cart, purchases (~18M events/month). No star ratings. Limited negative signal via search refinements. Rules out RMSE-based approaches entirely.

03
What's my catalog size, and how fast does it grow?
▸
10K items: score everything directly. 10M items: retrieval + ranking pipeline mandatory. 10B items: two-tower + ANN non-negotiable. Fast growth (news, social): cold-start architecture required. Slow growth (movies, books): embeddings can be pre-computed.
Kartify's answer

480K products. Moderate growth (~2K new products/week). Large enough for two-stage pipeline, not so large that extreme ANN infrastructure is needed. Cold-start for new products is important — 2K new arrivals weekly need recommendations from day one.

04
What's my user base size, and session pattern?
▸
Do users return frequently (Netflix, Spotify) or rarely (Airbnb, cars)? Are sessions short (news) or long (Netflix binge)? Session-dominant domains need session-aware models; returning-user domains can invest in long-term preference models.
Kartify's answer

2.3M users. Average session: 4.2 minutes, 12 product views. Users return ~2.3 times/month. Mixed pattern — both session-level intent (browsing for specific occasion) and long-term taste (preferred styles) matter. Need both session-based and user-history models.

05
What's my latency budget?
▸
~50ms (inline feed): retrieval model must be ANN-indexable. ~300ms (homepage load): full funnel fits. Multi-second (email recs, batch): almost any algorithm works. The budget constrains architecture first, algorithm second.
Kartify's answer

~250ms total for homepage. ~100ms for product page "similar items." These are standard e-commerce budgets. A two-stage pipeline with pre-computed retrieval embeddings + real-time ranking fits comfortably.

06
What's the primary metric, and what are the guardrails?
▸
Pick ONE primary metric (must move up). Pick 2–3 guardrail metrics (must not regress). The guardrails protect against Goodhart effects — your model will try to game the primary metric, and guardrails catch the damage. This is the most important design decision in the entire project.
Kartify's answer

Primary: Purchase conversion rate (did the user buy something they were recommended?). Guardrails: 30-day return rate (must not increase — prevents clickbait-style optimisation), category diversity (must maintain — prevents echo chambers), new-product exposure rate (must not decrease — prevents popularity lock-in).

07
What's the cold-start story?
▸
New user: do they see popularity, a content-based bootstrap, an onboarding quiz? New item: is there content signal (text, image) to hot-start from, or does it rely on first-day exposure? A recsys without cold-start answers will break on its first day live.
Kartify's answer

New user: Popularity baseline (Module 06), segmented by entry point (which ad or category brought them). After 3+ clicks in first session, switch to session-based model. New item: Content-based similarity from product metadata + images (Module 07). No interaction history needed — the item's features place it in the catalog from day one.

08
What explainability is required?
▸
None (TikTok feed): pure embeddings fine. Light ("because you liked X"): attention models or neighborhood methods. Heavy (healthcare, finance): knowledge-graph-based paths. The explainability requirement narrows your algorithm choices significantly.
Kartify's answer

Light. "Based on your recent browsing" and "Customers who bought this also bought" are sufficient for fashion e-commerce. No regulatory requirement for full explainability. This keeps the algorithm space wide open.

09
What are the fairness / safety requirements?
▸
Exposure fairness for sellers? Protected-class parity? Child-safety filters? Each of these shows up as a constraint in the reranking stage and sometimes in training. Ignoring fairness doesn't make it go away — it makes it a PR crisis.
Kartify's answer

Seller exposure fairness: new and small sellers must get minimum exposure to survive on the platform. Size-inclusive representation: recommendations shouldn't bias toward specific body types. No adult content to minors. These constraints live in the re-ranking stage.

10
How will I evaluate before shipping?
▸
Offline eval? Interleaving? Shadow testing? A/B test? What's the power calculation — how much traffic for how long to detect the effect size that matters? A recsys without a planned eval pipeline ships on vibes.
Kartify's answer

Stage 1: Offline evaluation on held-out 30-day window (nDCG@10, Recall@50). Stage 2: Shadow deployment (model runs in parallel, doesn't affect users, compare logged decisions). Stage 3: A/B test with 10% traffic for 14 days. Required effect size: +0.5% conversion rate (need ~200K sessions per arm for 80% power at α=0.05).

How to use these questions

Don't treat them as a gate. Treat them as a conversation driver. Take them to the first stakeholder meeting for any recsys project. Questions 1, 2, 3, 6 are usually well-defined. Questions 4, 5, 7 need investigation. Questions 8, 9, 10 are almost always under-specified. The under-specified ones are where the project will later fail. Resolving them before building is the highest-leverage activity in recsys engineering.

03 / THE TRAP
The Goodhart trap — when your metric becomes your enemy
Charles Goodhart's law: when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure. Every recsys metric starts as a proxy and degrades under optimisation.

Question 06 asked you to pick a primary metric and guardrails. Here's why the guardrails matter: your model will attack your primary metric. Not maliciously — mechanically. An optimizer finds the shortest path to the target, and the shortest path is rarely the path you intended.

Watch what happens when you optimise each common metric without guardrails:

Optimise click-through rate → The model learns that sensational thumbnails, misleading titles, and curiosity-gap descriptions generate clicks. Users click, feel cheated, leave. CTR is up. User trust is down.

Optimise dwell time → The model learns that auto-playing next videos, infinite scroll, and anxiety-inducing content keep users on-screen. Session length is up. User wellbeing is down.

Optimise purchase conversion → The model learns that aggressive discounts and impulse-buy products drive purchases. Revenue per session is up. Return rate is up. Customer lifetime value is down.

Optimise Precision@K → The model learns that recommending items the user has already bought or items identical to their recent purchase is "safe." Precision is up. Discovery is dead.

The defense

No single metric is safe to optimise alone. The defense is composite objectives (weighted blends of multiple metrics) plus guardrail metrics that must not regress even if the primary metric improves. Kartify's guardrails — return rate, diversity, new-product exposure — exist specifically to catch the Goodhart effects that purchase conversion optimisation would otherwise produce.

04 / THE GAP
The offline-online gap — why winning offline loses online
Your model gets nDCG 0.88 on the test set. You ship it. Online metrics are flat. This happens to everyone. Here are the five reasons why.

Illustrative

0.88

+2.1%

+0.8%

−0.3%
User satisfaction
−1.2%

A realistic gap: offline dominance + small online engagement gains + retention and satisfaction regressions. The model is optimising short-term clicks at the cost of long-term trust.

Five reasons the gap exists

1. Selection bias. Your offline test set contains only items users were exposed to by the old system. Your new model recommends different items — items that have no ground truth in the test set. The offline eval can't measure whether those new recommendations are good.

2. Position bias. Training data reflects position effects. Items shown at the top got more clicks. Your model learned "top position = relevant" — a circular signal that offline eval doesn't penalise.

3. Distribution shift. Offline data is historical. Online traffic is now. User preferences drift, catalogs change, seasons shift. A model trained on winter data evaluated in summer will degrade.

4. Exposure effects. A better model changes what users see, which changes what users do, which changes the data distribution. Offline eval holds behaviour fixed — reality doesn't.

5. Short vs. long horizon. Offline metrics measure "did you rank the right item in this session?" Online metrics measure "did the user come back next week?" These can and do diverge.

The mindset

Assume the offline-online gap exists. Plan for it. Design your evaluation system to close it — through shadow deployments, interleaving experiments, and A/B tests with long-horizon metrics — rather than pretending it doesn't exist. This mindset matters more than any specific metric.

05 / KARTIFY
Kartify — all ten answers in one place
This is the reference card you'll carry through the rest of the track. Every algorithm decision we make will trace back to these answers.
Kartify project brief — complete

Task: Slate recommendation (homepage), next-item (product page), session continuation (cart). Three surfaces, three tasks, shared retrieval layer.

Feedback: Implicit positive only. Clicks, views, add-to-cart, purchases. No star ratings.

Catalog: 480K products, ~2K new/week. Two-stage pipeline appropriate.

Users: 2.3M, returning ~2.3x/month, 4.2-min sessions. Mixed session + history signals.

Latency: 250ms homepage, 100ms product page. Standard e-commerce budgets.

Primary metric: Purchase conversion rate. Guardrails: Return rate, category diversity, new-product exposure.

Cold start: New users → popularity baseline. New items → content-based similarity from metadata.

Explainability: Light — "based on your browsing," "also bought."

Fairness: Seller exposure minimums, size-inclusive representation.

Evaluation: Offline → shadow → A/B (10% traffic, 14 days, 200K sessions/arm).

Pause — reflect

Look at the ten answers above. Which ones would you have gotten wrong if you'd jumped straight to building a model?

The most commonly missed answers:

Q6 (metric): Most engineers default to CTR or nDCG without guardrails. Kartify's return rate guardrail prevents the Goodhart trap of optimising for impulse purchases that get returned.

Q7 (cold start): Most engineers build the personalised model first and bolt on cold-start later. Kartify gets ~2K new products weekly — without a cold-start solution, those products are invisible for their first week.

Q9 (fairness): Almost always under-specified. New sellers on Kartify need minimum exposure or they'll leave the platform — but the model's popularity bias actively starves them. This needs explicit re-ranking constraints.
