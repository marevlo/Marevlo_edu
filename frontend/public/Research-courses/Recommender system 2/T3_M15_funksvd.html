FunkSVD — the blog post
that won a million dollars.

Module 14 showed what MF is. This module shows how to train it. In 2006, a programmer named Simon Funk published a blog post describing a simplified SGD-trained matrix factorization that beat Netflix's own system. The technique he described — now called FunkSVD — became the foundation of modern recommendation. Here's the complete story, the algorithm, and the lesson Netflix learned too late.

01 / THE STORY
A blog post that changed a field
In 2006, the Netflix Prize was the most watched competition in machine learning. And a single blogger cracked it open.

October 2006. Netflix releases 100 million movie ratings and challenges the world: beat our recommendation algorithm by 10%, win $1 million. Hundreds of teams from universities and research labs compete. The leaderboard stalls at around 6% improvement — short of the 10% target.

Then Simon Funk — a programmer with no academic affiliation — publishes a blog post titled "Netflix Update: Try This At Home." In it, he describes a stunningly simple algorithm: give each user a vector of k numbers, give each movie a vector of k numbers, and train them so their dot product predicts the rating. Train with stochastic gradient descent. That's it.

His submission reaches 0.8914 RMSE — beating Netflix's own Cinematch system. The blog post doesn't use complex matrix algebra or SVD decomposition. It uses a for loop, a prediction, an error, and an update. Grad students around the world implement it in an afternoon.

The idea wasn't new — matrix factorization had been studied in linear algebra for decades. But Funk showed you could train it with SGD, one rating at a time, on a laptop. He made it practical. The field pivoted overnight.

02 / THE ALGORITHM
Biased MF — four components, one prediction
The simplest MF predicts r̂ = u·v. Biased MF adds three terms that capture non-latent patterns and dramatically improve accuracy.

Funk's original formulation predicted $\hat{r}_{ui} = \mathbf{u}_u \cdot \mathbf{v}_i$. But the BellKor team (who eventually won the Prize) added three bias terms that capture patterns the dot product alone misses:

# Biased MF prediction — the four components
r_hat = mu + b_u + b_i + dot(u_vec, v_vec)

# μ  = 3.5  — global average rating. "A random movie gets 3.5 stars on average"
# b_u = +0.3 — Priya's bias. "Priya rates 0.3 stars above average on everything"
# b_i = +0.8 — Saree's bias. "This product is rated 0.8 above average by everyone"
# u·v = +0.4 — the personal match. "Priya's taste specifically aligns with this saree"
# Total: 3.5 + 0.3 + 0.8 + 0.4 = 5.0

Why biases matter: without them, the dot product has to do everything — it has to learn that The Godfather is generally high-rated, that certain users rate everything high, AND that specific users have specific taste matches. That's too many jobs for one mechanism. Biases offload the "average quality" and "personal baseline" patterns, leaving the dot product to focus on the personalized component — the part that makes recommendations actually personal.

Pause — decomposition

Looking at the prediction μ + b_u + b_i + u·v: which component handles the cold-start problem? If a new user arrives with no interactions, what does the model predict?

For a new user: $b_u = 0$ (no learned bias), $\mathbf{u} \approx \mathbf{0}$ (initialized near zero), so $\hat{r} \approx \mu + b_i$. The model predicts "global average + item quality." This is exactly popularity ranking (Module 06) — the graceful fallback. As the user interacts with items, $b_u$ and $\mathbf{u}$ get trained, and the prediction becomes personalized. The biased MF architecture has a built-in cold-start graceful degradation.
03 / TRAINING
SGD — watching the model learn, one rating at a time
For each observed rating: predict, compute error, nudge embeddings. Repeat 50 million times.

Training MF with SGD is remarkably simple. For each observed rating $(u, i, r_{ui})$:

Step 1: Predict. Compute $\hat{r}_{ui} = \mu + b_u + b_i + \mathbf{u}_u \cdot \mathbf{v}_i$.

Step 2: Error. Compute $e_{ui} = r_{ui} - \hat{r}_{ui}$. How far off were we?

Step 3: Update. Move each parameter in the direction that reduces the error:

# One SGD step — the core of FunkSVD
e = r_actual - (mu + b_u + b_i + dot(u_vec, v_vec))  # error

# Update biases (move toward reducing error)
b_u += lr * (e - reg * b_u)
b_i += lr * (e - reg * b_i)

# Update embeddings (move user toward item if error positive, away if negative)
u_vec += lr * (e * v_vec - reg * u_vec)
v_vec += lr * (e * u_vec - reg * v_vec)

# That's it. Run this for every observed rating, repeat for 20-50 epochs.

The intuition: if we predicted 3.2 but the true rating is 5.0, the error is +1.8. The updates push the user and item embeddings closer together in latent space — so their dot product increases. If we predicted too high, they're pushed apart. Over millions of ratings, the embeddings settle into positions where the dot products approximate all observed ratings simultaneously — and, crucially, produce reasonable predictions for the unobserved ones.

The training budget

Netflix had 100M ratings. At 20 epochs, that's 2 billion SGD updates. Each update is $O(k)$ — with $k = 128$, that's ~256 billion floating-point operations. On a modern GPU: about 15 minutes. On Funk's 2006 laptop: about 8 hours. The algorithm's simplicity is its power — no complex matrix inversions, no second-order optimization. Just predict, error, nudge, repeat.

04 / THE PRIZE
The Netflix Prize — and the irony that killed it
The winning algorithm was never deployed. The lesson is more valuable than the prize.

The leaderboard climbed over three years. Funk's 2006 blog post sparked an arms race. Teams refined biased MF with temporal dynamics (TimeSVD++), implicit signals (SVD++), and eventually, massive ensembles blending hundreds of models.

In September 2009, BellKor's Pragmatic Chaos won with RMSE = 0.8567 — clearing the 10% threshold by 0.06%. Their solution blended 800+ models. Prize: $1,000,000.

Netflix never deployed it.

By 2009, Netflix had pivoted from DVD-by-mail (where star-rating prediction mattered) to streaming (where what matters is which movie appears at the top of the row). Rating prediction (RMSE) doesn't correlate well with ranking quality (nDCG). The Prize optimized the wrong metric — the Module 04 lesson in $1M form.

The lesson

The Netflix Prize established MF as the structural foundation of the field. But it also proved that the algorithm that wins the offline contest isn't always the algorithm that ships. MF won the Prize; BPR-MF (Module 16) won production. The right loss function matters more than the right architecture.

05 / KARTIFY
Kartify's MF baseline — the strongest starting point
Biased MF trained on Kartify's implicit data gives us a baseline that every subsequent model must beat.

For Kartify, biased MF serves as the retrieval model baseline. With $k = 128$ and trained on 18M interactions:

Training: ~15 minutes on a single GPU. ~45 minutes on CPU. Compare to item-kNN's 45-minute offline similarity computation — similar cost, but MF produces embeddings that can score the entire catalog for any user, not just items with co-interactions.

Serving: Each user's top-1000 items can be retrieved via approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) search on the item embeddings in ~10ms. This replaces item-kNN's Phase 2 lookup with a more powerful retrieval mechanism.

Quality: On Kartify's held-out test set, biased MF typically delivers nDCG@10 improvements of 15–25% over item-kNN with shrinkage. The gain comes from transitive generalization — MF scores item pairs that kNN couldn't even see.
