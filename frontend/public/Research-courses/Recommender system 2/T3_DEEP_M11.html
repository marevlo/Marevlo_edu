D1 / DERIVATION
Shrinkage — Bayesian interpretation
↳ corresponds to M11 S2: Shrinkage — The One-Line Fix
Real-life analogy

Imagine you're estimating a coin's bias. You flip it 3 times — all heads. The raw estimate says P(heads) = 100%. But your PRIOR belief is that most coins are fair (P ≈ 50%). With only 3 flips, you should trust your prior more than the data. With 1000 flips (all heads), you'd trust the data. Shrinkage implements exactly this: blend the raw estimate with a prior (similarity = 0), weighted by sample size.

Shrinkage as Bayesian posterior mean
$$\text{sim}_{\text{shrunk}}(i, j) = \frac{n_{ij}}{n_{ij} + \lambda} \cdot \text{sim}_{\text{raw}}(i, j) + \frac{\lambda}{n_{ij} + \lambda} \cdot 0$$ $$= \frac{n_{ij}}{n_{ij} + \lambda} \cdot \text{sim}_{\text{raw}}(i, j)$$
$n_{ij}$Number of co-interactions (users who interacted with BOTH $i$ and $j$). This is the "sample size" for this similarity estimate
$\lambda$Shrinkage strength — "how many pseudo-observations of similarity=0 do we add?" Higher $\lambda$ = more conservative. Typical: 50–200
$n_{ij}/(n_{ij}+\lambda)$Data weight. When $n_{ij} = 1$: weight = $1/101 = 0.01$ (trust data 1%). When $n_{ij} = 100$: weight = $100/200 = 0.50$ (trust data 50%). When $n_{ij} = 500$: weight = $500/600 = 0.83$ (trust data 83%)
$\lambda/(n_{ij}+\lambda)$Prior weight. Pulls similarity toward 0 (our prior: assume items are unrelated until proven otherwise). This is the "innocent until proven guilty" principle in math
D2 / WORKED
Shrinkage on 6 Kartify item pairs — reordering
↳ corresponds to M11 S3: Before and After
Apply shrinkage (λ=100), watch rankings change
Step by step
RAW SCORES (BEFORE)
$$\begin{array}{lccc} \text{Item pair} & \text{raw cos} & n_{ij} & \text{rank} \\ \hline \text{Pichwai Art Frame} & 1.000 & 1 & \#1 \\ \text{Vintage Brass Lamp} & 1.000 & 2 & \#2 \\ \text{Embroidered Lehenga} & 0.780 & 320 & \#3 \\ \text{Chikankari Palazzo} & 0.720 & 190 & \#4 \\ \text{Gold Jhumka Earrings} & 0.610 & 280 & \#5 \\ \text{Cotton Kurti Set} & 0.580 & 240 & \#6 \end{array}$$
APPLY SHRINKAGE
$$\text{Art Frame: } 1.000 \times \frac{1}{1+100} = 1.000 \times 0.0099 = 0.010$$ $$\text{Brass Lamp: } 1.000 \times \frac{2}{2+100} = 1.000 \times 0.0196 = 0.020$$ $$\text{Lehenga: } 0.780 \times \frac{320}{320+100} = 0.780 \times 0.762 = 0.594$$ $$\text{Palazzo: } 0.720 \times \frac{190}{190+100} = 0.720 \times 0.655 = 0.472$$ $$\text{Earrings: } 0.610 \times \frac{280}{280+100} = 0.610 \times 0.737 = 0.450$$ $$\text{Kurti: } 0.580 \times \frac{240}{240+100} = 0.580 \times 0.706 = 0.409$$

$$\text{Lehenga } (0.594) > \text{Palazzo } (0.472) > \text{Earrings } (0.450) > \text{Kurti } (0.409) \gg \text{Lamp } (0.020) > \text{Art Frame } (0.010)$$

The garbage items (Art Frame, Brass Lamp) dropped from #1–2 to #5–6. The legitimate recommendations with hundreds of co-interactions now dominate. Same algorithm, same data, one multiplication changed everything.

D3 / FORMALIZATION
IDF dampening and user normalization
↳ corresponds to M11 S4: Four More Production Fixes
IDF popularity dampening
$$\text{sim}_{\text{IDF}}(i, j) = \frac{\sum_{u \in U_{ij}} \frac{1}{\log(1 + |\mathcal{H}_u|)} \cdot r_{ui} \cdot r_{uj}}{\text{norm}_i \cdot \text{norm}_j}$$
$1/\log(1+|\mathcal{H}_u|)$IDF-style weight for user $u$. Users who interact with many items ($|\mathcal{H}_u|$ large) contribute LESS per co-interaction. A user who clicked 1,000 items contributes $1/\log(1001) = 0.145$ per pair. A user who clicked 10 items contributes $1/\log(11) = 0.417$ — 3× more weight per co-interaction
IntuitionA power user who buys everything is less informative than a selective user. If Alice buys 500 products, her co-purchase of items A and B is weak evidence they're related — she buys everything. If Bob buys 5 products including A and B, that's strong evidence of a real connection
k-selection by cross-validation
$$k^* = \arg\max_{k \in \{10, 20, 50, 100, 200\}} \text{nDCG@10}_{\text{val}}(k)$$
Procedure1. Split data temporally: train (first 80% of time) / validation (next 10%) / test (last 10%). 2. For each candidate $k$: build item-kNN with $k$ neighbors on train data, evaluate on validation users. 3. Pick $k$ with highest validation nDCG@10. 4. Re-train on train+val, final evaluation on test
Typical range$k = 20$: too few neighbors, insufficient coverage. $k = 200$: too many noisy neighbors. Sweet spot for Kartify (480K items): $k \approx 50$–100
D4 / INTERVIEW
Interview problems — shrinkage
Problem: Optimal λ
Interview
PROBLEM

With $\lambda = 100$, how many co-interactions are needed for the shrunk similarity to reach 90% of the raw similarity?

SOLUTION
$$\frac{n}{n + 100} = 0.90 \quad \Rightarrow \quad n = 0.90(n + 100) = 0.90n + 90 \quad \Rightarrow \quad 0.10n = 90 \quad \Rightarrow \quad n = 900$$

900 co-interactions needed to reach 90% of raw similarity. At Kartify's average of 37.5 interactions per item, most item pairs will have far fewer than 900 co-interactions — shrinkage has a significant effect on most pairs, which is exactly the desired behavior.

D5 / ADDITIONAL INTERVIEW
More interview problems — shrinkage & production kNN
Real-life analogy

Shrinkage is like a restaurant review system. A new restaurant with 2 reviews averaging 5.0 stars shouldn't outrank an established restaurant with 500 reviews averaging 4.7. Shrinkage pulls the small-sample restaurant toward the global average (say 3.8), so its shrunk score becomes ~4.2 — still good, but not unreasonably inflated. The λ parameter controls how many reviews you need before the raw average is trusted. High λ means "I need a LOT of reviews before I trust you." This is exactly what shrinkage does to item-item similarity: pairs with few co-interactions get pulled toward zero.

Problem: Choosing λ for a new catalog
Interview
PROBLEM

You're building item-kNN for a fashion marketplace with 50K items. 80% of item pairs have fewer than 5 co-interactions. What λ would you start with, and how would you tune it?

SOLUTION

Start with λ = the median number of co-interactions. If 80% of pairs have < 5 co-interactions, the median is likely around 2–3. Start with λ = 50–100 (well above the median) to aggressively shrink unreliable pairs. This means a pair needs ~50 co-interactions before its similarity is trusted at half strength.

Tune by grid search on validation nDCG@10: try λ ∈ {10, 25, 50, 100, 200, 500}. Plot nDCG vs λ — you'll typically see an inverted-U: too low (noisy similarities hurt), optimal at 50–200, too high (everything shrinks to zero). The optimal λ scales with data sparsity: sparser data → higher λ.

Problem: Shrinkage vs. minimum support
Interview
PROBLEM

A colleague suggests: "Instead of shrinkage, just filter out item pairs with fewer than 10 co-interactions." Is this better or worse than shrinkage? When might the simple filter be preferred?

SOLUTION

Shrinkage is almost always better. The hard threshold (min support = 10) creates a cliff: a pair with 9 co-interactions has zero similarity, but a pair with 10 has full similarity. This discards useful partial information and creates arbitrary cutoffs. Shrinkage is a smooth transition: pairs with 9 co-interactions get shrunk more than pairs with 100, but neither is thrown away completely.

The one case where a hard filter wins: computation speed. If the similarity matrix is too large to fit in memory, filtering out pairs below a threshold reduces the matrix size dramatically. In this case, use the hard filter for sparsification, then apply shrinkage to the remaining pairs. You get both the memory savings and the noise reduction.
