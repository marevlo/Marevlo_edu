Your data is
lying to you.

Every recommender system learns from data. And every piece of data it learns from is biased, incomplete, or misleading in specific, predictable ways. This module is about learning to see the lies — so you can build systems that survive them.

01 / THE PROBLEM

In 2019, a major streaming platform discovered that 30% of its "plays" were actually autoplay — the user fell asleep and episodes kept rolling. Their recommendation model thought these users loved the content. In reality, the users couldn't even name the shows the next morning. The model was optimizing for unconsciousness, not enjoyment. This is what Module 03 is about: the data lies, and the model believes it.

Open Kartify's data. 99.998% of it is empty. What does "empty" mean?
The most important question in recommender systems is not about what's in your data. It's about what's missing.

In Module 01, we saw the user-item matrix — rows of users, columns of items, a few filled cells, and vast emptiness. Let's feel that emptiness at Kartify's real scale.

At Kartify's scale — 2.3M users, 480K items — there are 1.1 trillion possible user-item pairs. Of those, about 18 million have any observed interaction. That's 0.0016%. The matrix is 99.998% empty.

Pause — this matters

When a cell in the matrix is empty — when Priya hasn't interacted with a particular dress — what does that actually mean? Is it one of these?

(a) Priya saw it and didn't like it   (b) Priya never saw it   (c) Priya saw it, liked it, but didn't click   (d) Any of the above

(d) Any of the above — and that's exactly the problem. An empty cell is ambiguous. It could mean "disliked," "didn't see," "saw but was busy," or "would love it but the previous recommender never showed it." Most algorithms treat empty cells as either "negative" or "missing at random." Neither is accurate. This single ambiguity — what does absence mean? — is the most fundamental challenge in recommender systems. Every algorithm in this track handles it differently, and none handle it perfectly.

This is the first lie in your data: absence isn't rejection. A user who hasn't clicked on an item probably hasn't seen it — because the previous system never showed it. Training a model to treat "not clicked" as "not interested" bakes the old system's biases into the new one.

02 / DISCOVERY
Four types of signal — and each one lies differently
Not all data is created equal. Understanding the four feedback regimes is the first real decision in any recsys project.

Every recommender system learns from some form of user-item signal. But signals come in four fundamentally different flavours, each with different strengths, different weaknesses, and different lies.

Regime 1
Explicit Ratings
The user directly scores an item — 1 to 5 stars, thumbs up/down, a numeric rating. This is the clearest signal: the user actively chose to express an opinion.

The fundamental tradeoff

Explicit signals are high quality but rare. Implicit signals are abundant but noisy. Every production recsys uses implicit data as its primary fuel — because there's 1000× more of it. But learning from implicit data is harder, because a click is ambiguous (accidental? curiosity? genuine interest?), and absence is even more ambiguous (didn't see? didn't like? didn't care?).

03 / INSIGHT
Not all clicks are equal — the confidence spectrum
A single click whispers. A purchase shouts. A return screams. Learning to weight signals by confidence is what separates toy models from real ones.

Here's a key insight that will change how you think about recommendation data: every interaction is really a pair — (preference signal, confidence level). A single page view is weak evidence of interest. A purchase after three return visits is strong evidence. Ten seconds of dwell time on a product page could be genuine interest or just slow internet.

This isn't just a nice diagram — it's a design decision you have to make for every recsys. When you build Kartify's recommender, you'll need to decide: does a page view count as positive signal? How much more should a purchase weigh than a click? What about a return — should it cancel the purchase signal, or is it actually more informative (the user cared enough to buy AND cared enough to send it back)?

The algorithm that made this thinking concrete is WRMF (Hu, Koren, Volinsky 2008) — you'll learn it in detail around Module 16. Its key innovation: treat every user-item pair as a data point (not just the observed ones), but weight them by confidence. A purchase gets high weight. An unobserved pair gets weight 1 — not zero, because "didn't interact" still carries information. This simple reframing unlocked production-scale implicit feedback recsys.

04 / THE LIES
Three lies hiding in every recommendation dataset
Your data doesn't reflect what users like. It reflects what a previous system showed them — filtered through position, popularity, and self-selection.

This is the section that changes how you think about ML data forever. Every recommendation dataset contains at least three systematic biases. Not noise — bias. Predictable, directional distortions that corrupt your model in specific ways if you don't account for them.

The three biases — tap to explore each one
Critical
Popularity Bias ▸
Popular items look better than they are. Niche items look worse.

Popular items get more clicks simply because more people see them. A dress that appears on every user's homepage gets 10,000 clicks. A better dress that was only shown to 50 users gets 12 clicks. By raw click count, the first dress "wins." But if both had been shown to the same audience, the second might have had a higher click-through rate.

Kartify example: A ₹599 cotton kurti appears in Kartify's "Trending" section (shown to 500K users, 15K clicks = 3% CTR). A hand-embroidered ₹2,199 kurti from a new designer is buried on page 4 (shown to 2K users, 180 clicks = 9% CTR). The algorithm trained on raw data thinks the cheap kurti is 3× more popular. It's actually the opposite — but the data can't tell you that without adjusting for exposure.
Position Bias ▸
Items at the top get clicked regardless of quality.

Users click on items shown higher in the list — not because they're better, but because they're there. In search results, position 1 gets 10× more clicks than position 10, even if position 10 is a better match. Your model learns this pattern and interprets it as: "items that were shown first are more relevant." That's circular — the model will keep showing the same items first, and keep "confirming" they're the best.

Kartify example: In Kartify's search results for "blue jeans," the first result gets clicked 40% of the time. The fifth result gets clicked 8% of the time. A model trained on this data will always put the first result on top — even if you randomly shuffle the results and re-measure, finding that the fifth result actually has a higher quality signal. Position is confounded with relevance.
Selection Bias ▸
Users only rate things they chose to consume — and they chose things they expected to like.

This is the deepest bias and the hardest to fix. Your data only contains interactions for items users were exposed to — which were chosen by a previous recommendation system (or by the user's own search). You're training a new model on the old model's outputs. If the old model never showed horror movies to comedy fans, your new model has zero data on whether comedy fans might enjoy horror. The data has a hole shaped exactly like the old model's blind spots.

Kartify example: Kartify's current system never shows menswear to users who previously browsed only womenswear. Result: zero cross-category data. A man shopping for a gift for his wife, or a woman browsing for her partner, never gets cross-category recommendations — because the training data says "this user only likes womenswear." The model faithfully reproduces the old system's limitation.
The compounding problem

These three biases don't just coexist — they compound. Popular items get shown first (position bias), which makes them more popular (popularity bias), which means the system keeps showing them (selection bias), which generates more data confirming they're popular. The rich get richer. Niche items, new items, and items in underserved categories get starved of exposure, which starves them of data, which makes the model ignore them even more. This is the feedback loop that makes recsys bias self-reinforcing.

05 / KARTIFY
Kartify's data — an honest audit
Let's apply everything we've learned to the data Kartify actually has. This is the audit every recsys project should start with.
Kartify's data profile

Primary signal: Implicit positive (clicks, page views, add-to-cart, purchases) — Regime 2. Volume: ~18M events/month.

Secondary signal: Limited explicit negative (search refinements like "not red," "under ₹1000" — weak Regime 3). Volume: ~200K events/month.

No explicit ratings. No star ratings. No thumbs up/down on products. This rules out RMSE-based approaches from Era 1/2.

Rich content metadata: Product title, description, images, brand, price, size, colour, style tags, category hierarchy. This is Kartify's cold-start advantage — new products can be placed in semantic space from metadata alone.

Sparsity: 0.0016% fill rate. The matrix is 99.998% empty.

Bias exposure: All three biases are present. The current non-personalised "Trending" page creates extreme popularity bias. Category-based navigation creates selection bias within categories. Position bias exists in search results.

Pause — apply

Given Kartify's data profile, what's the single most important thing to get right when building their first recommender?

Don't treat absence as negative. With 99.998% of the matrix empty, if you treat every un-interacted pair as "user doesn't like this item," you'll train a model that thinks every user dislikes 99.998% of the catalog. The model will learn to predict "not interested" for everything and fall back to recommending only the most globally popular items. Instead, you need an algorithm designed for implicit feedback — one that treats unobserved pairs as "unknown with low confidence," not as negatives. This is exactly what BPR and WRMF do, and why they were invented.
Kartify's data team proposes training a model to predict purchase probability. They plan to label all purchases as positive (1) and all non-purchases as negative (0). What's the flaw?
There aren't enough purchases to train a model✗ Not the core issue
Non-purchase doesn't mean "not interested" — it usually means "never saw it"✓ Correct
Purchase data is too noisy to learn from✗ Purchases are high-confidence
They should use click data instead of purchase data✗ Partially right but misses the point
The core flaw: labelling all 1.1 trillion non-purchased pairs as "0" creates a massively imbalanced dataset where the model learns to predict "won't purchase" for everything. A user who hasn't purchased a product almost certainly hasn't seen it — absence of purchase is not evidence of disinterest. The fix: use pairwise ranking (BPR) which only requires "user preferred A over B," or weighted approaches (WRMF) that give low confidence to unobserved pairs. You'll learn both techniques in Act II.
06 / THE TRUTH
The uncomfortable truth — and why we build anyway
Every algorithm in this track is wrong. Here's why, and here's why we use them anyway.

Let's be honest about what we've just learned. Every algorithm you'll encounter in this track makes at least one of these assumptions, and every one is false:

Assumption: the data reflects user preference. It doesn't. It reflects preference filtered through an exposure mechanism that was itself a recommender system. The data is shaped by the system that generated it.

Assumption: absence means irrelevance. It doesn't. It usually means "never saw it." In a 99.998%-sparse matrix, almost all empty cells are unexplored, not rejected.

Assumption: the data is representative. It isn't. Popular items dominate. Items at top positions dominate. Categories the old system favoured dominate. The data is a funhouse mirror of reality.

So why build on this foundation?

Because biased data, properly handled, is still vastly better than no data. The algorithms that work — BPR, WRMF, two-tower models, contextual bandits — don't pretend the data is unbiased. They account for it: through pairwise learning that avoids hard negatives, through confidence weighting that treats absence as uncertainty, through exploration mechanisms that deliberately show items the model is uncertain about. Module by module, you'll learn not just the algorithms but the debiasing strategies that make them work despite the lies.

The mindset to carry forward

From this point on, every time you see a recommender system algorithm, ask two questions: (1) What assumption is this algorithm making about the data? and (2) How is that assumption violated in my real data? If you can answer both, you can anticipate where the algorithm will fail — and either fix it or choose a better one. That's the difference between someone who can implement a recsys and someone who can debug one.

After this module, you see what's invisible to most ML engineers:

You know the four feedback regimes — explicit, implicit, negative, conversational — and their fundamental tradeoffs between quality and volume.
You understand the confidence spectrum — not all interactions are equal, and weighting them by confidence is a design decision that shapes the entire system.
You can name the three systematic biases — popularity, position, selection — and explain how they compound through the feedback loop.
You know why treating absence as negative is the single most common mistake in recsys, and why algorithms like BPR and WRMF were invented to avoid it.
You've done an honest data audit of Kartify and know exactly what its data can and cannot tell you.
