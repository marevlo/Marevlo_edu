01 / THE SURPRISE

In 2006, a Netflix engineer switched the similarity metric in their item-kNN system from cosine to adjusted cosine. No model change. No architecture change. Just one line of math. nDCG improved 4%. The CEO sent a company-wide email congratulating the team. The engineer's response: "I just changed the denominator." This module is about that denominator — and why choosing the right similarity metric matters more than choosing the right model.

Same algorithm, different similarity metric, different recommendations
This is the hyperparameter that changes what the system recommends — not just how well.

In Module 09, we computed item-item similarity using cosine. But what if we used Jaccard? Or conditional probability? The item-kNN algorithm is identical — only the similarity function in Phase 1 changes. And yet the recommendations are qualitatively different.

Cosine normalises for popularity — a niche item with 10 interactions can be highly similar to another niche item with 12 interactions. It surfaces hidden gems.

Jaccard rewards co-occurrence — items that are frequently bought together rank high, but it's biased toward pairs of items with similar (small) audiences. Two niche items with 5 overlapping users score higher than two popular items with 50 overlapping users but 5,000 users each.

Conditional probability is asymmetric — P(earrings|saree) ≠ P(saree|earrings). "Given that someone bought a saree, what's the probability they also bought earrings?" This is the "also bought" metric — directional and purchase-intent-aware.

Each captures a genuinely different definition of "similar." Choosing the wrong one doesn't just reduce quality — it changes the kind of recommendations the system makes.

02 / THE THREE
Three metrics, three intuitions
Don't memorise formulas — understand what each metric captures about the relationship between two items.
Cosine Similarity
cos(a, b) = a · b / (|a| × |b|)
Measures the angle between two item vectors. Ignores magnitude (how popular each item is) and focuses on direction (do the same users interact with both?). Two items can have very different popularity levels and still have cosine similarity ≈ 1 if their audiences overlap proportionally.
✓ Best for: Implicit binary data (click/no-click). Normalises for activity level. Default for most modern recsys.
✗ Watch for: Treats all interactions equally — a 1-star rating and a 5-star rating are both "1" in binary.
Jaccard Similarity
J(A, B) = |A ∩ B| / |A ∪ B|
Measures set overlap. How many users interacted with BOTH items, divided by how many interacted with EITHER. Pure co-occurrence — ignores interaction strength entirely. Two items with identical audiences have Jaccard = 1 regardless of how they were rated.
✓ Best for: "Also bought" when interaction is binary. Simple, interpretable, no hyperparameters.
✗ Watch for: Biased toward items with similar popularity. Two niche items get high Jaccard easily; a niche + popular pair get low Jaccard even if co-purchase rate is high.
Conditional Probability
P(j | i) = count(i ∩ j) / count(i)
Asymmetric — "given item i, how often do users also interact with j?" This is NOT a similarity metric in the traditional sense — P(j|i) ≠ P(i|j). It captures directional association: "people who buy sarees often also buy earrings" is different from "people who buy earrings often also buy sarees."
✓ Best for: "Customers who bought X also bought Y" modules. Directional, purchase-intent-aware.
✗ Watch for: Biased toward popular items. P(popular_item | any_item) is always high because popular items are bought by everyone.

Cosine puts the Embroidered Lehenga and Chikankari Set high — they have proportionally overlapping audiences with the saree, even though they're smaller items. Cosine normalises for popularity, so niche items with strong overlap score well.

Jaccard shifts the ranking — items with similar-sized audiences to the saree score higher, because Jaccard's denominator (A ∪ B) penalises pairs where one item is much more popular than the other.

Conditional probability pushes popular items up — Gold Jhumka Earrings climb because P(earrings|saree) is high (many saree buyers also buy earrings), even though P(saree|earrings) would be lower. The asymmetry changes the recommendation direction.
04 / THE RULE
The decision framework — which metric for which data
A simple rule that covers 90% of real-world cases.
Your data typeDefault metricWhy
Implicit binary
(click / no-click)		CosineNormalises for activity level. A power user with 500 clicks and a casual user with 5 clicks contribute proportionally. The default for most modern recsys.
Explicit ratings
(1–5 stars)		Pearson correlationAdjusts for per-user bias — some users rate everything 4+, some rate harshly. Pearson mean-centres before comparing, so a "4 from a harsh rater" is weighted more than a "4 from an easy rater."
"Also bought"
module		Jaccard or
conditional prob		Jaccard for symmetric "related items." Conditional probability for directional "bought X → recommend Y." Both are interpretable and fast to compute.
Mixed signal
(view + cart + buy)		Asymmetric cosine
(advanced)		Weights different interaction types differently. α controls the balance. Tuned per domain. Covered in the Companion deep dive.
Kartify's choice

For the product page "Similar items" widget: Cosine similarity on implicit binary (clicked / not-clicked). This is the default and works well for Kartify's click-dominated data.

For the cart page "Complete your look" widget: Conditional probability P(accessory | clothing item). This is directional — "given you're buying this saree, what do people also buy?" — which matches the cart upsell intent perfectly.

Two surfaces, two metrics, same algorithm. The metric is the product decision, not the engineering decision.

Multi-source retrieval into a ranking stage is how every production recsys at scale works. The next 15 modules will make each component more sophisticated. But the shape is already correct.

The ceiling ahead

Neighborhood methods have a fundamental limitation: they operate directly on the raw interaction matrix. As the matrix gets sparser (and at Kartify's 99.998% sparsity, it's very sparse), the overlap between item vectors shrinks, and similarity estimates become noisy. Matrix factorization — the next major algorithm family — solves this by compressing the matrix into a low-dimensional latent space where even items with zero co-interactions can have meaningful similarity. That's the leap from Modules 14–18.
