Feedback loops —
the system that eats itself.

Your recommender shows popular items. Users click popular items. The model learns that popular items get clicks. It shows them more. They get more clicks. The rich get richer. New items never get shown. Niche tastes are never discovered. The catalog narrows. User satisfaction decays. This is the feedback loop — the most insidious failure mode in production recommendation. This module teaches you to see it, measure it, and break it.

01 / THE TRAP
How recommenders eat their own tail
The model creates the data it trains on. This breaks the assumption of every ML algorithm: that training data is independent of the model.

Month 1: Kartify launches BPR-MF. The model recommends items based on historical interactions. Popular items get recommended more → get more interactions → become even more popular in the next training cycle. Meanwhile, a new artisan collection from Rajasthan gets zero recommendations → zero interactions → the model learns it has zero value → it's never recommended again.

Month 3: 80% of homepage recommendations come from the top 5% of items. User retention starts declining. Exit surveys say: "I keep seeing the same things." The model's nDCG is improving (it's getting better at predicting the data IT created), but the business is getting worse.

Month 6: The product team demands more diversity. The ML team adds MMR reranking. It helps — but the underlying model still believes the Rajasthani collection is worthless, because it was never given a chance. The damage from 6 months of feedback loop is baked into the training data.

Pause — the fundamental violation

Every ML algorithm assumes that training data is sampled independently of the model. Why does a recommender violate this assumption? What's the consequence?

The model CHOOSES which items to show. Users can only interact with shown items. The next training set is the interactions generated by the current model's recommendations. The model trains on data it created — a self-fulfilling prophecy. The consequence: the model becomes increasingly confident in its own biases. Items it never shows have no data, so it rates them as irrelevant, so it never shows them. This is confirmation bias at scale.
02 / POSITION BIAS
Position bias — the most measurable bias in recsys
An item in position 1 gets 10× more clicks than an item in position 10 — regardless of quality. This poisons your training data.

On Kartify's homepage, the item in position 1 (top-left, large image) gets ~15% CTR. The same item in position 10 gets ~1.5% CTR. The item didn't change — only its position. But the training data records: "item in position 1 was clicked" (positive) and "item in position 10 was not clicked" (negative). The model learns: "position-1 items are good, position-10 items are bad." It's learning the layout, not the item quality.

This creates a reinforcing loop: items currently ranked high get more clicks → model ranks them higher → they get even more clicks. Items ranked low get fewer clicks → model ranks them lower → they get even fewer clicks. The ranking calcifies.

Pause — estimate the bias

If Kartify shows 20 items in a grid with position CTR: [15%, 12%, 10%, 8%, 6%, 5%, 4%, 3.5%, 3%, 2.5%, ...], and you train a model on these clicks as positive/negative labels, how biased is the model toward top positions?

Massively biased. Position 1 generates 15% positive labels; position 10 generates 2.5% — a 6× difference that has nothing to do with item quality. A model trained on this data will attribute the 6× CTR difference to item quality, not position. Fix: use position as a feature during training (so the model learns "this item had 15% CTR AND was in position 1"), then set position = average at serving time (so the model predicts quality-only CTR without position boost).
03 / DEBIASING
Inverse Propensity Scoring — weighting by exposure probability
If an item was shown to users 100 times and clicked 10 times, its CTR is 10%. But if it was only shown because it was already popular, that 10% is inflated by selection bias. IPS corrects for this.

The idea: weight each training example by the inverse of its probability of being shown. If an item was very likely to be shown (high propensity), give it less weight in training — the click was "easy" to get. If an item was unlikely to be shown (low propensity), give it more weight — the click is a stronger signal of genuine quality.

# IPS-weighted loss for BPR training
for user, pos_item, neg_item in sample_triples(data):
    pos_score = dot(user_emb, item_emb[pos_item])
    neg_score = dot(user_emb, item_emb[neg_item])

    # Standard BPR:
    # loss = -log(sigmoid(pos_score - neg_score))

    # IPS-weighted BPR:
    propensity = exposure_probability[pos_item]  # estimated from logging policy
    weight = 1.0 / max(propensity, 0.01)      # cap to prevent extreme weights
    loss = -weight * log(sigmoid(pos_score - neg_score))

# Items that were rarely shown but still clicked get high weight
# Items that were always shown (popular) get low weight
# This corrects for the popularity feedback loop

Estimating propensity: The exposure probability can be estimated from the logging data. If the current model showed item X in 10% of sessions, propensity ≈ 0.10. If it showed item Y in 0.1% of sessions, propensity ≈ 0.001. The weight for Y's clicks is 100× higher than X's — correctly reflecting that Y's click is much more informative.

The variance problem

IPS is unbiased but high-variance. A rarely-shown item with propensity 0.001 gets weight 1000 — a single click from this item dominates the gradient. In practice, clip the weights (max weight = 100) or use doubly robust estimation (combine IPS with a direct estimator to reduce variance while maintaining unbiasedness). SNIPS (Self-Normalized IPS) divides by the sum of weights — simpler and more stable in practice.

04 / EXPLORE-EXPLOIT
The explore-exploit dilemma — when NOT to show the "best" item
Always showing the model's top prediction maximizes short-term engagement but starves the model of data about alternatives. Sometimes you should deliberately show a worse item to learn.

Imagine a restaurant with 50 dishes. You've tried 5 and loved dish #3. A "pure exploit" recommender says: "always order dish #3." But you might love dish #27 even more — you've never tried it. A "pure explore" recommender says: "try a random dish every time." You'll discover #27 eventually, but you'll also suffer through many bad dishes.

The optimal strategy is in between: mostly order dish #3 (exploit what you know), but occasionally try a random dish (explore what you don't). How often to explore depends on how uncertain you are about the untried dishes and how much you're willing to sacrifice short-term satisfaction for long-term discovery.

In recommendation, exploration means occasionally showing items the model is uncertain about. Benefits: discover unexpectedly good items, collect data for new/niche items, prevent feedback loops. Costs: some users see less relevant recommendations (short-term engagement dip).

Pause — when is exploration most valuable?

In which of these scenarios would you invest more in exploration: (a) a mature platform with 5 years of data and a stable catalog, or (b) a new platform with 3 months of data and 500 new products per day?

(b) by far. A new platform has high uncertainty (few interactions per item), rapid catalog change (500 new items/day need exposure), and a growing user base (new users have no interaction history). Exploration is most valuable when uncertainty is high and the catalog is dynamic. A mature platform with stable data has lower uncertainty — the model already knows most items well — so less exploration is needed. But even mature platforms should explore ~5–10% of recommendations to prevent calcification.
05 / THOMPSON SAMPLING
Thompson sampling — exploration that adapts automatically
Instead of fixed ε-greedy exploration, sample from the model's uncertainty. Items the model is uncertain about get explored more. Items it's confident about get exploited.

The ε-greedy approach (show random item with probability ε = 0.1) is simple but wasteful. It explores uniformly — spending as much exploration on items the model already knows well as on items it's never seen. Thompson sampling is smarter: it explores proportionally to uncertainty.

# Thompson Sampling for recommendation
# Instead of: score = model.predict(user, item)  → deterministic
# Do:         score = model.predict(user, item) + noise(uncertainty)

def thompson_recommend(user, items, model, k=20):
    scores = []
    for item in items:
        mean_score = model.predict(user, item)
        uncertainty = model.get_uncertainty(item)  # e.g., 1/sqrt(n_interactions)
        # Sample from posterior: score ~ Normal(mean_score, uncertainty)
        sampled_score = np.random.normal(mean_score, uncertainty)
        scores.append(sampled_score)

    # Rank by SAMPLED scores, not mean scores
    return top_k(items, scores, k)

# Effect: items with high uncertainty occasionally get HIGH sampled scores
# → they get shown → user interacts → uncertainty decreases
# → next time, their sampled score is closer to their mean
# → exploration of that item decreases AUTOMATICALLY
# Items already well-known have low uncertainty → sampled ≈ mean → pure exploit

Thompson sampling naturally balances: new items with high uncertainty get explored aggressively (their sampled scores swing wildly). Well-known items with low uncertainty get exploited reliably (their sampled scores are stable near the mean). As the model collects data, exploration of each item decreases automatically. No tuning of ε needed.

Thompson sampling in the retrieval stage

In practice, Thompson sampling is often applied at the RETRIEVAL stage, not ranking. Add noise proportional to uncertainty to the retrieval scores before ANN search. This means some uncertain items enter the top-1000 candidates that wouldn't otherwise. The ranking model then has a chance to score them properly. If they rank well, they get shown. If not, they're filtered out. This is less risky than exploring at the final display stage — the ranking model acts as a safety net.

06 / KARTIFY
Kartify's debiasing and exploration strategy
The complete anti-loop system.
Kartify bias & exploration protocol

Position bias correction:
• Position is a feature in the ranking model (DCN-v2). During training: actual position is used. During serving: position is set to the mean position (position = 5). This removes position bias from predictions while still learning from position-biased data.

Popularity debiasing:
• IPS weighting in BPR-MF training with SNIPS normalization. Weight cap: 50×. Propensity estimated from 30-day exposure logs.
• New item boost in reranking: items < 48hrs get 1.2× score multiplier to ensure initial exposure.

Exploration:
• Thompson sampling at retrieval stage. Uncertainty = $1/\sqrt{\max(n_\text{interactions}, 1)}$. ~8% of candidates are exploration items.
• Mandatory exploration slot: position 7 in every recommendation list is reserved for a high-uncertainty item. Users learn to expect "something new" in this slot — reducing the engagement cost of exploration.

Monitoring:
• Item coverage: % of catalog recommended at least once per week. Target: > 60%. Below 40% = feedback loop alert.
• Gini coefficient of exposure: measures how evenly exposure is distributed. Target: < 0.7. Above 0.8 = severe popularity concentration.
• New item time-to-first-impression: median hours until a new product is shown to at least 100 users. Target: < 6 hours.

After this module, you understand bias and exploration:

Feedback loops: The model creates its training data. Popular items get more popular. New items get suppressed. The catalog narrows.
Position bias: Position 1 gets 6× more clicks than position 10. If you train on raw clicks, you're learning the layout, not item quality.
IPS debiasing: Weight training examples by 1/propensity. Rarely-shown items that get clicked deserve high weight.
Thompson sampling: Explore proportionally to uncertainty. New items get explored aggressively. Well-known items get exploited. No tuning needed.
Monitoring: Item coverage, Gini coefficient, new item time-to-first-impression. The metrics that detect feedback loops before users complain.
