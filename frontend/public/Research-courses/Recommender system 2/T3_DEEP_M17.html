D1 / DERIVATION
ALS closed-form — from gradient to matrix equation
↳ corresponds to M17 S3: ALS — Why Not SGD
Real-life analogy

Imagine fitting a line through data points (linear regression). You don't need gradient descent — there's an exact formula: $\mathbf{w} = (X^\top X)^{-1} X^\top \mathbf{y}$. ALS does the same thing, but in a higher-dimensional space. For each user, fix all item embeddings, and the optimal user embedding has an exact formula. Solve it. Then fix all user embeddings, solve for items. Alternate. Each step is exact — no learning rate, no convergence issues.

WRMF loss (for one user u, fixing V)
$$\mathcal{L}_u = \sum_{i=1}^{N} c_{ui}\left(p_{ui} - \mathbf{u}_u^\top \mathbf{v}_i\right)^2 + \lambda \|\mathbf{u}_u\|^2$$
$p_{ui}$Binary preference: 1 if observed, 0 otherwise
$c_{ui}$Confidence: $1 + \alpha \cdot r_{ui}$. High for observed, 1 for unobserved
$\sum_{i=1}^N$Sum over ALL items — not just observed ones. This is the key difference from BPR

Take the derivative, set to zero

Gradient w.r.t. user embedding
$$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}_u}{\partial \mathbf{u}_u} = -2 \sum_i c_{ui}(p_{ui} - \mathbf{u}_u^\top \mathbf{v}_i)\mathbf{v}_i + 2\lambda \mathbf{u}_u = 0$$ $$\text{In matrix form with } C^u = \text{diag}(c_{u1}, \ldots, c_{uN}):$$ $$V^\top C^u V \, \mathbf{u}_u + \lambda I \, \mathbf{u}_u = V^\top C^u \mathbf{p}_u$$ $$(V^\top C^u V + \lambda I) \, \mathbf{u}_u = V^\top C^u \mathbf{p}_u$$
$V \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times k}$Item embedding matrix (all items stacked). Each row is one item's embedding
$C^u$Diagonal $N \times N$ matrix with confidence values for user $u$ on the diagonal. Most entries = 1 (unobserved); a few = $1 + \alpha \cdot r_{ui}$ (observed)
$\mathbf{p}_u$Binary preference vector for user $u$: $(p_{u1}, p_{u2}, \ldots, p_{uN})^\top$
Closed-form solution for user u
$$\mathbf{u}_u = (V^\top C^u V + \lambda I)^{-1} V^\top C^u \mathbf{p}_u$$
$(V^\top C^u V + \lambda I)$A $k \times k$ matrix (e.g., $128 \times 128$). This is the "normal equation" — same as weighted linear regression. Invertible because of the $\lambda I$ term (regularization ensures positive definiteness)
CostNaively $O(k^2 N)$ per user. With the computational trick below: $O(k^2 |\mathcal{I}_u| + k^3)$ per user, where $|\mathcal{I}_u| \ll N$

The computational trick — avoid O(k²N)

Decompose C^u into identity + sparse correction
$$V^\top C^u V = V^\top V + V^\top (C^u - I) V$$
$V^\top V$A $k \times k$ matrix computed ONCE and reused for all users. Cost: $O(k^2 N)$ one time. This is the "base" — what the product would be if all confidences were 1
$V^\top(C^u - I)V$Correction for user $u$'s observed items. $C^u - I$ is zero everywhere except observed items. So this sum only involves $|\mathcal{I}_u|$ items (typically ~8 for Kartify). Cost: $O(k^2 |\mathcal{I}_u|)$
SavingsInstead of $O(k^2 \times 480{,}000)$ per user, we pay $O(k^2 \times 8)$ per user + $O(k^2 \times 480{,}000)$ once. For 2.3M users: saves ~$480{,}000 / 8 = 60{,}000\times$ computation
D2 / WORKED
One ALS user update — 2 users × 3 items, k=2
↳ corresponds to M17 S3
Solve for user Alice's embedding (k=2, α=40)
Step by step
SETUP

3 items. Alice interacted with item A (count=2) and item C (count=1). Item B is unobserved.

$$\mathbf{p}_\text{Alice} = [1, 0, 1]^\top \qquad c_A = 1+40 \times 2 = 81, \quad c_B = 1, \quad c_C = 1+40 \times 1 = 41$$

Current item embeddings ($k=2$):

$$\mathbf{v}_A = [0.8, 0.2], \quad \mathbf{v}_B = [0.3, 0.7], \quad \mathbf{v}_C = [0.5, 0.4]$$ $$V = \begin{bmatrix} 0.8 & 0.2 \\ 0.3 & 0.7 \\ 0.5 & 0.4 \end{bmatrix}, \quad \lambda = 0.1$$
COMPUTE V⊤V (base, shared)
$$V^\top V = \begin{bmatrix} 0.8 & 0.3 & 0.5 \\ 0.2 & 0.7 & 0.4 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 0.8 & 0.2 \\ 0.3 & 0.7 \\ 0.5 & 0.4 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0.64+0.09+0.25 & 0.16+0.21+0.20 \\ 0.16+0.21+0.20 & 0.04+0.49+0.16 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0.98 & 0.57 \\ 0.57 & 0.69 \end{bmatrix}$$
COMPUTE CORRECTION V⊤(C^u - I)V

Only observed items contribute: $c_A - 1 = 80$, $c_C - 1 = 40$.

$$V^\top(C^u - I)V = 80 \cdot \mathbf{v}_A \mathbf{v}_A^\top + 40 \cdot \mathbf{v}_C \mathbf{v}_C^\top$$ $$= 80 \begin{bmatrix} 0.64 & 0.16 \\ 0.16 & 0.04 \end{bmatrix} + 40 \begin{bmatrix} 0.25 & 0.20 \\ 0.20 & 0.16 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 51.2+10 & 12.8+8 \\ 12.8+8 & 3.2+6.4 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 61.2 & 20.8 \\ 20.8 & 9.6 \end{bmatrix}$$
ASSEMBLE AND SOLVE
$$A = V^\top C^u V + \lambda I = \begin{bmatrix} 0.98+61.2+0.1 & 0.57+20.8 \\ 0.57+20.8 & 0.69+9.6+0.1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 62.28 & 21.37 \\ 21.37 & 10.39 \end{bmatrix}$$
$$\mathbf{b} = V^\top C^u \mathbf{p}_u = 81 \cdot \mathbf{v}_A \cdot 1 + 1 \cdot \mathbf{v}_B \cdot 0 + 41 \cdot \mathbf{v}_C \cdot 1 = 81[0.8, 0.2] + 41[0.5, 0.4]$$ $$= [64.8, 16.2] + [20.5, 16.4] = [85.3, 32.6]$$
$$\mathbf{u}_\text{Alice} = A^{-1} \mathbf{b} = \begin{bmatrix} 62.28 & 21.37 \\ 21.37 & 10.39 \end{bmatrix}^{-1} \begin{bmatrix} 85.3 \\ 32.6 \end{bmatrix}$$

Solving the 2×2 system (det = 62.28×10.39 − 21.37² = 647.1 − 456.7 = 190.4):

$$\mathbf{u}_\text{Alice} = \frac{1}{190.4}\begin{bmatrix} 10.39 & -21.37 \\ -21.37 & 62.28 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} 85.3 \\ 32.6 \end{bmatrix} = \frac{1}{190.4}\begin{bmatrix} 886.3 - 696.7 \\ -1822.9 + 2030.3 \end{bmatrix} = \frac{1}{190.4}\begin{bmatrix} 189.6 \\ 207.4 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0.996 \\ 1.089 \end{bmatrix}$$

Alice's new embedding: $[0.996, 1.089]$. Let's verify: $\hat{r}_\text{Alice,A} = 0.996 \times 0.8 + 1.089 \times 0.2 = 0.797 + 0.218 = 1.015$. With $p_A = 1$ and $c_A = 81$, the model predicts ≈1.0 for the strongly-observed positive — correct!

D3 / FROM SCRATCH
WRMF-ALS — complete NumPy implementation
↳ corresponds to M17 S3 & S5
# ═══ WRMF-ALS — from scratch, NumPy only ═══
import numpy as np
from scipy.sparse import csr_matrix

class WRMF:
    def __init__(self, k=128, alpha=40, reg=0.1, n_iter=15):
        self.k, self.alpha, self.reg, self.n_iter = k, alpha, reg, n_iter

    def fit(self, R):
        """R: sparse (users × items) interaction matrix."""
        R = csr_matrix(R)
        M, N = R.shape
        # Initialize embeddings
        self.U = np.random.normal(0, 0.01, (M, self.k))
        self.V = np.random.normal(0, 0.01, (N, self.k))
        # Confidence: C = 1 + alpha * R
        # P: binary preference (1 if observed, 0 otherwise)

        for it in range(self.n_iter):
            # Fix V, solve for each user
            VtV = self.V.T @ self.V  # k×k, precompute once
            for u in range(M):
                # Items user u interacted with
                items_u = R[u].indices
                counts_u = R[u].data
                if len(items_u) == 0: continue
                # Confidence correction (only observed items)
                Cu_minus_I = self.alpha * counts_u  # c_ui - 1 for observed
                Vu = self.V[items_u]  # (|I_u|, k)
                # A = VtV + V_u^T diag(Cu-I) V_u + λI
                A = VtV + (Vu.T * Cu_minus_I) @ Vu + self.reg * np.eye(self.k)
                # b = V_u^T * C_u * p_u (p_u=1 for observed)
                b = Vu.T @ (1 + Cu_minus_I)  # c_ui * p_ui, p=1
                self.U[u] = np.linalg.solve(A, b)

            # Fix U, solve for each item (symmetric)
            UtU = self.U.T @ self.U
            Rt = R.T.tocsr()
            for i in range(N):
                users_i = Rt[i].indices
                counts_i = Rt[i].data
                if len(users_i) == 0: continue
                Ci_minus_I = self.alpha * counts_i
                Ui = self.U[users_i]
                A = UtU + (Ui.T * Ci_minus_I) @ Ui + self.reg * np.eye(self.k)
                b = Ui.T @ (1 + Ci_minus_I)
                self.V[i] = np.linalg.solve(A, b)

            print(f"ALS iteration {it+1}/{self.n_iter} complete")

    def recommend(self, user_id, seen, top_k=10):
        scores = self.U[user_id] @ self.V.T
        scores[seen] = -np.inf
        return np.argsort(scores)[-top_k:][::-1]

Problem 1: ALS convergence
Interview
PROBLEM

Why does ALS converge? Is it guaranteed to find the global optimum?

SOLUTION

Why it converges: Each ALS step solves an exact sub-problem. When V is fixed, solving for U gives the global optimum for U given that V. The loss can only decrease (or stay the same) at each step. Since the loss is bounded below (≥ 0), a monotonically non-increasing, bounded-below sequence must converge.

Global optimum? No — ALS converges to a local minimum (or saddle point). The joint optimization over (U, V) is non-convex (the product $\mathbf{u}^\top \mathbf{v}$ is bilinear). Different random initializations can converge to different solutions. In practice, the solutions are usually good enough — the regularization landscape is smooth enough that most local minima have similar quality.

Problem 2: α sensitivity
Interview
PROBLEM

With α=40, a user who clicked once gets confidence 41. With α=1, they get confidence 2. How does α affect the model? When would you choose high vs low α?

SOLUTION

High α (40): Observed interactions dominate training. The model strongly pushes predictions toward 1 for observed pairs. Good when interactions are high-quality (purchases, not just page views). Risk: overfitting to observed data, ignoring the signal in unobserved patterns.

Low α (1): Observed and unobserved pairs have similar influence. The model treats the data more democratically. Good when interactions are noisy (lots of accidental clicks) or when the "didn't interact" signal is informative (user likely saw the item and chose not to click).

In practice: cross-validate α on held-out nDCG. For Kartify's purchase data (high quality): α ≈ 40–100. For click data (noisy): α ≈ 5–20.
