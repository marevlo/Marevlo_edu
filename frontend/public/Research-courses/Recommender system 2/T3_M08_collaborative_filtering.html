Act II · Build the FoundationsModule 08~30 minThe Big Idea

People who liked
this also liked…

Content-based filtering knows that kurtas are similar to other kurtas. But it can't know that people who buy silk sarees also tend to buy gold jhumka earrings — because sarees and earrings share zero tags. That pattern lives in the behaviour of users, not in the features of items. This module is about learning to read it.

01 / THE GAP
What content-based filtering can't see
Module 07's CBF finds items similar by features. But the most valuable recommendations cross feature boundaries.

Priya buys a Banarasi silk saree on Kartify. Content-based filtering recommends: more silk sarees. Kanjeevaram silk. Patola silk. Chanderi silk. All sarees. All the time.

But here's what the data actually shows: of the 4,200 users who bought a Banarasi silk saree last month, 2,800 also browsed gold jhumka earrings. 1,900 bought matching blouse fabric. 1,200 looked at clutch bags. 800 bought kolhapuri chappals.

None of these items share a single product tag with the saree. CBF will never find them. But the pattern is unmistakable — and it's exactly what Priya needs. She's shopping for an outfit, not just a saree.

This is the fundamental limitation of content-based filtering: it can only see similarity in feature space. It can't see that sarees and earrings go together, because "go together" is a pattern in human behaviour, not in product metadata. To find it, you need a completely different source of information: what other users did.

02 / THE INSIGHT
The collaborative filtering insight
You don't need to know what an item IS. You only need to know who interacted with it — and the structure in that data is enough.

Here's the idea, in one sentence:

The core idea

If users A and B both liked items 1, 2, and 3, and user A also liked item 4 but user B hasn't seen it, then item 4 is probably a good recommendation for user B.

No product features needed. No tags. No descriptions. No images. Just the pattern of who-liked-what. The algorithm doesn't know that items 1–4 are all silk sarees, or that they're all Bollywood movies, or that they're all jazz albums. It doesn't need to. The fact that users who like 1, 2, 3 also tend to like 4 is the signal. The structure in the interaction data is enough.

This insight, first implemented in the GroupLens system at the University of Minnesota in 1994, turned out to be extraordinarily powerful. It discovers cross-category patterns that content-based methods can never find. A user who likes a specific Coltrane album and a specific Bach recording shares a latent taste dimension that has zero lexical overlap. Collaborative filtering finds it. Content-based filtering can't.

Why "collaborative"

It's called collaborative filtering because users are collaborating — unknowingly — to filter the catalog for each other. Every time a user clicks, buys, or rates something, they're providing information that helps the system recommend to other users with similar tastes. The entire user base is a distributed preference-sensing network, and CF is the algorithm that reads it.

04 / COMPARISON
Content-based vs collaborative — two lenses on the same data
CBF asks "what items are similar?" CF asks "what users are similar?" They see different things.
Content-Based (Module 07)
Uses what items ARE
Looks at item features — tags, descriptions, categories, images. Finds items similar to what the user has liked. Never needs data from other users.
"If you liked this floral kurta, here are more floral kurtas."
Collaborative (This module)
Uses what users DO
Looks at interaction patterns — who clicked what, who bought what. Finds items that tend to be liked by the same users. Never needs item features.
"Users who bought this kurta also bought gold earrings."

Neither is universally better. They have complementary strengths and weaknesses:

CBF wins at cold-start items. A new product can be recommended from day one based on its metadata. CF can't recommend it until users interact with it.

CF wins at cross-category discovery. Sarees → earrings, jazz albums → wine, hiking boots → travel insurance. These patterns live only in user behaviour. CBF can never find them.

CBF creates filter bubbles. It only recommends items similar to what you've liked. CF can surface genuinely surprising items — if users similar to you liked something unexpected, CF surfaces it.

CF suffers from the cold-start problem for users. A new user with zero interactions can't be matched to similar users. CBF can personalise from the first click (by matching the clicked item's features to the catalog).

Kartify's pipeline — growing

Kartify's retrieval stage now has three candidate sources: popularity (M06), content-based similarity (M07), and soon collaborative filtering (M09). Each source covers a different regime. Popularity handles cold-start users. CBF handles cold-start items. CF handles cross-category discovery. The ranking stage merges and orders them. This is how real multi-source retrieval works.

05 / TWO PATHS
Two families of collaborative filtering
CF split into two schools early on. Both use the same data. They differ in HOW they extract signal from it.
Family 1
Neighborhood Methods
Find similar items (or similar users) by comparing their interaction vectors directly. Predictions come from aggregating neighbors. Transparent, interpretable, fast at inference. Pre-computable. The "Customers who bought this also bought..." algorithm.
→ Modules 09–10 (next two modules)
Family 2
Latent Factor Methods
Decompose the interaction matrix into low-dimensional user and item embeddings. Predictions come from dot products in latent space. More compact, better generalisation, less interpretable. The matrix factorization family (FunkSVD, BPR-MF, WRMF).
→ Modules 14–18 (coming in Act II)

In production, both families are commonly used — neighborhood methods as a retrieval source (fast, pre-computable, explainable), latent factor methods as the core retrieval or ranking model (better quality, more compact representation). They're not competitors — they're complements.

The next two modules take you deep into neighborhood methods. Module 09 builds item-based kNN for Kartify. Module 10 teaches you the similarity metrics that make or break the algorithm. Then we'll move to latent factors — the matrix factorization family — which is where the deep mathematics and the real power lives.

06 / KARTIFY
What changes for Kartify
CF unlocks a capability that neither popularity nor CBF could provide: cross-category discovery.

Before this module, Kartify's recommendations stayed within category boundaries. If you browsed ethnic wear, you got more ethnic wear. CF changes that. Now Kartify can say: "Users who bought this Banarasi saree also bought gold jhumka earrings, matching blouse fabric, and kolhapuri chappals." Not because those items share tags — but because real users bought them together.

This is the moment Kartify stops being a catalog browser and starts being a discovery engine. The next module builds the specific algorithm that makes it happen.

After this module, you understand the most important idea in recsys:

Collaborative filtering uses patterns in user behaviour to predict preferences — no item features needed.
CF discovers cross-category patterns that content-based methods can never find — sarees → earrings, jazz → wine.
CBF and CF are complementary, not competing. CBF handles cold-start items; CF handles cross-category discovery. Production systems use both.
CF has two families: neighborhood methods (next 2 modules) and latent factor methods (Module 14+).
