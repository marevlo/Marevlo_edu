D1 / FORMALIZATION
The MF objective function
↳ corresponds to M14 S2: R ≈ U·Vᵀ
Real-life analogy

Imagine you're a sculptor carving two sets of puzzle pieces — one set for "people" and one for "things." When you press Person A's piece against Thing B's piece, how well they fit (the dot product) should match whether Person A actually likes Thing B. Your chisel is gradient descent — each training example tells you "these pieces fit too loose / too tight" and you shave a bit off to improve the fit.

MF objective (regularized MSE on observed entries)
$$\mathcal{L} = \sum_{(u,i) \in \Omega} \left(r_{ui} - \mathbf{u}_u^\top \mathbf{v}_i\right)^2 + \lambda \sum_{u} \|\mathbf{u}_u\|^2 + \lambda \sum_{i} \|\mathbf{v}_i\|^2$$
$\Omega$Set of observed (user, item) pairs — the 0.002% of non-empty cells. We ONLY train on these
$r_{ui}$Actual observed value — rating (1–5) or binary (0/1)
$\mathbf{u}_u^\top \mathbf{v}_i$Predicted value — dot product of user and item embeddings. $= \sum_{d=1}^k u_{u,d} \cdot v_{i,d}$
$(r - \hat{r})^2$Squared prediction error for this pair. Summed over all observed pairs
$\lambda \|\cdot\|^2$L2 regularization — prevents embeddings from growing too large. Without this, the model memorizes the training data. $\lambda$ balances fit vs generalization

Gradients — how the model learns

Gradients for SGD
$$e_{ui} = r_{ui} - \mathbf{u}_u^\top \mathbf{v}_i \quad \text{(prediction error)}$$ $$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \mathbf{u}_u} = -2 \, e_{ui} \cdot \mathbf{v}_i + 2\lambda \, \mathbf{u}_u$$ $$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \mathbf{v}_i} = -2 \, e_{ui} \cdot \mathbf{u}_u + 2\lambda \, \mathbf{v}_i$$
$-2e_{ui} \cdot \mathbf{v}_i$Error gradient: if prediction was too low ($e > 0$), move $\mathbf{u}_u$ toward $\mathbf{v}_i$. If too high ($e < 0$), move away
$2\lambda \, \mathbf{u}_u$Regularization gradient: always pulls the embedding toward zero (smaller values). Prevents overfitting
Update rule$\mathbf{u}_u \leftarrow \mathbf{u}_u - \eta \cdot \nabla = \mathbf{u}_u + \eta(e_{ui} \cdot \mathbf{v}_i - \lambda \cdot \mathbf{u}_u)$ (absorbing the factor of 2 into $\eta$)
D2 / WORKED
SGD on a 2×3 matrix — by hand
↳ corresponds to M14 S2: The Decomposition
Train MF on a tiny matrix: 2 users × 3 items, k=2
Every number shown
SETUP
$$R = \begin{bmatrix} 5 & 3 & ? \\ ? & 4 & 2 \end{bmatrix} \quad \Omega = \{(1,A), (1,B), (2,B), (2,C)\}$$

$k = 2$, $\eta = 0.01$, $\lambda = 0.1$. Initialize randomly:

$$\mathbf{u}_1 = [0.5, 0.3], \quad \mathbf{u}_2 = [0.2, 0.6]$$ $$\mathbf{v}_A = [0.8, 0.1], \quad \mathbf{v}_B = [0.4, 0.5], \quad \mathbf{v}_C = [0.1, 0.7]$$
EPOCH 1, STEP 1: (user 1, item A, r=5)
$$\hat{r}_{1A} = \mathbf{u}_1^\top \mathbf{v}_A = 0.5 \times 0.8 + 0.3 \times 0.1 = 0.40 + 0.03 = 0.43$$ $$e_{1A} = 5 - 0.43 = 4.57$$ $$\mathbf{u}_1 \leftarrow [0.5, 0.3] + 0.01 \times (4.57 \times [0.8, 0.1] - 0.1 \times [0.5, 0.3])$$ $$= [0.5, 0.3] + 0.01 \times ([3.656, 0.457] - [0.05, 0.03])$$ $$= [0.5, 0.3] + [0.0361, 0.0043] = [0.536, 0.304]$$ $$\mathbf{v}_A \leftarrow [0.8, 0.1] + 0.01 \times (4.57 \times [0.5, 0.3] - 0.1 \times [0.8, 0.1])$$ $$= [0.8, 0.1] + 0.01 \times ([2.285, 1.371] - [0.08, 0.01])$$ $$= [0.8, 0.1] + [0.0221, 0.0136] = [0.822, 0.114]$$

Both embeddings moved toward each other — Priya liked the item, so her taste and the item's character should align more.

EPOCH 1, STEP 2: (user 1, item B, r=3)
$$\hat{r}_{1B} = 0.536 \times 0.4 + 0.304 \times 0.5 = 0.214 + 0.152 = 0.366$$ $$e_{1B} = 3 - 0.366 = 2.634$$ $$\mathbf{u}_1 \leftarrow [0.536, 0.304] + 0.01 \times (2.634 \times [0.4, 0.5] - 0.1 \times [0.536, 0.304])$$ $$= [0.536, 0.304] + 0.01 \times [1.000, 1.287] = [0.546, 0.317]$$

Smaller adjustment than step 1 because the error (2.634) is smaller — prediction was closer.

AFTER FULL EPOCH

After processing all 4 observed entries once, the embeddings have shifted toward better predictions. After ~50 epochs, the dot products will approximate the observed values closely and will ALSO produce reasonable predictions for the missing entries ($\hat{r}_{1C}$ and $\hat{r}_{2A}$).

The key insight: the model learns to predict unobserved entries by finding latent structure that explains the observed entries. User 1 likes A (5) and B (3). User 2 likes B (4) and dislikes C (2). If the model discovers a "quality" dimension where A > B > C, it can predict User 1 would rate C low and User 2 would rate A high — without ever seeing those entries.

MeaningMF assumes the $1.1\text{T}$-cell matrix can be explained by just 356M numbers. This works because user behavior has low-rank structure — a few dozen taste dimensions explain most of the variance
$k$ choice$k = 128$ is typical. Too low ($k = 8$): underfits, can't capture subtle taste differences. Too high ($k = 512$): overfits on sparse data, and most extra dimensions capture noise. Cross-validate on validation nDCG
Interview: Why does MF work on sparse data?
Interview
PROBLEM

If only 0.002% of entries are observed, how can MF learn from so little data? Doesn't it have more parameters (356M) than training examples (18M)?

SOLUTION

Yes, 356M parameters > 18M observations — the model is overparameterized. Without regularization, it would memorize the training data perfectly and generalize terribly. Three mechanisms prevent this:

1. Regularization ($\lambda$): Penalizes large embedding values, effectively reducing the model's capacity to memorize.

2. Shared structure: Each user embedding is constrained by ALL items they interacted with (not just one). If Priya clicked 8 items, her embedding must simultaneously predict all 8 — this provides 8 constraints on 128 parameters, preventing arbitrary memorization.

3. Low rank: The model is forced to use only $k$ dimensions. With $k = 128$ and billions of possible cells to predict, the model MUST generalize — it can't memorize 1.1T cells with 356M parameters. The low-rank constraint IS the inductive bias that enables generalization from sparse data.

D_EXTRA / INTERVIEW
Additional interview problems — matrix factorization
Problem: Embedding dimension trade-off
Interview
PROBLEM

Kartify has 2.3M users and 480K items. You're choosing the embedding dimension k for MF. What happens if k is too small (k=8) vs too large (k=512)? What's the practical sweet spot and why?

SOLUTION

k=8 (too small): Only 8 latent factors to capture all preference patterns — ethnic vs western, budget vs premium, casual vs formal, male vs female, etc. The model underfits. nDCG plateaus early. But training is fast and the model is small.

k=512 (too large): More parameters than many users have interactions to constrain them. User embeddings overfit to their sparse history. nDCG on training data is high; on test data it drops. Memory: 2.3M × 512 × 4 bytes = 4.7GB just for user embeddings. FAISS index size explodes.

Sweet spot: k=64–128. Enough dimensions to capture the major preference axes without overfitting. Memory is manageable (~1.2GB for k=128). This range consistently wins in grid search across e-commerce datasets. The rule of thumb: k ≈ log₂(min(M, N)) × 8, which for Kartify gives k ≈ log₂(480K) × 8 ≈ 19 × 8 ≈ 152 — close to 128.

Problem: Why MF beats kNN on sparse data
Interview
PROBLEM

On Kartify's data (99.998% sparse), BPR-MF outperforms item-kNN by 25% on nDCG@10. On MovieLens 1M (96% sparse), the gap shrinks to 5%. Explain why sparsity affects the MF-vs-kNN gap.

SOLUTION

kNN relies on co-interactions. Two items are similar only if users interacted with both. At 99.998% sparsity, most item pairs have ZERO co-interactions — the similarity matrix is extremely sparse, and many true similarities are invisible. kNN can only recommend items with direct co-interaction evidence.

MF generalizes transitively. Even if items A and B share zero users, MF can learn they're similar because A shares users with C, C shares users with D, and D shares users with B. The latent factors propagate similarity through chains of intermediate items. At 99.998% sparsity, these transitive chains are MF's primary advantage — they fill in the 99.998% of missing entries that kNN can't reach.

At 96% sparsity (MovieLens), most popular item pairs DO have co-interactions, so kNN can estimate similarities directly. MF's transitive advantage is smaller because direct evidence is available. The sparser the data, the more MF's generalization matters.
