Four popularity formulas —
derived from first principles.

Module 06 showed four popularity variants. This companion derives each formula, explains why exponential decay has a "half-life," derives the Bayesian average from the Beta-Binomial model, and provides a complete from-scratch implementation that compares all four on simulated Kartify data.

📖 Narrative: M06 — Your First Recommender Is Embarrassingly Simple
D1 / DERIVATION
All four popularity formulas — explained variable by variable
↳ corresponds to M06 S2: Four Popularity Variants
Variant 1: Raw count
$$\text{score}(i) = \sum_{e \in \text{events}(i)} 1 = |\text{events}(i)|$$
$\text{events}(i)$All interactions with item $i$ across all time — every click, purchase, view, ever
ProblemA product listed 2 years ago with 10,000 total clicks outranks a product listed yesterday with 200 clicks — even if yesterday's product has 10× higher click rate. No freshness
Variant 2: Time-windowed count
$$\text{score}(i) = \sum_{e \in \text{events}(i)} \mathbb{1}[\text{age}(e) \leq W]$$
$\mathbb{1}[\cdot]$Indicator function — equals 1 if the condition is true, 0 otherwise. "Only count events from the last $W$ days"
$W$Window size in days. For Kartify fashion: $W = 7\text{–}14$ days. For news: $W = 1$ day. For movies: $W = 30$ days
ProblemHard cutoff — an event at day $W-1$ counts fully, an event at day $W+1$ counts zero. No smooth transition
Variant 3: Exponential decay
$$\text{score}(i) = \sum_{e \in \text{events}(i)} e^{-\lambda \cdot \text{age}(e)}$$
$e^{-\lambda \cdot \text{age}(e)}$Decay weight for event $e$. Age = number of days since the event. A recent event (age=0) has weight $e^0 = 1.0$. An old event has weight near 0. The decay is SMOOTH — no hard cutoff
$\lambda$Decay rate. Higher $\lambda$ = faster forgetting. Lower $\lambda$ = longer memory. Controls the "half-life" (see D2 below)
Variant 4: Bayesian average
$$\text{score}(i) = \frac{C \cdot m + \sum_{j=1}^{n_i} r_j}{C + n_i}$$
$m$Global average rating across ALL items. Acts as the "prior belief" about any item's quality before seeing data
$C$Prior strength — "how many fake ratings equal to the global average do we pretend each item has?" Higher $C$ = more shrinkage toward the mean. Typical: $C = 25\text{–}100$
$n_i$Number of actual ratings for item $i$
$\sum r_j$Sum of actual ratings
BehaviorWhen $n_i \ll C$: score ≈ $m$ (shrinks to global mean — don't trust small samples). When $n_i \gg C$: score ≈ $\bar{r}_i$ (trust the item's own average). IMDB Top 250 uses exactly this formula
D2 / DERIVATION
Exponential decay — the half-life
↳ corresponds to M06 S2: Exponential Decay variant
Real-life analogy

Think of radioactive decay. A radioactive atom has a "half-life" — the time it takes for half the atoms to decay. Carbon-14 has a half-life of 5,730 years. After 5,730 years, half remains. After 11,460 years, a quarter remains. Exponential decay in popularity scoring works identically: the "half-life" is how many days it takes for an interaction to lose half its weight.

Half-life derivation
$$\text{Half-life} = t_{1/2} = \frac{\ln 2}{\lambda} = \frac{0.693}{\lambda}$$
$t_{1/2}$The number of days after which an interaction's weight drops to 50% of its original value
DerivationSet $e^{-\lambda t} = 0.5$. Take $\ln$: $-\lambda t = \ln(0.5) = -0.693$. Solve: $t = 0.693 / \lambda$
Worked: Choose λ for Kartify's fashion domain
Numerical
DESIRED HALF-LIFE

Fashion trends on Kartify cycle every ~2 weeks. We want a half-life of 7 days — an interaction from last week has 50% weight, from two weeks ago has 25%, from a month ago has 6.25%.

$$\lambda = \frac{\ln 2}{t_{1/2}} = \frac{0.693}{7} = 0.099 \approx 0.10$$
VERIFY: WEIGHT AT VARIOUS AGES
$$\text{Today (age=0):} \quad e^{-0.1 \times 0} = 1.000 \quad (100\%)$$ $$\text{3 days ago:} \quad e^{-0.1 \times 3} = e^{-0.3} = 0.741 \quad (74\%)$$ $$\text{7 days ago:} \quad e^{-0.1 \times 7} = e^{-0.7} = 0.497 \quad (50\% — half-life ✓)$$ $$\text{14 days ago:} \quad e^{-0.1 \times 14} = e^{-1.4} = 0.247 \quad (25\%)$$ $$\text{30 days ago:} \quad e^{-0.1 \times 30} = e^{-3.0} = 0.050 \quad (5\%)$$

A click from a month ago contributes only 5% of the weight of a click from today. This matches Kartify's fashion dynamics — last month's viral dress is mostly irrelevant to this week's trending page.

D3 / DERIVATION
Bayesian average — from the Beta-Binomial model
↳ corresponds to M06 S2: Bayesian Average variant
Real-life analogy

A new restaurant opens with 2 reviews, both 5-star. Does it deserve to outrank a restaurant with 500 reviews averaging 4.5 stars? Probably not — two reviews are too few to be reliable. The Bayesian average "pretends" each restaurant starts with some fake average-quality reviews, so a new restaurant with 2 amazing reviews still gets pulled toward the average. With 500 real reviews, the fake ones barely matter.

Bayesian average — derived from conjugate prior
$$\text{score}(i) = \frac{C \cdot m + n_i \cdot \bar{r}_i}{C + n_i} = \frac{C}{C + n_i} \cdot m + \frac{n_i}{C + n_i} \cdot \bar{r}_i$$
Second formA WEIGHTED AVERAGE of the global mean $m$ and the item's own mean $\bar{r}_i$. The weights are $\frac{C}{C+n_i}$ (prior) and $\frac{n_i}{C+n_i}$ (data). These sum to 1
When $n_i = 0$Score = $m$ (the global average). With no data, our best guess is the average item
When $n_i = C$Score = $(m + \bar{r}_i) / 2$. Prior and data get equal weight — 50/50 blend
When $n_i \gg C$Score $\approx \bar{r}_i$. The item's own average dominates. With enough data, the prior doesn't matter
Worked: Bayesian average on 3 Kartify products
Numerical
SETUP

Global average across all Kartify products: $m = 3.8$ stars. Prior strength: $C = 50$.

PRODUCT A: New arrival, 3 reviews, avg 5.0
$$\text{score}(A) = \frac{50 \times 3.8 + 3 \times 5.0}{50 + 3} = \frac{190 + 15}{53} = \frac{205}{53} = 3.868$$

Despite a perfect 5.0 average, the Bayesian score is only 3.87 — pulled heavily toward the global mean because 3 reviews is too few to trust.

PRODUCT B: Established, 200 reviews, avg 4.3
$$\text{score}(B) = \frac{50 \times 3.8 + 200 \times 4.3}{50 + 200} = \frac{190 + 860}{250} = \frac{1050}{250} = 4.200$$

With 200 reviews, the Bayesian score (4.20) is close to the item's true average (4.30). The prior barely pulls it toward the mean.

RANKING

Product B (score 4.20) outranks Product A (score 3.87) — even though A has a higher raw average (5.0 vs 4.3). This is correct behavior: we should trust 200 reviews over 3 reviews. Without Bayesian averaging, A would be ranked #1 on the basis of 3 reviews — a noise-driven mistake identical to the kNN shrinkage problem from Module 11.

D4 / FROM SCRATCH
All four popularity variants — complete implementation
↳ corresponds to M06 S3: Building It
# ═══ ALL 4 POPULARITY VARIANTS — from scratch ═══
import numpy as np
from collections import defaultdict
from datetime import datetime, timedelta

def raw_count(events):
    """Variant 1: total interaction count per item."""
    counts = defaultdict(int)
    for item_id, _ in events:
        counts[item_id] += 1
    return sorted(counts, key=counts.get, reverse=True)

def time_windowed(events, now, window_days=7):
    """Variant 2: count events within last W days only."""
    cutoff = now - timedelta(days=window_days)
    counts = defaultdict(int)
    for item_id, ts in events:
        if ts >= cutoff:
            counts[item_id] += 1
    return sorted(counts, key=counts.get, reverse=True)

def exponential_decay(events, now, lam=0.1):
    """Variant 3: decay-weighted sum. lam controls half-life."""
    scores = defaultdict(float)
    for item_id, ts in events:
        age_days = (now - ts).total_seconds() / 86400
        scores[item_id] += np.exp(-lam * age_days)
    return sorted(scores, key=scores.get, reverse=True)

def bayesian_average(item_ratings, C=50):
    """Variant 4: Bayesian-shrunk average. Needs explicit ratings."""
    # Compute global mean
    all_ratings = [r for ratings in item_ratings.values() for r in ratings]
    m = np.mean(all_ratings)
    # Score each item
    scores = {}
    for item_id, ratings in item_ratings.items():
        n = len(ratings)
        scores[item_id] = (C * m + sum(ratings)) / (C + n)
    return sorted(scores, key=scores.get, reverse=True)

# ═══ COMPARE ON SIMULATED DATA ═══
# Item A: old hit (1000 clicks, mostly 30+ days ago)
# Item B: recent trend (200 clicks, all in last 5 days)
# Raw count: A wins (1000 > 200)
# Time-windowed (7d): B wins (200 > ~30)
# Exponential decay (λ=0.1): B wins (~200 > ~100)
D5 / INTERVIEW
Interview problems — popularity baselines
↳ tests M06 concepts
Problem 1: Choose the decay rate
Interview
PROBLEM

A news platform wants trending articles. An article published 6 hours ago should have 75% of the weight of one published just now. What $\lambda$ should they use (in per-hour units)?

SOLUTION
$$e^{-\lambda \times 6} = 0.75 \quad \Rightarrow \quad -6\lambda = \ln(0.75) = -0.2877$$ $$\lambda = \frac{0.2877}{6} = 0.048 \text{ per hour}$$ $$\text{Half-life} = \frac{0.693}{0.048} = 14.4 \text{ hours}$$

An article's weight halves every ~14 hours. An article from yesterday morning has ~25% weight. This is appropriate for news — much faster decay than Kartify's fashion ($t_{1/2} = 7$ days).

Problem 2: Bayesian average shrinkage
Interview
PROBLEM

A product has 5 reviews all rated 5.0. Global mean is 3.5, $C = 100$. Another product has 500 reviews averaging 4.2. Which ranks higher? At what review count would the 5-star product overtake?

SOLUTION
$$\text{5-star product: } \frac{100 \times 3.5 + 5 \times 5.0}{105} = \frac{375}{105} = 3.571$$ $$\text{500-review product: } \frac{100 \times 3.5 + 500 \times 4.2}{600} = \frac{2450}{600} = 4.083$$

The 500-review product (4.08) ranks far above the 5-star product (3.57). To overtake:

$$\frac{100 \times 3.5 + n \times 5.0}{100 + n} > 4.083 \quad \Rightarrow \quad 350 + 5n > 408.3 + 4.083n$$ $$0.917n > 58.3 \quad \Rightarrow \quad n > 63.6 \quad \Rightarrow \quad n \geq 64 \text{ reviews}$$

The 5-star product needs 64 reviews (all 5-star!) to overtake. This is the Bayesian average working correctly: small samples are distrusted, and you need substantial evidence to claim a product is truly exceptional.
